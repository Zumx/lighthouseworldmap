Lighthouse-hopping is one of the most scenic road trips there is — a coastline
strung with towers, each with a view and a story. It just needs planning around
tides, access, and the gap between "see it" and "climb it."

## Step 1: Pick a lighthouse coast

Lighthouses cluster along historic shipping coasts: Maine and the Outer Banks
in the US, the Norwegian and Scottish coasts, Brittany, the Great Lakes.
Choose one stretch and follow it. Use the [map](/) to see how the towers line
up along the shore.

## Step 2: Sort access type per light

Three categories, very different trips: climbable/open (towers and quarters
museums — the core of the trip), exterior-only (photogenic stops), and
offshore/remote (boat or hike, or view-only). Plan the route around the
climbable ones and use the others as scenic punctuation.

## Step 3: Plan around tides and boats

Many of the best lights sit on tidal islands, causeways, or skerries reachable
only at low water or by a weather-dependent boat. Build the itinerary around
tide tables and confirmed crossings — this is the single most overlooked
lighthouse-planning factor.

## Step 4: Book climbs and stays ahead

Climbable towers sell timed tickets with strict group sizes that fill in
season. And many restored lighthouses are now guesthouses — staying in one is
the highlight of any lighthouse trip, but they book months ahead. Reserve
these first; build the route around them.

## Step 5: Sequence for light

Lighthouses photograph best at golden hour and look their part at dusk when
the lamp shows. Plan to reach the marquee tower for sunrise or sunset and use
midday for travel and the museums. Light is the trip.

## Step 6: Pace it realistically

Lighthouses are quick visits (30–60 minutes plus a climb), so a coast day can
take in several — but coastal roads are slow and winding. Three or four lights
plus one climb is a full, satisfying day.

## Step 7: Pack for the coast

Wind- and waterproof layers (lighthouse headlands are exposed and weather flips
fast), flat grippy shoes for tower stairs, sun protection, and tide-table and
ferry info offline. Storm-watching trips need extra warmth and patience.

## Step 8: Respect the sea and the sites

Stay back from surf and cliff edges (the dangerous sea is the whole reason
these exist), follow seabird-nesting rules, respect volunteer-run heritage
sites, and pay entry — it keeps the lamps and the doors open.

## Put it together

Coast, then access type, then tides and boats, then booked climbs and stays.
Open the [map](/), trace the shore, and a lighthouse trip becomes a beautifully
structured coastal journey.
