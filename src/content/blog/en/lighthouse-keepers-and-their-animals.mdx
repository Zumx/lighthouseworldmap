Solitude was the defining condition of the lighthouse keeper's life, and into that solitude crept companions of every kind. For the men and families posted to remote towers and wave-swept rocks, an animal was far more than a pet. It was company against the crushing loneliness, a worker that earned its keep, a barometer of mood and weather, and sometimes a lifeline. The history of lighthouses is threaded through with the stories of these creatures — cats curled by the lamp, dogs that learned to ring the fog bell, goats that supplied milk on a barren islet, and birds that arrived exhausted from the dark and stayed.

## The keeper's cat

The cat was the most universal lighthouse animal, and for sound practical reasons. Stations stockpiled flour, oil, rope and provisions for months at a time, and where there are stores there are rodents. A working cat earned its passage by keeping rats and mice away from food and from the tarred cordage they loved to gnaw. The keepers of Scotland's offshore rock lights, served by the Northern Lighthouse Board, frequently kept cats for exactly this purpose, and accounts survive of individual cats credited with clearing infestations that had threatened a station's supplies. Beyond their work, cats suited the rhythm of the place — patient, self-contained, content to doze through the long daylight hours and prowl while the keeper kept his night watch beside the lamp.

## Dogs on the rock

Dogs brought a different gift: devotion, alertness and an extra set of senses tuned to danger. On exposed stations a dog's bark could warn of an approaching boat, a shift in the weather, or a stranger long before a human noticed. Some became famous in their own small way. The story of Milton, the dog of the Tillamook Rock light off Oregon, and similar tales from stations around the world, speak of animals that adapted entirely to a life most dogs would never know — no fields to run in, only a slab of rock ringed by ocean. Keepers' families on more accessible stations kept dogs as straightforward companions for children growing up far from any school or playmate, the animal becoming a sibling of sorts in an unusually small world.

## Goats, cows and the milk problem

Fresh milk was a luxury that supply boats could rarely deliver, and on stations with even a scrap of grazing the answer was to keep a goat or, where space allowed, a cow. Goats were ideal for the purpose: hardy, undemanding, able to live on coarse island vegetation and the kitchen scraps a small household produced. They gave milk for children and for the endless cups of tea that punctuated a keeper's day, and they asked little in return. On some islands the goats became semi-wild, clattering across the rocks and having to be coaxed back for milking. A cow was a greater commitment, demanding hay to be landed and a byre to be built, and only the larger island and headland stations with proper pasture could justify one.

## Birds, wild and tamed

Lighthouses sit on the great migratory highways of the sky, and on dark or foggy nights the lamp drew exhausted birds out of the night by the hundred. Keepers recorded the toll of those that struck the glass, but they also took in survivors, nursing storm-blown waifs back to health and sometimes keeping them. Caged songbirds were common comforts, their music a thread of ordinary life in an extraordinary place. Some keepers became accidental ornithologists, their meticulous logs of which species passed and when forming valuable early records of migration. The relationship ran both ways: the light that killed could also be the place where a man learned, season by season, the secret traffic of the air above his rock.

## Working animals and the supply line

On the larger shore and island stations, animals did the heavy hauling that machinery would later take over. Ponies and donkeys carried oil, coal and stores up steep tracks from the landing place to the tower, a back-breaking job in the days before mechanised hoists. Horses turned the carts that brought supplies overland to mainland lights. These working animals were as much part of the establishment as the lamp itself, stabled, fed and cared for on the official ration, their health a matter of real concern because the whole logistics of the station could depend on a single sure-footed pony picking its way up a cliff path in a gale.

## Companionship and the cost of leaving

The bond between keeper and animal carried a particular weight in such isolation, and so did its breaking. Transfers were a fact of service life; a keeper might be moved from one station to another at short notice, and not every new posting could take an animal. Pets were given away, left with relieving keepers, or in the hardest cases simply could not make the journey across open water. The grief of those partings runs quietly through keepers' memoirs. An animal had shared the worst of the loneliness, the storms that shook the tower, the months without another human face, and to lose it was to lose the one creature that had understood the strange shape of that life.

## What the animals left behind

The age of the resident keeper has almost entirely passed, automation having emptied the towers one by one through the late twentieth century. With the keepers went their animals, and the byres, kennels and pony tracks fell silent. Yet the stories endure, preserved in station logs, family photographs and the memoirs of the last generation to live by the light. They remind us that a lighthouse was never only a machine for warning ships. It was a home, often a precarious one, and like every home it filled itself with living things — purring, barking, bleating company that made the long watches bearable and turned a cold rock in the sea into a place where life, against every odd, went on.
