## Keepers Who Went Out

Lighthouse keepers were not, strictly speaking, rescue workers. Their duty was to maintain the light and the station, to log weather and shipping, and to ensure the apparatus functioned correctly through the night. But they lived at positions chosen precisely because ships wrecked there, and when a wreck occurred within sight of the lighthouse, the question of whether to attempt a rescue fell inevitably to whoever was at the station. The men and women who acted — who launched open boats in conditions that broke trained seamen, who waded into surf, who held lines and hauled survivors — did so as individuals responding to an emergency, not as agents of a formal rescue service. Several of them became famous for it.

The rescue tradition at lighthouses is older than any organised coastguard. Before the Royal National Institution for the Preservation of Life from Shipwreck (the precursor to the RNLI) was founded in 1824, and long before the US Lifesaving Service was formalised in the 1870s, lighthouse keepers were often the only people positioned and equipped — in the most minimal sense — to reach survivors of a wreck. This history is part of what makes the lighthouse something more than an engineering structure. It is also a record of individual courage at the water's edge.

## Grace Darling and the Forfarshire

On 7 September 1838, the paddle steamer SS Forfarshire, travelling from Hull to Dundee with sixty-three passengers and crew, ran into a severe gale in the Farne Islands off the Northumberland coast. The vessel's engines failed, and in the early hours of the morning she struck the Big Harcar rocks close to Longstone lighthouse, where the lighthouse keeper William Darling lived with his family.

Darling and his twenty-two-year-old daughter Grace were woken by the sound of the wreck. At first light they could see survivors clinging to the exposed portion of the rock. A party of nine — including a woman and her two children, who died before help arrived — was visible on the rock. The sea between Longstone and Big Harcar, some 1,400 metres of open water in a Force 9 gale, was breaking heavily over all the shallows.

William and Grace Darling launched the station's coble — a flat-bottomed open rowing boat about seven metres long — and rowed to the rock, taking the first survivors back to the lighthouse before William and two of the rescued sailors returned to collect the remainder. Grace rowed the boat and steadied it against the rock while her father and a rescued man climbed over the bow to help the survivors aboard. The operation required seamanship of a high order: the coble had to be held in position on a breaking sea by continuous rowing, without a motor and without modern safety equipment.

Five men and one woman survived. Grace Darling became immediately famous in Britain, the subject of paintings, ballads, and newspaper accounts that made her a national figure. She was offered money by theatre impresarios, proposed to by strangers, and awarded a gold medal by the Royal Humane Society. She died of tuberculosis in 1842, aged twenty-six, having received more public attention than almost any other woman in Britain for several years. Her coble is preserved at the Grace Darling Museum in Bamburgh, and Longstone lighthouse remains active, automated since 1990, its red lantern visible at seventeen nautical miles.

## Ida Lewis of Lime Rock

Ida Lewis began assisting in rescues from Lime Rock lighthouse in Narragansett Bay, Rhode Island, in her teens, eventually accumulating a record of eighteen confirmed saves over a career that spanned four decades. The precise tally is disputed — contemporary accounts inflated the number, and some rescues she claimed to have performed alone were likely accomplished with assistance — but the core of the record is well-documented.

Her best-known rescue occurred in 1869, when she pulled two soldiers from a capsized boat in the bay. The story attracted enormous press coverage and prompted a visit to the lighthouse by former President Ulysses S. Grant. Lewis received the Congressional Gold Medal and a pension from the state of Rhode Island. She continued as keeper of Lime Rock — where she had been the effective keeper since her father suffered a stroke when she was eighteen — until her own death at the station in 1911. The lighthouse was renamed Ida Lewis Rock lighthouse in her honour in 1924.

Lewis's rescues involved swimming and wading as much as boat work; the shallow water around Lime Rock made launching a boat impractical in some situations. She was noted for her physical strength and absolute composure in the water, qualities she attributed to a childhood spent rowing the bay in all conditions.

## Abbie Burgess and the Maine Coast

Abbie Burgess was the teenage daughter of the keeper of Matinicus Rock lighthouse, twenty-five miles offshore in Penobscot Bay, Maine. In January 1856, her father was absent for supplies when a severe winter storm struck the rock. Abbie, seventeen years old, maintained both lighthouse towers on the island — Matinicus Rock then had two separate towers — through a storm that lasted nearly four weeks, during which time the sea washed over the entire rock repeatedly and destroyed the keeper's dwelling. She moved her mother and her younger siblings to the stronger of the two lighthouse towers and continued to tend the lights through the entire period. The oil she used was almost exhausted by the time her father returned.

Burgess continued in lighthouse service for the rest of her working life, eventually marrying the son of the lighthouse keeper who succeeded her father at Matinicus Rock, and serving with her husband at Whitehead lighthouse on the Maine coast until her retirement. She died in 1892. Her story was rediscovered in the twentieth century and has been the subject of several books and a children's play.

## The Gallant Lifesaving Keepers

Many rescues that became locally famous never received national attention. The records of Trinity House and the Commissioners of Northern Lighthouses contain dozens of accounts of keepers who launched boats in extreme conditions, of keepers' wives who managed the light alone while their husbands attempted rescues, of signals exchanged between stricken vessels and lighthouse stations that delayed fatal groundings long enough for help to reach the scene.

At Lundy island in the Bristol Channel, multiple keepers over the nineteenth century assisted in rescues from vessels that failed to clear the island in contrary winds. The landing at Lundy was so difficult in bad weather that the lighthouse service eventually installed a derrick to lift supplies directly from the tender to the cliff edge — the same cliff from which keepers sometimes descended by rope to reach survivors on the rocks below.

At Cape Florida lighthouse in Biscayne Bay, keeper John Thompson and his assistant Aaron Carter survived a Seminole attack in 1836 in which Carter was killed and Thompson was badly burned after taking refuge in the lighthouse tower. Thompson attracted the attention of a passing naval vessel by firing signal rockets from the damaged lantern room, and was rescued after spending the night on the iron deck of the burned-out gallery. The lighthouse was rebuilt and Thompson eventually returned to lighthouse work elsewhere on the Florida coast.

## Rescue Apparatus at Lighthouse Stations

From the middle of the nineteenth century, lighthouse stations in Britain and the United States were increasingly equipped with rocket apparatus — the means to fire a line from shore to a stranded vessel so that survivors could haul themselves ashore in a breeches buoy. The Manby apparatus, developed around 1808, used a mortar to throw a weighted line; later designs used rockets. The breeches buoy itself, a ring life buoy with canvas shorts sewn through it that allowed a person to be hauled over the water, was the standard rescue tool for survivors who could not be approached by boat.

Training in rocket apparatus use was part of the duties at many lighthouse stations, particularly those on exposed sandy beaches or rocky coasts where the wreck of a sailing vessel might occur within range of a shore party but beyond the reach of a lifeboat. The combination of the lighthouse — which might warn the ship too late to prevent grounding — and the rocket apparatus — which could then connect the grounded vessel to the shore — made the lighthouse station a two-function facility: warning and rescue.

Modern lighthouse stations are automated, and the coastguard and lifeboat functions have been separated entirely from lighthouse operation. The rescue tradition of the lighthouse keeper belongs to the period before this specialisation. It represents a time when geography determined responsibility: if you lived at the lighthouse, you were, by default, the first and sometimes the only responder when the sea took a ship.
