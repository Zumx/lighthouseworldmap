The Åland Islands lie scattered across the mouth of the Gulf of Bothnia, a Swedish-speaking, self-governing region of Finland made up of some six and a half thousand islands and skerries. Sitting astride the historic sea lane between Stockholm and the Finnish and Baltic coasts, Åland has always been a place of pilots, ferrymen, and lighthouse keepers. Its waters are shallow, granite-strewn, and notoriously treacherous, frozen solid in hard winters and laced with channels that demanded careful marking. The lights of Åland range from lonely beacons far out in the open sea to elegant towers guarding the busy approaches to Mariehamn, and together they tell the story of a maritime culture built on sailing ships, herring, and the long crossing to the Swedish shore.

## Bogskär, the Loneliest Light

Far to the south of the main archipelago, on a low and barely visible rock in the open Baltic, stands the lighthouse of Bogskär, long reckoned the most isolated lighthouse in all of Finland. Built in 1882 and rebuilt after the wars, the slender tower rises directly from a skerry that all but disappears in heavy weather, more than fifty kilometres from the nearest inhabited island. Supplying it and relieving its keepers was a perpetual struggle, and storms could cut the light off for weeks. Today it is automated, a remote sentinel on the open route between Åland and the Baltic states, but it remains a byword among Finnish mariners for loneliness and exposure.

## Märket, the Border Lighthouse

On a bare skerry in the strait between Åland and the Swedish coast stands Märket, perhaps the most curious lighthouse in the Baltic. The rock is split by the Finnish-Swedish border, and when the lighthouse was built in 1885 it was placed, by accident, on the Swedish side of the line. The frontier was later redrawn in a famous zigzag so that the tower stood on Finnish soil while neither country lost any territory overall. The round stone tower, weathered and grey, marks a dangerous low rock that had wrecked many ships, and it is one of the most photographed and storied lights in the whole region.

## Lågskär, South of the Main Island

South of the Åland mainland, the lighthouse of Lågskär guards the approach from the open Baltic toward Mariehamn and the inner archipelago. The tall tower, first lit in the nineteenth century, stands on a low island that was once home to a small community of keepers and their families. The waters here are exposed to the full reach of the southern Baltic, and the light marks the point where ships bound for Åland leave the open sea and enter the sheltered channels. The island today is a quiet nature reserve, much visited by migrating birds.

## Nyhamn off Mariehamn

Close to the entrance of the main shipping channel into Mariehamn, the capital, the lighthouse of Nyhamn marks the seaward approach to Åland's only town. The elegant tower stands among a cluster of small islands at the threshold of the route that the great ferries from Sweden and Finland still follow daily. The pilot station here was long the busiest in the archipelago, guiding sailing ships and later steamers through the maze of skerries. The surrounding islets are a popular destination for small boats from Mariehamn in summer.

## Skälskär and the Inner Channels

Among the inner waters of the archipelago, the small light of Skälskär marks one of the channels threading between the larger islands. These inner lights, often little more than a beacon on a low rock, were essential to the local traffic of small cargo vessels, fishing boats, and the post-boats that carried mail and passengers between the islands before the age of car ferries and bridges. They speak to a maritime life lived entirely among the skerries, where the sea was the only road.

## Kobba Klintar, the Pilot Station

At the seaward edge of the Mariehamn approach stands Kobba Klintar, a rocky islet crowned by an old pilot lookout and beacon that has become an emblem of Åland itself. Generations of pilots watched from here for incoming sailing ships, rowing out in all weathers to guide them through the rocks. The distinctive buildings, lovingly restored, now house a small museum and café reachable by boat from Mariehamn, and the islet is one of the most beloved excursion spots in the whole archipelago, a symbol of Åland's seafaring past.

## Understen, on the Swedish Side of the Strait

Across the strait toward the Swedish coast, the lighthouse of Understen marks the approach from the Stockholm archipelago toward Åland. While it stands on the Swedish side of the boundary, it works in concert with the Åland lights to guide ships across the open water of the Sea of Åland, the deep strait that separates the islands from mainland Sweden. The crossing here, exposed and often rough, has always been the most demanding part of the route between the two countries.

## Flötjan and the Northern Approaches

In the northern reaches of the archipelago, toward the Gulf of Bothnia proper, the light of Flötjan marks the channels leading north past the islands. The northern approaches to Åland are a confusion of low skerries and shallow banks, and the lights here guided the timber ships and coastal traders that worked the route between the Bothnian ports and the Baltic. The waters freeze early and thaw late, and in deep winter the whole northern archipelago could be locked in ice.

## Korsö Tower

Near the entrance to the Mariehamn channels stands the old stone tower on Korsö, a daymark and beacon that has guided ships toward the harbour for generations. The tapering granite tower is a landmark visible far out to sea, and like Kobba Klintar it has become part of the iconography of seafaring Åland. The surrounding islets, with their wind-bent pines and bare pink granite, are characteristic of the outer archipelago landscape.

## Sälskär, the Northern Sentinel

Far to the northwest of the main island, the lighthouse of Sälskär stands on a remote skerry guarding the open approach from the Sea of Åland and the Gulf of Bothnia. One of the more isolated of the manned Åland lights, it marked a route used by sailing ships and later by the great steel barques of the Åland grain fleet, the last commercial sailing ships in Europe, which carried wheat from Australia and were based in Mariehamn well into the twentieth century. The light is a reminder of the days when Åland's small towns sent square-riggers around the world.
