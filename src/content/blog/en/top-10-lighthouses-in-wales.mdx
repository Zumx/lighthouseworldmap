The Welsh coast presents a concentration of navigational hazard that has occupied Trinity House and its predecessors since the earliest days of organised lighthouse administration in Britain. The approaches to Liverpool, once the busiest port in the world, ran directly past the Anglesey coast and the treacherous Skerries; the Irish Sea itself was crossed by the main passenger and mail routes between Britain and Ireland, where fog, Atlantic weather, and tidal races around headlands like the Pembrokeshire coast made navigation genuinely dangerous. Pembrokeshire's position at the corner where the Bristol Channel, the Irish Sea, and the Atlantic approaches converge made it the site of several of the most important lights on the British coast. Welsh lighthouses tend toward the robust and functional — these are working structures, not showpieces — and many are in settings of exceptional natural beauty. ## 1. South Stack Lighthouse, Anglesey

South Stack Lighthouse stands on a small island separated from Holyhead Mountain by a 100-metre tidal channel, reached by a suspension footbridge and 400 steps cut into the clifftop. Built in 1809 by Trinity House engineer Daniel Alexander, the 28-metre white tower marks the South Stack and the treacherous North Anglesey coast for vessels making the approach to the Irish Sea from the south. The light — Fl(4) W 20s, range 28 nautical miles — covers the Skerries approaches to the northwest and the tidal race off the Stack itself. The clifftop RSPB reserve above the lighthouse holds one of the largest concentrations of nesting seabirds in Wales: razorbills, guillemots, puffins, choughs, and peregrine falcons all breed on the vertical faces. The lighthouse is open to visitors; the approach involves a steep descent, and the footbridge is closed in high winds. The setting — white tower, sea-bird noise, Atlantic horizon — is among the most dramatic of any accessible lighthouse in Britain.

## 2. Strumble Head Lighthouse, Pembrokeshire

Strumble Head Lighthouse, completed in 1908 on the remote volcanic headland near Fishguard, guards the northern Pembrokeshire coast and the approach to Fishguard Harbour, the port for the Rosslare ferry. The 17-metre white tower stands on a tiny island, Ynys Meicel, connected to the headland by a footbridge. Its light — Fl(4) W 15s, range 26 nautical miles — covers the offshore approaches and the Pembrokeshire coast's northwestern shoulder. The headland is one of the finest cetacean-watching points in Wales; harbour porpoise, common dolphins and occasional minke whales pass regularly, and the tidal races off the head attract large concentrations of seabirds in late summer. The lighthouse is automated; the keeper's cottage and engine house are visible from the coastal path footbridge vantage point. The drive through the dramatic volcanic landscape of the Pencaer peninsula to reach the head is rewarding in itself.

## 3. Point Lynas Lighthouse, Anglesey

Point Lynas Lighthouse, on the northeastern tip of Anglesey, has guided ships into the Liverpool approaches for nearly 200 years. The first light here was a private establishment operated by the Society of Merchants of Liverpool from 1781; Trinity House took over when the lighthouse was rebuilt in 1835, producing the low, castellated white complex that stands today — part lighthouse, part coastguard station, part pilot lookout, the whole resembling a miniature castle on the headland. The 11-metre tower's light — Oc W 10s, range 20 nautical miles — marks the turning point where vessels heading for Liverpool begin their passage through the North Channel between Anglesey and the North Wales coast. Pilots historically boarded Liverpool-bound vessels here; the distinctive character of the station architecture reflects this dual navigational and pilotage function. The headland is accessible from the village of Amlwch.

## 4. Bardsey Island Lighthouse, Gwynedd

Bardsey Island — Ynys Enlli in Welsh — lies two miles off the tip of the Llŷn Peninsula, separated from the mainland by Bardsey Sound, one of the most turbulent tidal passages in Wales. The lighthouse, completed in 1821 and designed by engineer Joseph Nelson for Trinity House, is a square red-banded tower 30 metres tall, the only square tower lighthouse in Wales. The light — Fl(5) W 15s, range 26 nautical miles — marks the island and the sound's violent tidal stream for vessels rounding the Llŷn tip. Bardsey was a major medieval pilgrimage site — three pilgrimages to Bardsey were said to equal one to Rome — and the island retains a small permanent community. Boat trips from Aberdaron run in calm weather during the summer months; the island has no road access, and the sound's tidal streams can prevent landing even in summer. The lighthouse keepers' cottages are available for rent through the Bardsey Island Trust.

## 5. Skokholm Lighthouse, Pembrokeshire

Skokholm Lighthouse, on the southwestern corner of Skokholm island off the Pembrokeshire coast, marks the southern approach to St George's Channel and the entry to the Bristol Channel. The island, one of Britain's first bird observatories, is administered by the Wildlife Trust of South and West Wales and hosts the lighthouse, a 23-metre white tower completed in 1916, alongside research accommodation. The light — Fl(3) W 10s, range 20 nautical miles — covers the offshore approaches and works with the Smalls Lighthouse further out to sea to bracket the southern approach to the Irish Sea. Skokholm supports one of Europe's largest breeding colonies of Manx shearwater — over 100,000 pairs — and Atlantic storm petrels nest in the stone walls. Visitor places on the island are limited and booked well in advance for summer weeks; the combination of lighthouse, seabirds, and isolation makes it one of the most memorable wildlife experiences in Wales.

## 6. The Smalls Lighthouse, Pembrokeshire

The Smalls is a reef 20 miles west of Pembrokeshire in the open Irish Sea, and its lighthouse occupies a place in maritime lore as dark as any in British history. The first lighthouse here, built by Henry Whiteside in 1776 as an eccentric timber structure on iron piles, was the site of a terrible episode when one of two resident keepers died and his partner, fearing he would be accused of murder if he disposed of the body, kept the corpse on the platform for weeks — an experience that so disturbed him that Trinity House subsequently required a minimum of three keepers on any isolated station. The current granite tower, designed by James Walker and completed in 1861, is 41 metres tall; its light — Fl(3) W 15s, range 18 nautical miles — is visible well offshore. The lighthouse is automated and inaccessible to visitors.

## 7. Nash Point Lighthouse, Vale of Glamorgan

Nash Point Lighthouse, completed in 1832 on the Glamorgan Heritage Coast south of Cardiff, guards the Nash Sands, a notorious shoal extending southeast into the Bristol Channel's tidal stream. The 37-metre white tower is Trinity House's dominant structure on the Welsh side of the Bristol Channel; its light — Fl(2) WR 15s, range 21 white, 16 red nautical miles — marks the sands with a red sector. The Bristol Channel has one of the highest tidal ranges in the world — up to 15 metres at spring tides — and the Nash Sands' position across the main westward traffic lane has made it perpetually dangerous. The lighthouse is managed by Trinity House and is open for tours during summer open days; the Heritage Coast path along the clifftop gives excellent views of the tower.

## 8. Mumbles Lighthouse, Swansea

The Mumbles Lighthouse on Mumbles Head at the western entrance to Swansea Bay was established in 1794 — the first lighthouse in Wales — on the outer island of the Mumbles headland, accessible at low tide across the rocks. The current white octagonal tower, 18 metres tall, dates from 1794 with subsequent modifications; its light — Fl(4) W 20s, range 15 nautical miles — marks the headland and the approach to Swansea Harbour. The lighthouse is reached by a causeway walkable at low water from Mumbles pier; the setting is suburban but the tide-washed rocks of the headland and the view back over Swansea Bay have an austere beauty. A lifeboat station operates from the same rocky platform. The surrounding Mumbles village is a pleasant destination in itself, with seafront restaurants and easy transport connections from central Swansea.

## 9. Flatholm Lighthouse, Bristol Channel

Flatholm Island, in the middle of the Bristol Channel between Cardiff and Weston-super-Mare, holds a lighthouse that marks the shipping lane for vessels proceeding up or down the channel in its most congested section. The lighthouse, established in 1737 and rebuilt in 1820 and 1867, stands 29 metres tall. The island has remarkable ecological and historical interest: Viking raids, a cholera hospital, and Second World War gun emplacements all left traces, and the island now supports a breeding colony of lesser black-backed gulls. The Flatholm Society operates boat trips from Cardiff's Barrage during the summer season — typically May to September — and the lighthouse grounds are accessible during visits. The combination of the lighthouse, the island's layered history, and the dramatic estuary views make it an exceptional destination for the effort required.

## 10. West Beacon, Pembrokeshire

The chain of smaller navigation lights along the Pembrokeshire coast and in the waterways of Milford Haven — Wales's major oil port — represents a generation of twentieth-century lighthouse engineering less dramatic than the Victorian towers but equally essential. Milford Haven, with its deep natural harbour, handles a significant proportion of Britain's liquefied natural gas imports; the lights that guide tankers through the Haven's ten miles of waterway include a system of leading lights, sectored lights, and transit marks operating day and night. The Haven's outer approaches, marked by St Ann's Head Lighthouse (1714, rebuilt 1841) — technically outside our top ten but impossible to overlook — represent the critical safety margin for vessels of up to 330 metres carrying some of the most hazardous cargoes in maritime trade.

Welsh lighthouses are generally accessible by car and well-served by the Pembrokeshire Coast National Park infrastructure in the south and the Anglesey coastal path in the north. The cliff-top walks around South Stack, Strumble Head and Nash Point are among the finest coastal walks in Britain. Summer is the best time for seabird watching and lighthouse tours; the Pembrokeshire islands — Skokholm, Skomer, Grassholm — have limited summer access that should be booked months in advance.
