For most of the history of lighthouses the light itself was a flame. Wood fires, coal braziers, candles, and then increasingly sophisticated oil lamps produced the illumination that the great optical systems gathered and projected to sea. The transition from flame to electricity transformed the lighthouse as profoundly as the introduction of the Fresnel lens, but it was neither sudden nor straightforward. It unfolded over more than a century, ran ahead of the electrification of ordinary life in its earliest experiments, and lagged far behind it at remote stations where electric power simply could not be supplied. Understanding that transition means following the light source through several distinct technologies, each of which improved on the last in brilliance, reliability, or convenience.

## The Age of the Flame

The oil lamp reached a high state of refinement long before electricity was a practical option. The Argand lamp, introduced in the 1780s, used a tubular wick with a glass chimney to draw air through the centre of the flame, producing a far brighter and cleaner light than any previous lamp. Burning sperm whale oil, then colza (rapeseed) oil, and later mineral oil, the multi-wick Argand burner was the standard lighthouse illuminant through the nineteenth century. A large lamp might have several concentric wicks, consuming oil at a rate that required the keeper to maintain and refill the reservoir through the night.

The incandescent gas mantle, adapted for lighthouse use around the turn of the twentieth century, marked the final refinement of the flame. A mantle of fabric impregnated with rare-earth oxides, heated by a vaporised-oil or gas flame, glowed with an intense white light far brighter than the bare flame that heated it. The incandescent petroleum vapour burner, developed by Arthur Kitson and refined by David Hood of Trinity House, gave a light of remarkable power at low fuel cost and remained in service at many stations well into the twentieth century, in some cases outliving early electric installations that proved unreliable.

## The Electric Pioneers

Electric lighting reached the lighthouse astonishingly early. The South Foreland lighthouse near Dover was fitted with a carbon-arc lamp in 1858, powered by a magneto-electric generator, making it the first lighthouse in the world lit by electricity — at a time when electric street lighting was still an experiment and the incandescent bulb had not been invented. The Dungeness light followed, and the French installed arc lighting at La Hève near Le Havre.

The carbon-arc lamp produced an intensely brilliant light by striking an electric arc between two carbon rods. Its brilliance was unmatched, but it was a demanding light source. The carbon rods burned away and had to be advanced continuously by a regulating mechanism to maintain the gap; the arc hissed and flickered; the generating plant — a steam engine driving a magneto or dynamo — was bulky, costly, and required skilled attention. The arc light suited a few major landfall stations where its great range justified the expense and complication, but it was wholly impractical for the hundreds of minor lights around any coastline.

The verdict of the nineteenth-century lighthouse authorities was cautious. Extensive comparative trials, notably those conducted by Trinity House and observed by the physicist John Tyndall, weighed the arc light against the best oil and gas burners. The arc won on raw brilliance but lost on reliability and cost, and for decades the oil and incandescent-mantle burners remained the workhorses while arc lighting was confined to a handful of premier stations.

## The Incandescent Bulb and the Grid

The incandescent electric filament lamp changed the calculation entirely. Far simpler than the arc, requiring no continuous adjustment, the filament bulb could be made in a range of powers and was vastly easier to maintain. As electric supply networks spread through the early and middle twentieth century, lighthouses near inhabited coasts could be connected to the mains, and the filament lamp — later the tungsten-halogen lamp — became the standard illuminant at electrified stations.

The advantages compounded. An electric lamp could be switched instantly, controlled by a daylight sensor, and backed up by a standby lamp that swung into position automatically if the first failed. The labour of trimming wicks, cleaning chimneys, and refilling oil reservoirs through the night vanished. The clockwork that rotated the great lenses, formerly wound by hand and driven by falling weights, could be replaced by an electric motor. Each of these changes chipped away at the indispensability of the resident keeper.

For remote stations beyond the reach of the grid, other solutions were found. Acetylene gas, generated from calcium carbide and controlled by the ingenious sun valve of the Swedish engineer Gustaf Dalén — which extinguished the light automatically by day to save gas — automated thousands of minor lights and buoys from around 1910. Dalén's work, recognised with a Nobel Prize, allowed unattended lights to operate for months on a single charge of carbide, a crucial step toward automation that ran parallel to electrification rather than depending on it.

## Power Where There Was None

The remote rock and island stations posed the hardest problem. Connection to a mains supply was impossible, and a flame burner, however refined, still demanded a keeper. The solution came in stages. Diesel generators provided local electric power at larger stations, running the lamp, the rotation motor, and the fog signal, though they themselves required tending and fuel deliveries.

The decisive change came with low-power, long-life electric lamps and, above all, with reliable means of generating and storing electricity without human attendance. Banks of batteries, charged first by diesel generators and later by solar panels and small wind generators, could power a modern low-consumption lamp indefinitely. The light-emitting diode, with its tiny power demand and lifespan measured in years, made solar operation practical even at the most isolated stations. A modern automated light may draw so little current that a small photovoltaic array and a battery carry it through the longest winter night without any external supply.

## The End of the Keeper

Electrification did not by itself end the era of the lighthouse keeper, but it removed, one by one, every task that required a human presence. Once the lamp could switch itself on at dusk, change itself when a bulb failed, rotate under electric power, sound its fog signal automatically, and report its own faults by radio or telephone, the keeper's remaining duties were reduced to occasional maintenance that a visiting technician could perform.

The great wave of automation followed accordingly. Trinity House automated its last manned station, the North Foreland, in 1998. The United States Coast Guard automated its lights through the 1970s and 1980s, ending with the manned station at Boston Light, preserved deliberately as a historical exception. The Scottish and Irish lights, the great French stations, the Scandinavian coastal lights — all made the same transition within a few decades. The electrification that began as a brilliant experiment at South Foreland in 1858 ended, a century and a half later, by making the keeper himself unnecessary, leaving the towers to shine on alone.
