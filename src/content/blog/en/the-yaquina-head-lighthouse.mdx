A mile of dark basalt thrusts out into the Pacific north of Newport, and at its outermost point a slender white tower has flashed its light since 1873. The Yaquina Head Lighthouse is the tallest lighthouse in Oregon, ninety-three feet of brick and iron standing on a headland of black volcanic rock that the sea hammers from three sides. On the foggy central coast, where the marine layer can swallow the shoreline for days, this was a light that ships running the timber and grain trade between San Francisco and the Columbia River desperately needed.

## A Headland of Lava

Yaquina Head is geologically unusual. The basalt that forms the headland did not erupt here at all; it is part of the Columbia River Basalt Group, vast floods of lava that poured out of eastern Oregon and Washington some fifteen million years ago and flowed hundreds of miles to the sea. The headland is one of the westernmost expressions of that ancient flood, a hard dark rock that has resisted the Pacific while the softer sediments around it have worn away, leaving the head jutting boldly into the ocean.

That same hardness made the headland a magnet for shipping hazards. The offshore reefs and the steep rocky shore gave no shelter, and in the fog that so often blankets this coast a vessel could be on the rocks before the lookout ever saw them. The tip of Yaquina Head was the obvious place to mark, and the lighthouse was built there to warn ships off the headland and to guide them along the long featureless run of the central Oregon coast.

## Building the Tower

Construction began in 1871 and the light was first lit in August 1873. The tower was built of brick, more than three hundred and seventy thousand of them, plastered over and painted white, with an iron spiral staircase of one hundred and fourteen steps winding up to the lantern room. Materials had to be landed on the exposed shore and hauled up the headland, a difficult business on a coast with no harbour and a heavy swell.

The light was fitted with a first-order Fresnel lens manufactured in Paris, a towering assembly of hand-ground prisms that focused the flame of an oil lamp into a beam visible far out to sea. That original lens still crowns the tower today, one of the relatively few first-order Fresnel lenses left in its original American lighthouse. The keepers lived in quarters on the headland and tended the light through the long wet winters, when storms drove spray over the lantern gallery ninety feet above the rocks.

## The Tallest Light in Oregon

At ninety-three feet, Yaquina Head is the tallest lighthouse on the Oregon coast, taller than the better-known Heceta Head light to the south or the squat tower at Yaquina Bay just a few miles away in Newport. Its height was a deliberate response to the geography: the headland itself is not especially high, so the tower had to carry the lens up to a useful elevation above the sea. The combination of headland and tower gives the light a focal plane high enough to be seen well out into the shipping lanes.

There is a long-told local confusion between Yaquina Head and the Yaquina Bay Lighthouse in Newport, the smaller wooden light that was built first, briefly served, and was then made redundant by the larger headland tower. The Bay light survives as a museum and a much-loved landmark, but it is Yaquina Head that has done the real work of guarding this coast for a century and a half.

## The Lights of the Pacific Coast

Yaquina Head is one link in the chain of lights that mark the long Pacific shore of the United States, though its closest neighbours are the other Oregon headland lights. Far to the south and west, beyond the contiguous coast, the lights of Hawaii guard a different ocean: the Makapu'u Point Lighthouse on Oahu with its giant hyperradial lens, the Kilauea Lighthouse on Kauai's dramatic north shore, the Diamond Head Lighthouse above Waikiki, and the Molokai Lighthouse at Kalaupapa. The Barbers Point Light and the Ninini Point Lighthouse mark harbour approaches in the islands.

These Pacific lights, from the basalt of Oregon to the volcanic shores of Hawaii, share a common ancestry in the work of the United States Lighthouse Service, which standardised the towers, the lenses, and the keeping of the lights across the whole sweep of American coast. The Cape Kumukahi Light on the Big Island, the easternmost point of Hawaii, and the Ka Lae Lighthouse at the southernmost tip of the United States, stand at the far edges of that system, just as Yaquina Head marks its rugged northern Pacific reach.

## A Marine Garden of Stone

What makes Yaquina Head exceptional today is not just the light but the headland itself, now protected as the Yaquina Head Outstanding Natural Area. The rocky intertidal zone at the foot of the headland is one of the most accessible tide-pool environments on the Oregon coast, where visitors at low water can walk among anemones, sea stars, and urchins in the basalt pools. Offshore rocks host nesting seabirds in their thousands, and gray whales pass close to the head on their migration along the coast.

The black sand and cobble of Cobble Beach, the seabird colonies, and the long views up and down a wild coast make the headland a place that rewards a visit even when the fog hides the tower. The natural area is managed to protect both the wildlife and the historic light, a rare combination of working maritime heritage and protected marine environment in a single dramatic landform.

## Visiting Yaquina Head

The lighthouse stands just a few miles north of Newport, easily reached off Highway 101, and the visitor centre interprets both the geology of the headland and the history of the light. Tours climb the iron staircase to the lantern room, where the great Fresnel lens still throws its beam, and the surrounding trails lead out onto the headland and down to the tide pools. The light remains an active aid to navigation, automated now but still flashing its distinctive signal over the same fog-prone water it has guarded since 1873.

On a clear evening the headland offers one of the finest sunsets on the Oregon coast, the white tower turning gold against the darkening Pacific. On a foggy one, you understand exactly why it is here, as the beam sweeps out into the grey and the foghorn sounds its long warning over rocks that have not changed in fifteen million years.
