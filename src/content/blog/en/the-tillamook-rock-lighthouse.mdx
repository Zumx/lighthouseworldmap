A mile off the headland of Tillamook Head on the northern Oregon coast, a flat-topped sea stack of black basalt rises sheer from the Pacific. On its crown sits a squat lighthouse and dwelling, weathered grey, its lantern long dark. This is the Tillamook Rock Lighthouse, known to generations of Oregonians and to its own keepers as Terrible Tilly, and it has one of the strangest histories of any light in the United States: built at terrible cost, manned through decades of punishing storms, abandoned, and finally turned into a columbarium for the ashes of the dead.

## A Rock Nobody Wanted

By the late 1870s the United States Lighthouse Board had concluded that the approaches to the Columbia River, the great gateway to the Pacific Northwest, needed a coastal light north of the river mouth. The obvious site was Tillamook Head itself, a high forested promontory, but surveys showed its summit was so often wrapped in fog that a light there would be useless. Attention turned to the bare basalt rock offshore, exposed to the full force of the open ocean.

The rock was small, with no beach and no safe landing. The survey party that first attempted to assess it could not even get ashore for days, and the master mason sent to lead the work, John Trewavas, was swept off the rock and drowned within sight of the boat. His death nearly ended the project before it began. Workers had to be landed by being swung across on a line from a pitching vessel, one at a time, in a manoeuvre that allowed no error.

## Building Against the Sea

Construction began in 1879. The top of the rock had to be blasted flat to make a building platform, lowering the summit by several metres and removing thousands of tonnes of basalt. The crew lived on the rock in a temporary shelter through the building season, cut off from the mainland whenever the sea rose, with supplies and men transferred by the same precarious breeches-buoy line.

The completed station was first lit on the first of January 1881. It consisted of a single-storey dwelling and work building with a short tower rising from the centre, the focal plane of the light standing about 41 metres above the sea. The first-order Fresnel lens threw a beam visible far out into the shipping lanes. Almost as if in warning, a sailing ship, the Lupatia, was wrecked near the rock during the final days of construction, killing all aboard but the ship's dog, a grim prelude to the station's working life.

## Terrible Tilly

The nickname was earned honestly. Tillamook Rock sits where Pacific storms arrive with nothing to break them, and the seas it raised were extraordinary. Waves regularly broke clean over the entire structure, which stood with its lantern some 30 metres above normal high water. In the great storms of 1894 and again in 1934, water and stones were hurled high enough to smash the lantern glass and damage the lens itself. Boulders torn from the rock were thrown onto the roof. Keepers described the whole building shuddering under the impact, and the noise as beyond anything they could convey.

In one storm a mass of rock and water broke sixteen panes of the lantern and put the light out, flooding the interior and contaminating the fresh-water supply. The keepers worked through the night to keep a temporary light burning. Living on Tilly meant accepting that the sea could, on any winter night, attempt to take the building apart around you. Tours of duty were limited and relief was frequently delayed by weather, and the psychological toll of the isolation and the constant assault of the ocean was severe enough that the Lighthouse Service struggled to keep the station staffed.

## Life and Work on the Rock

For the keepers, daily routine on Tilly was a matter of discipline against monotony and fear. The light had to be lit at dusk and tended through the night, the lens cleaned, the clockwork that turned the optic wound, the fog signal kept ready. Fresh water came from rain caught in cisterns; food and fuel arrived by tender and were swung ashore by derrick. There was no soil for a garden, no room to walk far, and in heavy weather no possibility of going outside at all.

Keepers passed the long hours with reading, letters, and the meticulous logbooks that the service required. Some kept records of the birds that crashed into the lantern on foggy nights, drawn and confused by the beam, a hazard the keepers tried to mitigate. Relief crews arrived by boat when the sea allowed, and the transfer of a man from a heaving deck to the rock by breeches buoy remained as dangerous on the last day of the station's working life as it had been on the first.

## Decommissioning and Decay

By the mid-twentieth century the cost and danger of maintaining Tilly could no longer be justified. Automated buoys and improved navigation had reduced the need for so exposed a station. The Tillamook Rock Lighthouse was deactivated in 1957, its first-order lens replaced by a whistling buoy moored offshore, and the keepers withdrawn for the last time. After more than seventy-five years, the rock fell silent.

The empty station passed through private hands over the following decades. Storms continued to batter it, breaking windows and stripping paint, and the building slowly decayed in the salt air. It became a forlorn landmark, visible from the Oregon Coast Trail on Tillamook Head and from the beaches around Cannon Beach and Seaside, a dark silhouette on its lonely rock that drew the eye of every visitor to the coast.

## A Lighthouse for the Dead

In 1980 the rock was bought by a group who established it as a columbarium, a repository for the ashes of the dead, under the name Eternity at Sea. Urns were placed within the old building, and for a time the station found an unexpected second life as one of the most remote resting places in America. The venture was troubled, however; the columbarium lost its licence after questions about record-keeping and the storage of remains, and burials there ceased. The urns already placed remain on the rock.

Today Tillamook Rock and its lighthouse are part of the Oregon Islands National Wildlife Refuge, valued as a nesting site for seabirds. Landing is prohibited, both to protect the wildlife and because the rock remains as dangerous as ever. The building stands weathered and closed, watched over by the birds and the sea, its strange triple identity as engineering feat, working light, and place of the dead now part of the folklore of the Oregon coast.

## Seeing Terrible Tilly

You cannot set foot on the rock, but Tilly is one of the most photographed lighthouses on the West Coast precisely because it must be seen from a distance. The best views are from Ecola State Park, just north of Cannon Beach, where the coastal trail along Tillamook Head looks straight out at the rock from the clifftop. On a clear day the lighthouse is unmistakable, a small grey shape on a black stack with the surf breaking white around its base. In winter storms, watchers gather to see the waves explode over it just as the keepers once endured them.

The Oregon coast offers many other lights with easier access. Heceta Head, Yaquina Head, Yaquina Bay, the Cape Meares light near Tillamook Bay, and the Cape Blanco light to the south are all open to visitors and tell the broader story of navigation along this dangerous shore. But none has the grip on the imagination that Terrible Tilly holds, the unattainable rock that defied its keepers and now keeps only the dead and the birds.
