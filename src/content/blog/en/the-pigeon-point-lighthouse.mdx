On a low rocky point on the San Mateo coast, halfway between San Francisco and Santa Cruz, a slender brick tower rises one hundred and fifteen feet against the Pacific sky. The Pigeon Point Lighthouse is one of the tallest lighthouses in the United States, and one of the most photographed on the whole California coast. It has stood here since 1872, guarding a stretch of shore where the cold current, the persistent fog, and the offshore rocks made a deadly combination for the ships of the Gold Rush era and after.

## A Ship Named the Carrier Pigeon

The point takes its name not from birds but from a shipwreck. In 1853 the clipper ship Carrier Pigeon, on her maiden voyage from Boston around Cape Horn to San Francisco, ran onto the rocks here in the fog and was lost. The point was named for her, and her wreck was one of several that established this stretch of coast as a place that badly needed a light. As traffic to the booming port of San Francisco grew, the toll of vessels lost on the unmarked coast south of the Golden Gate became impossible to ignore.

The decision to build was taken in the years after the Civil War, as the United States Lighthouse Board extended its system along the newly important Pacific coast. Pigeon Point was chosen as the site for a major coastal light, one of a series intended to give ships a continuous chain of guidance along the run between San Francisco and the southern California ports. The light would mark the point itself and serve as a landfall and coasting light for the busy shipping lanes offshore.

## Building the Tower

The tower was completed and first lit in November 1872. It was built of brick, rising one hundred and fifteen feet to the focal plane of the lantern, making it tied with Point Arena to the north as the tallest lighthouse on the West Coast of the United States. The brick was brought in and the tower raised on the exposed point, with keepers' dwellings, an oil store, and the other buildings of a self-contained light station clustered at its base.

Pigeon Point was fitted with a first-order Fresnel lens, the largest class of lighthouse optic, manufactured in France. This great assembly of glass prisms, standing taller than a man and weighing several tons, focused the light of the lamp into a powerful beam that swept far out over the Pacific. The lens revolved on its mounting to produce the characteristic flash by which mariners could identify Pigeon Point and distinguish it from the other lights along the coast.

## Fog, Current, and the California Coast

The danger of this coast lies in its weather as much as its rocks. The cold California Current sweeping south brings the persistent summer fog that can blanket the shore for days, and the prevailing northwest winds drive ships towards a lee shore studded with rocks and reefs. A vessel coasting south in fog, uncertain of its position, could find itself on the rocks at Pigeon Point before any lookout saw the danger. The light, and the fog signal that accompanied it, were the answer to exactly this hazard.

The stretch of coast that Pigeon Point guards is one of wild beauty: the long beaches and bluffs around Pescadero and Año Nuevo, where the elephant seals haul out in their thousands each winter, and the rocky shore running south towards Santa Cruz. It is a coast of tide pools, kelp forests, and seabird rocks, and the lighthouse stands at its heart, a white finger of brick against the dark line of the sea.

## The Lights of the American Coasts

Pigeon Point is one of the great coastal lights of the United States, a system that spans the continent and reaches far out into the Pacific. To the north along the same California coast stand its tall companions, while across the country the same Lighthouse Service standardised lights from Maine to Texas. The Gulf coast lights — the Sabine Pass Lighthouse, the Port Isabel Lighthouse, the Bolivar Point Lighthouse near Galveston, and the Matagorda Island lighthouse — guard the approaches to the Texas and Louisiana ports.

Far out in the Pacific, the lights of Hawaii belong to the same national system: the Makapu'u Point Lighthouse on Oahu with its giant hyperradial lens, the Kilauea Lighthouse on Kauai, the Diamond Head Lighthouse above Waikiki, and the Molokai Lighthouse at Kalaupapa. The Cape Kumukahi Light on the Big Island and the Ka Lae Lighthouse at the southernmost point of the United States mark the far reaches of American territory, just as Pigeon Point marks a vital point on the mainland Pacific shore.

## A Light at Risk and Restored

Pigeon Point has faced its own threats over the years. The brick tower, exposed to a century and a half of salt air and weather, deteriorated until falling masonry forced the closure of the tower to climbing and the removal of the historic Fresnel lens for safety. For years the great lens was displayed at the base of the tower while the structure awaited restoration, and the light itself was maintained by a modern automated beacon mounted nearby.

A major restoration of the tower has been undertaken to stabilise the brickwork and ultimately to return the original lens to the lantern room, ensuring that one of the country's tallest and most beloved lighthouses survives for future generations. The work is a reminder that these towers, for all their solidity, are fragile heritage that must be actively cared for if they are to endure.

## Visiting Pigeon Point

The lighthouse stands beside Highway 1, an easy drive south from San Francisco or north from Santa Cruz, and the station is open to visitors as part of a California state park. The former keepers' quarters operate as a hostel, allowing guests to stay overnight at the foot of the great tower, falling asleep to the sound of the surf and the sweep of the light. The grounds offer some of the finest coastal walking and tide-pooling on this stretch of shore.

The light is most famous for its annual lighting of the historic Fresnel lens, a celebration that draws crowds to see the great optic glow once more as it did for the ships of the nineteenth century. Whether seen blazing at that event, looming out of the summer fog, or silhouetted against a Pacific sunset, Pigeon Point remains one of the iconic images of the California coast.
