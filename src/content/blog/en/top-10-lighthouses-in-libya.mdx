Libya presents the longest Mediterranean coastline of any African country — roughly 1,770 kilometres of low sandy shore, rocky headlands, and shallow gulfs facing directly north into the central Mediterranean. It is a coast with a deep maritime past: the harbours of Tripoli, Benghazi, Derna, and the ancient cities of Sabratha, Leptis Magna, and Cyrene drew shipping for three thousand years. The lights that mark this shore today are a layered inheritance — Ottoman-era harbour beacons, Italian colonial towers built during the early twentieth century, and modern automated structures maintained by the Libyan ports authority. Many stand on or beside classical ruins, so that a single headland may carry both a Roman foundation and a steel lattice light. The names below are given in Arabic as they appear in local records, with their locations identified.

## منارة طرابلس — Tripoli Light

The principal light of the Libyan capital marks the entrance to the old harbour of Tripoli, where the medina, the Red Castle, and the Italian-era waterfront meet the sea. Tripoli has been a port since Phoenician and Roman times, and the modern light guides shipping into one of North Africa's busiest commercial harbours. The approaches are shallow and the offshore reefs that gave the old harbour its protection also made the entrance hazardous to vessels that misjudged the channel, which is why a marked light has long been considered essential here.

## منارة بنغازي — Benghazi Light

Benghazi, Libya's second city and the principal port of Cyrenaica, carries a harbour light guiding vessels into the gulf of Sidra's eastern shore. The records also note منارة بنغازي (سيدي خريبيش) — a light associated with the Sidi Khrebish district, near the site of ancient Berenice on which modern Benghazi partly stands. The harbour was heavily fought over and damaged in the twentieth century, and its navigation aids have been rebuilt more than once.

## منارة درنة — Derna Light

Derna, set where the Jebel Akhdar — the Green Mountain — comes closest to the sea on the eastern coast, has a long maritime history and a harbour light marking its approach. The town sits in a fertile coastal pocket unusual for the Libyan shore, watered by a wadi descending from the highlands. The light guides coastal traffic along a stretch where the mountains rise steeply behind a narrow shore.

## منارة الخمس — Al-Khums Light

The light at Al-Khums marks the approach to the modern port and, just to the east, the spectacular Roman ruins of Leptis Magna — one of the best-preserved Roman cities in the Mediterranean, a UNESCO World Heritage Site whose harbour was once among the busiest in Roman Africa. The modern Al-Khums light serves the present commercial port while standing within sight of the silted basin that served the ancient city.

## منارة صبراتة — Sabratha Light

West of Tripoli, the light near Sabratha stands beside another great classical site: the Roman city of Sabratha, with its restored theatre facing the sea. Sabratha was a Phoenician trading post that became a prosperous Roman port, and the modern coastal light marks the same low shore that ancient mariners once approached. The juxtaposition of the working navigation light and the marble ruins is one of the more striking on the Libyan coast.

## منارة سوسة — Susah (Apollonia) Light

The light at Susah marks the coast at the site of Apollonia, the ancient port of Cyrene. Apollonia's harbour works, partly submerged by subsidence and sea-level change, lie just offshore, and the modern light guides vessels along this stretch of the Cyrenaican coast below the Jebel Akhdar. The combination of a partly drowned classical harbour and an active light makes this a site of particular interest to maritime archaeologists.

## منارة طلميثة — Tolmeita (Ptolemais) Light

At Tolmeita, the light marks the coast at ancient Ptolemais, one of the cities of the Pentapolis of Cyrenaica. The modern beacon stands above a shore strewn with classical remains, guiding the limited coastal traffic of this less-developed eastern stretch.

## منارة رأس عزاز — Ras Ajdir Light

At the far western end of the coast, near the Tunisian border, the light at Ras Ajdir marks the frontier waters where Libyan and Tunisian territorial seas meet. It is the westernmost of the principal Libyan lights and the first landfall for shipping arriving along the coast from the west.

## منارة البردي — Bardia Light

At the eastern extreme, near the Egyptian border, the light at Bardia (Al-Bardi) marks the gulf and the small port that saw heavy fighting in the Western Desert campaigns of the Second World War. It is the easternmost of the Libyan lights, framing the long coast that the western Ras Ajdir light opens.

## منارة فروة — Farwa Light and فنار القرابولي — Garabulli Light

Two further beacons complete the western system: the light at Farwa, on the low sandy island and lagoon near the Tunisian frontier, and the light at Garabulli (Al-Qarabulli) east of Tripoli, marking the shore between the capital and Al-Khums. Together with the Tripoli and Sabratha lights they illuminate the densely settled north-western coast, the busiest stretch of the Libyan shore.
