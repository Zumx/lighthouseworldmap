The Pacific coast of the United States is a coast of cliffs rather than sandbars, and its lighthouses sit high and exposed rather than low among dunes. From the warm headlands above San Diego to the fog-wrapped capes of northern California and the Oregon border, the western shore was lit later than the Atlantic but with no less urgency, because the gold rush of 1849 sent hundreds of ships north into waters that had never been properly charted. The result is a string of towers perched on bluffs, balanced on rocks offshore, and tucked into harbours, many of them now within state parks and open to visitors who climb their spiral stairs for the view.

## Point Loma and the San Diego Approach

The southernmost light on this list stands at Point Loma, guarding the entrance to San Diego Bay. The original Old Point Loma Lighthouse, completed in 1855, sits at 422 feet above the sea within Cabrillo National Monument, and that elevation proved its undoing: fog and low cloud frequently hid its light from ships below. In 1891 it was abandoned in favour of the New Point Loma Lighthouse lower on the point, a skeletal iron tower that remains active. The old tower is preserved as a museum, furnished as it would have been in the 1880s, and its hilltop position offers one of the finest coastal panoramas in southern California.

## Point Vicente and the Palos Verdes Cliffs

North of Los Angeles harbour, the white tower at Point Vicente stands on the Palos Verdes Peninsula, lit in 1926 to mark the cliffs that had claimed several ships. Its 67-foot tower carries a third-order Fresnel lens visible far out into the San Pedro Channel, and the surrounding bluffs are a noted spot for watching the grey whale migration in winter. The light is operated by the Coast Guard and the grounds are open on limited days, but the tower itself is visible at any time from the adjacent interpretive centre.

## Piedras Blancas on the Big Sur Approach

Where the coast turns wild north of San Simeon, the Piedras Blancas Light Station marks the white rocks that gave it its name. Built in 1875, it once stood 100 feet tall, but earthquake damage in 1948 forced the removal of its upper stages and lantern, leaving a stubby tower that is nonetheless among the most atmospheric stations on the coast. The site is managed as an outstanding natural area, reached by guided tour, and the elephant seal colony on the beach below draws thousands of visitors each winter.

## Point Sur and the Big Sur Headland

Few lighthouses occupy a more dramatic setting than Point Sur, which sits atop a great volcanic rock connected to the Big Sur shore by a sandspit. Lit in 1889, the stone light station is reached by a steep half-mile walk up the rock, and the guided tours that climb it pass the keepers' dwellings, the blacksmith shop, and the barn, all built of granite quarried on site. The setting, with the Santa Lucia Mountains rising behind and the Pacific stretching to the horizon, is among the most photographed on the western seaboard.

## Pigeon Point, the Tallest in California

Halfway between Santa Cruz and San Francisco stands Pigeon Point Light Station, at 115 feet the tallest lighthouse on the US West Coast and tied for the tallest in the country. Lit in 1872 after the clipper Carrier Pigeon wrecked on the point, its tower once held a first-order Fresnel lens with a thousand prisms. The lens is now displayed on site while the tower carries a modern beacon, and the former keepers' quarters operate as a hostel where guests sleep within sight of the breaking surf.

## Point Bonita at the Golden Gate

Guarding the northern approach to the Golden Gate, Point Bonita Lighthouse occupies a knife-edge of rock reached by a tunnel hand-cut through the headland and a suspension footbridge swaying above the surf. First lit in 1855 high on the headland, it was moved lower in 1877 to escape the fog, and today it is the last manually maintained lighthouse in California to remain in its original setting. The walk out to it, through the Marin Headlands within the Golden Gate National Recreation Area, is one of the great short coastal hikes in the country.

## Point Reyes and the Foggiest Coast

Point Reyes Lighthouse clings to the seaward end of a long peninsula north of San Francisco, in what is reputedly the foggiest and one of the windiest places on the Pacific coast. Rather than build a tall tower on the already-high headland, the engineers in 1870 placed the light low on the cliff face, reached by 308 steps down the rock. The first-order Fresnel lens, with over a thousand prisms, still sits in the lantern room, and on clear days the descent rewards visitors with sweeping views toward the Farallon Islands.

## Point Arena, Rebuilt After the Earthquake

Point Arena Lighthouse, on a windswept headland in Mendocino County, holds the distinction of being the first lighthouse in the United States built of reinforced concrete. The original 1870 brick tower was so badly damaged by the 1906 earthquake that struck San Francisco that it had to be demolished; the present 115-foot tower replaced it in 1908. It is open daily for climbing, and from its gallery the coast unfolds in both directions toward the Mendocino bluffs.

## Battery Point at Crescent City

Near the Oregon border, Battery Point Lighthouse sits on a small island that becomes a peninsula at low tide, so that visitors must time their crossing with the sea. Lit in 1856, it is one of the oldest lights on the coast and survived the tsunami that devastated Crescent City in 1964. It remains an active aid and a museum, staffed by resident keepers who lead tours when the tide allows access across the rocky causeway.

## St. George Reef, the Loneliest Tower

The northernmost and most formidable light associated with this coast stands offshore from Crescent City on a barely submerged rock called Northwest Seal Rock. St. George Reef Lighthouse took ten years and an enormous sum to build, finally lighting in 1892, and the granite tower rising from its wave-swept base was among the most expensive and dangerous lighthouse projects ever undertaken in America. Decommissioned in 1975, it is now restored as a museum reached only by helicopter, a fitting end to a list of lights that grow wilder the further north you travel.
