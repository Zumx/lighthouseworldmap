A lighthouse is built to be conspicuous, and the qualities that make it visible to a mariner make it irresistible to a thundercloud. It is tall, it stands alone, it occupies the highest point of an exposed headland or a rock surrounded by conducting seawater, and it is frequently the only significant structure for several miles in any direction. Every one of those attributes increases its probability of being struck by lightning. Keepers in the great era of manned lights came to regard severe thunderstorms not as spectacle but as a genuine occupational hazard, and the history of lighthouse engineering includes a long, slow education in how to keep a building from being destroyed by the very prominence that justified its existence.

## Why the Tower Attracts the Strike

Lightning seeks the path of least resistance to ground, and it tends to originate from the points where the electric field at ground level is most intense. A tall, slender object concentrates that field at its summit in a way that a low, broad landscape does not. A lighthouse perched on a cape rising a hundred metres above the sea presents its lantern to the cloud base far above the surrounding terrain. On an isolated rock station — the Bell Rock off the Scottish coast, Eddystone off Plymouth, Fastnet off southwest Ireland — the tower is surrounded by salt water, which is a far better conductor than dry land, so the structure becomes the preferred terminus for a discharge that would otherwise have to find ground through the relatively resistive seabed.

The result is a strike frequency that vastly exceeds what an equivalent building inland would experience. Records from exposed European and American stations document multiple strikes in a single season, and a handful of severe events in which a tower was hit several times during one storm. The lantern room, with its metal framing and its high position, is the usual point of contact.

## What a Strike Can Do

An unprotected masonry tower struck by lightning suffers in characteristic ways. The current, seeking ground, will follow whatever conductive path it finds — damp mortar, iron cramps binding the stonework, the metal of the lantern, internal pipework, the chains and weights of the clockwork that rotated the lens. Where the path is inadequate to carry the enormous current, the energy is dissipated as heat and mechanical force. Mortar is blown out of joints, stones are cracked or displaced, iron fittings are fused, and glass in the lantern is shattered. There are documented cases of stone copings hurled clear of the tower and of internal staircases damaged by current passing through the structure.

The danger to the keepers was real. A person inside a tower carrying a partial discharge could be killed or injured by side-flash — current jumping from the structure to a nearby conductive body. The optical apparatus, with its delicate clockwork and later its electrical components, was vulnerable to damage that could disable the light for the duration of repairs. For a navigational aid whose entire purpose was reliability, an unpredictable disabling event of this kind was intolerable.

## The Lightning Conductor Arrives

The principle of the lightning conductor was established by Benjamin Franklin in the 1750s: a pointed metal rod connected by a continuous low-resistance conductor to the ground would intercept the strike and carry the current safely past the structure to earth. Lighthouses were among the obvious candidates for protection, but adoption was neither immediate nor universal, and early installations were frequently inadequate.

The problems were practical. A conductor must be continuous and of sufficient cross-section to carry the current without fusing. It must terminate in a genuinely low-resistance earth. On a rock station surrounded by seawater the earth connection is excellent, since the sea is a vast conductor; the difficulty there is corrosion of the conductor itself in the marine environment. On a dry headland the earth connection is the weak point, and an inadequate earth could cause the conductor to do more harm than good by raising the whole structure to a high potential during a strike.

The engineer Robert Stevenson, builder of the Bell Rock light and head of the family that designed most of Scotland's lighthouses, gave serious attention to conductor design, as did the authorities of Trinity House in England. By the later nineteenth century a properly engineered installation — copper conductor of generous section, multiple down-conductors, a sea earth on rock stations — had become standard on new towers and was retro-fitted to older ones.

## Engineering the Modern Protection

The contemporary understanding of lightning protection, codified in international standards, treats the lighthouse as a structure requiring a complete protection system rather than a single rod. An air termination at the summit — historically a finial above the lantern, now often a short mast — intercepts the strike. Down-conductors carry the current to an earth termination system designed for the local ground conditions. Bonding ensures that all the metalwork of the structure is at the same potential during a strike, eliminating the side-flashes that endangered keepers.

The shift to electrical and then electronic equipment created a second category of risk. A direct strike to a structure can be survived by the masonry, but the surge it induces in electrical wiring and electronic control systems can destroy them even when the building is undamaged. Modern automated lighthouses, dependent on electronics for their lamps, their monitoring, and their communications, require surge protection on every conductor entering the structure — power lines, signal cables, antenna feeds. A station protected against the mechanical effects of a strike can still be silenced by an induced surge that fries its controller.

## Living Through the Storm

The human record of lighthouse storms survives in the keepers' logs. Entries describe the approach of thunder, the count of strikes, the smell of ozone in the lantern, ball lightning rolling along the gallery rail, and the discharges that lit the whole sea white. Keepers learned to stay clear of metalwork during the worst of it, to avoid the lantern at the height of a storm, and to inspect the conductor and the structure afterward for damage. On rock stations, where there was nowhere to go and no help to summon, a severe electrical storm was endured rather than escaped.

There is a particular folklore of the lighthouse storm — the corposant or St Elmo's fire glowing on the lantern finial, the hair standing on end as the field built before a strike, the concussion of a near miss felt through the stone. These were not embellishments but the ordinary sensory reality of working in the most struck buildings on the coast. The conductor did not prevent the strikes; it merely ensured that the tower, and ideally the people inside it, would still be there when the storm passed.
