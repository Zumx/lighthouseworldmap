The Frisian Islands stretch in a gentle arc from Den Helder in the Netherlands to the German-Danish border near Esbjerg, separating the shallow, treacherous Wadden Sea from the open North Sea. This 500-kilometre chain of barrier islands — West Frisian, East Frisian, and North Frisian — has been one of the most intensively lit coastlines in Europe since the seventeenth century. The combination of shifting sands, powerful tidal currents, dense fog, and relentless shipping traffic through the German Bight made lighthouses here not a luxury but an absolute necessity. Many of the towers in this region are among the tallest in continental Europe, needing height to project their lights above the low, flat land and across the extensive shallow approaches. The keepers of these lights were long regarded as the most skilled and well-paid in the respective national lighthouse services, working in conditions of exceptional exposure and tedium. ## 1. Borkum Lighthouse (Neuer Leuchtturm), Borkum

Borkum, the westernmost of the East Frisian Islands, is home to two lighthouses — an old tower of 1576 that once formed part of the church — and the Neuer Leuchtturm, the New Lighthouse, completed in 1879 to designs incorporating a striking black and white banded tower 60 metres tall. The newer tower was engineered to provide a major landfall light for vessels entering the German Bight from the North Sea. Its light character is a flashing white with a range of 26 nautical miles, and its distinctive band pattern serves as a daymark distinguishing it from the Westerturm. The tower is owned by the German Federal Maritime and Hydrographic Agency (BSH) and is open to visitors in the summer season; the 248 steps lead to a gallery with views across the Wadden Sea and out over the North Sea swell. Borkum is reached by ferry from Emden; the lighthouse lies within easy walking distance of the ferry terminal.

## 2. Wangerooge Lighthouse, Wangerooge

Wangerooge is the easternmost of the five inhabited East Frisian Islands, and its lighthouse has a history of particular drama. The original tower, completed in 1855, stands 60 metres tall and served until 1969, when a modern structure was built. The 1855 tower — a white cylindrical masonry tower — now serves as a water tower and observation platform, its lantern long since decommissioned, but it dominates the island skyline and is the most architecturally distinguished lighthouse building in the East Frisians. The light itself is now exhibited in a local museum. Wangerooge is a car-free island reached by ferry from Harlesiel; the old lighthouse is visible on approach and stands in the village centre. The character of the active modern light at the island's western end is a flashing white sequence with a range around 23 nautical miles.

## 3. Norderney Lighthouse, Norderney

Norderney is the third-largest of the East Frisian Islands and the most densely populated, with a long tradition as a North Sea resort that dates from 1797 when the first German seawater bath was established here. The lighthouse, completed in 1874, is an impressive red and white banded brick tower reaching 54 metres above the dune landscape. It was designed by Prussian engineers to mark the Norderney Seegat, the tidal inlet between Norderney and Juist. The light character is an occulting white flash visible 23 nautical miles, and the tower continues to serve as an active aid to navigation. The tower is open to visitors on summer weekends; the climb involves 234 steps. The surrounding dune landscape is protected as a national park, and the lighthouse stands at the edge of the built-up resort area with a clear view seaward.

## 4. Ameland Lighthouse (Bornrif / Noord), Ameland

The Ameland lighthouse, known as the Noord (North) lighthouse, stands at the western tip of the island near the hamlet of Hollum and is the most visited lighthouse in the West Frisian Islands. Built in 1880 and standing 57 metres tall, the red brick tower was constructed by the Dutch state lighthouse service on the instructions of the Rijkswaterstaat. Its light character is a group flashing white signal with a range of 28 nautical miles, one of the strongest in the Frisian chain, marking the Ameland Gat approach to the Wadden Sea. The lighthouse was automated in 1980 but is open to visitors from April to October; guided climbs are popular. The island is served by ferry from Holwerd; the lighthouse is a 3-kilometre cycle ride from the ferry landing at Nes. The name Bornrif refers to an extensive sandbank offshore, the principal hazard the light guards against.

## 5. Terschelling (Brandaris) Lighthouse, Terschelling

The Brandaris is the oldest functioning lighthouse in the Netherlands and one of the oldest in Europe, its tower having been in continuous service since 1594. Standing 54 metres tall on the dune ridge above West-Terschelling, the white masonry tower is an icon of Dutch maritime history. The original purpose was to mark the Vliestroom, the main deep-water channel between the West Frisian Islands, which served as a key route for the merchant fleets of Amsterdam and the VOC. The present tower largely dates from the seventeenth century, rebuilt after a fire. Its light character is flashing white visible 29 nautical miles. The tower is open for guided visits in summer; the view from the gallery encompasses the Wadden Sea, the island's forests, and the North Sea horizon. Terschelling is served by ferry and fast catamaran from Harlingen; West-Terschelling has a small but well-organised lighthouse museum in a former keeper's cottage.

## 6. Vlieland Lighthouse, Vlieland

Vlieland, west of Terschelling, is the least visited of the inhabited West Frisian Islands — car-free and quiet. Its lighthouse marks the Vliestroom from the south, complementing the Brandaris on Terschelling. The white cylindrical tower dates from 1881 and reaches 53 metres, with a group flashing white character visible 20 nautical miles. The tower stands at the southern tip of the island on the edge of the Wadden Sea shore; the surroundings are dune forest and reed marsh, dramatically different from the open beach of the seaward side. The lighthouse was automated in the 1980s. Vlieland is served by ferry from Harlingen; bicycles are the standard transport, and the tower is reachable by a pleasant ride through the dunes.

## 7. Texel Lighthouse (Eierland), De Cocksdorp

The northernmost tip of Texel, the largest West Frisian Island, is marked by the Eierland lighthouse near the village of De Cocksdorp. Completed in 1864, the red brick tower rises 35 metres — modest compared with some Frisian lights, but sufficient given the elevation of the surrounding dunes. Its character is a group flashing white light with a range of 28 nautical miles, marking the Schulpengat approach channel and the northern shipping separation scheme. The tower is named for the Eierland polder, an area of former Wadden Sea reclaimed in the seventeenth century. Open to visitors in summer, the lighthouse is a popular destination for the cycling tourists who come to Texel in large numbers; Texel is the only West Frisian Island served by a short car ferry from Den Helder.

## 8. Sylt Lighthouse (Kampen), Kampen

Kampen lighthouse stands on the central west coast of Sylt, the largest North Frisian Island and Germany's northernmost island. Completed in 1914 and rising 62 metres — the tallest lighthouse in Germany — the red and white striped tower marks the Sylt outer bank and identifies the approach to the Lister Tief and Hörnum Tief, the channels on either side of the island. Its light character is a group flashing white sequence visible 27 nautical miles. Sylt is connected to the German mainland by the Hindenburgdamm causeway, and the lighthouse at Kampen stands on the edge of the famous red cliff (Rotes Kliff) above the North Sea beach; the combination of dramatic coastal scenery and architectural height makes it one of the most striking lighthouse locations in the entire Frisian chain.

## 9. Föhr Lighthouse (Nieblum), Föhr

Föhr is a North Frisian Island accessible by ferry from Dagebüll; its lighthouse at Nieblum marks the southern approach to the Föhrde and the ferry route. The white painted tower, of modest height, was built in the nineteenth century and serves as a harbour approach light rather than a major offshore lantern. The island has a strong Frisian cultural identity — the local dialect, Fering, is still spoken — and the lighthouse grounds include a small garden maintained by the local maritime association.

## 10. Schiermonnikoog Lighthouse, Schiermonnikoog

The easternmost inhabited West Frisian Island, Schiermonnikoog, has been a national park since 1989. Its lighthouse, a modest white cylindrical tower dating from 1854, stands at the western end of the island and marks the Westgat entrance to the Wadden Sea between Schiermonnikoog and Ameland. The light character is a flashing arrangement with a range of about 16 nautical miles. The island is car-free; visitors arrive by ferry from Lauwersoog and travel by foot or bicycle. The lighthouse can be reached on a half-day walk from the ferry landing and offers a quiet, undeveloped landscape that is rare on the otherwise busy Frisian coastline.

## Visiting the Frisian Islands Lighthouses

Lighthouse touring along the Frisian chain naturally follows the ferry routes. The West Frisian Islands are served from North Holland and Friesland ports; the East Frisians depart from Lower Saxony. Summer — late May to September — is the practical season for tower openings, though many islands operate lighthouse visits on guided-tour schedules that require advance booking. Cycling is the dominant mode of transport on most islands, and a bicycle is essential for reaching outlying towers efficiently. The Wadden Sea World Heritage Site, which encompasses the tidal flats behind all the islands, adds birdwatching and mudflat walking to any lighthouse itinerary.
