Sicily sits at the crossroads of the Mediterranean, where the routes between east and west, Africa and Europe, have crossed for three thousand years. Its coastline of more than a thousand kilometres is studded with lighthouses, some marking the narrow and treacherous strait of Messina, others guarding the volcanic outlines of the Aeolian and Egadi islands, still others standing on the long sweeping bays of the south coast. To follow the lights of Sicily is to trace the maritime history of the whole Mediterranean, from Greek and Phoenician traders to the busy ferry and cargo traffic of today.

## The Lighthouse of Messina

At the northeastern tip of the island, where Sicily comes within three kilometres of the Italian mainland, the lighthouse on the sickle-shaped spit of San Raineri guards the strait of Messina. A light has stood here since the Renaissance, the original tower designed by the sculptor Montorsoli in the 16th century. The strait is one of the most navigationally demanding waters in Europe, where the meeting of the Tyrrhenian and Ionian seas produces the whirlpools that the ancient Greeks knew as Scylla and Charybdis. The ceaseless ferry traffic between Messina and Reggio Calabria makes this one of the busiest lights in the Mediterranean.

## Capo Peloro

Close to Messina, at the very northeastern corner of Sicily, the lighthouse of Capo Peloro marks the narrowest point of the strait. The tall tower stands beside the lagoons of Ganzirri, famous for their mussel beds, and looks across to the Calabrian shore. The currents here run so strongly that the swordfish boats, with their long spotting masts and forward harpoon walkways, have hunted these waters in a tradition that goes back to antiquity.

## Capo d'Orlando on the Tyrrhenian Coast

Along the northern, Tyrrhenian coast, the lighthouse of Capo d'Orlando stands on a rocky promontory crowned by a sanctuary and the ruins of a medieval castle. The light marks the approach to the small port and the long beaches that stretch east and west, with the Aeolian Islands visible offshore on clear days. The headland takes its name, by legend, from a paladin of Charlemagne, and the combination of light, sanctuary, and ruined fortress makes it one of the most picturesque stations on the coast.

## The Lighthouse of Stromboli

Among the Aeolian Islands north of Sicily, the lighthouse on the islet of Strombolicchio guards the approach to the perpetually erupting volcano of Stromboli. The light stands atop a sheer volcanic plug rising from the sea, reached by a long stair cut into the rock. At night the glow of the volcano's eruptions, visible from far out at sea, has served sailors as a natural beacon for millennia, earning Stromboli the name of the lighthouse of the Mediterranean long before any tower was built.

## Capo Gallo near Palermo

West of Palermo, the lighthouse of Capo Gallo marks the headland that closes the gulf of Mondello, within a protected marine reserve. The tower stands on a limestone promontory honeycombed with sea caves, looking across to the island of Isola delle Femmine. The light guides shipping into the great port of Palermo, the busiest in Sicily, while the surrounding waters are popular with divers drawn to the clear sea and the rich marine life of the reserve.

## San Vito lo Capo

On the northwestern cape of Sicily, the lighthouse of San Vito lo Capo stands at the foot of the dramatic limestone massif of Monte Monaco, above one of the most beautiful beaches in Italy. The tall tower marks the cape that closes the gulf and guards the approach to the Zingaro nature reserve, the first protected coastal area established in Sicily. The combination of white sand, turquoise sea, and towering cliffs makes this one of the most visited corners of the island.

## Favignana in the Egadi Islands

West of Trapani, the Egadi archipelago is marked by several lights, the most important on the butterfly-shaped island of Favignana. The light guards the channel between the islands and the Sicilian mainland, waters once famous for the tonnara, the elaborate tuna trap whose annual mattanza was one of the great rituals of Mediterranean fishing. The old tuna processing works on Favignana, now a museum, recall a fishery that sustained these islands for centuries.

## Marettimo, the Outermost Egadi

The westernmost of the Egadi Islands, Marettimo rises steep and rugged from the sea, its lighthouse marking the most remote inhabited point of the archipelago. The island has no roads and few cars, and its waters are among the clearest in the Mediterranean, ringed by sea caves and home to a rich variety of fish and seabirds. The light here marks the western edge of Sicilian waters, looking out toward the open sea and the distant African coast.

## Capo San Marco at Sciacca

On the southern coast, the lighthouse near Sciacca marks the approach to one of the island's most important fishing ports, famous for its anchovy and sardine fleets. The south coast of Sicily faces Africa across the Mediterranean, and its lights guard a shore of long beaches and ancient Greek temples, with Agrigento's Valley of the Temples not far inland. The thermal springs of Sciacca, known since antiquity, add to the appeal of this stretch of coast.

## Capo Passero at the Island's Southern Tip

At the very southeastern tip of Sicily, the lighthouse of Capo Passero stands near the small island of the same name, marking the point where the Ionian Sea meets the Mediterranean. This is the southernmost lighthouse of mainland Sicily, closer to Africa than to much of Italy, and it guards the approach to the fishing ports of Portopalo and Marzamemi. The tuna fishery here, like that of the Egadi, was once among the richest in the Mediterranean, and the old tonnara buildings still stand by the shore.
