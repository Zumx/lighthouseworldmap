The Bahamas form one of the most treacherous archipelagos in the western hemisphere, a chain of nearly 700 islands and cays and more than 2,000 rocks stretching from the latitude of Palm Beach to the latitude of Haiti, lying almost entirely on the Bahama Bank — a vast shallow-water platform of limestone, coral reef and sand where depths rarely exceed 6 metres over huge areas, and where coral heads lurk just below the surface. The combination of clear tropical water (which makes depth deceptively hard to judge), strong tidal currents, predictable northeast trade winds, and the sheer number of isolated reefs and cays produced a record of shipwreck unmatched in the Americas. The British colonial lighthouse service began equipping the Bahamas with Imperial lighthouses in the 1830s, and the towers they erected — iron columns, stone towers, and the remarkable hand-wound structures still in operation at Hope Town — are among the finest examples of Caribbean lighthouse architecture.

## 1. Hope Town Lighthouse (Elbow Reef), Elbow Cay, Abaco

The Hope Town Lighthouse on Elbow Cay in the Abacos is one of the last hand-wound kerosene lighthouses still in active operation anywhere in the world. The red-and-white spiral-striped tower was built in 1863 under the superintendence of the Imperial Lighthouse Service and stands 36.6 metres tall, making it the tallest lighthouse in the Bahamas. The light character is a group flashing white signal visible around 17 nautical miles, marking the Elbow Reef — a deadly coral ledge that has claimed dozens of vessels over the centuries. What makes Hope Town extraordinary is its mechanism: the original clockwork rotation driven by a descending weight, wound by hand every two hours, has never been replaced by electric motors. The local community has steadfastly maintained the historic mechanism, and tours of the tower allow visitors to observe the clockwork at close quarters. The lighthouse is a working aid maintained by the Bahamas Department of Ports; the tower is open for climbing, and the view from the lantern gallery over the Sea of Abaco is magnificent.

## 2. Great Isaac Lighthouse, Great Isaac Cay

Great Isaac Cay, a tiny rock outcrop at the northwestern edge of the Little Bahama Bank, was the site of one of the most important British Imperial lighthouses in the western Atlantic. The tower, built in 1859 on orders from the Imperial Lighthouse Service, rises 45 metres above the sea and originally displayed a first-order dioptric apparatus with a range of around 20 nautical miles, guiding ships through the Northwest Providence Channel — the main approach to Nassau from Florida. The lighthouse was staffed by keepers until 1969, when two keepers disappeared without explanation, their fate never determined; the mystery of the Great Isaac keepers has become part of Bahamian lighthouse folklore. The tower has been unmanned since 1969 and is now automated, though it fell into disrepair. Access is by charter boat from Bimini or Nassau, and the remote rock with its crumbling keeper's cottages retains a haunting atmosphere appropriate to its history.

## 3. Gun Cay Lighthouse, Gun Cay, Bimini

Gun Cay sits at the southern end of the Bimini chain, at the edge of the Gulf Stream where the fast-moving current begins its acceleration northward along the Florida coast. The lighthouse at Gun Cay was established by the British in the nineteenth century to mark this critical waypoint for vessels crossing the Florida Straits, the passage between the Bahama Bank and the American coast. The Gulf Stream at Gun Cay is navigated daily by hundreds of recreational boaters crossing from Florida to the Bahamas, and the lighthouse serves as a visual reference and night landfall for this enormous recreational traffic as well as for commercial shipping. Gun Cay is uninhabited and accessible by boat from Bimini; the lighthouse is automated and can be approached from the beach on the island's western shore.

## 4. Dixon Hill Lighthouse, San Salvador

San Salvador Island holds an almost unique distinction in the history of navigation: it is believed by many historians to be the site of Columbus's first landfall in the Americas on 12 October 1492. The Dixon Hill Lighthouse, built in 1887 on the island's northeastern coast, stands 46 metres above sea level and is one of the last hand-wound kerosene lighthouses in the western hemisphere, operating on a similar principle to Hope Town. The light character is a flashing white group visible around 19 nautical miles, and the lighthouse was for many decades the principal navigational mark for vessels approaching San Salvador from the Atlantic. The keeper's quarters at Dixon Hill are still occupied by a lighthouse keeper who winds the mechanism every two hours. San Salvador is reached by scheduled light aircraft from Nassau.

## 5. San Salvador Lighthouse (see Dixon Hill)

The primary lighthouse reference for San Salvador is Dixon Hill; the island also has lesser navigational aids at its approaches, and several historical markers on the island relate to the Columbus landfall traditions and the navigational history of the outer Bahamas. San Salvador's position on the eastern edge of the Bahamas Bank, facing the open Atlantic, made it both a hazard and a landmark for transatlantic navigators, and its lights have guided vessels from the age of sail to the present.

## 6. Hole in the Wall Lighthouse, Abaco

At the southern tip of Great Abaco Island, the Hole in the Wall lighthouse marks the dangerous passage between the Bahama Bank and the deep waters of the Northeast Providence Channel. The lighthouse takes its name from a natural rock arch — the 'hole in the wall' — visible in the limestone cliffs below it. It was built in 1836 as one of the earliest British Imperial lighthouses in the Bahamas, and the stone tower has been modified and maintained through successive lighthouse authority regimes. The southern tip of Abaco sees strong tidal currents where the Bank and the deep channel meet, and the Hole in the Wall light has guided vessels through this transition for nearly two centuries. Access is difficult, requiring a long drive on unpaved roads to the southern end of Abaco.

## 7. Castle Island Lighthouse, Acklins Island

Castle Island at the southern end of Acklins Island marks the Crooked Island Passage — one of the principal channels between the Atlantic and the Caribbean through the outer Bahamas. The lighthouse here, built in 1867, is a round masonry tower of characteristic British colonial construction, displaying a light visible around 15 nautical miles to guide vessels through the passage and warn of the Castle Island shoals. The Crooked Island Passage was heavily used by sailing ships running between Europe and the Spanish Main before steam, and Castle Island's lighthouse reduced a notorious hazard on this route. The remote location and difficult access by boat make Castle Island one of the least-visited lighthouses in the Bahamas.

## 8. Bird Rock Lighthouse, Crooked Island

The Bird Rock Lighthouse at the northern end of Crooked Island, guarding the southern entrance to the Crooked Island Passage, was built by the British in 1876 and is another example of the Imperial programme of lighting the outer Bahamian islands. The tower is a round white structure of modest height but critical importance given its position at the convergence of the Crooked Island and Mayaguana passages. Both passages were used extensively by blockade runners during the American Civil War, when the Bahamas served as a base for Confederate shipping evading the Union naval blockade — history that gives the island's lighthouse an unexpected American resonance.

## 9. Elbow Cay Light (Marsh Harbour approach)

In addition to the famous Hope Town tower, the approaches to Marsh Harbour — the main commercial centre of the Abacos — are marked by several minor lights and a key directional light guiding vessels through the shallow Sea of Abaco toward the harbour entrance. The Abacos are the most popular sailing destination in the Bahamas, with a large permanent ex-patriot boating community and enormous seasonal yacht traffic; the accuracy of the navigational aids in this area is critical for the thousands of vessels that pass through annually.

## 10. Nassau Harbour Light, New Providence

The lighthouse at the entrance to Nassau Harbour marks the passage into the capital of the Bahamas and one of the Caribbean's busiest ports, handling cruise ships, container vessels, inter-island ferries and a large recreational boating fleet. The Nassau harbour approach through the breakwater at the eastern end of New Providence has been lit since the nineteenth century, when Nassau was already the commercial and administrative hub of the British Bahamian colony. The present lighthouse is a modern functional structure that serves the enormous cruise ship and commercial traffic that makes Nassau one of the most-visited ports in the Caribbean.

## Practical Information

Visiting the Bahamas' lighthouse circuit requires combining air travel, ferry and charter boat services given the archipelago's extraordinary dispersal. Hope Town and the Abacos are the most rewarding destination for lighthouse enthusiasts, offering both the Hope Town and Hole in the Wall towers and a superb sailing environment accessible from Florida. Dixon Hill on San Salvador is best reached by light aircraft from Nassau. The outer islands (Castle Island, Bird Rock, Great Isaac) require specific charter arrangements and sea conditions that allow safe landings on often-exposed rocks and beaches.
