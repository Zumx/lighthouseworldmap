Turkey's coastline, stretching more than 8,300 kilometres from the Black Sea and the Bosphorus through the Aegean to the eastern Mediterranean, has been one of the most intensively navigated in the world since antiquity. The straits of Istanbul — the Bosphorus and Dardanelles — are among the busiest waterways in maritime history, funnelling Black Sea trade through a narrow, current-driven passage that has claimed ships since the Argonauts. Ottoman engineers and later the Turkish Republic's coastal administration built lighthouses of considerable character along these coasts, many of them reflecting the distinctive Ottoman architectural vocabulary in their lantern rooms and keeper's buildings. The Turkish lighthouse network, administered by the Ministry of Transport and Infrastructure's General Directorate of Coastal Safety, covers the Bosphorus, the Sea of Marmara, the Aegean and the Mediterranean. ## 1. Şile Lighthouse, Black Sea Coast

Şile Lighthouse, completed in 1859 on a small island just off the Black Sea coast of Anatolia northeast of Istanbul, is one of the oldest and most prominent lighthouses in Turkey. The 50-metre stone tower, built during the Ottoman reform period, marks the first navigable approach from the Black Sea toward the Bosphorus entrance — the critical turning point for vessels that have crossed the Black Sea from Odessa, Novorossiysk or Batumi. The light — Fl(2) W 15s, range 25 nautical miles — covers the sea approaches to the strait and the rocky coastal shoals along the Anatolian Black Sea shore. The lighthouse sits on a rock promontory connected to the mainland and is visible from the summer resort town of Şile, which has grown up around it. The lighthouse grounds and the adjacent beach make Şile a popular day trip from Istanbul for Turkish visitors.

## 2. Rumeli Lighthouse (Rumeli Feneri), Bosphorus

Rumeli Feneri stands on the European shore at the northern entrance to the Bosphorus, where the strait opens into the Black Sea. A lighthouse has stood on this point since Ottoman times; the current tower, substantially rebuilt in the twentieth century, works in conjunction with Anadolu Feneri on the Asian shore opposite to mark the northern gateway of the most commercially important strait in the western Eurasia sphere. The currents at the Bosphorus entrance are powerful and variable; on the surface, water flows from the Black Sea into the Sea of Marmara, while a countercurrent runs in the opposite direction below the thermocline — a two-layer system that has baffled sailors and fascinated oceanographers. The lighthouse stands above the village of Rumeli Feneri, where fishing boats work the Black Sea waters and a small café serves fresh fish to visitors who make the drive along the Bosphorus from Istanbul.

## 3. Ahırkapı Lighthouse, Istanbul

Ahırkapı Lighthouse, a red-and-white-banded iron tower at the Sea of Marmara waterfront in Istanbul's historic Sultanahmet district, is among the most unusual lighthouses in Europe: it sits within sight of the Hagia Sophia and the Blue Mosque, marking the northern shoreline of the Marmara for vessels navigating through the city's maritime heart. The lighthouse dates from the mid-nineteenth century, when European engineers were engaged to modernise Istanbul's port installations. Its modest height — barely 15 metres — reflects the sheltered character of the Marmara coast in this section, where the main hazard is shipping traffic density rather than coastal rocks. The lighthouse is enclosed within the Kennedy Caddesi coastal highway and is most easily seen from the waterfront walking path that runs along the Marmara shore past the old Byzantine sea walls.

## 4. İnceburun Lighthouse, Black Sea

İnceburun, the northernmost point of the Anatolian peninsula, carries a lighthouse that marks the Turkish mainland's outermost projection into the Black Sea. The cape — İnceburun translates roughly as 'Thin Cape' — creates a local current disturbance that has always made the passage hazardous for vessels working the Black Sea coast. The lighthouse, a white-painted masonry tower, serves the coastal shipping routes between the Bosphorus and the ports of the Black Sea coast of Turkey: Zonguldak with its coal trade, Samsun, Trabzon, and the smaller fishing harbours in between. The cape's remote position in the Sinop Province means it is rarely visited by tourists, though the Sinop peninsula itself — a narrow isthmus connecting the cape area to the mainland — is one of the more scenic sections of the Turkish Black Sea coast.

## 5. Kadıköy Lighthouse (Haydarpaşa), Istanbul

The small lighthouse near Haydarpaşa Station on the Asian shore of Istanbul's Bosphorus waterfront marks the ferry and shipping lanes of the upper Bosphorus and the entrance to Kadıköy harbour. Istanbul's relationship with its lighthouses is unique — the city's position on both sides of the strait means navigation marks are embedded in the urban fabric in a way found nowhere else. The Haydarpaşa area, formerly the terminus of the Anatolian railway system and now being redeveloped, still preserves several small navigation structures that guided the ferry traffic between the European and Asian shores. The lighthouse is within walking distance of the ferry piers and the Kadıköy market district, one of the most vibrant neighbourhood markets in Istanbul.

## 6. Bozburun Lighthouse, Aegean Coast

The lighthouse at Bozburun marks the southern approach to the Gulf of Hisarönü on Turkey's Aegean coast in the Marmaris district — a stretch of deeply indented coastline of limestone cliffs, pine forests and turquoise water that constitutes the Turkish section of what gulet sailors call the Blue Voyage. The Aegean coast of Turkey is not as densely lit as the straits — the hazards are primarily the shoaling peninsulas and island approaches rather than open-sea shipping hazards — but lighthouses at critical capes like Bozburun serve the yacht charterers and commercial fishing fleets that work the inner Aegean year-round. The Bozburun peninsula, accessible by road from Marmaris, is known for traditional wooden gulet building; the boatbuilding yards and the lighthouse occupy opposite ends of a bay of considerable beauty.

## 7. Karabiga Feneri, Sea of Marmara

The lighthouse at Karabiga marks the southern coast of the Sea of Marmara near the entrance to the Biga Peninsula — a section of coast where the shallow northern Marmara transitions to deeper water and where vessels taking the inshore route between Istanbul and the Dardanelles must navigate carefully. The Sea of Marmara's navigation lights form a critical system for one of the busiest enclosed seas in the world; tankers, bulk carriers, container ships, ferries and fishing vessels all share these waters, and the lighthouse network gives the chain of passage marks that professional pilots rely on when conning large vessels through the sea in poor visibility.

## 8. Galata Feneri, Istanbul

The Galata lighthouse, near the Golden Horn in Istanbul, is a smaller navigation aid serving the Bosphorus ferry traffic and the entrance to the Golden Horn itself — the natural harbour that has been Istanbul's commercial anchorage since Byzantine times. The lighthouse sits in a section of the Istanbul waterfront where the Bosphorus currents are strongest; the meeting of Black Sea and Marmara water creates surface current speeds of up to 7 knots in some conditions, making the navigation of even small vessels through central Istanbul a matter requiring care. The surrounding Karaköy waterfront, once dominated by Ottoman customs buildings and ferryboats, is now one of Istanbul's most actively gentrifying districts.

## 9. Keçiada Feneri, Sea of Marmara

The lighthouse on Keçiada (Goat Island) in the Sea of Marmara marks this small island's position in the shipping lanes and guides traffic through the middle passage of the Marmara. The Sea of Marmara's islands — Marmara Adası, İmralı, Avşa, Paşalimanı — each represent potential navigation hazards to vessels cutting the direct route between the Bosphorus and the Dardanelles, and each carries some form of navigation mark. The Marmara islands are served by regular ferry services from Istanbul's Yenikapı terminal, making them accessible as day trips from the city; the combination of Greek Orthodox architectural heritage, quiet beaches and relatively undeveloped coastlines gives them a character distinct from the more crowded Princes' Islands.

## 10. Çanakkale Strait Lights, Dardanelles

The Dardanelles — Çanakkale Boğazı in Turkish — is the southern of the two Turkish straits, connecting the Sea of Marmara to the Aegean. Navigation lights at Çanakkale, Kilitbahir, Kepez and the cape at the southern entrance to the strait collectively mark one of history's most contested waterways: the passage that Allied fleets attempted and failed to force in 1915, and that has been the gateway between Europe and Asia for maritime trade for three millennia. The tidal currents in the Dardanelles run consistently from north to south — Black Sea water flowing to the Mediterranean — at speeds that slow northbound vessels and accelerate southbound ones. The lighthouse at Çanakkale marks the narrowest point, barely 1.2 kilometres wide; the castle of Kilitbahir on the European shore facing the castle of Çimenlik on the Asian side frames the passage in a visual reminder of its historical significance.

Visiting Turkish lighthouses requires patience and flexibility. Some are within easy reach of Istanbul or the coastal resort towns; others are in genuinely remote sections of the Black Sea or Aegean coast accessible only by boat or long dirt roads. The Turkish Coast Guard administers the lighthouses and access is not always available, though the surrounding coastlines of many towers can be enjoyed freely. The best seasons are spring (April–June) and autumn (September–October), when temperatures are moderate and maritime traffic makes the straits most dramatic.
