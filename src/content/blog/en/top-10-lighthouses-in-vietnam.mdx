Vietnam's coastline stretches more than 3,400 kilometres from the Chinese border in the north to the Gulf of Thailand in the south, an S-shaped coast of considerable variety: the steep rocky headlands of the central coast, the low sandy shores of the Mekong Delta, the bay and island geography of Ha Long and Cat Ba in the north. This coast has been navigated by Chinese junks, Indian traders, and Arab dhows since at least the first millennium AD; the Cham people maintained port cities along the central coast from the second to the fifteenth centuries, and Chinese ceramics traded through Vietnamese ports have been recovered from shipwrecks across the Indian Ocean. France's colonial administration of Indochina from the 1860s brought a systematic lighthouse programme — the Service des Phares et Balises de l'Indochine built a chain of towers along the coast that remains the backbone of modern Vietnamese navigation. ## 1. Kê Gà Lighthouse, Bình Thuận

Kê Gà lighthouse stands on a rocky island about 500 metres offshore in Bình Thuận province, south-central Vietnam, and has the distinction of being the oldest lighthouse in Vietnam still in operation. Construction began in 1897 and the light was first exhibited in 1899, the work of French colonial engineers under the Service des Phares et Balises. The octagonal granite tower stands approximately 35 metres tall, built of dressed stone quarried on the mainland and transported by barge; the masonry quality is exceptional and the tower has survived typhoons and wars without structural damage. The light character is a group flashing white sequence with a range of about 22 nautical miles, marking the rocky island and the approach to the central coast from the South China Sea. The island is accessible by boat from Ham Thuan Nam district; the crossing takes around 15 minutes in reasonable weather. Visitors can climb the tower and the surrounding coral reef is clear enough for snorkelling.

## 2. Long Châu Lighthouse, Hải Phòng

Long Châu is an isolated island about 55 kilometres southeast of Hải Phòng in the Gulf of Tonkin, site of one of the most technically demanding lighthouse constructions in French Indochina. The lighthouse was completed in 1894 on a small rocky islet — barely 1 hectare of usable surface at the island's high point — and the white cylindrical tower stands 25 metres above the sea, carried on a masonry base that required considerable blasting and levelling of the rocky summit. The light character is a flashing white sequence visible about 20 nautical miles, marking the western approaches to the Gulf of Tonkin and the deep-water channel used by vessels entering and leaving Hải Phòng. The station was strategic during the Vietnam War and saw military action; the buildings were subsequently repaired. Today the island is part of a national marine protected area, and the lighthouse serves both navigation and as a waypoint for the growing volume of container traffic entering Hải Phòng's port complex.

## 3. Mũi Điện Lighthouse (Đại Lãnh), Phú Yên

Mũi Điện — Lightning Cape, in some translations — marks the easternmost point of mainland Vietnam, a rocky headland above which the French colonial engineers constructed a lighthouse in 1890. The site is among the most dramatic on the Vietnamese coast: the cape drops steeply to the sea on three sides, and on clear days the horizon extends to the Spratly Islands. The white masonry tower, approximately 26 metres tall, carries a flashing white light with a range of about 26 nautical miles — the longest range light on the central coast. The cape is within the Đại Lãnh nature reserve; access requires a drive from the coastal road and a walk to the headland. The lighthouse compound, including the keeper's quarters, is maintained by the Vietnam Border Guard rather than the maritime authority, reflecting the cape's continued strategic significance.

## 4. Vũng Tàu Lighthouse, Vũng Tàu

Vũng Tàu, the port city at the entrance to the Saigon River (Sài Gòn River), was known in colonial times as Cap Saint-Jacques and served as the main approach waypoint for vessels bound for Saigon. The lighthouse on the Small Mountain (Núi Nhỏ) headland was first established in 1862 and the present tower dates from a late nineteenth-century reconstruction. The white cylindrical structure stands approximately 18 metres tall on a hill whose own elevation adds considerably to the effective range; the light is visible about 17 nautical miles. Vũng Tàu was a rest-and-recreation destination during the Vietnam War and is now a weekend resort for Ho Chi Minh City's population. The lighthouse is accessible via the winding road up Núi Nhỏ and can be combined with visits to the giant Christ figure at the summit and views over the Mekong approaches.

## 5. Hòn Dấu Lighthouse, Hải Phòng

Hòn Dấu (Dấu Island) lies at the entrance to the Bạch Đằng River approach to Hải Phòng and is the northernmost significant lighthouse on Vietnam's east coast. The lighthouse on this island was first established by the French in 1892 and the current tower, a white cylindrical structure, dates from a reconstruction after wartime damage. The light character is flashing white with a range of about 17 nautical miles, marking the main fairway for vessels entering Hải Phòng — Vietnam's most important northern port for container traffic. The island is accessible by ferry from Đồ Sơn, a peninsula resort southeast of Hải Phòng. The island retains a small permanent community of lighthouse keepers and their families, and the surrounding waters are part of the Cat Bà-Ha Long UNESCO World Heritage nomination area.

## 6. Cù Lao Chàm Lighthouse, Quảng Nam

Cù Lao Chàm is an island group about 15 kilometres offshore from Hội An, the ancient trading port designated a UNESCO World Heritage Site. The lighthouse here marks the approach to the Thu Bồn River mouth and the anchorage used by merchant vessels from Arabia, India, China, and Japan from the fifteenth through the eighteenth centuries — Hội An was one of Southeast Asia's most prosperous trading cities in this period. The white tower, of moderate height, carries a flashing character visible around 15 nautical miles. The island is now a marine protected area, and the surrounding reef systems have been recovering since fishing restrictions were implemented. Visitors can reach Cù Lao Chàm by speedboat from Cửa Đại beach in roughly 20 minutes.

## 7. Lý Sơn Lighthouse, Quảng Ngãi

Lý Sơn Island in the South China Sea, about 24 kilometres offshore from Quảng Ngãi, carries a lighthouse that marks one of the traditional departure points for Vietnamese fishing and trading vessels heading to the Paracel and Spratly Islands. The island has a long history of Cham and Vietnamese maritime activity and the lighthouse, of French Protectorate origin, continues to guide the fishing fleet that operates from the island's harbour. Lý Sơn is accessible by high-speed ferry from Sa Kỳ port; the island's garlic cultivation on volcanic soil has made it famous throughout Vietnam, and lighthouse visits can be combined with agricultural and cultural heritage.

## 8. Phú Quý Lighthouse, Bình Thuận

Phú Quý Island, isolated in the South China Sea about 120 kilometres from the Bình Thuận coast, carries a lighthouse that marks this otherwise featureless stretch of ocean. The island serves as a waypoint for vessels navigating between Vietnam's southern coast and the offshore fishing grounds; the lighthouse, a white tower of moderate height, is the island's most visible landmark from seaward. The island is accessible by passenger ferry from Phan Thiết (the crossing takes several hours), and it has been developing slowly as a tourist destination.

## 9. Núi Đèn Lighthouse, Kiên Giang

Núi Đèn (Lighthouse Mountain) in Kiên Giang province on the Gulf of Thailand coast takes its very name from the lighthouse that stands on its summit. The tower marks the approach to Rạch Giá and the western coastal shipping lane, and the mountain's prominence makes it visible across the flat Mekong Delta landscape as well as from seaward. The lighthouse is incorporated into a local heritage site; the mountain's name confirms how thoroughly the light structure has defined the geography of this corner of southern Vietnam.

## 10. Côn Đảo Lighthouse, Côn Đảo

The Côn Đảo archipelago — known in colonial times as Poulo Condore — lies about 180 kilometres south of Vũng Tàu in the South China Sea and was the site of Vietnam's most notorious prison complex, used by French and later American-backed governments to incarcerate political prisoners. The lighthouse here marks the main island and guides vessels to the growing tourist infrastructure. The island group is now a national park of exceptional ecological quality; the offshore coral reefs are among the healthiest in Vietnamese waters. The lighthouse is accessible on the island and can be combined with a visit to the Con Dao Museum, which documents the prison history, and snorkelling excursions on the surrounding reefs.

## Visiting Vietnam's Lighthouses

The practical logistics of Vietnamese lighthouse visiting depend heavily on the island character of many of the key sites. Kê Gà, Cù Lao Chàm, Hòn Dấu, and Côn Đảo all require boat connections, with varying degrees of formality; the trend is toward more accessible heritage tourism at the French-built towers. The winter months — October to April — offer the best coastal visibility and calmest seas on the central coast; the south is more consistent year-round. Many of the major French-built towers are still manned by VINAMARINE personnel, and access to the towers themselves may require advance arrangement through local tour operators.
