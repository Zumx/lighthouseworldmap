Few coastlines have been as hostile to their lighthouses as Louisiana's. The Mississippi delta is not bedrock but a raft of soft, water-laden mud that is actively subsiding, while the river itself shifts its passes and builds new land seaward almost faster than charts can follow. Add the most hurricane-battered shore in the United States and you have a place where towers tilt, sink, are swept away, or have to be moved bodily to survive. The result is a melancholy and fascinating heritage: a handful of survivors on Lakes Pontchartrain and Maurepas, a couple of offshore screwpiles built on iron legs against the mud, and a long roll of lights now vanished beneath the Gulf.

## 1. Frank's Island Lighthouse

The oldest chapter in Louisiana's lighthouse story belongs to Frank's Island, at the Northeast Pass of the Mississippi delta. Designed by Benjamin Henry Latrobe, architect of the U.S. Capitol, its first ambitious masonry tower of 1818 began sinking into the delta mud almost at once; a lighter replacement was finally lit in 1823. Decades of subsidence left the abandoned tower leaning and isolated in the marsh. It survives in ruin, reachable only by boat through delta channels, a monument to the impossibility of building heavy stone on a floating coast.

## 2. New Canal Lighthouse

The most visited lighthouse in Louisiana stands on the south shore of Lake Pontchartrain in New Orleans. A light has marked the entrance to the New Basin Canal here since 1838, the structure rebuilt several times in its squat wooden screwpile-and-platform form. Battered by Hurricanes Katrina and Rita in 2005, it was painstakingly reconstructed using salvaged timbers and reopened as a museum and education centre run by the Lake Pontchartrain Basin Foundation. It is the easiest lighthouse in the state to visit, right on the lakefront.

## 3. Tchefuncte River Lighthouse

At Madisonville, where the Tchefuncte River meets the north shore of Lake Pontchartrain, a brick tower has guided vessels since 1837. Damaged in the Civil War, it was rebuilt in 1868, the conical whitewashed tower that stands today, around 12 metres tall. Still an active aid maintained with the help of a local preservation society, it is opened to the public on special days, and the river mouth setting among cypress and live oak is among the prettiest on the lake.

## 4. Port Pontchartrain Lighthouse

The little Milneburg light, properly the Port Pontchartrain Lighthouse, dates from 1855 and once marked the end of the railroad pier where New Orleanians took the train out to lakeside resorts. Land reclamation has left the small white tower stranded inland, now within the University of New Orleans campus on the Pontchartrain shore. Squat and cylindrical, it is a survivor of the vanished resort world of Milneburg and is preserved as a campus landmark.

## 5. Southwest Reef Lighthouse

Southwest Reef was an offshore screwpile-and-iron light of 1858, built to mark the reef at the entrance to Atchafalaya Bay. When it was retired, the whole square iron structure was moved ashore in 1987 and re-erected as a centrepiece in Brownell Memorial Park at Berwick, across the river from Morgan City. Painted black above a white base, it can now be visited on dry land, a rare chance to walk around the legs and underside of a genuine Gulf screwpile light.

## 6. Ship Shoal Lighthouse

Ship Shoal is Louisiana's great offshore survivor: a tall screwpile tower of 1859 standing far out in Caillou Bay, southwest of the Terrebonne marshes, on wrought-iron legs driven into the shoal. The skeletal black tower rose to carry its light high above a notorious shipping hazard with no land for miles. Long automated and now disused, it stands isolated in the Gulf, reachable only by a long boat trip, and remains one of the most dramatic iron screwpile structures left anywhere in American waters.

## 7. Sabine Pass Lighthouse

Marking the pass between Texas and Louisiana at the mouth of the Sabine River, the Sabine Pass Lighthouse of 1856 is unusual for its buttressed brick tower, built with eight flying buttresses to spread its weight on the soft marsh ground. Just over the line on the Louisiana side of the boundary, it saw action during the Civil War battle of Sabine Pass. Decommissioned in 1952, the abandoned tower survives in the marsh, the subject of long-running preservation efforts.

## 8. Pass Manchac Lighthouse

At Pass Manchac, the channel linking Lake Pontchartrain and Lake Maurepas, a lighthouse has stood since the mid-nineteenth century, the present small brick tower dating from after the Civil War. Surrounded now by water and marsh as the surrounding land subsides, it is a familiar landmark to anglers in the Manchac swamp country. Disused and weathered, it is best seen from the water and is a favourite of boaters exploring the cypress swamps between the two lakes.

## 9. West Rigolets Lighthouse

The Rigolets is the natural pass connecting Lake Pontchartrain to Lake Borgne and the Gulf beyond. The West Rigolets light, a wooden cottage-style lighthouse on the shore of the pass, guided traffic through this strategic channel for generations before fire and storms took their toll. Repeatedly damaged by hurricanes including Katrina, the site illustrates the relentless toll the Gulf storms have taken on the timber lights of the Pontchartrain basin.

## 10. Chandeleur Island Lighthouse

The Chandeleur Islands, a long arc of barrier islands off the delta, carried a lighthouse from 1848 to mark the eastern approaches. Rebuilt as an iron skeleton tower after earlier losses, the light was finally destroyed by Hurricane Katrina in 2005, which also tore away much of the islands themselves. Its disappearance is the starkest single emblem of Louisiana's vanishing coast: a lighthouse, and the very ground it stood on, swept into the Gulf within living memory.

## Explore on the map

Louisiana's lighthouses divide between the easily reached lake lights and the remote delta and offshore ruins. For a practical visit, base yourself in New Orleans for New Canal and Madisonville's Tchefuncte light, then drive west toward Morgan City to walk around the relocated Southwest Reef tower at Berwick. The offshore and delta sites, from Ship Shoal to Frank's Island, demand a boat and settled weather.
