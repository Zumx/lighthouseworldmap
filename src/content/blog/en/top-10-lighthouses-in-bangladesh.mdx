Few coastlines in the world test a navigator's nerve like the head of the Bay of Bengal. Here the combined outflow of the Ganges, Brahmaputra, and Meghna rivers builds the largest delta on earth, a vast and restless apron of silt where channels migrate season by season and sandbanks appear where deep water ran the year before. Add the funnel shape of the bay, which concentrates cyclone surges into walls of water, and you have a place where lights matter as much as anywhere on the planet. Bangladesh's lighthouses are mostly modest steel and concrete structures, but they mark approaches of extraordinary difficulty.

## A Delta That Refuses to Stay Still

The defining fact of Bangladeshi navigation is that the land itself is provisional. The rivers carry roughly a billion tonnes of sediment to the sea each year, and the seabed off the coast is a living thing, constantly remodelled by monsoon discharge and tidal scour. The "outer bar" off Chittagong — the shallow threshold a deep-draught vessel must cross to reach the port — shifts position and depth so often that surveys are repeated continually and the marking has to be flexible. This is why so much of the country's navigation depends on range lights and lit buoys rather than grand towers: a system that can be moved is worth more than a monument that cannot.

## Chittagong and the Karnaphuli Approach

Chittagong, on the Karnaphuli River, is the country's principal seaport and the focus of its most important navigation aids. The Chittagong Outer Bar Range provides leading lights that vessels align to find the safe water across the bar. A range, or transit, works by placing two lights at different heights and distances: when a ship sees them vertically aligned, it is on the correct line of approach, and any drift left or right separates the lights and warns the helmsman instantly. The Outer Bar Range Rear Lighthouse is the taller of the pair, standing back from the shore so that it appears above its front companion. For the busy traffic of bulk carriers, container ships, and tankers serving Chittagong, these unspectacular structures are indispensable.

## Patenga and the City Edge

At Patenga, where the Karnaphuli meets the sea on the southern fringe of Chittagong, stands the light known locally as পতেঙ্গা বাতিঘর. Patenga is also one of the few places where lighthouse and leisure overlap directly: the beach here is among the most visited in the country, and the maritime infrastructure shares the shoreline with food stalls, sea-bathers, and evening crowds. The light marks the river entrance and works in concert with the range lights further offshore, part of the layered system that funnels ships from the open bay into the dredged channel.

## The Sundarbans and Hiron Point

South-west of the delta proper lies the Sundarbans, the largest mangrove forest on earth, a UNESCO World Heritage maze of tidal creeks straddling the Bangladesh–India border. Within it, near the approach to Mongla, the country's second major port, sits Hiron Point — Nilkamal — long a navigation and pilotage station. Ships bound for Mongla traverse the Pussur River channel through this wilderness of mangrove and mudbank, where the only solid landmarks are the lights and the occasional forest department post. Hiron Point is also famous for tigers and saltwater crocodiles, a reminder that some of these stations sit in genuinely wild country.

## Kutubdia and the Island Lights

Kutubdia, a low island off the Cox's Bazar coast, carries one of the older-established lights in the region, marking the approaches along this stretch of the south-eastern seaboard. Island lights here serve a double purpose: they guide coastal traffic and they warn of the islands themselves, which are low, sandy, and easily overrun by storm surge. Kutubdia has suffered repeatedly from cyclones and erosion, and the history of its light is bound up with the history of the island's shrinking, retreating shoreline — a vivid case study in how the sea is reclaiming parts of this coast.

## Cox's Bazar and the Tourist Coast

Cox's Bazar boasts what is often described as the longest natural sand beach in the world, an unbroken sweep of more than 120 kilometres, and it has become the country's leading seaside resort. The Cox's Bazar light marks this section of the coast for the coastal shipping and fishing fleets that work these waters. For visitors, the combination of a long surf beach, a busy fishing harbour, and navigation marks makes the area an accessible introduction to Bangladesh's maritime life. Offshore lie St Martin's Island and the contested marine boundary with Myanmar, where lights take on a quietly strategic dimension.

## Teknaf, the Far South, and Cross-Border Lights

At the country's southern tip, near Teknaf, the Naf River separates Bangladesh from Myanmar, and the maritime approaches here are marked on both sides of a sensitive frontier. The Tetulia channel further west, threading between deltaic islands, also carries lights and beacons that guide local craft through shallow, braided waterways. Navigation in these reaches is as much about local knowledge as about charted aids: pilots who have worked the rivers for decades read the water in ways no buoy can capture, and the lights serve as confirmation rather than sole guidance.

## Cyclones, Surge, and the Reason the Lights Endure

No account of Bangladesh's coast is complete without the cyclones. The 1970 Bhola cyclone, which killed hundreds of thousands of people, and the 1991 storm that devastated the Chittagong region, define the stakes of working this sea. Storm surge funnelled up the narrowing bay can rise many metres and sweep far inland over the flat delta. Lighthouses and beacons here are built and maintained against this backdrop, and the country's investment in cyclone shelters, embankments, and warning systems is the landward counterpart to its seaward marking. The lights are one thread in a much larger fabric of survival on a coast where the sea is never far from taking back the land.
