California's 1,350-kilometre coastline presents navigators with a combination of hazards that drove an intensive programme of lighthouse construction from the 1850s onward. The rocky headlands of the north and central coast are frequently shrouded in fog generated by the cold California Current; the offshore rocky shelves at Point Reyes, Point Arena, and Pigeon Point extend the hazard far from the visible shore; and the bar harbours at the mouths of rivers from the Eel to the Russian are notorious for breaking swells. When the Gold Rush of 1849 transformed San Francisco into one of the busiest ports in the Pacific, the demand for navigational aids on this difficult coast became acute. Congress authorised a Pacific Coast lighthouse programme in 1850, and the first California lighthouse was lit at Alcatraz in 1854. What followed was a century of building, rebuilding, and improvement that produced a collection of towers along the California coast still largely active today.

## 1. Pigeon Point Lighthouse, San Mateo County

The Pigeon Point lighthouse, completed in 1872 south of Half Moon Bay, is one of the tallest lighthouses on the Pacific Coast at 35 metres. The brick tower was designed by Paul Pelz of the United States Lighthouse Board and named for the clipper ship Carrier Pigeon, which wrecked near the point in 1853. The original first-order Fresnel lens, a massive apparatus of 1.08-metre focal length, is now displayed in the lighthouse's fog signal building after a section of the lantern room gallery collapsed in 2001, requiring removal of the lens for safety reasons. The characteristic is a white flash every ten seconds, now produced by a modern optic; the nominal range is 24 nautical miles. The lighthouse is managed as a state park and hostel; overnight accommodation in the historic keepers' quarters is available through California State Parks. The tower has been undergoing seismic stabilisation and restoration work; the current access status of the tower climb should be checked before visiting.

## 2. Point Reyes Lighthouse, Marin County

Point Reyes is the foggiest point on the Pacific Coast, averaging more than 2,700 hours of fog per year, and the lighthouse built here in 1870 occupies one of the most dramatic and uncomfortable positions on the California coast. The cast-iron tower stands only 13 metres tall — there was no need for height on a clifftop 91 metres above the sea — but reaching it requires descending 308 steps down the cliff face to the lighthouse platform at the cliff's edge. The first-order Fresnel lens installed in 1870 and decommissioned in 1975 remains in place in the original lantern room and is open to view during the limited hours the lighthouse is accessible. The characteristic of the current light is a single white flash every five seconds, with a range of 24 nautical miles. The Point Reyes National Seashore manages the site; access is by shuttle from the Bear Valley Visitor Center on weekends and holidays due to parking limitations.

## 3. Point Sur Lighthouse, Monterey County

Point Sur light station, completed in 1889, sits atop a massive volcanic rock rising 91 metres above the ocean on the Big Sur coast south of Carmel. The station is among the best-preserved historic lightstations in California: the original brownstone tower, fog signal building, three keepers' dwellings, and utility buildings all survive largely intact. The tower stands 11 metres on the summit of the rock; the light character is two white flashes every fifteen seconds, range 23 nautical miles. Point Sur has been continuously active since 1889. The Central Coast Lighthouse Keepers offer guided tours on selected weekend mornings; the tour involves a two-mile round walk and a climb to the rock summit.

## 4. Old Point Loma Lighthouse, San Diego

The original Point Loma lighthouse, completed in 1855, is one of the eight lighthouses lit on the Pacific Coast in the 1854-1855 period authorised by the 1850 Congressional act. The Gothic Revival sandstone tower stands 9 metres on the summit of Point Loma, 121 metres above sea level — but this very height was the problem: the light was so often obscured by the clouds that formed at the hilltop that mariners complained it was useless in bad weather. The station was decommissioned in 1891 and replaced by a lower light nearer the water's edge. The old lighthouse was preserved and is now the centrepiece of the Cabrillo National Monument, managed by the National Park Service. The third-order Fresnel lens is displayed in the original lantern room and the station buildings have been restored to their 1880s appearance. The monument is open daily and the lighthouse is free to view.

## 5. Battery Point Lighthouse, Del Norte County

Battery Point, at the northern end of Crescent City in Del Norte County, was completed in 1856 and is one of the best-preserved early California lighthouses. The tower and dwelling are built as a combined structure in the New England Cape Cod style — a design rarely used on the Pacific Coast — on a tidal island accessible only at low tide. The original fourth-order Fresnel lens installed in 1856 remains in the lantern room and operates as an active aid. The characteristic is a white flash every four seconds. The Del Norte County Historical Society operates the lighthouse as a museum; access is on foot across the causeway at low tide, and tours are conducted by volunteers.

## 6. Point Arena Lighthouse, Mendocino County

The Point Arena lighthouse on the Mendocino coast was first built in 1870, destroyed by the 1906 San Francisco earthquake, and rebuilt in 1908 — the only earthquake-rebuilt lighthouse in the United States. The replacement tower, designed to withstand future seismic events, is a 35-metre reinforced concrete cylinder, one of the earliest uses of reinforced concrete for lighthouse construction in the country. The original first-order Fresnel lens was replaced by a modern optic in 1977; the Fresnel lens is now displayed in the adjacent fog signal building, one of the largest Fresnel lenses on display in the United States. The current light character is two white flashes every fifteen seconds, range 24 nautical miles. The Point Arena Lighthouse Keepers Association operates the tower, museum, and keepers' cottage overnight accommodation.

## 7. Piedras Blancas Lighthouse, San Luis Obispo County

The Piedras Blancas lighthouse north of San Simeon was completed in 1875 as a first-order station to mark the approach to San Simeon Bay and the rocky headland's offshore shoals. The original Italianate brick tower stood 34 metres until 1948, when the top was removed after cracking from the 1947 offshore earthquake and replaced with an automated light of diminished height. The current tower stands 19 metres, showing a white flash every ten seconds with a range of 22 nautical miles. The grounds include significant northern elephant seal haul-out beaches where several thousand seals can be observed at close range during the breeding season from December to March. The Bureau of Land Management manages the site; the lighthouse grounds are open for access and guided tours are offered on weekday mornings.

## 8. East Brother Light Station, Richmond

East Brother lighthouse on a small island in the northern San Francisco Bay near Richmond is exceptional among California lighthouses for its history as a bed-and-breakfast inn. The 1874 Italianate Victorian structure — tower, foghorn building, and keeper's house on a small rocky island — has operated as a lighthouse inn since 1980, offering four bedrooms with meals included in a setting accessible only by a small motor launch from the Richmond waterfront. The light character is a fixed white and red sector light. The station is listed on the National Register of Historic Places; booking, particularly in summer, is essential.

## 9. Farallon Islands Lighthouse, San Francisco County

The Farallon Islands lighthouse, on Southeast Farallon Island 43 kilometres west of San Francisco, is one of the most remote manned lighthouse sites in California. The original lighthouse of 1855 was replaced by the current tower, completed in 1855 and substantially rebuilt after the 1906 earthquake. The first-order Fresnel lens produced a light visible for 24 nautical miles over the approaches to the Golden Gate. The islands are now part of the Greater Farallones National Marine Sanctuary and are managed by the US Fish and Wildlife Service as a critical seabird breeding site; access by members of the public is not permitted. The light is maintained as an active aid; the Farallon Islands are visible on the horizon from Point Reyes and other coastal high points in clear conditions.

## 10. Cape Mendocino Lighthouse, Humboldt County

Cape Mendocino marks the most westerly point of California and, for centuries, was considered among the most dangerous headlands on the Pacific Coast: the cape generates powerful turbulence where the California Current meets the coastal upwelling, and the fog here is dense and persistent. The original lighthouse of 1868 was replaced by the current structure following earthquake damage; the cast-iron and brick tower of 1951 stands 14 metres on a cliff 115 metres above the sea. The old 1868 tower was moved in 1998 to Shelter Cove, where it is preserved as a historical exhibit. The active light shows a single white flash every fifteen seconds with a range of 28 nautical miles.

## Visiting California's Lighthouses

California's lighthouse sites are distributed along the entire coast, from the Oregon border to San Diego, and most are reasonably accessible by public road. The best overall concentration for a focused visit is the stretch from San Mateo County to Mendocino County, where Pigeon Point, Point Montara, Point Reyes, and Point Arena can all be visited over two to three days. The summer fog that makes the California coast so atmospherically distinctive also provides the best conditions for photography.
