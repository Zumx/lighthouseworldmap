Off the rocky southern tip of Sakhalin Island, in the cold seas between the Sea of Okhotsk and the Pacific, a grey concrete tower rises straight from a wave-battered pinnacle of rock. Streaked with rust, its windows empty and its rooms stripped bare, the Aniva Rock Lighthouse is one of the most haunting abandoned structures in the Russian Far East. Built by Japanese engineers when the southern half of Sakhalin was Japanese territory, later fitted with a nuclear-powered generator and then abandoned, it stands today as a stark monument to a lost era and a remote, dangerous coast.

## A Japanese Sakhalin

To understand the Aniva Rock Lighthouse, you have to understand the strange history of Sakhalin. For four decades, from 1905 to 1945, the southern half of the island, then known to the Japanese as Karafuto, was Japanese territory, won from Russia in the war of 1905. The Japanese developed the south of Sakhalin with towns, railways, paper mills and fishing ports, and they built the navigational infrastructure that a busy and dangerous coast demanded.

The waters around the southern cape of Sakhalin, where the Aniva Bay opens onto the strait separating the island from Hokkaido, are treacherous: cold, fog-bound, swept by strong currents, and dangerous to the shipping that passed between Japan and its northern colony. A lighthouse on the small rock known as Sivuchya, off Cape Aniva, was needed to guard this approach, and in 1939 the Japanese completed one of the most remarkable lighthouses of the era.

## Building on the Rock

The lighthouse was the work of the Japanese engineer Miura Shinobu, and it was an extraordinary feat of construction. The chosen site was a tiny rock standing alone in rough open water, lashed by waves and difficult to reach. Onto this pinnacle the engineers raised a round concrete tower some 30 metres tall, rising in stages from a wider base, with the living quarters, machinery and fog signal built into the structure itself. The tower seems almost to grow out of the rock, its rounded form designed to shed the force of the sea.

Inside, the lighthouse was a self-contained world. Keepers lived in the tower for long stretches, cut off by the weather, tending the light, the diesel generators and the fog signal that warned ships away from the rock in the frequent thick fog. The original optic was a rotating lens, and the station included an ingenious mercury-bath mechanism on which the heavy lens assembly floated and turned. It was a sophisticated, modern light for its day, set in one of the most hostile positions imaginable.

## From Japanese to Soviet Hands

In 1945, at the end of the Second World War, the Soviet Union took the whole of Sakhalin, and the Japanese population of Karafuto was expelled. The lighthouse, like the rest of the southern island's infrastructure, passed into Soviet hands. For some decades it continued to be staffed and maintained as part of the Soviet navigational network along this strategically sensitive coast, close to the disputed waters between the USSR and Japan.

The cost and difficulty of keeping keepers on so remote and dangerous a rock, supplied by sea in a harsh climate, eventually told. As elsewhere in the Soviet Arctic and Far East, the authorities turned to an automated solution that would let them dispense with permanent crews: the radioisotope thermoelectric generator, a device that produces electricity from the heat of decaying radioactive material and can run unattended for years.

## The Nuclear Heart

In the latter part of the twentieth century the Aniva Rock Lighthouse was converted to run on a radioisotope thermoelectric generator, or RTG. These devices, fuelled by strontium isotopes, were installed at remote lighthouses and beacons across the Soviet north and east precisely because they needed no fuel deliveries and no keepers; they simply produced power from radioactive decay, year after year, in places too remote to supply easily.

The system worked, after a fashion, but it left a dangerous legacy. After the collapse of the Soviet Union, the network of RTG-powered lights fell into neglect, and the abandoned generators, with their radioactive cores, became a serious safety and security concern. Across the Russian north a major effort was made to recover these sources before they could be lost, stolen, or breached. The RTG was removed from Aniva Rock, and the lighthouse, no longer needed and far too costly to maintain, was left to the sea.

## A Ruin on the Rock

Today the Aniva Rock Lighthouse stands abandoned, a stripped concrete shell battered by the waves and the weather. Its machinery is gone or wrecked, its windows broken, its interior open to the wind and the seabirds. The mercury from the old lens mechanism and the radioactive source are long removed, but the tower remains, rusting and slowly decaying on its lonely pinnacle. It has become a place of pilgrimage for photographers, urban explorers and adventurous travellers drawn to its beauty and its desolation.

Reaching it is not easy. The rock lies off the southern cape of Sakhalin, accessible only by boat across open and often rough water, and landing on the rock is difficult and at times impossible. Those who do reach it find a structure of strange, melancholy grandeur, a monument to Japanese engineering, Soviet pragmatism, and the unforgiving sea, slowly being reclaimed by the elements it was built to defy.

## Sakhalin and the Far Eastern Lights

The Aniva Rock Lighthouse is the most famous of a scattered network of lights that guard the coasts of Sakhalin, the Kuril Islands and the Russian Far East, a region of fog, ice, volcanoes and storm. Many of these lights were, like Aniva, automated with RTGs in the Soviet era, and many have shared the same fate of neglect and abandonment as the human presence on these remote coasts has dwindled. They mark a region of extraordinary natural drama, where the cold seas teem with fish and marine mammals and the shores are largely wild and empty.

This is one of the least-visited corners of the lighthouse world, far from the heritage trails of Europe and North America. Its towers are not museums with gift shops but ruins on lonely rocks, their stories bound up with war, displacement, the nuclear age and the vast, harsh geography of the Russian Far East.

## Visiting Aniva and Sakhalin

Sakhalin is a long, mountainous island, the largest in Russia, with a landscape of forests, peat bogs, salmon rivers and a dramatic, weather-beaten coast. Its main city, Yuzhno-Sakhalinsk, preserves traces of its Japanese past alongside its Soviet and Russian history, and the island is a destination for fishing, hiking and wildlife. The southern cape, with its views over Aniva Bay, is among the island's most striking coastal landscapes.

For the lighthouse itself, the only realistic approach is by organised boat trip from the south of the island in the short summer season, weather permitting. Such trips circle the rock and, when the sea allows, attempt a landing. Visitors should treat the structure with respect and caution: it is a decaying ruin in a remote and dangerous place. But for those drawn to the strange poetry of abandoned places, few lighthouses in the world are as powerfully atmospheric as the grey tower rising from its battered rock off the end of Sakhalin.
