Washington State occupies the northwestern corner of the contiguous United States, and its coastline is among the most complex and demanding on the Pacific seaboard. The outer coast faces the full weight of the North Pacific, an ocean that drives long swells and violent winter gales against headlands of volcanic basalt and sandstone that erode inexorably. The Strait of Juan de Fuca, Puget Sound, and the San Juan Islands constitute an inland sea of extraordinary complexity, where tidal ranges of four metres, strong currents through constricted channels, and persistent fog have defined the navigation challenge since the first American settlement. The United States Lighthouse Service began establishing lights on the Washington coast from the late 1850s — just as the Oregon Territory became Washington Territory — and the lighthouse buildings that survive from this period are among the finest examples of Pacific Lighthouse Establishment architecture. ## 1. North Head Lighthouse, Cape Disappointment State Park

North Head stands on the headland north of Cape Disappointment at the Columbia River mouth, built in 1898 because mariners approaching from the north found Cape Disappointment lighthouse obscured by the headland above them. The 28-metre-tall white cylindrical masonry tower carries a first-order Fresnel lens that, in its original configuration, produced a light visible 25 nautical miles. The Columbia River bar — known as the 'Graveyard of the Pacific' — is one of the most dangerous river mouths in the world, where powerful river current meets ocean swell in a shoaling bar that has taken some 2,000 vessels. North Head works in conjunction with Cape Disappointment lighthouse to provide overlapping coverage. The tower is within Cape Disappointment State Park and is open for climbing through the Washington State Parks lighthouse programme; keepers' cottages are available as overnight accommodation.

## 2. Cape Disappointment Lighthouse, Ilwaco

Cape Disappointment was the first lighthouse established on the Pacific Coast north of the Columbia River, completed in 1856 on the rocky headland that John Meares named in 1788 after failing to find the great river he was seeking. The white conical tower stands 27 metres tall and was built by the contractor Francis Kelly using basalt blocks quarried on the headland. The original lens was a fourth-order Fresnel; the tower now carries a modern optic with a range of about 22 nautical miles. The station sits within Cape Disappointment State Park at the site of Lewis and Clark's 1805 Pacific terminus. The adjacent Lewis and Clark Interpretive Center occupies a former gun battery. The lighthouse is accessible by trail through the park; the trail from the park entrance passes Fort Canby, one of the coastal artillery installations that defended the Columbia mouth in the World War II era.

## 3. Cape Flattery Lighthouse, Tatoosh Island

Cape Flattery is the northwesternmost point of the contiguous United States, and its lighthouse occupies Tatoosh Island, a volcanic rock about 800 metres offshore from the cape. The lighthouse was completed in 1857, one of the first on the Washington coast, and its 27-metre white masonry tower is visible 16 nautical miles, marking the entrance to the Strait of Juan de Fuca for vessels arriving from the Pacific. The station was crewed continuously for over a century under conditions of remarkable isolation: Tatoosh Island is exposed to the full force of North Pacific storms, and the combination of violent weather, constant fog signals, and the psychological pressures of isolation made it one of the hardest keeper assignments on the coast. The island is now managed by the Makah Tribe, on whose reservation the cape lies. Access is by permission from the Makah Tribe; the cape viewpoint on the mainland is freely accessible from Neah Bay.

## 4. Admiralty Head Lighthouse, Whidbey Island

Admiralty Head lighthouse, on the southern tip of Whidbey Island where it overlooks the entrance to Puget Sound, was built in 1903 in the brick Craftsman style typical of the Pacific Coast lighthouse station architecture of this period. The two-story keepers' dwelling and attached tower stand on the bluff above Admiralty Inlet, with the dramatic profile of Mount Rainier visible on clear days across the sound. The lighthouse was decommissioned in 1922 when the Fort Casey military installation displaced it; a buoy now marks the adjacent navigation hazard. Admiralty Head is a Washington State Parks historic site, open for tours and housing a lighthouse interpretive centre. The adjacent Fort Casey has well-preserved concrete gun emplacements that together with the lighthouse form one of the most complete late nineteenth and early twentieth-century coastal defence and navigation complexes in the Pacific Northwest.

## 5. New Dungeness Lighthouse, Dungeness Spit

Dungeness Spit extends about 8 kilometres into the Strait of Juan de Fuca from the north Olympic Peninsula, the longest naturally occurring sand spit in the United States. At its tip stands the New Dungeness lighthouse, first lit in 1857 and still active, its white octagonal masonry tower rising 23 metres above the spit. The light character is a group flashing white and red, the red sectors covering the shallow water over the spit. The lighthouse is maintained by a volunteer keepers' programme — civilian volunteers stay for one-week rotations and assist with maintenance and visitor interpretation in a tradition that has made this one of the most participated-in lighthouse heritage programmes in the United States. The hike along the spit to the lighthouse is 8 kilometres each way, entirely on loose gravel and sand; the surrounding national wildlife refuge is outstanding for birdwatching and marine mammal watching.

## 6. Grays Harbor Lighthouse, Westport

The Grays Harbor lighthouse at Westport is the tallest lighthouse in Washington and the third tallest on the Pacific Coast, its white brick tower rising 38 metres from the dune landscape south of the harbour entrance. Completed in 1898, the tower was built in response to the increasing traffic entering Grays Harbor — a significant timber and fishing port — and the difficulty of identifying the harbour approach against the featureless low coast north of the Columbia mouth. Its third-order Fresnel lens produced a light visible 21 nautical miles; the lens is now on display in the Westport Maritime Museum, which occupies the former lighthouse watch house. The tower itself, now within the Westport Light State Park, is open for climbing on summer weekends through the Washington State Parks lighthouse keeper programme. The drive south along the Ocean Shores peninsula gives excellent views of the tower from the north.

## 7. Mukilteo Lighthouse, Mukilteo

Mukilteo, on the east shore of Puget Sound north of Seattle at the southern end of Saratoga Passage, carries a lighthouse that is among the most charming small towers on the Washington coast. The white octagonal wooden tower, completed in 1906 and standing 12 metres tall, is attached to a white clapboard keeper's dwelling in a design typical of the Pacific Coast Lighthouse Establishment's smaller stations. Its light character covers the ferry crossing between Mukilteo and Clinton on Whidbey Island, one of the most active ferry routes in the state. The lighthouse and grounds are a Washington State Park and the tower is open for weekend tours; the ferry terminal adjacent to the lighthouse adds an element of living maritime activity to the heritage site that many preserved lighthouse stations lack.

## 8. Point Wilson Lighthouse, Fort Worden State Park

Point Wilson marks the turn from Admiralty Inlet into the Strait of Juan de Fuca, an important navigational corner where the deep-draft vessels heading for Seattle, Tacoma, and Olympia must alter course. The lighthouse, first built in 1879 and the current octagonal wooden tower dating from a 1914 rebuilding, stands 18 metres tall and was equipped with a foghorn whose distinctive sound was a constant of the Port Townsend soundscape for generations. The station is within Fort Worden State Park, which preserves the entire infrastructure of a turn-of-the-century coastal artillery fort. Port Townsend itself is a remarkably intact Victorian seaport town; the combination of lighthouse, fort, and Victorian streetscape makes this one of the richest maritime heritage destinations in the Pacific Northwest.

## 9. Turn Point Lighthouse, Stuart Island

Stuart Island in the San Juan archipelago carries the Turn Point lighthouse at its western tip, marking the boundary between US and Canadian waters and the turn that vessels making from the Strait of Juan de Fuca toward the Gulf Islands and British Columbia must negotiate. The white masonry tower, completed in 1893, stands 18 metres tall. Stuart Island is accessible only by private boat or float plane; its two small communities of Prevost Harbour and Reid Harbor are popular with the cruising yacht fleet that explores the San Juans each summer. The lighthouse is operated by the US Coast Guard; the grounds are open to visitors arriving by water.

## 10. Lime Kiln Lighthouse, San Juan Island

Lime Kiln lighthouse on the western shore of San Juan Island is best known not for its navigational character but for the orca whale watching that takes place from the adjacent park. The white tower, completed in 1919 and standing 17 metres tall, overlooks the Haro Strait where the Southern Resident killer whale population passes with regularity in summer, feeding on Chinook salmon along the kelp beds. The station is a Washington State Park and whale-watching preserve; the lighthouse is open for tours, and the resident naturalists can explain both the light's history and the whale's ecology. The combination makes Lime Kiln among the most visited lighthouse sites in the Pacific Northwest.

## Visiting Washington's Lighthouses

Washington's lighthouses divide between the outer coast (Cape Flattery, North Head, Cape Disappointment, Grays Harbor), accessible by road with varying distances from accommodation, and the Puget Sound and Strait sites, which are woven into the ferry-dependent island geography. Cape Disappointment State Park and Fort Worden State Park both offer camping and accommodation, making extended lighthouse stays possible. The summer months of June to September offer the longest days and most reliable weather, though the outer coast remains subject to fog at any time. The Washington State Parks lighthouse keeper programme at several sites gives visitors an unusually direct engagement with these buildings.
