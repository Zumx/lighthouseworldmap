The Baltic Sea is an almost enclosed body of water some 1,600 kilometres long, bounded by nine countries and connected to the North Sea only through the narrow Danish straits. Its peculiar geography creates a set of navigational problems unlike any other sea in the world. The water is brackish, not salt, because river inflow exceeds evaporation; tidal ranges are negligible, a few centimetres at most; but in winter the northern reaches freeze solidly from December to April, and the pack ice can extend as far south as the approaches to Stockholm. The southern shallows contain vast fields of shoals and sandbanks — the Pomeranian Bight, the Bay of Gdansk, the approaches to Riga — while the western entrance through the Øresund is barely 8 kilometres wide at its narrowest. Against this backdrop, the Baltic nations built a network of lighthouses beginning in the seventeenth century, accelerating through the age of industrialisation, and spanning the full range of construction techniques: solid stone towers on exposed offshore reefs, elegant cast-iron columns on flat sandy capes, and dramatic mid-sea rock stations visible across open water for thirty or more nautical miles.

## 1. Bengtskär Lighthouse, Finland

Bengtskär, a small rocky island 25 kilometres southwest of Hanko in the outer Finnish archipelago, carries the tallest lighthouse in Scandinavia in terms of tower height from base to lantern: 52 metres of Finnish granite, built in 1906 to the design of engineer Bruno Granholm. The focal plane is 65 metres above sea level; the white flash every five seconds is visible for 25 nautical miles across the approaches to the Gulf of Finland. The lighthouse marks the gap between the Finnish outer skerries and the open Baltic, one of the busiest sea lanes in the world for container traffic, tankers, and passenger ferries serving Helsinki, Tallinn and St Petersburg. Bengtskär was the scene of fierce fighting in July 1941 when Soviet naval infantry attempted to take the island; the defenders repelled the attack and a small memorial chapel in the lighthouse commemorates the fallen. The lighthouse offers accommodation in the keeper's quarters, accessible by summer ferry from Kasnäs.

## 2. Kõpu Lighthouse, Estonia

Kõpu on the island of Hiiumaa in the western Estonian archipelago is one of the oldest continuously operating lighthouses in the world, first lit in 1531 on the orders of the Hanseatic League to guide trade vessels through the treacherous approach to the Gulf of Finland. The extraordinary buttressed limestone tower, 36 metres tall and reinforced by massive triangular stone piers at each corner, has the solidity of a medieval fortification. It was built to last without a keeper — early keepers proved unreliable — and fires were burned on the top platform for centuries before a conventional lantern was installed. The current light shows a white flash every fifteen seconds with a range of 26 nautical miles, marking the western cape of Hiiumaa and the passage between the island and the Swedish island of Gotland. The tower is open to visitors and the surrounding forest reserve is a pleasant walking area; the lighthouse is accessible by ferry from Rohuküla on the Estonian mainland.

## 3. Långe Jan Lighthouse, Sweden

Långe Jan at the southern tip of Öland is the tallest lighthouse in the Scandinavian peninsula at 42 metres; its focal plane at 45 metres above sea level gives a range of 23 nautical miles across the southern Baltic approaches. Built in 1785 and substantially rebuilt in 1844, the lighthouse guides vessels around the tip of Sweden's second-largest island and into the Kalmar Sound, the historically important passage between Öland and the Swedish mainland. The southern tip of Öland is a UNESCO World Heritage Site — the Alvar limestone plain supports unique flora and hosts massive bird migrations in spring and autumn. The lighthouse forms the focal point of the Ottenby nature reserve, and the site has been a migration watchpoint since the nineteenth century; a bird observatory operates from the keeper's buildings. Tower tours are available in summer.

## 4. Dueodde Lighthouse, Denmark

Dueodde stands at the southernmost tip of the Danish island of Bornholm, marking the approach to the island from the Baltic Sea lanes running between Sweden, Poland and Germany. Built in 1962, the concrete tower is 47 metres tall and represents the modern generation of Danish lighthouse engineering; its two white flashes every ten seconds are visible for 19 nautical miles. The lighthouse marks the southern approaches to Bornholm's harbour at Rønne and warns vessels off the shallow water extending from the cape. The surrounding landscape is the finest white sand dune coast in Denmark, backed by pine plantations. Dueodde beach is among the most celebrated in northern Europe, and the lighthouse provides the visual focal point for the entire southern coast. The tower is open to visitors in summer and the climb offers panoramic views across the Baltic to the Swedish coast to the north and the hazy Polish coast to the south.

## 5. Hel Lighthouse, Poland

The lighthouse at Hel, on the narrow sandy spit that encloses the Bay of Puck on the southern Baltic coast of Poland, is one of the most distinctive lighthouse structures in the Baltic region: a red-brick Gothic Revival tower built in 1875 during the period of Prussian administration, when the Hel Peninsula was part of the German Empire. The tower is 41 metres tall and shows a white flash every five seconds with a range of 16 nautical miles, marking the tip of the Hel spit and guiding vessels into the Bay of Gdansk. The spit is only a few hundred metres wide at its narrowest and the lighthouse sits among pine trees with sea on both sides. The peninsula was the site of the last Polish military resistance following the German invasion in September 1939; the garrison held out until October 2. The lighthouse is managed by the Polish Maritime Office and is open to the public in summer.

## 6. Stilo Lighthouse, Poland

The Stilo lighthouse, near the village of Stilo on the Pomeranian coast between Gdansk and Słupsk, is the oldest lighthouse in Poland and one of the most elegant in the Baltic, a slender white-painted cylindrical tower built in 1904. It stands 33 metres tall on a low sandy cliff above the Pomeranian Bay coast and shows a white flash every five seconds visible for 22 nautical miles. The surrounding area is part of the Słowiński National Park, a UNESCO Biosphere Reserve featuring mobile sand dunes that are among the largest in Europe and move perceptibly from year to year. The lighthouse can be reached on foot from the village of Stilo through pine forest; it is managed by the Polish Maritime Office and is open for tower climbs at weekends and during summer.

## 7. Ölands Norra Udde (Långe Erik), Sweden

At the northern tip of Öland, the lighthouse called Långe Erik — built in 1845 and 32 metres tall — marks the other end of the island from Långe Jan and completes the pair of powerful white lights that frame the Kalmar Sound. The northern cape is less visited than the southern tip but offers its own distinct character: a long limestone reef extends offshore, and in winter pack ice grinds against it while the lighthouse continues to flash through the frozen night. The station has been converted to holiday accommodation, making it possible to stay in the keeper's cottage. The northern tip of Öland is connected by road to the main island and the ferry from Byxelkrok runs to the Swedish mainland.

## 8. Gotska Sandön Lighthouse, Sweden

The island of Gotska Sandön, a national park in the open Baltic north of Gotland, carries a lighthouse built in 1858 on its flat sandy southeastern shore. The island is entirely uninhabited except for a seasonal national park ranger and the automated lighthouse; it is the most remote inhabited place in Sweden during summer. The lighthouse is a modest tower serving primarily as a waypoint for Baltic crossing traffic between Sweden, Finland and the Baltic states. The island is famous for its seal colonies, migrating raptors, and the remarkable isolation of its pine-forested dunes. Access is by irregular summer ferry from Nynäshamn or Fårösund; the crossing can take up to five hours and is weather-dependent.

## 9. Falsterbo Lighthouse, Sweden

At the southwestern tip of Sweden where the Øresund opens into the Baltic proper, the Falsterbo lighthouse marks the Swedish side of one of the world's busiest shipping lanes. Built in 1796 and rebuilt in 1885, the octagonal brick tower stands on the flat Falsterbo Peninsula with a focal plane of 24 metres; the light is visible for 14 nautical miles. The point is critical for Baltic navigation: vessels heading into the Baltic from the North Sea through the Øresund must round Falsterbo Reef to avoid the shallow ground extending from the peninsula. The lighthouse is surrounded by the Falsterbo nature reserve, one of the finest bird migration watchpoints in Europe — in September and October, raptors, cranes and thousands of passerines funnel through on their way south. The town of Vellinge maintains the lighthouse as a heritage site and it is open in summer.

## 10. Akmenrags Lighthouse, Latvia

The Akmenrags lighthouse on the Latvian coast north of Liepāja marks a rock reef extending from the otherwise straight sandy shore of the eastern Baltic, one of the most featureless coastlines in the sea basin. The white cylindrical tower, built in 1879 during the Russian Imperial administration of Courland province, is 21 metres tall with a focal plane of 34 metres; the white flash every four seconds carries 16 nautical miles. The coast here is low and densely forested; the lighthouse is visible from the sea long before the tree-line itself, making it an essential landfall mark for vessels approaching Liepāja port. Latvia regained independence in 1991 and now maintains its lighthouse network through the Latvian Maritime Administration. The lighthouse is accessible from the coastal road south of Liepāja; the surrounding pine-dune coast is protected as part of the South Kurzeme Nature Park.

## Visiting the Baltic Sea Lighthouses

The Baltic lighthouse trail passes through nine countries, each with its own heritage of maritime administration and lighthouse architecture. Finland's Bengtskär and Estonia's Kõpu are the standout historic destinations; the Polish lighthouses at Hel and Stilo combine maritime interest with remarkable natural landscapes. Summer — June through August — is the practical season for the northern and eastern Baltic; the Danish and Swedish lighthouses accessible from Copenhagen are rewarding year-round.
