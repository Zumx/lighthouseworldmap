Okinawa is where Japan dissolves into the Pacific. From the main island near Naha, the Ryukyu archipelago scatters more than a thousand kilometres southwest toward Taiwan, a string of coral-fringed islands set in water that shades from jade to deep indigo within a single bay. The lights that mark these islands are unlike anything in the cold north: white towers standing over uplifted limestone capes, fringing reefs that dry at low tide, and channels where the Kuroshio current runs warm and fast. They guard a subtropical sea that breeds typhoons in summer and stays swimmable in winter. Here are ten lighthouses worth seeking out across the Ryukyus, from the climbable capes of the main island to Japan's southernmost and westernmost lights.

## 1. Zanpa Misaki Lighthouse (Cape Zanpa, Yomitan)

The light at Cape Zanpa is the one most visitors meet first, and it sets the tone for the whole archipelago. A tall white tower roughly 31 metres high, it stands on a low limestone headland on the west coast of central Okinawa Island, near the old US training ground at Bolo Point. You can climb it: a tight spiral staircase delivers you to a gallery looking out over jagged coral rock where the East China Sea breaks white. The light shows a white flash and reaches well over 18 nautical miles, marking the cape for ships running the coast between Naha and the northern villages. At sunset the rock turns gold and divers gather on the reef below.

## 2. Cape Hedo Lighthouse (Hedo Misaki)

At the northern tip of Okinawa Island, the land rises into limestone cliffs and ends abruptly at Cape Hedo. The lighthouse here marks the northernmost point of the main island, where the East China Sea and the Pacific meet over a shallow reef shelf. On clear days you can see the outline of Yoron and the other Amami islands to the north. The white tower is modest in height but commanding in position, perched above grassland and cliff where habu vipers shelter and sea eagles ride the wind. The drive north along Route 58 to reach it is itself part of the pilgrimage.

## 3. Higashi-Hennazaki Lighthouse (Cape Higashi-Henna, Miyako)

If you climb only one Ryukyu light, make it this one. Cape Higashi-Henna is a long, narrow spit of upthrust limestone reaching nearly two kilometres into the sea at the eastern end of Miyako-jima. The white lighthouse, around 24 metres tall and standing about 43 metres above the sea, is open to climb, and the gallery gives one of the most dramatic seascapes in Japan: cliffs dropping on both sides into water so clear the reef shows through it like stained glass. The light flashes white and ranges roughly 17 nautical miles. The cape is a designated place of scenic beauty, and on a calm day the contrast of white tower, green grass and turquoise sea is almost unreal.

## 4. Hateruma Lighthouse (Hateruma-jima)

Hateruma is the southernmost inhabited island of Japan, deep in the Yaeyama group, and its lighthouse is the southernmost in the country. It is a plain working light, not built for tourists, standing among sugarcane fields on an island famous for two things: the clearest night skies in Japan, where the Southern Cross briefly rises above the horizon in spring, and the Cape Takana monument marking the southern limit of Japanese territory. Reaching the light means a ferry from Ishigaki across often rough water, and that effort is part of why so few people ever see it. The sense of standing at the bottom of the country is real.

## 5. Hirakubozaki Lighthouse (Cape Hirakubo, Ishigaki)

At the northern tip of Ishigaki Island, Cape Hirakubo points toward the open Pacific. The white lighthouse here stands on a grassy headland above pandanus and hibiscus, looking out over reef water and, on the clearest days, the silhouette of distant islands. The light marks the approach to the channels among the Yaeyama Islands, where the Kuroshio runs strong. It is a long but rewarding drive from Ishigaki town along the island's spine, past the famous blue of Kabira Bay, and the cape is a favourite spot to watch both sunrise over the Pacific and the stars afterward.

## 6. Kabira / Ishigaki Approach Lights

Kabira Bay on Ishigaki is one of the most photographed places in the Ryukyus, a sheltered lagoon of layered turquoise where black-pearl cultivation rafts float over white sand. Swimming is forbidden because of the currents, so the bay is best seen by glass-bottomed boat. The minor lights and beacons around Ishigaki's harbour and the reef edges guide the ferries that fan out daily to Taketomi, Iriomote and the other Yaeyama islands. They are humble structures, but they keep the busiest small-boat network in the southern Ryukyus running safely through coral passages.

## 7. Yonaguni Lighthouse (Westernmost Japan)

Yonaguni is the westernmost inhabited point of Japan, closer to Taiwan than to Okinawa Island, and on rare clear winter evenings the mountains of Taiwan appear across the strait. The lighthouse stands above cliffs lashed by the open ocean, marking an island known for its wild horses, its strong currents, and the controversial underwater rock formations offshore that divers come to debate. This is a true frontier light, exposed to the full force of the Pacific and the typhoon track, and to stand beneath it is to be at the edge of the country looking west.

## 8. Kudaka Island Light

Kudaka-jima, a low coral island off the Chinen peninsula in the southeast of Okinawa Island, is considered the most sacred place in the Ryukyu religion — the island where the creator goddess Amamikiyo is said to have descended. A small light guides the ferry that crosses from Azama port. The whole island is treated with reverence; certain groves and shores are off-limits to visitors, and bicycles are the only practical way to explore its flat coral lanes. The light is unassuming, but the passage it marks carries pilgrims as much as travellers.

## 9. Tsuken-jima Light

Tsuken-jima, a small island off the Katsuren peninsula reached by ferry from Heshikiya, is famous for carrots and for some of the clearest swimming water near the main island. The light marking its approach stands over reef flats that dry at low tide into broad coral pavements. The island makes an easy day trip, and the modest beacon is a reminder that even the smallest Ryukyu islands depend on a working light to keep their ferry lifelines open across shallow, reef-strewn channels.

## 10. Kerama Islands Lights (Tokashiki and Zamami)

The Kerama Islands, a short fast-ferry ride west of Naha, are ringed by the water that gave the world the term "Kerama blue" — a luminous transparency that draws snorkellers and humpback whales alike, the latter calving here each winter. Small lights and beacons guide the ferries among Tokashiki, Zamami and Aka, threading reef passages where green sea turtles graze in the shallows. The Keramas are now a national park, and their lights mark some of the most protected and beautiful coral seas in all of Japan.

## Visiting the Ryukyu Lights

A few practicalities. The climbable towers — Zanpa and Higashi-Henna above all — charge a small fee and keep daytime hours; check seasonal closures. The far southern lights on Hateruma, Yonaguni and the Yaeyama islands require ferries or flights from Ishigaki, and crossings are routinely cancelled in rough weather, so build in spare days. Typhoon season runs roughly from late summer into autumn, when even the main island can be shut down for days; winter and spring bring calmer seas, swimmable water and the best visibility. Rent a car for Okinawa Island and the larger outer islands, carry sun protection and water, and watch your footing on the sharp, eroded limestone of the capes. Above all, give yourself time: the distances here are oceanic, and the joy is in the slow island-hopping between one white tower and the next.
