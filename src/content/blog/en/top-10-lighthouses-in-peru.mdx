The Peruvian coast is a paradox: a 2,400-kilometre ribbon of desert pressed against one of the most productive seas on earth. The cold Humboldt Current sweeps up from the south, suppressing rainfall almost to nothing along the shore while feeding an explosion of marine life that has supported fishing fleets and guano industries for centuries. For the lighthouse keeper, this means a coast of extremes: brilliant sun and bone-dry air, broken by the grey sea fog the Peruvians call the garúa that can blot out the headlands for days. The lights run from the busy approaches to Callao, the great port serving Lima, southward past the guano islands and the fishing towns to the Chilean border, and north through Talara's oilfields to the mangroves near Ecuador.

## 1. Faro La Marina, Miraflores, Lima

The most visited lighthouse in Peru stands not at a remote cape but on the clifftop promenade of Miraflores, in the heart of Lima's most fashionable district. The Faro La Marina rises from the Malecón above the Pacific, surrounded by parks, paragliders launching from the cliffs, and the steady traffic of the capital. Painted in white and black bands, it has become an emblem of the modern city, photographed against the sunset over the ocean far more often than it guides any ship. Yet it still marks the coast off Lima, and its setting above the surf breaks of Miraflores makes it one of the most accessible lighthouses to visit anywhere in South America.

## 2. Faro Punta San Juan, Marcona

Punta San Juan, on the desert coast near the iron-mining town of Marcona in the Ica region, marks one of the richest wildlife sites in Peru. The headland and its protected guano reserve shelter colonies of Humboldt penguins, South American fur seals, sea lions and seabirds in numbers that recall the abundance of the whole coast before exploitation. The light guides vessels along this exposed southern shore, where the desert reaches the sea in cliffs of bare rock. The combination of the beacon, the wildlife reserve and the stark Atacama-edge scenery makes Punta San Juan one of the most evocative points on the coast.

## 3. Faro de San Nicolás, San Juan de Marcona

Close to Punta San Juan, the port of San Nicolás serves the great iron-ore terminal of Marcona, one of the principal mineral export points of southern Peru. The lighthouse here marks the approach for the bulk carriers that load ore bound for the world's steelworks. The bay of San Nicolás is one of the few sheltered anchorages on this otherwise pitiless coast, and the light is a working tool of Peruvian industry, standing above a shore where the desert and the cold, fish-rich sea meet in stark contrast.

## 4. Faro Punta Coles, Ilo

Punta Coles, near the southern port of Ilo in the Moquegua region, is a peninsula and protected wildlife reserve marking the approach to one of Peru's deep-south harbours. Ilo serves the copper industry of the southern highlands and is the proposed Pacific outlet for landlocked Bolivia. The lighthouse stands above a coast of sea lions and seabirds, where the Humboldt Current is at its coldest and the desert at its most absolute. The reserve protects breeding colonies on the rocky point, and the light guides the mineral carriers and fishing boats that use the port.

## 5. Antiguo Faro de Puerto Eten, Lambayeque

On the northern coast at Puerto Eten, in the Lambayeque region, stands one of the oldest and most historic lighthouses in Peru. The old lighthouse dates from the nineteenth century, when Eten was a thriving export port connected to the sugar estates of the interior by one of Peru's first railways. The harbour has long since declined, but the iron tower remains as a monument to the age when this stretch of coast shipped sugar and cotton to the world. The setting, near the colonial towns of Lambayeque and the great archaeological sites of the Moche and Sicán cultures, gives the lighthouse a rich historical backdrop.

## 6. Faro de Puerto Eten, Lambayeque

The working light at Puerto Eten continues to mark the coast where its historic predecessor once stood, serving the fishing boats and the few vessels that still use this quiet northern shore. The Lambayeque coast is famous for its caballitos de totora, the reed boats that fishermen have used here for thousands of years, and for the long beaches that stretch toward Pimentel and Santa Rosa. The light stands above a coast that blends working fishery with a deep pre-Hispanic past, near the desert valleys where the great pyramids of Túcume rise from the plain.

## 7. Faro de Cerro Azul, Cañete

Cerro Azul, on the coast of the Cañete valley south of Lima, is a fishing port and surf town whose lighthouse marks the approach along the central Peruvian shore. The town grew as an export point for the cotton of the Cañete valley and retains the iron pier and warehouses of its commercial heyday. Today Cerro Azul is better known to surfers, who come for the long left-hand point break, but the light still guides the fishing fleet and the coastal traffic. The ruins of the pre-Inca fortress above the bay add to a setting that joins the maritime present with a long history.

## 8. Faro de Ancón, Lima

Ancón, a historic seaside resort north of Lima, was the fashionable bathing place of the capital's elite in the nineteenth and early twentieth centuries, and its sheltered bay carries a lighthouse marking the approach. The town's belle-époque villas line the curving beach, and the bay was the scene of the signing of the treaty that ended a chapter of the War of the Pacific. The light guides the yachts and fishing boats that use the protected anchorage, and the resort's faded grandeur gives the setting a particular nostalgic charm, an easy excursion from Lima along the desert coast.

## 9. Faro de Talara, Piura

Talara, on the far northern coast in the Piura region, is the centre of Peru's oil industry, a town built around refineries and offshore production. The lighthouse marks the approach to this industrial port and the tanker traffic that serves it. The Talara coast is arid in the extreme, a place of oil derricks against bare desert, but offshore the warm and cold currents meet, producing the rich fishing grounds and the famous big-game fishing that drew anglers to nearby Cabo Blanco in the mid-twentieth century. The light is a working beacon of the northern oil coast.

## 10. Faro Pariñas, Punta Pariñas

Punta Pariñas, near Talara, is the westernmost point of mainland South America, a desert headland thrusting into the Pacific. The lighthouse here marks this significant geographical extremity and guides vessels rounding the point along the Piura coast. The cape lies amid the oilfields and the rich offshore waters where the Humboldt Current begins to give way to warmer tropical water further north. As the continent's farthest west, Pariñas holds a special place, and its light stands at a true edge of the South American landmass.
