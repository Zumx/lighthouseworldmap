Michigan is the lighthouse capital of the United States. No state has more — over 120 lighthouses, including active towers, historic structures, and decommissioned stations. The reason lies in geography: Michigan is surrounded by four of the five Great Lakes, and its two peninsulas give it more freshwater coastline than any other state in the nation. Lake Superior, Lake Michigan, Lake Huron, and Lake Erie between them carry some of the world's heaviest inland freight traffic — iron ore, coal, limestone, and grain moving on enormous bulk carriers — and the rocky shoals, shifting sandbars, and sudden violent storms of the Great Lakes have claimed thousands of vessels since the first European navigation of these waters. From the mid-nineteenth century onward, the United States Lighthouse Service built stations at every significant hazard, harbour, and turning point. Many of those towers are now managed by local historical societies, state parks, and the National Park Service, while the United States Coast Guard maintains the active aids. ## 1. Big Sable Point Lighthouse, Mason County

Big Sable Point Lighthouse stands at the end of a two-kilometre walk through sand dunes on a remote stretch of Lake Michigan shoreline in Mason County. The tower, completed in 1867, is a white cylindrical brick structure originally 34 metres tall; its distinctive black-and-white banded daymark was applied in the mid-twentieth century when the surrounding brick was sheathed in cast-iron plates painted in contrasting sections. The light character is a white flash every ten seconds, visible for about 23 nautical miles across the open lake. The site is within Ludington State Park and is managed by the Sable Points Lighthouse Keepers Association, which has restored the keeper's dwelling and opens the tower for climbing during summer months. The isolation of the location — no road access to the tower — preserves the atmosphere of a working nineteenth-century lighthouse station.

## 2. Point Betsie Lighthouse, Benzie County

Point Betsie Lighthouse, completed in 1858, marks a critical turning point on southern Lake Michigan where northbound vessels moving along the coast must alter course to enter the Manitou Passage between the mainland and the Manitou Islands. The white cylindrical tower is roughly 13 metres tall, with an attached keeper's dwelling in brick. The light — a white flash every five seconds — is visible for about 15 nautical miles. The point is also significant as the southern entrance to the Manitou Passage, which is itself littered with wrecks. The lighthouse has been transferred to Benzie County and the Friends of Point Betsie Lighthouse operate the site; the keeper's house serves as a museum during summer. The beach on either side of the lighthouse is one of the most scenic on the eastern shore of Lake Michigan, and the dunes of Sleeping Bear behind give the view a dramatic scale.

## 3. Au Sable Point Lighthouse, Alger County

Au Sable Point Lighthouse stands on the south shore of Lake Superior within Pictured Rocks National Lakeshore, at a site that was once one of the most dangerous on the entire Great Lakes system. The point extends into the lake on a shoal of submerged rock that wrecked many vessels before the lighthouse was built in 1874. The red brick tower stands 33 metres tall on a forested bluff, with a white light flashing every four seconds visible for about 17 nautical miles. The lighthouse is accessible only by foot — a three-kilometre hike along the Lake Superior shore through Pictured Rocks — giving it a remoteness unusual in the region. The National Park Service operates the site; the keeper's quarters are preserved and sometimes open for ranger-led tours during the summer season. The surrounding Pictured Rocks sandstone cliffs are extraordinary.

## 4. Grand Haven Lighthouse, Ottawa County

Grand Haven Lighthouse at the mouth of the Grand River on Lake Michigan is actually a two-tower range light system: the inner pierhead light, a white conical tower completed in 1895, and the outer red steel pier light of 1905 stand at the end of a long concrete pier extending into the lake. The inner light is 21 metres tall; the outer light, connected to it by a catwalk, is roughly 16 metres. The range system guides vessels through the harbour entrance. Grand Haven is a popular summer resort town, and the pier and lighthouse are one of the most visited attractions on the eastern Lake Michigan shore. The pier walk is a public promenade; lighthouse access is restricted to exterior viewing. The combination of the lighthouse, the pier, and the fountain and boardwalk in the Grand River channel makes this a particularly photogenic evening destination.

## 5. Holland Harbor Lighthouse (Big Red), Ottawa County

The Holland Harbor Lighthouse is known universally as "Big Red" — an immediate reflection of its distinctive vermilion paint. The current lighthouse structure, a square wooden building with an attached tower, was erected in 1907 at the entrance to Lake Macatawa, the inland lake that connects Holland to Lake Michigan. The wooden lighthouse with its vivid colour is unique on the Great Lakes; the keeper's quarters and the tower are unified in a single utilitarian building rather than the more common separated tower-and-dwelling arrangement. Its red light is visible for about 8 nautical miles. The Holland Harbor Light Historical Commission, a volunteer organisation, maintains the lighthouse. It can be viewed from the north pier in Holland State Park, and the beach on either side of the harbour entrance is among the finest on Lake Michigan.

## 6. Whitefish Point Lighthouse, Chippewa County

Whitefish Point is the oldest active lighthouse on Lake Superior, established in 1849 and rebuilt in its current skeletal steel tower form in 1861. The 27-metre metal structure, white-painted with a characteristic of a white flash every 7.5 seconds visible for about 22 nautical miles, stands at the most northeasterly turning point on the American side of the lake. The point is well named — it provides the first shelter for vessels rounding into Whitefish Bay from the open lake in eastbound passages. The Edmund Fitzgerald, the most famous shipwreck in Great Lakes history, went down in a storm in 1975 only about 27 kilometres northwest of the point. The Great Lakes Shipwreck Museum at Whitefish Point is housed in the lighthouse complex, and the museum displays the Edmund Fitzgerald's bell. The site also hosts a major hawk and owl migration count each spring.

## 7. Round Island Lighthouse, Mackinac County

Round Island Lighthouse stands on the uninhabited Round Island in the Straits of Mackinac, the passage connecting Lake Huron and Lake Michigan, one of the most critical shipping chokepoints in the Great Lakes. The brick tower, built in 1895 in a distinctive red-and-cream colour scheme with Romanesque-style detailing, stands about 18 metres tall. It was decommissioned in 1947 and subsequently deteriorated significantly before a restoration campaign by the Round Island Lighthouse Preservation Fund and the US Forest Service arrested the decline. It is now partially stabilised; passenger ferries between Mackinaw City and Mackinac Island pass close to the lighthouse, and it is a prominent feature of the view from the island. Mackinac Island — where cars are banned — is one of Michigan's major heritage tourism destinations, and Round Island Lighthouse is visible from the island's harbour.

## 8. Marblehead Lighthouse, Ottawa County (Lake Erie)

Michigan's Lake Erie shoreline is limited to a small section of Monroe County, but a brief mention of Marblehead — just over the state line in Ohio's Ottawa County — is warranted as the oldest continuously operating lighthouse on the Great Lakes, established in 1822. The 21-metre limestone tower, painted white with a green lantern, stands on a limestone peninsula jutting into Lake Erie and is managed as a State Memorial open to the public. Its history spans nearly the entire era of American lighthouse construction. For Michigan visitors, Sanilac Shores Underwater Preserve in Lake Huron also preserves a remarkable collection of Great Lakes wrecks accessible to sport divers.

## 9. Presque Isle Lighthouse (New), Presque Isle County

The "New" Presque Isle Lighthouse, built in 1870 to replace the original 1840 tower a short distance away, stands 34 metres tall — one of the tallest lighthouse towers on Lake Huron — on a peninsula in Presque Isle County at the northern end of the Michigan mitten. The tower is white brick with a flashing white light visible for about 25 nautical miles across Lake Huron. The attached keeper's house is now a museum operated by the township; the tower is open for climbing during summer months and the view from the top extends over the wooded peninsula and the open lake. The adjacent "Old" Presque Isle Lighthouse of 1840 is also preserved as a museum, making this one of the few places where visitors can compare lighthouses from two different eras of American lighthouse construction side by side.

## 10. Seul Choix Point Lighthouse, Schoolcraft County

Seul Choix Point (pronounced "Sis-shway" by locals) stands on the Upper Peninsula's Lake Superior south shore, at the only harbour of refuge — the French name means "only choice" — between the Straits of Mackinac and Munising. The white brick tower, 24 metres tall, was completed in 1895 and its light flashes white every four seconds, visible for about 17 nautical miles. The keeper's dwelling and outbuildings are maintained by the Michigan Historical Museum system, and the site is noted for having one of the best-preserved fog signal buildings on the Great Lakes. The lighthouse museum is open daily in summer. The harbour itself, a small natural bay sheltered by the point, remains in use by fishing vessels and is a tranquil place entirely different from the busy tourist harbours of the Lower Peninsula.

## Planning Your Visit

Michigan's lighthouse season runs from late May through October, when most museums are staffed, tower climbs are offered, and roads in the Upper Peninsula are reliably clear. The western Upper Peninsula and Lake Superior shore are genuinely remote; fuel, lodging, and services should be planned in advance. The Lower Peninsula's Lake Michigan shore from Traverse City to Holland is well served by tourist infrastructure. The Michigan Lighthouse Conservancy and the Great Lakes Lighthouse Keepers Association both publish guides to lighthouse visiting.
