## Beauty and Function

The lighthouse was never built to be beautiful. It was built to be seen, to be tall enough and bright enough to reach a mariner who might be fifteen or twenty miles offshore in darkness. Yet the same requirements that made lighthouses functional — a position on the most dramatic headland, a form that rose cleanly against sky and sea, materials quarried from the local rock — produced, in the hands of skilled engineers and their teams of masons, some of the most striking pieces of architecture anywhere in the coastal world.

Beauty in a lighthouse takes several forms. There is the pure geometrical elegance of a well-proportioned masonry column. There is the drama of a tower placed at the absolute extremity of land, a finger of stone pointed into the void. There is the colour pattern of a painted tower that functions as a daymark, its stripes or chequer pattern identifying it at a glance from the sea. And there is the larger composition: lighthouse, cottages, fog signal building, and boat slip arranged on a rock or a headland with the completeness of a self-sufficient village.

## Skerryvore Lighthouse, Scotland

Alan Stevenson, son of Robert and uncle of the novelist, spent six years building Skerryvore on a submerged basalt reef about twelve miles south-west of Tiree in the Scottish Inner Hebrides. The lighthouse was completed in 1844. Its tower stands 48 metres above the sea and tapers from a massive granite base — 12 metres in diameter — to a lantern room whose proportions Stevenson refined with the eye of an architect as much as an engineer. The stone is a dark grey Hynish granite, contrasted with lighter dressed stone around the door and porthole openings, and the tower's gentle hyperbolic curve is visually perfect from every angle.

Robert Louis Stevenson, whose family built it, called Skerryvore 'the noblest of all extant deep-sea lights.' Mariners see its Fl(2) W 20s character — two white flashes every twenty seconds — to a range of 23 nautical miles. The tower has never been seriously damaged by the sea.

## Portland Head Light, Maine, USA

The oldest lighthouse in Maine, first lit on 10 January 1791 on the orders of George Washington, sits on a basalt headland at the entrance to Portland Harbour where the Maine coast turns its shoulder toward the Atlantic. The tower, 24 metres tall, is painted white with a red lantern room and shows an occulting light (Occ W 4s) visible for 24 nautical miles. What makes Portland Head beautiful is not the tower alone but the ensemble: the keeper's house, attached to the base of the tower, is a gabled Victorian structure with dormer windows; a broad lawn falls toward the cliff edge; and the whole composition is framed by the dark spruce forest and the Atlantic horizon.

Fort Williams Park surrounds the lighthouse and provides the best vantage points; the keeper's house operates as a museum with exhibits on the tower's more than two centuries of continuous service.

## Tourlitis Lighthouse, Andros, Greece

Tourlitis stands on a stack of rock just offshore from Chora, the clifftop capital of the island of Andros in the Cyclades, and the combination of the pale tower, the red lantern, the dark volcanic rock, and the Aegean blue creates something that photographers return to year after year. The original lighthouse was built in 1897; it was destroyed in the Second World War and rebuilt to the same form in 1994. The tower is not especially tall — perhaps 9 metres — but its position at the foot of a cliff beneath the medieval Venetian castle gives it a verticality that belies its actual dimensions.

Access is by a narrow footbridge from the cliff path, and in summer the approach is thick with day-trippers making the descent from the village above.

## Farol do Cabo de São Vicente, Portugal

The south-western corner of continental Europe — Cape St Vincent in the Algarve — has been considered a spiritual as well as a navigational threshold since antiquity. The Romans called it Promontorium Sacrum, the sacred promontory. The current lighthouse, rebuilt in 1846, occupies the ruins of a Franciscan convent whose inhabitants tended the original coal-fire light for centuries. The white painted tower stands within the convent's fortified walls; the lantern room, 28 metres above the platform and 86 metres above the sea, houses a rotating first-order Fresnel lens that was the most powerful in Portugal when installed.

The cape receives extreme westerly Atlantic swells, and on wild days in winter the spray reaches the lighthouse walls. The Fl(2) W 15s character is visible for 32 nautical miles.

## Hook Lighthouse, County Wexford, Ireland

Hook Head in County Wexford, Ireland, claims to be among the oldest operational lighthouses in the world, with a monastic fire maintained on the headland from at least the fifth century and a stone tower built by the Norman lord William Marshal around 1172. The current tower is substantially Marshal's structure: a thick round column of local limestone, 36 metres tall, that has been maintained, repaired, and heightened over eight centuries. The lower section is Norman work, identifiable by the style of its ashlar; the upper sections are later repairs. The lighthouse is painted in black-and-white horizontal bands and is open to visitors year-round, making it one of the most accessible historic lighthouses in Ireland.

Its light shows Fl(3) W 8s with a range of 23 nautical miles.

## Kéréon Lighthouse, Brittany, France

If any lighthouse can claim to be beautiful on the inside as much as the outside, it is Kéréon, built between 1907 and 1916 on the Men Tensel reef in the Fromveur Passage between Ushant and the Breton mainland. The construction was funded by the estate of the Chevalier de Kéréon, a naval officer guillotined in the Revolution, and the bequest was so generous that the lighthouse authority built not a functional granite cylinder but something closer to a small palace: the interior features carved oak panelling, Art Nouveau mosaics, sculpted metalwork, and mahogany fittings that belong more naturally in a gentlemen's club than a rock lighthouse. The tower itself, 47 metres tall, shows Oc(2+1) WR 24s and has a range of 17 nautical miles.

Kéréon is among the most sought-after postings in the French lighthouse service and remains crewed by two keepers — one of the last in France to have resisted full automation.

## Ponta dos Capelinhos, Azores

The Capelinhos lighthouse on the island of Faial in the Azores is unique among candidates for the world's most dramatic lighthouse sites because the landscape around it was created by a volcanic eruption that occurred during its own operational lifetime. The tower was built in 1894. In 1957, a series of eruptions over thirteen months added a new peninsula of volcanic cinder to the west coast of Faial, partially burying the keeper's quarters and the ancillary buildings in ash and lava. The lighthouse tower itself survived, and the resulting landscape — a white tower rising from a field of grey and black volcanic tuff, the ocean blue on three sides — is otherworldly.

The buried keeper's house and the surviving lighthouse are now a UNESCO-recognised geotourism site with a visitor centre beneath the ash layer. The tower shows Fl(4) W 15s and is visible for 27 nautical miles.

## Lindau Lighthouse, Bavaria, Germany

Not all beautiful lighthouses stand on the open sea. The lighthouse at Lindau, on Lake Constance near the Austrian border, stands on a pier at the entrance to a Bavarian harbour, white and round with a red lantern, and is flanked by a medieval Lion Gate tower. The lake beyond is a sheet of deep blue, with the Alps rising beyond the southern shore. The Lindau light was built in 1856 and stands 33 metres above the lake, making it one of the most photographed landmarks in Bavaria. It is accessible on foot from the old town and is open for climbing during the summer season.
