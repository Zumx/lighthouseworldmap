It is tempting to think that a taller lighthouse must be a better one, throwing its beam ever farther across the sea. Yet height in a lighthouse is a careful compromise, balanced against wind, weight, cost, fog and the simple geometry of a round planet. Some towers soar to over a hundred metres; many of the most important lights in the world stand barely thirty. The question of how tall a lighthouse can be turns out to be less about engineering ambition and more about where the light needs to sit to do its job, and where the curve of the earth makes extra height pointless.

## The record holders

The tallest traditional lighthouse in the world is the Jeddah Light in Saudi Arabia, a steel-and-concrete tower rising about 133 metres. In the United States, the brick tower at Cape Hatteras in North Carolina is the tallest in the country at roughly 60 metres, a figure that already pushed nineteenth-century masonry to its limits. France's Île Vierge lighthouse off Brittany, at about 82 metres, is the tallest traditional stone lighthouse in Europe and required an interior staircase of nearly four hundred steps. These are exceptional structures, and their height was driven by specific local needs rather than a desire for records.

## Why height matters at all

The reason a lighthouse is raised at all is the curvature of the earth. The higher the light, the farther away the point at which it slips below the horizon for an observer at sea. This is the geographic range of the light, and it grows with the square root of the height: doubling the elevation does not double the range. A light 30 metres above the sea is visible to a ship's lookout at roughly twelve to thirteen nautical miles; pushing the tower far higher yields steadily diminishing returns. This square-root relationship is the single most important fact in lighthouse design, and it explains why towers rarely needed to be enormous.

## When the cliff does the work

Many of the world's most far-reaching lights are not tall at all. They are short towers perched on high headlands, where the cliff supplies the elevation and the tower needs only to lift the lantern clear of the rock. Lights along the rugged coasts of Scotland, Norway and California often sit a hundred metres or more above the waves while the tower itself is modest. The elevation that matters for range is the height of the light above the sea, not the height of the masonry. A tall tower on a low, flat coast and a short tower on a cliff can have exactly the same range.

## The engineering ceiling

For a tower built on low ground, height brings real structural problems. Wind load rises sharply with height, and a slender tower must resist the constant battering of coastal gales without cracking or swaying enough to disturb the optic. Masonry towers solved this by tapering, with massively thick walls at the base narrowing toward the lantern, but stone has a practical ceiling: beyond a certain height the base must be so broad and the walls so heavy that the structure becomes impractical and ruinously expensive. The arrival of cast iron and later reinforced concrete and steel pushed that ceiling higher, which is why the very tallest towers are modern rather than historic.

## The fog penalty

There is a further, counter-intuitive reason not to build too high. Fog and low cloud frequently sit in a layer above the sea surface, and a light placed too high can disappear into that layer while the sea below remains clear. A lower light may stay visible beneath the fog when a high one is swallowed. For this reason some important coastal lights were deliberately kept low, and a few stations even maintained a secondary low light for use in conditions when the main high light was obscured. Height, in other words, can be a liability as well as an asset.

## Counting the cost

Every extra metre of tower multiplies the difficulty of construction, the volume of material to be hauled to often remote sites, and the burden of maintenance for generations afterward. A keeper climbing several hundred steps several times a night, hauling oil and trimming wicks, paid a human price for height. Repainting, repointing and re-glazing a very tall tower in an exposed marine environment is a perpetual and costly task. Engineers therefore built only as tall as the geometry of range, terrain and fog genuinely demanded, and not a metre more.

## The practical answer

So how tall can a lighthouse be? Tall enough that modern steel and concrete can carry a light past 130 metres if a flat coast and a long required range truly demand it, as at Jeddah. But the more useful answer is that most lighthouses are exactly as tall as they need to be and no taller, because the curve of the earth, the threat of fog and the cost of every stone conspire to make excess height a waste. The art of the lighthouse engineer was never to build the tallest tower, but to put the light at precisely the right elevation for the coast it served.
