Japan's coastline runs for more than 29,000 kilometres around four main islands and thousands of smaller ones, threading through some of the most complex sea-passages in Asia. The Seto Inland Sea between Honshu, Shikoku, and Kyushu, the Tsugaru Strait separating Honshu from Hokkaido, and the open Pacific face of Shikoku and the Kii Peninsula are all demanding waters that have required reliable light aids since the Meiji government began modernising maritime infrastructure in the late 1860s. Many of Japan's finest lighthouses were designed or advised on by Richard Henry Brunton, a Scottish engineer engaged by the Meiji government from 1868 to 1876, who oversaw the construction of more than thirty towers — earning the title "Father of Japanese Lighthouses." Today the Japan Coast Guard operates and maintains the network. Eight lighthouses have been designated as "Luminous Lighthouses of Japan" for their historical significance and visitor value. ## 1. Inubosaki Lighthouse, Chiba Prefecture

Inubosaki Lighthouse stands on the easternmost point of the Chiba Peninsula, where the Kujukuri coast meets the strong tidal currents of the Pacific. Designed by Richard Henry Brunton and first lit in 1874, the tower is one of Japan's original Western-style brick lighthouses; it stands 31.3 metres tall with walls up to 1.8 metres thick at the base. The light character is a white flash every 15 seconds, with a range of approximately 19.5 nautical miles. The lighthouse is one of Japan's eight officially designated "Luminous Lighthouses" and is open to the public; the lantern gallery offers views north along the Kujukuri Beach, one of the longest stretches of straight coast in Japan. A small museum at the base exhibits lighthouse history and Brunton-era instruments. The lighthouse is active and maintained by the Japan Coast Guard.

## 2. Cape Shiriya Lighthouse, Aomori Prefecture

Cape Shiriya (Shiriyazaki) at the northeastern tip of Honshu marks one corner of the Tsugaru Strait, the channel separating the main island from Hokkaido. The lighthouse, a white cylindrical tower roughly 33 metres tall, was first established in 1876 and stands on a wave-cut platform of basalt. Its light flashes white every 15 seconds and is visible for about 18 nautical miles across the strait, where tidal currents are strong and winter conditions severe. Cape Shiriya is also known for a herd of semi-wild horses — the Kandachime, adapted to the bitter winter conditions of this exposed headland — that graze around the lighthouse grounds, giving the site an unusually evocative character. The lighthouse is designated as one of Japan's "Luminous Lighthouses" and is open for visits; the cape is accessible by road from Higashidori village.

## 3. Daiosaki Lighthouse, Mie Prefecture

Daiosaki Lighthouse stands on a rocky headland on the Shima Peninsula in Mie Prefecture, overlooking the Pacific approaches to Ise Bay. The white octagonal tower, about 23 metres tall, was first lit in 1927 and stands on cliff tops that drop sharply to the sea below. Its light character is a white flash every five seconds, visible for about 18 nautical miles — important guidance for vessels approaching the bay that leads to the Grand Shrine of Ise, the most sacred site in Shinto tradition. The lighthouse is active, and the headland is a popular tourist destination within the Ise-Shima National Park, which was also the location of the 2016 G7 summit. An observation terrace near the lighthouse offers panoramic views of the Pacific, and the area is noted for Ama — traditional female pearl and shellfish divers — who work the waters below.

## 4. Cape Muroto Lighthouse, Kochi Prefecture

Cape Muroto is the dramatic southern tip of Shikoku, an exposed headland jutting into the Kuroshio Current, the powerful warm ocean current that flows north from the Philippine Sea. The lighthouse, a white cylindrical tower about 22 metres tall, was first lit in 1899 on a cliff-top 155 metres above sea level, giving the light a reach of approximately 26 nautical miles. The light character is a white flash every four seconds. Cape Muroto is part of the Muroto UNESCO Global Geopark, known for its dramatic uplifted sea-floor geology, and the lighthouse shares the headland with the temple of Hotsumisakiji, the 24th station on the famous Shikoku 88-temple pilgrimage. The cape is accessible by road; the lighthouse and surrounding park are open to visitors, and the combination of geological, spiritual, and maritime heritage makes it one of Shikoku's most rewarding destinations.

## 5. Izu Oshima Lighthouse, Tokyo Metropolis

The lighthouse on Oshima, the largest island of the Izu Archipelago south of Tokyo Bay, stands on the northern cape of an island that is dominated by the active volcano of Mihara-yama. The white cylindrical tower, roughly 18 metres tall, first lit in 1869, is one of Japan's oldest Western-style lighthouse structures and was among the first commissioned by the Meiji government with Brunton's involvement. Its light — a white flash every five seconds — is visible for about 18 nautical miles across the approaches to Tokyo Bay and the channels of the Izu Islands. The tower is designated as one of Japan's "Luminous Lighthouses." Oshima is accessible by high-speed ferry from Tokyo or Atami; the lighthouse is within walking distance of the ferry port at Motomachi. The island also offers excellent hiking on and around the volcanic cone.

## 6. Kannonzaki Lighthouse, Kanagawa Prefecture

Kannonzaki on the western shore of the entrance to Tokyo Bay is home to Japan's first Western-style lighthouse, originally lit in 1869 under the direct supervision of Richard Henry Brunton. The current white octagonal tower — the third structure on this point after earthquake damage to the earlier buildings — stands 19.1 metres tall and continues to guide ships into and out of one of the world's busiest port complexes. Its white flash every 15 seconds is visible for about 15 nautical miles across the strait to the Chiba shore opposite. A lighthouse museum (Tôdai Kinenkan) at the base, operated by the Japan Coast Guard, displays Brunton-era equipment, Fresnel lenses, and the history of Japanese lighthouse development. The surrounding Kannonzaki Park, a protected woodland of coast pine, is open year-round.

## 7. Jogashima Lighthouse, Kanagawa Prefecture

Jogashima Island, connected to the Miura Peninsula by a road bridge, is the southernmost point of Kanagawa Prefecture and commands views across the Pacific toward the Izu Peninsula. The lighthouse, a white cylindrical tower first established in 1870 on the island's southwestern tip, stands about 12 metres tall on a basalt headland. Its light — a white flash every five seconds — is visible for approximately 17 nautical miles. The island and lighthouse were damaged in several major earthquakes, including the 1923 Great Kanto Earthquake, and the present tower reflects subsequent reconstruction. The coastal path around Jogashima passes dramatic sea-eroded rock formations and tide pools; the lighthouse is the focal point of the park at the island's western end, and the area around the tower is freely accessible on foot.

## 8. Cape Ashizuri Lighthouse, Kochi Prefecture

Cape Ashizuri is the southernmost point of Shikoku and one of the most dramatically positioned capes in Japan, where the Pacific beats against white cliffs of Tertiary-era limestone. The lighthouse, a white cylindrical tower about 18 metres tall on the cliff edge, was first lit in 1914 and produces a white flash every ten seconds, visible for about 28 nautical miles toward the open Pacific. The headland is the 38th station on the Shikoku pilgrimage; a white bronze statue of Kobo Daishi stands near the lighthouse. The cape is within the Ashizuri-Uwakai National Park, and the lighthouse grounds are accessible by road from the small resort town of Ashizuri Onsen below. The combination of pilgrimage culture, dramatic coastal geology, and powerful maritime lighthouse makes this one of the most atmospheric lighthouse settings in Japan.

## 9. Nojimazaki Lighthouse, Chiba Prefecture

Nojimazaki Lighthouse stands at the southernmost tip of the Boso Peninsula, on a rocky headland above the Pacific where Sagami Bay meets the open sea. The white octagonal tower, 29 metres tall, was first lit in 1869 as one of the original Meiji-era lighthouses commissioned under Richard Henry Brunton, and the current structure dates from 1925 following the 1923 Kanto Earthquake. It is designated as one of Japan's "Luminous Lighthouses" and is fully open to public visits including the lantern level. The light character is a white flash every fifteen seconds with a range of about 19.5 nautical miles. The lighthouse grounds contain a small museum with preserved keeper's accommodation and historical lighthouse equipment. The nearby rocky shore is noted for fishing and the scenic Nojimazaki Park makes this accessible from the Boso Peninsula railway terminus.

## 10. Tsurugisaki Lighthouse, Kanagawa Prefecture

Tsurugisaki on the eastern shore of the Miura Peninsula guards the western side of the entrance to Tokyo Bay, working in conjunction with Kannonzaki on the opposite shore. The white cylindrical tower, about 15 metres tall, was first established in 1877 and marks the shallow Uraga Channel through which all Tokyo Bay shipping passes. Its occulting white light is visible for about 12 nautical miles. The headland is part of a marine park noted for its intertidal rockpools and coral communities, which are unusually diverse for this latitude. The lighthouse itself is within a Japan Coast Guard compound and is not generally open to interior visits, but the surrounding park and the view across the busy shipping lanes of the Uraga Channel — freighters, container ships, and ferries passing in both directions — make the headland worth visiting.

## Planning Your Visit

Japan's lighthouses are managed by the Japan Coast Guard and a small number are officially designated as visitor attractions, particularly the eight "Luminous Lighthouses" which typically have museums, open lantern galleries, and accessible grounds. Spring (April to May) and autumn (October to November) offer the best combination of clear weather and comfortable temperatures for coastal travel. Many lighthouses are in national parks or at the end of peninsula roads that require private transport or infrequent local buses. The dedicated lighthouse tourist programme run by the Japan Lighthouse Society (Nihon Tôdai Kyôkai) is a useful resource in Japanese.
