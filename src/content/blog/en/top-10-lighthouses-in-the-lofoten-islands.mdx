The Lofoten and Vesteralen archipelagos sit above the Arctic Circle on Norway's far northern coast, where a wall of glaciated granite drops straight into the cold Norwegian Sea. For three months of summer the sun never sets, and for weeks of midwinter it never clears the horizon. Between those extremes lies the Lofotfisket, the great seasonal cod fishery that for a thousand years drew open boats across the treacherous Vestfjorden to the spawning grounds off Reine and Henningsvaer. The lights strung along these islands were built to bring those boats home through gales, snow squalls, and the long polar dark. They mark a coast of staggering beauty and genuine danger, including the Moskstraumen, the maelstrom that Edgar Allan Poe made famous. These are ten that reward the journey north.

## 1. Skomvaer Lighthouse, Rost

Skomvaer stands on a low, treeless islet at the very tip of the Rost archipelago, the outermost inhabited point of Lofoten, roughly twenty-five nautical miles out into open water. Commissioned in 1887, its cast-iron tower rises about 33 metres, making it one of the tallest in northern Norway, and its light once reached far across the approaches where the Norwegian Sea meets the Vestfjorden. The surrounding skerries host enormous seabird colonies, and the keeper families lived in profound isolation, supplied by boat across some of the most exposed water on the coast. Automated in the twentieth century, Skomvaer remains a powerful symbol of the reach of the old lighthouse service.

## 2. Glapen Lighthouse, near Sorvagen

At the western mouth of Lofoten, near Sorvagen and the fishing village of A, Glapen guards the approaches close to the Moskstraumen tidal current. The light helps vessels distinguish the safe channel from the maelstrom's pull as the tide races between Moskenesoya and Mosken island. Set against the abrupt peaks of the Lofoten Wall, the station sits where weather changes in minutes and where southwesterly gales drive straight in from the open sea. It is a working navigational mark on one of the most photographed stretches of the archipelago.

## 3. Tind Lighthouse, Moskenes

Tind is a small coastal light near the southwestern villages of Moskenes municipality, watching over the inshore route that local fishing boats follow beneath the great granite ridge. Lights of this kind, modest in height but precise in placement, formed the backbone of the Lofoten system: not towering offshore giants but carefully sited lantern stations that turned a lethal lee shore into a navigable coast. In winter the surrounding peaks catch the brief, low Arctic light while the lantern burns through the long blue twilight.

## 4. Skrova Lighthouse, Skrova

Skrova is a green island in the Vestfjorden between Svolvaer and the mainland, long famous as a fishing and whaling station. Its lighthouse marks the deep-water approaches that carry traffic toward Svolvaer and Kabelvag, the historic capitals of the Lofoten fishery. Skrova sees some of the mildest weather in the archipelago thanks to the Gulf Stream's reach, and its light has guided generations of cod boats across the fjord during the late-winter Lofotfisket, when the sea fills with vessels chasing the spawning skrei.

## 5. Brettesnes Lighthouse, Austvagoya

On the northern side of Austvagoya, Brettesnes marks the narrow sounds among the islets east of Svolvaer. This was once a busy fishing settlement, and the light served the dense small-boat traffic threading the skerries. The character of a light like this is calibrated to be recognisable against a backdrop of scattered shore lights and snow, so that a skipper running for shelter in a squall can identify it instantly. The setting, beneath sharp peaks and across sheltered sounds, is classic inner-Lofoten.

## 6. Andenes Lighthouse, Vesteralen

At the northern tip of Andoya in Vesteralen stands Andenes, a tall red cast-iron tower of about 40 metres, one of the most striking lights in northern Norway. First lit in 1859, it marks the approach to the fishing town of Andenes and the edge of the continental shelf, where deep water close to shore makes Andenes a celebrated base for whale-watching. The exposed position takes the full force of Arctic storms, and the slim red column against the sea is visible for many nautical miles in clear weather. It remains an icon of the Vesteralen coast.

## 7. Litloy Lighthouse, Vesteralen

Litloy sits on a tiny island off Vesteralen and is now one of Norway's most coveted lighthouse retreats, operated as a small guesthouse where visitors stay in the keeper's quarters surrounded by open sea. Established in the late nineteenth century, the station was automated and later given a new life in hospitality, letting travellers experience the silence, the seabirds, and the midnight sun from the same rooms the keepers knew. It is a vivid example of how decommissioned Arctic lights have been preserved through reuse.

## 8. Hovsund Lighthouse, Gimsoya

Hovsund marks the waters off Gimsoya, the flatter island between Austvagoya and Vestvagoya, where the coast opens toward the Vestfjorden and the long beaches catch the northern light. The station guided fishing and coastal traffic along this central stretch of Lofoten. Gimsoya is one of the best places in the islands to watch the aurora in winter and the midnight sun in summer, and the light stands as a quiet sentinel over a deceptively gentle-looking shore that still demands respect in heavy weather.

## 9. Svolvaer Approach Lights, Austvagoya

Svolvaer, the largest town in Lofoten, is reached through a cluster of harbour and approach lights guiding the Hurtigruten coastal steamers and the fishing fleet past the jagged Svolvaergeita peaks into a tight, rock-girt harbour. These are working lights rather than monumental towers, but together they manage some of the busiest navigation in the archipelago, where ferries, fishing boats, and cruise traffic converge beneath the mountains. In the polar night they burn against a backdrop of snow and town lights reflected on black water.

## 10. Kabelvag and Storvagan Lights, Austvagoya

Just west of Svolvaer lies Kabelvag, the oldest fishing settlement in Lofoten, whose sheltered bay and Storvagan area have been a cod-fishery hub since the Viking age. The lights serving this approach mark the gateway to a coast steeped in the history of stockfish, the air-dried cod that made the islands rich and that still hangs on wooden racks every spring. The setting, with the great Lofoten Cathedral nearby and the peaks rising behind, ties the navigational mark to a thousand years of Arctic fishing life.

## Visiting the Lights

The practical season runs from late May through August, when the midnight sun keeps the coast lit around the clock and the ferries, the E10 road, and the Hurtigruten all run reliably. Reach Lofoten by air to Svolvaer or Leknes, by the long scenic drive across the bridges of the E10, or by coastal steamer. Rost and the offshore islets require ferry or boat crossings that depend entirely on the weather, so build slack into any itinerary. Winter visits, from February to April, coincide with the Lofotfisket and the aurora, but daylight is brief and storms close roads and harbours without warning. Dress for wind and sudden cold even in July, carry tide information near the Moskstraumen, and treat every exposed headland with the caution the old keepers did.
