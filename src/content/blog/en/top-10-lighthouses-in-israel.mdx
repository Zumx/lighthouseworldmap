Israel's Mediterranean coastline stretches roughly 190 kilometres from the border with Lebanon near Rosh HaNikra in the north to the Gaza Strip in the south. It is a coast without natural deep harbours — the sandstone ridges that run parallel to the shore offered little shelter, and the anchorages at Acre, Jaffa, Haifa, and Caesarea were all precarious roadsteads exposed to winter westerlies. Marking this coast has been a navigational necessity since antiquity; the port of Jaffa appears in Egyptian records more than three thousand years ago, and the Romans built a breakwater harbour at Caesarea Maritima expressly to create shelter on a harbourless coast. The lighthouses that exist today are a mixture of late Ottoman structures, British Mandate constructions, and modern Israeli installations operated by the Israel Ports Authority and the Israel Navy. They are concentrated around the main commercial ports of Haifa and Ashdod, with historic towers surviving at Jaffa and at the Carmelite monastery of Stella Maris.

## 1. Jaffa Lighthouse, Jaffa

The Jaffa Lighthouse is one of the most recognisable landmarks of Tel Aviv-Yafo, its white cylindrical tower rising from the ancient hilltop of Old Jaffa and visible across the city's beachfront. The tower was built in 1865 under Ottoman administration, replacing earlier arrangements for marking this ancient port, and stands approximately 17 metres tall. The original light used a fixed dioptric lens. Today the lighthouse is active and maintained by the Israel Ports Authority, showing a flashing white light visible around 14 nautical miles. The Old Jaffa hill is a tourist destination in its own right, packed with galleries, restaurants, and archaeological remains, and the lighthouse is freely accessible as part of a walk through the ancient port quarter.

## 2. Stella Maris Lighthouse, Haifa

The Stella Maris Lighthouse stands on the northwestern slope of Mount Carmel, adjacent to the Carmelite Monastery of Our Lady of Mount Carmel — the spiritual home of the Carmelite religious order. The tower is integrated into the monastery complex, a white octagonal structure of approximately 12 metres, and marks the approach to Haifa Bay and the entrance to Israel's principal commercial port. The light was established during the British Mandate period and remains active. The monastery church, built over what tradition identifies as the cave of the Prophet Elijah, is open to visitors and draws pilgrims from around the world; the lighthouse can be viewed from the monastery grounds, which offer panoramic views across Haifa Bay to Acre and the hills of the Galilee.

## 3. Akko Lighthouse, Acre

Acre (Akko) is one of the world's continuously inhabited cities, a Crusader stronghold, Ottoman provincial capital, and now a UNESCO World Heritage Site. The lighthouse at Akko stands on the seawall of the old city, a white tower of Ottoman construction that marked the harbour entrance through the centuries when Acre was the chief port of the Levantine coast. The tower is approximately 15 metres tall and retains its historic character as part of the old port quarter, now largely preserved as a museum complex. The light itself is no longer a primary aid to navigation — the modern port of Haifa has superseded Acre's commercial role — but the structure is actively maintained and lit, and it forms an integral part of the historic seascape of one of Israel's most compelling cities.

## 4. Ashdod Lighthouse, Ashdod

Ashdod is Israel's largest port by cargo volume, a modern deep-water facility handling container traffic, grain, bulk materials, and petroleum products. The lighthouse at Ashdod marks the northern breakwater of the port entrance, a white cylindrical concrete tower of about 18 metres showing a flashing white light with a range of approximately 14 nautical miles. The tower is of relatively recent construction, built alongside the port's post-independence development, and is strictly functional in character. The port is a secure facility, but the lighthouse can be seen from the breakwater area accessible to the public on the northern edge of the port complex, and from the beach south of the marina.

## 5. Haifa North Breakwater Light, Haifa

The main approach to Haifa Port is guided by lights on both breakwater heads, and the north breakwater light is the primary fixed aid marking the harbour entrance for large commercial vessels. Haifa handles a substantial share of Israel's import trade and is also the departure point for passenger ferries to Cyprus and Greece. The breakwater light is a modern steel structure — less architecturally notable than the historic Stella Maris tower above — but operationally critical to the constant flow of shipping through this busy port. It can be seen from the western approach roads along the harbour front.

## 6. Caesarea Lighthouse, Caesarea

A modern lighthouse at Caesarea marks the approach to the ancient harbour of Sebastos, built by Herod the Great around 22–10 BCE — one of the greatest engineering achievements of the ancient world, constructed with hydraulic concrete in the open sea. While no ancient lighthouse survives, the Romans certainly maintained a pharos here; archaeological excavations have identified possible lighthouse foundations on the breakwater. The present lighthouse is a modest modern structure erected near the ruins of the Crusader-era port, maintaining the tradition of marking this historically critical anchorage. The Caesarea National Park and marina area are widely visited; the lighthouse is visible from the marina promenade.

## 7. Nahariya Pier Light, Nahariya

Nahariya, the northernmost coastal city in Israel proper, has a small harbour serving fishing and recreational vessels. A pier head light marks the entrance to the marina, a compact but active structure. Nahariya's coast is close to the Lebanese border and the dramatic chalk cliffs of Rosh HaNikra, where sea grottos have been carved by millennia of wave action. The pier light serves the local maritime community and the pleasure craft from the marina that explore this northern stretch of coast. The town's beach and marina are pleasant areas to explore, and the pier light is easily visible from the seafront.

## 8. Ashkelon Marina Light, Ashkelon

Ashkelon on the southern Mediterranean coast has one of Israel's largest marinas, and the entrance is marked by a pier head light. The ancient city of Ashkelon — one of the five Philistine cities of the Biblical period — had its own harbour, and continuity of maritime use at this location spans more than three thousand years. The modern marina serves pleasure craft and fishing vessels, and the light that marks its entrance, while utilitarian, stands on ground heavy with historical significance. The national park preserving the ancient Canaanite and Philistine city walls lies nearby.

## 9. Haifa Carmel Beacon, Haifa Bay

Within Haifa Bay, a series of minor lights and beacons mark the shallow areas and the passage reserved for the large tankers that deliver oil to the Haifa refineries. One of the more prominent of these is a red sector beacon marking the shoals on the southern side of the bay approach, visible from the extensive beach at Kiryat Yam. These functional aids form the skeleton of Haifa's complex port approach system, coordinated with the Haifa Port Authority's vessel traffic service.

## 10. Tel Aviv Marina Light, Tel Aviv

The Tel Aviv Marina at the northern end of the city's beachfront has a small entrance light marking the breakwater of this large pleasure-craft facility. The marina is surrounded by high-rise hotels and the characteristic towers of central Tel Aviv, giving the lighthouse environs a thoroughly urban setting unlike any other on the Israeli coast. The marina is a hub of recreational sailing and powerboating, and the breakwater walk offers excellent views along the Tel Aviv beachfront toward Jaffa and the old lighthouse on the hill above.

## Visiting Israel's Lighthouses

Israel's most accessible lighthouse is undoubtedly the Jaffa Lighthouse, freely visible from the tourist-focused hilltop of Old Jaffa, which can be reached by bus or taxi from central Tel Aviv. The Stella Maris Lighthouse in Haifa is best visited as part of a day that includes the monastery and the Baha'i Terraced Gardens nearby. Akko is a short train ride from Haifa and rewards a full day's exploration. The Ashdod and Haifa harbour lighthouses are operational port facilities not open to the public. Most of the coast is accessible year-round; spring and autumn are the most pleasant seasons, avoiding the summer heat and the winter rains.
