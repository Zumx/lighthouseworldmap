## What Remote Means in Lighthouse Terms

Distance from shore is not quite the same thing as remoteness in lighthouse terms. A tower 20 kilometres offshore in the North Sea may be supplied by helicopter in forty minutes, have satellite communications, and receive maintenance visits several times a year. A tower 3 kilometres from land on a subantarctic island may be accessible only during a narrow seasonal window, in sea conditions that can make that short crossing impassable for weeks. The remotest lighthouses are defined not by a simple measurement of distance but by the combination of inaccessibility, severity of environment, and the consequent difficulty of maintaining them.

By this measure, the candidates are spread across the southern hemisphere, the far north, the exposed eastern seaboards, and the island groups of the open Pacific. They were built in the first place because shipping routes passed through territory where navigation hazards existed and where no other form of marking was practicable. Many were staffed by keepers who accepted assignments that amounted, for the duration, to complete separation from the rest of the world.

## Faro Les Eclaireurs, Tierra del Fuego

The Faro Les Eclaireurs stands on a small rocky island in the Beagle Channel, east of Ushuaia in Argentine Tierra del Fuego, and is often described as the southernmost lighthouse in the world — though the claim is contested by the Argentine Navy's lighthouse on Isla de los Estados, further east and genuinely more isolated. Les Eclaireurs, lit in 1920, is a red-and-white banded masonry tower 11 metres tall, flashing a white light every five seconds. Its location, on the threshold of the Drake Passage where the Atlantic and Pacific meet in famously violent conditions, gives it a dramatic visual setting. Almost every cruise ship transiting the Beagle Channel passes within a few hundred metres, and the tower has become the region's most photographed landmark.

The Faro San Juan de Salvamento on Isla de los Estados, by contrast, is genuinely isolated. The island has no permanent inhabitants, is accessible only by sea or aircraft, and has no facilities beyond the lighthouse station itself. The original tower, built in 1884 and abandoned in 1902 when a more powerful automated light was installed, was the subject of Jules Verne's 1905 novel 'The Lighthouse at the End of the World.' It has been restored as a heritage structure, and the Argentine Navy maintains the active light. The Drake Passage, which passes to the south, is one of the most dangerous stretches of ocean in the world.

## Muckle Flugga, Shetland

At the northern tip of Britain, the Muckle Flugga lighthouse sits on a rock stack at 60 degrees 51 minutes north, making it the northernmost lighthouse in the United Kingdom. The rock barely qualifies as land — a narrow ridge of gneiss projecting from the sea a kilometre and a half north of Unst, which is itself the northernmost inhabited island in Britain. The tower was built by David and Thomas Stevenson in 1854 during the Crimean War, using prefabricated components landed on the rock in summer conditions; the war had created an urgent need for a light on the route from the east coast naval bases to the North Sea.

The tower stands 20 metres tall on the rock, which itself rises to 46 metres above sea level, giving an elevation that makes the light visible for 22 nautical miles. The character is a flashing white light every 20 seconds. The station was automated in 1995, and the tower is now served by helicopter from Lerwick. The keepers who lived there described it as among the most exposed postings in the Northern Lighthouse Board's service: the wind rarely dropped, the sea between the rock and Unst was rarely calm enough for a comfortable boat crossing, and the darkness of the winter months was relieved only by the aurora.

## Stannard Rock, Lake Superior

The distinction of being the most remote lighthouse in the United States is generally given to Stannard Rock Light, which stands on a shoal in Lake Superior, 40 kilometres from the nearest land — Huron Island to the south — and 65 kilometres from the port of Marquette, Michigan. It was built between 1877 and 1882 by the US Army Corps of Engineers, a cast-iron caisson filled with concrete and faced with granite ashlar. The tower rises 34 metres above the lake surface. In a body of water that can generate waves of four metres or more during autumn gales, the exposed mid-lake position subjected the station to conditions comparable to offshore Atlantic sites.

The keepers assigned to Stannard Rock were rotated on a schedule that provided for regular relief in season, but the lake itself closed to navigation from December to April, and the station was provisioned for winter isolation. Summer storms could trap keepers for extended periods — the lake can change state in a matter of hours. The light was automated in 1962, and the US Coast Guard now services it by boat or helicopter. The character is a red flash every ten seconds, visible for 14 nautical miles through a fourth-order Fresnel lens that remains in the tower.

## Eddystone and the Exposed Channel Stations

The exposed Channel stations — Eddystone, Wolf Rock, Bishop Rock — are remote in the operational sense that matters most to lighthouse maintainers: they can only be approached safely in calm conditions, and the Channel is calm only intermittently. Wolf Rock, 13 kilometres off Land's End on a group of submerged granite boulders, was particularly hard to staff. The tower, built by James Walker and lit in 1870, stands 42 metres tall and is visible for 16 nautical miles with a white flash every fifteen seconds. The difficulty of building on a rock that was never fully exposed above water made it one of the more difficult offshore construction projects of the Victorian era. It was automated in 1988.

Bishop Rock, at the western tip of the Scilly Isles, marks the outermost edge of British territorial waters in the west and was historically the formal start and finish point of transatlantic speed records. The tower, completed in its present form in 1887 by James Douglas, is 49 metres tall and was for much of the twentieth century the most powerful lighthouse in Britain, its first-order Fresnel lens producing a beam visible for 24 nautical miles with a character of two white flashes every fifteen seconds.

## Sule Skerry, North Atlantic

Twenty-four kilometres west of the Orkney coast, Sule Skerry is a low, flat rock barely two metres above sea level at its highest point. It was described by the Northern Lighthouse Board's surveyors in the early nineteenth century as the most dangerous land in the British Isles precisely because of its invisibility: unlike a prominent headland, it lay in the path of vessels coming from the south-west without any topographic feature to suggest its presence. The lighthouse, built in 1895 to designs by David Alan Stevenson, is 30 metres tall and rises from a rock so flat that the tower base is barely above the sea in heavy weather. Automated in 1982, it exhibits a white flash every five seconds with a range of 20 nautical miles.

The rock is also a gannet colony — one of the more important in the North Atlantic — and keepers stationed there coexisted with thousands of nesting seabirds. The maintenance crews who service it by helicopter today land on a small helipad and carry out their work among birds that, after decades without predators, have little fear of human visitors.

## Finding the Remote Lights

The full geographical spread of the world's remote lighthouses covers the sub-Antarctic islands of the South Atlantic, the Aleutian chain in Alaska, the exposed western coasts of Ireland and Scotland, the offshore reefs of the South China Sea, and the storm-battered capes of the southern Pacific. Many were built by imperial and colonial authorities at a period when the economics of maritime trade justified extraordinary construction costs in the most hostile locations.
