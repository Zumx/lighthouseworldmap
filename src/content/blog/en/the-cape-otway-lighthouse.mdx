On a windswept headland where the Great Ocean Road meets the cold water of Bass Strait, a squat stone tower has stood since 1848. The Cape Otway Lighthouse is the oldest surviving lighthouse on mainland Australia, and for more than a century it was the most important landfall light in the southern hemisphere. For the migrant ships that had run their easting down across the Southern Ocean from Britain, threading the narrow gap between the Victorian coast and King Island was the most dangerous moment of a three-month voyage, and Cape Otway was the light they strained to see.

## The Eye of the Needle

The approach to Melbourne and the ports of the Victorian coast led through Bass Strait, the shallow and storm-prone channel separating the Australian mainland from Tasmania. Ships running before the westerly winds of the Roaring Forties aimed to pass between Cape Otway on the mainland and King Island to the south, a gap of about ninety kilometres. It sounds wide, but after months at sea with no reliable means of fixing longitude, navigators often could not be certain of their position to within that distance.

The consequences of error were catastrophic. King Island became one of the deadliest shipwreck sites in Australian history, its reefs claiming vessels whose crews believed they were safely in open water. The Cataraqui, wrecked on King Island in 1845 with the loss of around four hundred lives, remains Australia's worst civilian maritime disaster. It was disasters like this that drove the colonial authorities to build a light at Cape Otway, to give the migrant ships a positive fix as they entered the strait.

## Building on the Cape

Cape Otway was a difficult place to build. The headland is remote, backed by the dense temperate rainforest of the Otway Ranges, and in the 1840s there was no road to it through the forest. The sandstone for the tower was quarried locally, and the construction crew worked in conditions of considerable isolation. The light was first exhibited in 1848, a solid tapering tower of pale stone around twenty metres tall, built to last on its exposed clifftop.

The lighthouse was paired with a substantial settlement: keepers' quarters, store rooms, and eventually a telegraph station that connected the cape to Tasmania by the first submarine cable laid in Australian waters. The keepers and their families formed a small isolated community on the headland, dependent on supply by sea and later by the rough track through the forest. The buildings of this settlement survive today and form the heart of the visitor experience.

## The Light and the Coast It Guarded

Cape Otway showed a powerful fixed and later flashing light from a first-order optic, designed to be seen far out in the strait by ships still uncertain of their landfall. The stretch of coast it guarded, running west towards Warrnambool and Port Fairy, became known as the Shipwreck Coast for the sheer number of vessels lost along it. The most famous wreck, the Loch Ard, went down near Port Campbell in 1878 with only two survivors, and the limestone stacks and gorges of that coast — the Twelve Apostles, Loch Ard Gorge, London Bridge — are now the scenic heart of the Great Ocean Road.

The light operated continuously from 1848 until it was decommissioned as an active aid to navigation in 1994, when a smaller automated beacon took over the task. After nearly a century and a half, the original tower fell dark, but by then it had guided generations of migrants safely into their new country and its place in Australian history was secure.

## A Continent's Lights

Cape Otway is the senior member of a great family of Australian lighthouses, many of them built in the same wave of colonial expansion. On Kangaroo Island in South Australia, the Cape Willoughby Lighthouse, the Cape du Couedic Lighthouse, and the Cape Borda Lighthouse mark the approaches to the Backstairs Passage and the Investigator Strait. The Cape Jervis Lighthouse on the mainland opposite completes the marking of that approach. These South Australian lights guard the entrance to the Gulf St Vincent and the port of Adelaide.

Along the Victorian coast itself the lights cluster thickly. The Split Point Lighthouse at Aireys Inlet, with its distinctive red cap, and the Cape Nelson Lighthouse near Portland guard the western approaches, while the Queenscliff Black Lighthouse and its companion mark the entrance to Port Phillip Bay and the notorious tidal race known as the Rip. The Griffiths Island Lighthouse at Port Fairy marks one of the small but important fishing harbours of the western coast.

## Western and Tasmanian Lights

Far to the west, on the corner of the continent, the Cape Leeuwin Lighthouse stands where the Indian and Southern Oceans meet, the tallest lighthouse on the Australian mainland and the landfall for ships rounding the south-west cape. The Cape Naturaliste Lighthouse to its north marks Geographe Bay, and around Perth the Wadjemup Lighthouse on Rottnest Island, the North Mole Lighthouse and the South Mole Lighthouse at Fremantle guard the western capital's approaches.

Across Bass Strait in Tasmania, the Mersey Bluff Lighthouse at Devonport and the Bonnet Island Lighthouse at the entrance to Macquarie Harbour mark the northern and western coasts. The Entrance Island Lighthouse and the Round Hill Lighthouse near Burnie complete the marking of the Tasmanian shore of the strait that Cape Otway helped to make navigable. Together these lights tell the story of how a continent at the far end of the world was bound into the network of global navigation.

## Visiting Cape Otway

Today Cape Otway is one of the most visited lighthouses in Australia, reached by a short road off the Great Ocean Road that winds through the Otway forest. Visitors can climb the original tower, explore the keepers' quarters and the telegraph station, and walk the headland where the keepers once watched for ships. The site interprets its own history thoroughly, from the migrant voyages to the koalas that now famously inhabit the manna gums along the access road.

The setting remains as wild as it ever was. The wind off Bass Strait is constant, the sea below the cliffs is rarely calm, and on a clear day the view stretches across the strait towards the distant low line of King Island, the killer of the Cataraqui. Standing at the foot of the old tower, it is easy to understand why this light mattered so much, and why its dark lantern still draws people to the edge of the continent.
