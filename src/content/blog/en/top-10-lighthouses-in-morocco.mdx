Morocco's Atlantic and Mediterranean coasts have been among the most consequential maritime frontiers in history. The Strait of Gibraltar, at the country's northern tip, is one of the busiest sea lanes on earth, funnelling traffic between the Atlantic Ocean and the Mediterranean Sea through a passage barely 14 kilometres wide. Further south, the Atlantic coast runs more than 3,000 kilometres to the Mauritanian border, past the ancient trading ports of Casablanca, Essaouira, and Agadir, and through waters where the Canary Current pushes cold, fog-prone upwellings against the shore. France and Spain competed for lighthouse authority here through the nineteenth and twentieth centuries, constructing towers that often reflect the colonial engineering standards of their respective metropolitan lighthouse services. The French-built towers tend to be robust masonry cylinders following the practices of the Phares et Balises administration; the Spanish-built northern lights reflect the Comisión de Faros traditions of Catalonia and the Levante. After independence in 1956 the Moroccan Direction des Ports took over maintenance of this varied inheritance.

## 1. Cap Spartel Lighthouse, near Tangier

Cap Spartel stands at the northwestern corner of the African continent, where the Atlantic meets the Mediterranean and the cliffs of the Maghreb rise above the blue-green water. The lighthouse here — a white octagonal masonry tower 30 metres tall, completed in 1864 — was the product of an unusual international agreement: it was built and maintained by a consortium of European powers and the United States, recognising the importance of the cape to all nations trading through the Strait. The original first-order Fresnel lens by Henry-Lepaute illuminated the lantern room until electrification in the twentieth century. The light shows a group-flashing white character with a range of 26 nautical miles, one of the strongest on the Moroccan coast. The keeper's complex and surrounding eucalyptus woods are a popular excursion from Tangier. The ruins of the Roman settlement of Cotta lie nearby on the cliff.

## 2. El Hank Lighthouse, Casablanca

El Hank (also written El-Hank) sits on the western headland of Casablanca's bay, a white cylindrical tower approximately 30 metres tall built in 1916 under French colonial authority to serve the rapidly expanding port of Casablanca. Its fourth-order Fresnel optic produces a flashing white light visible for about 20 nautical miles. The lighthouse stands within the grounds of an active military installation, which has historically restricted public access, but the tower is visible from the sea and from the Corniche road to the north. It marks the northern approach to Casablanca's commercial harbour, one of the busiest on the African Atlantic coast, and provides a critical sector light distinguishing the port entry from the rocky shoals of the Roches Noires point to the northwest. The structure has been repainted and its lantern room modernised, but the masonry tower dates from the French Protectorate period.

## 3. Cap Beddouza Lighthouse, near Safi

Cap Beddouza (formerly Cap Cantin) marks an important headland some 40 kilometres north of Safi, where the Atlantic coast changes direction and the offshore fishing grounds of the sardine fleet begin. The white masonry tower here, built under French supervision in the 1920s, stands approximately 30 metres above a clifftop already well elevated above the sea, giving a combined elevation of over 60 metres. The light shows a group-flashing white character with a range of about 24 nautical miles. The cape is a significant point of maritime reference for vessels transiting between the Strait of Gibraltar and the southern Moroccan ports, and the lighthouse is staffed. The surrounding area is largely unspoiled and the lighthouse can be approached from the coastal track running south from the Oualidia lagoon.

## 4. Rabat Lighthouse (Oudayas), Rabat

The lighthouse at the Kasbah of the Oudayas in Rabat occupies a site of exceptional historical layering: a white square minaret-form tower of around 18 metres, converted to lighthouse use in the nineteenth century, standing at the mouth of the Bou Regreg river where Rabat's medina faces the city of Salé across the water. The Bou Regreg estuary has been a navigational challenge for centuries — the bar at the river mouth is shallow and shifts with the seasons, and the corsairs of the Salé Republic used the difficult approach as natural protection for their harbour. The existing lighthouse is a simple lantern installation on a historic structure, automated and active, providing a reference mark for vessels approaching the capital's river mouth. The Kasbah itself is one of Morocco's finest medieval fortifications and the lighthouse visit is inseparable from the broader heritage of the site.

## 5. Cap Sim Lighthouse, near Essaouira

The headland of Cap Sim, about 25 kilometres south of Essaouira, carries a modest white lighthouse built in the 1930s. The tower stands on a low cape where the coastal plain gives way to sandy cliffs, and its light — flashing white, visible for around 14 nautical miles — marks the approach to the ancient port of Essaouira, known to the Portuguese as Mogador. The cape is a traditional fishing ground and the waters offshore are part of the productive Canary upwelling system that makes this coast one of the world's richest for pelagic fish. The lighthouse is accessible from the coastal track south of Essaouira and the surrounding area of argan woodland and seasonal wetland makes the walk rewarding beyond the tower itself. The wind here is consistent and strong, which is why Essaouira developed as a major kitesurfing destination.

## 6. Cap Malabata Lighthouse, near Tangier

Across the Strait of Gibraltar from Cap Spartel on the Atlantic side, Cap Malabata guards the eastern side of Tangier Bay, where the strait narrows toward the Spanish coast. The lighthouse here, a white square tower built in the early twentieth century, displays a flashing white light with a red sector covering the shoals off Punta Cires to the east, with a range of about 14 nautical miles. Cap Malabata is a popular local excursion from Tangier and the cape affords one of the clearest views of the Spanish coastline available from Morocco — on a clear day Tarifa and the Sierra de la Plata are plainly visible, separated by a strip of intensely blue water through which hundreds of vessels pass daily.

## 7. Cap Spartel to Ceuta Coast Lights

Between Cap Spartel and the Spanish enclave of Ceuta, the Moroccan coast of the Strait of Gibraltar carries several secondary lights marking the rocky capes and reefs of this treacherous narrows. The most significant is the light at Ras Leona (Cape Leona), a small tower on a cape approximately halfway along the strait's Moroccan shore, showing a flashing white character. This stretch of coast sees more vessel traffic per square kilometre than almost anywhere in the world, and the interaction between Atlantic swell and Mediterranean sea state creates unpredictable wave conditions. The Moroccan lighthouse service maintains these minor lights as part of the same international maritime corridor management system that governs the Vessel Traffic Service operating from Tarifa.

## 8. Agadir Harbour Lighthouse, Agadir

The commercial port of Agadir, rebuilt entirely after the devastating 1960 earthquake that killed over 15,000 people, has a pier-head lighthouse at the end of the main breakwater. The current tower is a post-earthquake construction dating from the early 1960s, utilitarian in design, but active and important to the sardine fleet and cargo trade that sustains the port. The Agadir light displays a flashing green character to distinguish the harbour entrance from the white sector lights offshore, with a range of about 10 nautical miles. The old kasbah above the modern city, also destroyed in 1960, is now a viewing point from which the harbour layout and lighthouse position can be appreciated in their full geographical context.

## 9. Cap Ghir Lighthouse, near Agadir

Cap Ghir is the most significant turning point on the Moroccan Atlantic coast south of Cap Beddouza, where the coastline swings from its north-south alignment to a more southwesterly direction. The lighthouse here, a white masonry tower of the French colonial period, stands on a clifftop above the cape and shows a flashing white light visible for about 18 nautical miles. The cape marks the beginning of the Sous valley coastal zone and the landscape becomes noticeably drier south of this point, the argan and cedar forests giving way to desert scrub as the influence of the Sahara increases. The lighthouse is maintained by the Moroccan port authority and staffed; the nearest town is Taghazout, which has grown significantly as a surf tourism destination.

## 10. Sidi Boulfdail Lighthouse, near Essaouira

The secondary light at Sidi Boulfdail, north of Essaouira on the stretch of coast between Cap Sim and Cap Beddouza, marks a patch of shoal water close inshore that has caused problems for the local fishing fleet. The tower is a simple rendered masonry structure dating from the mid-twentieth century, automated and solar-powered. Though lacking the grandeur of the major headland towers, it typifies the working infrastructure of the Moroccan coast: inexpensive, functional lights maintained to serve the hundreds of small fishing vessels working these rich offshore grounds year-round. The coast road north of Essaouira passes close by and the combination of Atlantic surf, argan forest, and modest lighthouse makes for an authentic view of the working maritime landscape.

## Visiting Morocco's Lighthouses

Cap Spartel is the most accessible major lighthouse, just 15 kilometres from central Tangier by taxi or rental car, and the surrounding Diplomatic Forest makes it a pleasant half-day excursion. El Hank in Casablanca is visible from the Corniche but military restrictions apply to close approach. Cap Beddouza and Cap Sim require a vehicle and some tolerance for rough tracks; both are best in spring or autumn when temperatures are moderate. The Rabat Kasbah lighthouse is free to walk past at any time. Morocco's lighthouse service does not operate a general visitor programme, so most external access is informal.
