The Normandy coast runs from the white chalk cliffs of the Pays de Caux to the granite headlands of the Cotentin Peninsula, and across that span lie some of the most distinctive lighthouses in France. This is a coast of strong tides, of the great estuary of the Seine, and of the landing beaches of June 1944, and its lights reflect every part of that history. Some are tall granite towers built to mark deadly capes; others are modest harbour lights at the mouths of fishing ports. Together they make Normandy one of the most rewarding stretches of the French coast for anyone drawn to maritime navigation.

## Gatteville, the Granite Giant

At the northeastern tip of the Cotentin, the lighthouse of Gatteville-Phare rises 75 metres above the Pointe de Barfleur, one of the tallest lighthouses in Europe and in the world. Built between 1829 and 1834 of local granite, its tower contains 365 steps and 52 windows, a deliberate echo of the days and weeks of the year. It marks the dangerous Raz de Barfleur, a stretch of fierce tidal races and reefs that has wrecked countless ships, including vessels of the Norman fleet across the centuries. The climb to the gallery is open to visitors and rewards them with a vast panorama over the Channel.

## La Hague at the Cotentin's Western Tip

At the opposite, western corner of the Cotentin stands the Goury lighthouse at Cap de la Hague, marking the Raz Blanchard, one of the strongest tidal currents in Europe. The granite tower, built offshore on a reef and reached by a causeway at low water, guards waters where the tide can run at nearly ten knots. The nearby lifeboat station, with its distinctive circular slipway, has launched into some of the most dangerous seas in France. The setting, all heath and granite and racing water, is among the wildest in Normandy.

## Antifer above the Chalk Cliffs

West of Étretat, the Cap d'Antifer lighthouse stands atop the chalk cliffs that inspired Monet and Maupassant. The present tower, rebuilt after wartime destruction, marks the approach to the great oil terminal of Antifer and the port of Le Havre beyond. From the clifftop the famous arches and needle of Étretat are visible to the south, and the contrast between the brilliant white chalk and the green of the clifftop fields is unmistakably Norman.

## La Hève above Le Havre

The twin lighthouse station of Cap de la Hève, on the cliffs north of Le Havre, has marked the entrance to the Seine estuary since the 18th century, when two coal-fired towers stood here. The present single tower guides shipping into one of the busiest ports in France, the gateway for transatlantic traffic that once made Le Havre the principal French port for the great ocean liners. The cliffs here are crumbling chalk, constantly retreating, so that the lighthouse has been rebuilt further inland more than once.

## Honfleur and the Estuary Mouth

Across the Seine estuary, the small lights that mark the channel into Honfleur guide vessels past the great tidal flats where the river meets the sea. The picturesque port, beloved of the Impressionist painters, retains its old harbour and the narrow channel that demands careful navigation against the powerful tides of the estuary. The lights here are modest compared with Gatteville, but their setting among the slate-fronted houses of the Vieux Bassin is unmatched.

## Ouistreham at the Caen Canal

The lighthouse of Ouistreham, at the mouth of the Caen Canal, is a tall, slender tower of brick and stone, open to visitors who climb its 171 steps for a view over the landing beaches. Ouistreham marks the entrance to the canal that leads inland to Caen and the eastern end of the D-Day beaches, beside Sword Beach where British and French commandos came ashore in June 1944. The light remains an active aid for the ferry traffic to Portsmouth and the leisure craft that crowd the marina.

## Saint-Vaast-la-Hougue and the Vauban Forts

On the eastern shore of the Cotentin, the harbour of Saint-Vaast-la-Hougue is guarded by lights and by the great stone towers built by Vauban after the naval battle of 1692. The lighthouse on the jetty marks the entrance to the harbour, sheltered by the island of Tatihou with its own fortifications. The oyster beds of Saint-Vaast are famous throughout France, and the lights guide the small fishing and oyster boats home across the broad tidal flats.

## Carteret on the West Coast

The lighthouse of Cap de Carteret, on the western Cotentin coast, stands on a heath-covered headland looking out toward the Channel Islands, which are visible on clear days. Built in 1837, the granite tower marks the approach to the harbour of Carteret-Barneville and the ferry route to Jersey. The coastal path that runs past it, the GR 223 around the Cotentin, offers some of the finest cliff walking in northern France.

## Fécamp and the Fishing Port

The port of Fécamp, once the great centre of the Newfoundland cod fishery, is marked by lights on its long jetties that guide the boats home past the chalk cliffs. The town's maritime history runs deep, from the cod fleets that sailed for the Grand Banks to the Bénédictine distillery that grew rich on their trade. The jetty lights, simple and functional, mark a harbour mouth that the powerful seas of the Channel can make treacherous in a westerly gale.

## Barfleur and the Norman Conquest

The small port of Barfleur, near the great Gatteville light, has its own harbour lights and a place in history, for it was from here that the ships of the Norman dukes sailed, and near here that the White Ship sank in 1120, drowning the heir to the English throne. The granite-built village, one of the most beautiful in France, looks out across the same dangerous waters that Gatteville guards, and its modest harbour light continues to guide the fishing boats that work the rich grounds offshore.
