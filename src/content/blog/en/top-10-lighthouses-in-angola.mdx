Angola's coast runs for some 1,650 kilometres down the southwest edge of Africa, facing the open South Atlantic. It is a coast shaped by the cold Benguela Current, which sweeps up from the south, cooling the water, suppressing rainfall and turning the southern shore into one of the driest places on earth, where the Namib desert reaches the sea. For more than four centuries this was a Portuguese colony, and its lighthouses are part of that maritime heritage — towers built to guide the shipping that carried slaves, then coffee and diamonds, and now oil, the foundation of the modern Angolan economy. The lights run from the great natural harbour of Luanda, the capital, southward past the ports of Lobito and Benguela to the desert coast of Namibe, where beacons stand on shores of bare dune and rolling Atlantic surf.

## 1. Farol da Ilha, Luanda

The Farol da Ilha stands on the Ilha de Luanda, the long, low sand spit that curves out from the capital to enclose the great Bay of Luanda, forming one of the finest natural harbours on the African Atlantic coast. The light guides the heavy shipping into Luanda, Angola's principal port and the heart of its oil economy, past the spit lined with restaurants, bars and beaches that face the open ocean on one side and the bay on the other. Behind it the city rises in a skyline of new towers, and the historic fortress of São Miguel, founded in 1576, looks down over the harbour the light protects.

## 2. Farol de Lobito

Lobito, on the central coast, possesses one of the best natural harbours in Africa, formed by a long sandy spit, the Restinga, that curves to enclose a deep, sheltered bay. The lighthouse marks the approach to this great port, the Atlantic terminus of the Benguela railway that once carried copper from the Congo and Zambian copperbelt across the continent. The light guides the bulk carriers and the rebuilt railway traffic into the harbour, while the Restinga, lined with hotels and beaches, makes Lobito one of the most attractive coastal cities of Angola, its bay among the calmest anchorages on the coast.

## 3. Farol de Moçâmedes, Namibe

At Namibe, the old Portuguese Moçâmedes in the far south, a lighthouse marks the approach to the principal port of Angola's desert coast. The city sits where the Namib desert meets the Atlantic, in a landscape of bare dunes and rocky shore cooled by the Benguela Current. Moçâmedes was founded as a fishing and trading port, and its light guides the vessels that work these rich but forbidding waters, where the cold current produces abundant fish but almost no rain. The town, with its Portuguese colonial architecture and its setting between desert and sea, is the gateway to the spectacular landscapes of southern Angola.

## 4. Farol da Ponta Albina

Ponta Albina, on the desert coast south of Namibe, carries one of the most remote and atmospheric lighthouses in Angola, a tower standing alone where the dunes of the Namib roll down to the Atlantic surf. Built in the colonial era, the light marks this lonely headland on a coast almost without inhabitants, where the cold current and the bare sand create a landscape of stark beauty. The point lies near the Iona National Park, Angola's largest, a vast desert wilderness of dunes, welwitschia plants and gemsbok. The light at Ponta Albina is a sentinel on one of the emptiest and most dramatic shores of the continent.

## 5. Farol dos Flamingos, Namibe

South of Namibe, the Flamingos coast takes its name from the flamingos that gather on its lagoons and salt flats, where the desert meets the sea in a landscape of dunes and shallow water. The lighthouse marks this stretch of the Namibe coast, guiding vessels along a shore renowned for its surreal scenery, where the Welwitschia desert plant grows and the dunes of the Namib begin their long roll south toward Namibia. The Flamingos beaches, with their dramatic dune backdrop and the cold rich water offshore, are among the most striking on the Angolan coast, and the light stands at the threshold of the great southern desert.

## 6. Farol do Cabo Ledo

Cabo Ledo, south of Luanda, marks a bold headland on a coast of cliffs, beaches and a celebrated surf break that draws wave-riders from the capital. The lighthouse guides vessels along this stretch of the northern coast, where red cliffs drop to long sandy beaches and the Atlantic rolls in clean lines onto the shore. Cabo Ledo has become one of Angola's most popular weekend escapes, its bay sheltered for swimming and its point famous for one of the best left-hand waves in Africa. The light stands above a coast where the dry savanna of the north meets the ocean in dramatic red headlands.

## 7. Farol de Santa Marta

The Farol de Santa Marta stands on a headland of the Benguela coast, marking the approach along a stretch of shore between the great ports of Lobito and the southern desert. The light, a Portuguese-era tower, guides the coastal traffic and the fishing fleet that works the rich Benguela Current waters, among the most productive fishing grounds on the African Atlantic. The headland looks out over a coast of beaches and rocky points, where the cooler southern water begins to make itself felt, and the light is one of the chain of beacons that mark the central Angolan shore.

## 8. Farol da Barra do Dande

North of Luanda, the Barra do Dande marks the mouth of the Dande river, where it meets the Atlantic on a coast of beaches and river estuaries. The lighthouse guides vessels along this northern stretch and marks the river bar, a hazard for craft entering the estuary. The Dande coast, north of the capital toward the old town of Caxito and the Bengo province, is a region of beaches, fishing villages and the broad river valleys that drain the northern interior. The light stands where river and ocean meet on a green, watered coast quite unlike the deserts of the far south.

## 9. Farol da Ponta das Salinas

Ponta das Salinas, on the coast south of Benguela, marks a headland near the salt flats that give it its name, where the shallow coastal lagoons evaporate under the sun to leave deposits of salt. The lighthouse guides vessels along this central-southern shore, a coast of beaches, salt pans and the rich fishing waters of the Benguela Current. The point lies on the long, sparsely inhabited stretch between Benguela and the desert ports of the south, where the landscape grows steadily drier toward the Namib. The light is a marker on one of the quieter sections of the Angolan coast.

## 10. Farol do Giraúl, Namibe

The Farol do Giraúl stands near the mouth of the Giraúl river on the desert coast near Namibe, where a seasonal river crosses the Namib to reach the Atlantic. The lighthouse marks this stretch of the southern desert shore, guiding vessels past the river mouth on a coast of bare dunes and cold, fish-rich water. The Giraúl valley, green where the river runs, cuts a line of life across the parched desert, and the light stands where this thread of water meets the ocean. The setting, on one of the most arid coasts in Africa, makes it among the most remote of Angola's beacons.
