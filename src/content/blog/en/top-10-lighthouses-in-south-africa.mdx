South Africa's coastline stretches nearly 2,800 kilometres from the Limpopo River mouth on the Mozambique border, southwest around the Cape Peninsula, and north again along the Namaqualand shore to the Orange River. For the great sailing ship routes of the seventeenth through nineteenth centuries it was the central landmark of the entire voyage between Europe and Asia: the rounding of the Cape of Good Hope, later found to be not the southernmost point of Africa at all — that distinction belongs to Cape Agulhas, 150 kilometres to the southeast. The coast between those two capes, and stretching westward along the cold Benguela Current shores and eastward along the warm Agulhas Current, is characterised by fierce southwesterly gales, persistent sea fog produced by the collision of warm and cold water, and a pattern of wrecks that drove the establishment of a lighthouse network beginning in the 1820s. The South African lighthouse service today falls under the South African Maritime Safety Authority, which maintains active stations from Cape Point to Richards Bay and beyond.

## 1. Cape Agulhas Lighthouse, Western Cape

Cape Agulhas lighthouse, built in 1848 and restored to working condition in 1988 after a period as a derelict, is the second oldest lighthouse in South Africa and one of the most historically resonant on the continent. The tower is 27 metres tall and was modelled on the Pharos of Alexandria — an unusual design choice that gives it an Egyptian character unique among South African lighthouses. The light shows a white flash every five seconds with a range of 30 nautical miles. The cape marks the official boundary between the Atlantic and Indian Oceans and the southernmost point of the African continent; ships rounding it in the great age of sail knew they had achieved the most critical navigation milestone of any voyage between Europe and Asia. The lighthouse was decommissioned in 1968 when the lantern was damaged, then restored; it now serves as both an active aid to navigation and a museum open to visitors, with a tower climb available.

## 2. Cape Point Lighthouse, Western Cape

Cape Point, part of the Table Mountain National Park, carries two lighthouses: an original 1859 tower built at the summit of the 249-metre cliff, and a replacement built in 1919 lower down on Dias Point at 87 metres above sea level after fog repeatedly obscured the upper light from vessels at sea level. The newer tower is the active station, showing a white flash every ten seconds with a range of 63 nautical miles — among the most powerful on the South African coast. The original tower is preserved as a heritage structure and is reached by the funicular railway that serves the Cape Point tourist complex within the national park. The point was not the original Cape of Good Hope — that cape, though more famous by name, lies a few kilometres to the northwest — but the Point's dramatic appearance from seaward made it the definitive landmark for generations of mariners.

## 3. Green Point Lighthouse, Cape Town

The Green Point lighthouse, built in 1824 on the low headland between the foreshore and the Sea Point suburb of Cape Town, is the oldest working lighthouse in South Africa. The round white tower is relatively modest at 14 metres, but it has guided vessels around the rocky Green Point promontory and into the Table Bay anchorage for two centuries. The light shows a white and red character; the red sector covers the reef extending from the point. Green Point was the site of an important fog signal station using cannons and later compressed-air diaphones; the fog signal installations have since been decommissioned. The lighthouse is active and maintained by SAMSA. It stands within the Green Point urban park and is visible from the Cape Town waterfront; the exterior is freely accessible and the setting — with Table Mountain as backdrop — makes it one of the most photographed lighthouses in Africa.

## 4. Slangkop Lighthouse, Kommetjie

The Slangkop lighthouse at Kommetjie on the Cape Peninsula's west coast is the tallest cast-iron lighthouse in South Africa, built in 1919 by the Imperial Lighthouse Service. The black-painted tower is 33 metres tall with a focal plane of 88 metres; the light shows a white group flash of three every fifteen seconds, visible for 33 nautical miles. The cape marks the turn from False Bay into the open Atlantic along the west coast; the cold Benguela Current runs northward here and produces persistent cold sea fog that lies low over the inshore waters while the cliff-top sky remains clear. The lighthouse is managed by SAMSA and is open to the public for guided tours on specific days; the Kommetjie community runs these visits. The surrounding coastal fynbos is part of the Cape Floral Region World Heritage Site, and whale watching is excellent in the bay below from August to November.

## 5. Danger Point Lighthouse, Gansbaai

Danger Point lighthouse was built in 1895 on the same cape near Gansbaai where the troopship HMS Birkenhead sank in 1852, drowning 445 soldiers and crew in an event that gave rise to the phrase 'Birkenhead drill' — the convention of women and children first into the lifeboats. The wreck lies in about 30 metres of water a few kilometres offshore and the reef on which she struck remains visible at low water. The lighthouse was built precisely because of this and similar wrecks; the white tower is 20 metres tall with a focal plane of 72 metres and shows a white flash every five seconds with a range of 26 nautical miles. The station is automated but the grounds are open to visitors; the nearby Kleinbaai harbour is South Africa's principal shark cage diving departure point, and white sharks cruise the waters beneath the lighthouse's beam routinely.

## 6. Cape Recife Lighthouse, Port Elizabeth

Cape Recife marks the eastern end of Algoa Bay and the outer approach to Port Elizabeth (now Gqeberha), the principal port of the Eastern Cape. The lighthouse was built in 1851 and the current tower, which replaced it in 1932, stands 14 metres tall on a low sandy cape; the focal plane is 28 metres and the range approximately 15 nautical miles. The cape marks a reef system that extends several kilometres offshore and has been responsible for numerous wrecks including Portuguese carracks in the sixteenth century. The Cape Recife Nature Reserve surrounds the lighthouse and protects a stretch of coastal fynbos, penguin colony and seabird rookeries. The nature reserve is open to the public and guided lighthouse tours are available at weekends; African penguins breed on the beach directly adjacent to the lighthouse compound.

## 7. Mossel Bay Lighthouse, Western Cape

The lighthouse at Cape St Blaize, Mossel Bay, stands on the cliff above the town and marks the approach to one of the Garden Route's principal harbours. The tower was built in 1864 and rises 18 metres above the cliff to a focal plane of 77 metres; it shows a white group flash every fifteen seconds with a range of 25 nautical miles. The cape was Bartolomeu Dias's first landfall after rounding Cape Agulhas in 1488, and a plaque marks the site. The lighthouse is managed by SAMSA and the station is part of a heritage precinct that includes the Bartolomeu Dias Museum Complex. Guided tours are available and include access to the lantern room; the cave beneath the lighthouse headland was used by indigenous Khoikhoi people for millennia and is now an archaeological site within the museum complex.

## 8. Noordhoek Lighthouse Ruins, Western Cape

The original Cape Point lighthouse site above Chapman's Peak is not a working light, but the ruins of the first lighthouse attempt in this area — a structure begun but never completed in the 1850s — are historically significant. More notable near Noordhoek is the wrecked SS Kakapo (1900) visible on the beach below Chapman's Peak, a potent reminder of why this coast needed its lights. The active navigation mark for this stretch is Slangkop to the south, but the Chapman's Peak stretch was itself marked by an unlit structure for several decades. The coastal path from Noordhoek to Kommetjie along the Atlantic shoreline passes several historical wreck sites and is one of the finest shoreline walks in the Cape Peninsula.

## 9. Robben Island Lighthouse, Western Cape

The Robben Island lighthouse, standing on the island most famous as the location of Nelson Mandela's eighteen-year imprisonment, was first established in the mid-nineteenth century. The current white tower is about 18 metres tall and remains an active aid to navigation in Table Bay; it shows a white flash visible across the approaches to Cape Town's harbour. The island's maritime history predates its role as a prison by centuries: it was used as a depot for Dutch East India Company vessels from the 1650s, and a beacon of some kind stood here to guide ships into the anchorage under Table Mountain. Today the island is a World Heritage Site and a major tourism destination; ferry tours from the V&A Waterfront include passage past the lighthouse and the penguin colony on the island's western shore.

## 10. Stony Point Penguin Colony and Light, Betty's Bay

The small navigation light at Stony Point in Betty's Bay is modest in engineering terms, but its setting within one of South Africa's largest mainland African penguin colonies gives it singular character. The stone-built light marks the rocky reef at the eastern approach to Walker Bay; the surrounding nature reserve protects approximately 3,000 breeding pairs of African penguin as well as Cape cormorants, kelp gulls and seals. The light is best visited at dawn or dusk when penguin activity is at its peak. Betty's Bay is on the R44 coast road between Gordon's Bay and Hermanus, making it a natural stop on the Whale Coast route. The combination of working navigation light, intact fynbos and charismatic wildlife makes Stony Point one of the more rewarding lighthouse visits on the southwestern Cape coast.

## Visiting South Africa's Lighthouses

The Western Cape concentrates the country's most significant lighthouse heritage, and a coastal drive from Cape Town to Port Elizabeth along the Garden Route and the Whale Coast passes within sight of most of the major towers. Cape Agulhas and Green Point both offer excellent visitor facilities; Slangkop at Kommetjie opens for public tours on specific days. The best months for coastal touring are September through November, when whale numbers in the bays peak and the fynbos is in flower, or April and May before the winter swells begin.
