## France and the Art of the Lighthouse

France lit its coasts earlier and more systematically than almost any other nation. The Service des Phares et Balises, established in the early nineteenth century under the Ponts et Chaussées engineering corps, oversaw the construction of a lighthouse network that combined remarkable engineering ambition with the technical revolution of the Fresnel lens — a French invention that transformed lighthouse optics worldwide from the 1820s. The result is a coastline that stretches from Dunkirk to Menton on the Mediterranean, and from Biarritz to the Breton headlands on the Atlantic, strewn with towers that are simultaneously great works of engineering and great works of maritime art. Brittany alone — the peninsula that extends furthest into the Atlantic and bears the full force of the Bay of Biscay and the English Channel approaches — concentrates more significant lighthouses than most entire countries. The Phares Service remains active, and many towers are open to the public, often with interpretive exhibitions of the highest quality.

## 1. Phare de Cordouan, Gironde Estuary

Cordouan is the oldest lighthouse still in operation in France, and one of the most architecturally distinguished lighthouses in the world. Standing on a sandbank at the mouth of the Gironde Estuary — where the Garonne and the Dordogne meet the Atlantic — the tower was built between 1584 and 1611 under the direction of engineer Louis de Foix for Henri III and Henry IV of France. It is a Renaissance palace of a lighthouse: the lower section incorporates royal apartments, a chapel with a domed ceiling, a vestibule with carved columns, and ornamental stonework of extraordinary quality. The round tower rises to 68 metres above the sea, making it the tallest lighthouse in France. The current first-order Fresnel lens, exhibiting a white flash every twelve seconds visible at 22 nautical miles, was installed in the nineteenth century. UNESCO inscribed Cordouan on its World Heritage List in 2021. Accessible by boat from Royan and Le Verdon-sur-Mer when tides permit, it receives thousands of visitors each year.

## 2. Phare d'Eckmühl, Penmarc'h, Finistère

Phare d'Eckmühl, completed in 1897 near the southern tip of Brittany at Penmarc'h, was built with funds bequeathed by the daughter of Marshal Davout, whose title was Prince of Eckmühl. It is among the most elegant lighthouse towers in France: a slender granite cylinder 65 metres tall, designed by architect Victor Dufour, rising from the flat coastal plain with exceptional vertical purity. The tower is clad in stone quarried at Kersanton and Coray; the workmanship throughout is of the first order. The light character is a white flash every five seconds, visible at 23 nautical miles. The Pointe de Penmarc'h has been one of the most feared headlands on the Breton coast since antiquity — the hazards include offshore rocks and the complex tidal streams of Audierne Bay. The lighthouse is open to visitors throughout the year, and the 307 steps to the top reward those who climb with views across the flat Bigouden countryside and the Atlantic horizon.

## 3. Phare de la Jument, Ouessant, Finistère

La Jument stands alone on a reef three kilometres off the southwestern tip of the Île d'Ouessant, the last land before the open Atlantic. Its tower, completed in 1911 after years of engineering difficulties caused by the exposed site, was designed and built by Armand Considère using reinforced concrete — one of the first French lighthouses to use the material. It stands 47 metres above the reef, its red granite base and concrete upper tower painted in distinctive red and white horizontal bands. The light character is a red and white flash every four seconds, visible at 22 nautical miles. La Jument became internationally famous through Jean Guichard's 1989 photograph of the keeper standing at the base of the tower as a giant wave engulfs the rock — an image that has come to define the exposed lighthouse. The lighthouse was automated in 1991. It can be seen from the cliffs of Ouessant and from the ferry crossing from Brest or Camaret.

## 4. Phare du Créac'h, Ouessant, Finistère

The Créac'h lighthouse, on the western tip of Ouessant, is the most powerful lighthouse in Europe and arguably in the world. The current tower, built in 1863, is 55 metres tall and its massive optic — a first-order hyper-radial lens — gives it a nominal range of 34 nautical miles, with two white flashes every ten seconds. Every vessel crossing from the English Channel to the Atlantic, or from the Atlantic into the Channel, uses the Créac'h as its landfall. The lighthouse is automated and managed by the French lighthouse authority; the former engine rooms and keeper's quarters now house the Musée des Phares et Balises (Museum of Lighthouses and Buoys), which contains an exceptional collection of Fresnel lenses, lighthouse equipment and navigational history. The museum is open year-round and is the best-interpreted lighthouse site in France. The distinctive black-and-white horizontal banding of the tower is the standard daymark of the French lighthouse system at its most emphatic.

## 5. Ar-Men Lighthouse, Finistère

Ar-Men stands six kilometres off the western tip of the Île de Sein, itself a sliver of rock barely above sea level west of Pointe du Raz. Its name means 'the rock' in Breton, and the construction of the lighthouse here — begun in 1867 and not completed until 1881 — is one of the most extreme stories in lighthouse engineering. Working time on the rock was measured in hours per year: storms and rough seas prevented work for months at a time, and the builders spent fourteen years erecting a tower that stands only 33 metres above a reef perpetually swept by Atlantic swells. The light character is a white flash every five seconds, range 23 nautical miles. Ar-Men was automated in 1990. It is sometimes called 'l'enfer des enfers' — the hell of hells — by the keepers who served there. It can be seen, in good weather, from the Île de Sein, accessible by ferry from Audierne.

## 6. Phare de Gatteville, Cotentin, Normandy

The Gatteville lighthouse, at the northeastern tip of the Cotentin Peninsula in Normandy, is one of the tallest in France at 75 metres, surpassed only by Cordouan. It was completed in 1835 to a design that uses a spiral staircase of 365 steps to reach the lantern — one step per day of the year, according to tradition. The tower marks the Barfleur Race, a notoriously dangerous tidal stream off the Pointe de Barfleur that has wrecked ships since the Norman era. William the Conqueror's fleet assembled near here in 1066; the White Ship, carrying the son of Henry I of England, was wrecked on the reef in 1120. The light character is a white flash every fifteen seconds, range 25 nautical miles. The lighthouse is open daily in summer; the keeper's cottage and outbuildings have been preserved and interpreted. The surrounding Pointe de Barfleur is a substantial tourist destination.

## 7. Phare des Baleines, Île de Ré

The Phare des Baleines — Lighthouse of the Whales — stands on the western tip of the Île de Ré, the island off La Rochelle connected to the mainland by a bridge since 1988. The current tower, completed in 1854 and 57 metres tall, replaced an earlier lighthouse of 1682 that still stands alongside it, one of the few places in France where successive lighthouse towers occupy the same site together. The light character is a white flash every five seconds, range 24 nautical miles. The name derives from the whales that were once beached on the western shores of the island, and the tower marks the seaward approach to the Pertuis d'Antioche and the approaches to La Rochelle and Rochefort. The lighthouse is open daily, and the adjacent old tower and natural reserve make the site one of the most rewarding on the Atlantic coast. Île de Ré is famous for cycling, salt marshes and the donkeys that once carried panniers of salt across the island.

## 8. Phare de la Petite Muette, Loire Estuary

The Petite Muette lighthouse marks the southern approach to the Loire Estuary fairway, a pair of channel leading-lights guiding vessels into and out of the port of Saint-Nazaire. The Loire Estuary is France's most important Atlantic port approach: Saint-Nazaire is a major industrial and shipbuilding centre, and the river above it leads to Nantes. The estuary is shallow, subject to shifting sandbanks and strong tidal currents, and the system of leading lights and marks that guides vessels through it represents precision maritime engineering. La Petite Muette is a white tower of modest height, its directional light essential for vessels on the specific heading required. The surrounding wetlands of the Loire Estuary — the Grande Brière natural park — are exceptional for birdwatching.

## 9. Phare de Port Manec'h, Finistère

Port Manec'h lighthouse stands on the headland between the Aven and Bélon rivers on the southern Finistère coast, marking the approaches to Riec-sur-Belon and Pont-Aven — the town whose art colony, led by Paul Gauguin in the 1880s, is one of the foundational episodes in modern art. The lighthouse is a white round tower of modest dimensions, its sectored light distinguishing safe water from the rocks of the approach. The surrounding coastline of the Aven-Belon estuary is among the most beautiful in Brittany, with wooded river valleys meeting the sea at narrow inlets. The area is accessible and popular, and the lighthouse headland offers views south along the Finistère coast.

## 10. Phare du Millier, Cap du Raz, Finistère

The Phare du Millier stands on the promontory east of Douarnenez Bay, a white tower marking the approach to the bay and the passages inside the Île Tristan. This section of the Finistère coast, between Douarnenez and the Pointe du Raz, concentrates an unusual number of significant lighthouses within a short distance — the Raz de Sein, the Île de Sein, Ar-Men all lie within sight on a clear day. The Millier light character is a white flash every three seconds visible at 13 nautical miles, covering the approaches from the northwest. The GR 34 coastal walking path passes the lighthouse, making it accessible as part of the famous Breton coastal trail.

## Visiting French Lighthouses

France has one of the most visitor-friendly lighthouse networks in the world. The Phares et Balises service opens many of its towers throughout the summer season, and several — Cordouan, Eckmühl, Créac'h, Gatteville — maintain year-round access. The Journées du Patrimoine in September open additional sites for one weekend. Brittany offers the densest concentration of significant lighthouses; the Ouessant ferry from Brest provides access to both Créac'h and a view of La Jument.
