Minnesota's lighthouse heritage belongs entirely to the western shore of Lake Superior, the largest freshwater lake by surface area on earth. The 150-mile stretch of shoreline from Duluth north to the Canadian border is arguably the most dramatic stretch of Great Lakes coast in the United States: volcanic basalt cliffs, boreal forest tumbling to the water's edge, and a lake notorious for sudden, lethal storms. The iron ore and timber trade of the late nineteenth century drove the federal government to light this coast with exceptional care, and many of those towers still stand in excellent condition. Unlike the salt-water seaboard, where brick erodes and iron rusts, Lake Superior's cold, fresh water and the region's dry winters have preserved these stations remarkably well. Several are now managed by the Minnesota Historical Society and draw hundreds of thousands of visitors each year, making this a genuine lighthouse tourism circuit rivalled in the Great Lakes only by Michigan's Lower Peninsula.

## 1. Split Rock Lighthouse, Two Harbors

Split Rock is the most-photographed lighthouse in the United States, and the claim is hard to dispute. Perched on a 130-foot sheer anorthosite cliff above Lake Superior, it was designed by engineer Ralph Russell Tinkham and built by the U.S. Lighthouse Service between 1909 and 1910, lit for the first time on July 31, 1910. The octagonal yellow-brick tower stands 54 feet tall but the cliff gives it an effective height of around 168 feet above lake level. Its third-order bivalve Fresnel lens produces a flashing white light with a nominal range of 22 nautical miles. The station was decommissioned by the Coast Guard in 1969 and transferred to the Minnesota Historical Society in 1976. The fog signal building, oil house, and two keepers' dwellings are intact. The lens still turns on November 10 each year in memory of the Edmund Fitzgerald, which sank in 1975.

## 2. Two Harbors Lighthouse, Two Harbors

Minnesota's oldest operating lighthouse, completed in 1892, stands on a low point at the entrance to Agate Bay in the iron-ore port of Two Harbors. The square brick tower, just 49 feet tall, was attached to the keeper's dwelling in the common Lake Superior Lighthouse Service style. Its fourth-order Fresnel lens originally showed a fixed red light to guide ore freighters past the breakwater; the light remains active today, operated by the Coast Guard. The station is managed by the Lake County Historical Society, which operates the attached keeper's quarters as a bed-and-breakfast — the only such arrangement at an active lighthouse in Minnesota. The fog signal building and boathouse are intact. The lighthouse is open for tours during summer months, and the harbor location makes the approach straightforward year-round. Proximity to the ore docks gives the site an authentic industrial-maritime atmosphere absent from the more scenic cliff stations to the north.

## 3. Duluth South Breakwater Inner Lighthouse, Duluth

Duluth's famous canal entry is guarded by a pair of pier-head lights, of which the South Breakwater Inner is the elder. The distinctive art-nouveau steel tower with its octagonal profile and red painted finish was erected in 1901, replacing an 1889 wooden structure. Standing 35 feet above the pier deck — and roughly 47 feet above lake level — it displays an isophase red light visible for about 12 nautical miles, warning mariners off the concrete pier walls as they enter the Duluth-Superior harbor, the busiest port on the Great Lakes by tonnage. The light is active and maintained by the U.S. Coast Guard. Access is possible by walking the South Pier from the Canal Park area, making this one of the few Minnesota lighthouses where visitors can stand within a few metres of the active tower. The neighboring North Pier Light, a simpler skeletal structure built in 1910, marks the opposite side of the same narrow channel.

## 4. Duluth Harbor North Pier Lighthouse, Duluth

The slender green steel skeleton tower at the end of the Duluth North Pier was built in 1910 and forms the northern bookend of the famous ship canal. At 37 feet, it is modest in height, but its position at the end of a long breakwater walk — with open lake to the north and the passing hulks of thousand-foot ore carriers close at hand — gives it an unmatched immediacy. The light shows a flashing green character, guiding vessels into the correct lane of the canal entrance. The fog signal equipment originally mounted here has been removed but the cast-iron structure is largely original. Entry to the North Pier requires passing under the Aerial Lift Bridge, one of only a handful of aerial transfer bridges remaining in the world, which adds considerable drama to the walk. The pier is publicly accessible from the Lakewalk.

## 5. Grand Marais Lighthouse, Grand Marais

The small harbor of Grand Marais, 110 miles north of Duluth, gained its first pier-head light in 1885 and the current cast-iron tower in 1922. The skeletal steel structure stands on the outer breakwater protecting the natural harbor, displaying a flashing red light with a range of roughly 8 nautical miles. Though modest in scale, it occupies one of the most scenic harbor settings on the lake: the volcanic headlands of the Sawtooth Mountains form the backdrop and the clear water of the bay is visible below the pier deck. The town of Grand Marais is a popular base for Boundary Waters Canoe Area Wilderness trips and the lighthouse is a short walk from the main street, making it accessible to visitors without special planning. The harbor also contains an inner range light from 1927 on the old Coast Guard station.

## 6. Duluth South Breakwater Outer Lighthouse, Duluth

The outer light of the South Pier differs architecturally from its inner companion: the 1874 original was replaced in 1901 with a cylindrical cast-iron tower painted white, 35 feet tall, rising from the very end of the breakwater. It shows an occulting white light with a range of about 14 nautical miles. Together with the Inner Light it creates a functional pair that mariners can use to determine their alignment with the canal. The two Duluth pier-head stations are among the most-visited working lighthouse structures in the Midwest, accessible on foot from the busy Canal Park entertainment district. Several vessels of historical significance including the William A. Irvin ore carrier are moored nearby as floating museums, giving the waterfront an unusually rich concentration of maritime heritage within a compact area.

## 7. Thirty Mile Point Area — Knife River to Castle Danger

The stretch of coast between Knife River and Castle Danger contains several minor range lights and automated beacons installed by the Lighthouse Service in the early twentieth century to serve small fishing harbors and to guide the ore-carrier lanes. Though none approaches the architectural status of Split Rock or Two Harbors, they document the everyday infrastructure that made commercial navigation on Lake Superior possible. Several small towers and skeletal structures from this era survive in various states of preservation on private land or small municipal piers. The Knife River Range lights, consisting of two small tower-lanterns on the river mouth, were built in the 1890s and are among the few surviving examples of a working range pair on Minnesota's north shore.

## 8. Two Harbors East Breakwater Light, Two Harbors

The outer pier-head light at Two Harbors was constructed in 1894 to complement the main lighthouse as ore-shipping traffic expanded. The square white cast-iron tower at the end of the breakwater stands 25 feet above pier level and displays a flashing green light, marking the northern edge of the entrance channel. It is visible from the grounds of the main lighthouse complex and from the ore dock vantage points nearby. Though heavily utilitarian in design — the Lighthouse Service built dozens of identical structures at Great Lakes ports — its position above the dark basalt of the pier and the backdrop of the Sawtooth Range gives it a natural drama that more ornate towers lack. The breakwater walk is publicly accessible and the combination of the two Two Harbors lights makes for one of the more complete lighthouse station visits in Minnesota.

## 9. Pigeon River Area and the Canadian Border

The extreme northeastern corner of Minnesota, where the Pigeon River flows into Lake Superior at the Canadian border crossing, contains several navigational lights associated with the historic Grand Portage trading post and the Isle Royale ferry route. The Canadian island of Isle Royale is a U.S. national park accessible from Minnesota, and the shipping lanes to it pass through waters lit by a succession of small range and sector lights. The Rock of Ages Lighthouse on Isle Royale itself, while technically in Michigan, is closely connected with the Minnesota port of Copper Harbor and was supplied historically from Duluth. Its 130-foot Art Deco concrete tower of 1910 is among the most imposing on Lake Superior and merits the short lake crossing to see in context.

## 10. Superior Entry Lighthouse, Superior (Wisconsin, near Duluth)

Immediately across the state line in Wisconsin but functionally part of the same Duluth-Superior harbor complex, the Superior Entry (Wisconsin Point) Lighthouse of 1913 stands at the end of the longest natural sand spit on Lake Superior. Its 66-foot brick tower with octagonal lantern displays an isophase white light visible for 16 nautical miles. Though not in Minnesota proper, every serious Minnesota lighthouse tour includes it: the drive out along Wisconsin Point passes through a cathedral of old-growth white pine, and the tower's isolation at the sandy point creates a very different atmosphere from the dramatic cliff stations to the north. The keeper's dwelling was demolished but the tower and fog signal building survive. The spit is accessible by a gravel road and is one of the best locations on western Lake Superior for migratory songbirds in spring.

## Visiting Minnesota's Lighthouses

Split Rock Lighthouse State Park, operated by the Minnesota Historical Society, is open from mid-May through October; admission fees apply to the historic site. Two Harbors Lighthouse is open year-round, with guided tours offered in summer and the keeper's quarters bookable as accommodation. The Duluth pier-head lights are accessible on foot at any time. Grand Marais is a four-hour drive from the Twin Cities on Highway 61 — the North Shore Scenic Drive — and the route passes virtually every lighthouse on this list. The best time to visit is late August through early October, when leaf color peaks and the weather remains mild. November storms make the lake most dramatic but conditions can be severe.
