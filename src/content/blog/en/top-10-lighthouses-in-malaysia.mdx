Malaysia's maritime geography is defined by two coastlines separated by the South China Sea. Peninsular Malaysia faces the Malacca Strait to the west — one of the world's most critical shipping lanes, through which some 80,000 vessels pass each year carrying a third of global trade and much of the oil supply to East Asia — and the South China Sea to the east. East Malaysia, comprising Sabah and Sarawak on the island of Borneo, has its own long and complex coastline along the South China Sea. The British colonial administration, through Trinity House and later the Marine Department of the Federated and Unfederated Malay States, built a systematic network of lighthouses from the mid-nineteenth century onward. Several of these survive as active navigation aids and as heritage structures of considerable architectural merit. Today Malaysia's lighthouses are administered by the Marine Department (Jabatan Laut Malaysia), and their role remains critical: the Malacca Strait demands precision navigation, and the waters off the east coast of the peninsula are dotted with shoals and islands that have wrecked vessels throughout maritime history.

## 1. Cape Rachado Lighthouse (Tanjung Tuan), Port Dickson

The Cape Rachado Lighthouse at Tanjung Tuan is the oldest operational lighthouse in Malaysia and one of the oldest in Southeast Asia, with a light established here under Portuguese administration in the sixteenth century. The current white cylindrical masonry tower, approximately 14 metres tall, was built by the Dutch in the seventeenth century and has been maintained and modified by subsequent colonial administrations. It stands on a forested headland 67 metres above the Malacca Strait, giving its flashing white light a remarkable range of around 22 nautical miles. The lighthouse marks a critical turning point in the Malacca Strait where the channel narrows and traffic is at its densest. The headland is within the Tanjung Tuan Forest Reserve; the forest here hosts one of Malaysia's finest raptor migration spectacles each spring, when thousands of honey buzzards stream across the strait from Sumatra. Access is by a forest trail from the reserve car park.

## 2. Pulau Pisang Lighthouse, Pulau Pisang

Pulau Pisang (Banana Island) in the Malacca Strait is a small island whose lighthouse has a unique administrative status: though on Malaysian territory, the lighthouse was built and is maintained by Singapore under a 1900 agreement with the Johor Sultanate, by which Singapore's predecessor, the Straits Settlements, undertook to keep the light in perpetuity in exchange for landing rights. The white masonry tower, built in 1914, is approximately 28 metres tall and shows a white flashing light visible around 20 nautical miles. The lighthouse marks a critical position in the central Malacca Strait. Pulau Pisang is not open to tourists; the island and lighthouse are maintained by a small Singapore-appointed keeper, making this one of the last traditionally kept lighthouses in the region.

## 3. One Fathom Bank Lighthouse, Malacca Strait

The One Fathom Bank Lighthouse is one of the most unusual structures in the Malacca Strait — a lighthouse built on an artificial foundation placed directly on the shallow One Fathom Bank shoal, far from any natural land. The current structure, a tall skeletal steel tower on a concrete caisson, replaced an earlier lighthouse vessel that had marked this dangerous shoal for many years. The bank lies in the southern approach to Port Klang, Malaysia's main container port, in some of the most traffic-dense water anywhere on earth. The lighthouse shows a white flashing light and has a modest visible range, supplemented by radar reflectors and AIS transponders for modern traffic. It is visible from vessels using the strait but not accessible to the public.

## 4. Pulau Undan Lighthouse, Malacca Strait

Pulau Undan is a small island south of Malacca town, and the lighthouse here marks a section of the Malacca Strait particularly encumbered by shoals and small islands. The tower is a white masonry cylindrical structure of British colonial construction, active with a flashing white light, standing perhaps 15 metres above the water. Malacca itself — a UNESCO World Heritage City — was for two centuries the greatest trading port in the world, the meeting point of Arab, Indian, Chinese, Malay, and European merchants. The lighthouse at Pulau Undan marks the maritime approaches to a city whose history is inseparable from the sea.

## 5. Tanjung Kelian Lighthouse, Pangkor Island

Tanjung Kelian at the northern tip of Pangkor Island marks the approach to Lumut, the harbour for Pangkor and the base for the Royal Malaysian Navy. The lighthouse is a white cylindrical tower, active with a flashing light, standing on the promontory where in 1874 the Pangkor Treaty was signed — the agreement by which Britain established its Resident system in Perak and effectively began its colonial control of the Malay Peninsula. A small museum at the site commemorates the treaty. The lighthouse and the historic site are accessible from Lumut by ferry to Pangkor, then by local transport to the northern tip of the island.

## 6. Tanjung Besar Lighthouse, Taiping Coast

Along the coast of Perak north of Lumut, several minor lights mark the shallow approaches to the river mouths and small ports that serve the tin-mining hinterland that was the original economic engine of British Malaya. These towers are modest structures of the late colonial period, white cylindrical or square towers showing fixed or flashing lights over the sandbanks and mangrove margins of the Perak coast.

## 7. Kuala Gula Lighthouse, Perak

Kuala Gula at the mouth of the Gula River in Perak is one of the last villages in Malaysia still involved in traditional migratory bird netting, and the lighthouse here marks the river approach through the extensive mangrove forests of the Matang Mangrove Forest Reserve — one of the best-managed mangrove systems in the world and a critical nursery for the fisheries of the Malacca Strait. The lighthouse is a white tower of moderate height, active as a minor navigation aid, surrounded by a landscape of extraordinary ecological richness.

## 8. Raffles Lighthouse (operated from Singapore), South of Johor

The Raffles Lighthouse on Pulau Satumu at the southern tip of the Singapore strait is administered by Singapore, but it marks the southern entrance to the Malacca Strait and is an inseparable part of the navigation framework for Malaysian and international shipping using these waters. Built in 1855, the white masonry tower stands 29 metres on a small rocky island; its light is visible around 16 nautical miles. The island is not open to the public.

## 9. Miri Lighthouse, Sarawak

Miri in Sarawak is the centre of Malaysian petroleum production — the first oil well in Malaysia was drilled here in 1910 — and the lighthouse at Miri marks the approach to this important port on the South China Sea. The tower is a modern white cylindrical structure, active with a flashing light, maintained by the Marine Department. The South China Sea coast of Sarawak is relatively straight with few islands offshore, making the fixed lights at each river mouth and port the principal navigation aids for vessels along this coast.

## 10. Kota Kinabalu Lighthouse, Sabah

Kota Kinabalu, the capital of Sabah in East Malaysia, has a small lighthouse or leading light marking the approach to its port and the ferry terminals for the offshore islands. The South China Sea off Kota Kinabalu is encumbered by a scattering of islands — Gaya, Sapi, Mamutik, and others that now form the Tunku Abdul Rahman Marine Park — and lights are essential for navigating among them. The Sabah coast is one of Malaysia's most visited tourist destinations, particularly for divers heading to Sipadan, regarded as one of the world's finest dive sites.

## Visiting Malaysia's Lighthouses

Cape Rachado at Tanjung Tuan is the most rewarding lighthouse visit in Malaysia, accessible on a day trip from Port Dickson or Seremban, best timed to coincide with the spring raptor migration (late March to mid-April). Pangkor Island, reachable by ferry from Lumut, combines the Tanjung Kelian historic site with pleasant beaches. One Fathom Bank and Pulau Pisang are not accessible to visitors. East Malaysia's lighthouses at Miri and Kota Kinabalu are easily visited as part of broader Borneo itineraries. The monsoon seasons affect each coast differently: the west coast is rougher October to April; the east coast is roughest November to March.
