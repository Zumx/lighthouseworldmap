The Dutch relationship with the sea is one of the most intimate and consequential in maritime history. A nation built on reclaimed land, perpetually negotiating between sea and shore, the Netherlands has long understood that reliable navigation marks are not a luxury but a necessity. The Wadden Sea, the Rhine-Meuse-Scheldt delta, and the exposed North Sea coast each present distinct hazards — shoaling sands that shift with every storm, narrow channels threading between islands, and the perpetual fog and haze of a temperate northern coast. Dutch lighthouse builders responded with towers that range from elegant red-brick cylinders on the Frisian Islands to squat cast-iron structures buried deep in the dunes. Explore them on [the interactive map](/map) and you will see how closely they trace the coastline's most dangerous passages.

## 1. Brandaris, Terschelling

Rising 54 metres above the Wadden Island of Terschelling, the Brandaris is the oldest functioning lighthouse in the Netherlands and one of the oldest in northern Europe. A tower has stood on this spot since 1323, when the island's position at the entrance to the Zuiderzee made it indispensable to Baltic trade. The current round brick tower was completed in 1594, rebuilt after its medieval predecessor collapsed. Its light — Fl(3) W 15s, visible 29 nautical miles — marks the western approach to the Terschelling Roads and the dangerous Vliestroom channel. The tower is painted white and stands in the centre of the village of West-Terschelling, its presence so central to island life that the locals are known as Brandarissen. The interior is open to visitors in summer; the climb rewards with panoramic views across the Frisian islands chain.

## 2. Vuurtoren van IJmuiden (Hoge Vuurtoren), IJmuiden

The Hoge Vuurtoren at IJmuiden, completed in 1878 to a design by engineer A.L. van Hasselt, stands 35 metres tall at the entrance to the North Sea Canal, the engineered waterway that connects Amsterdam's port to open sea. Built from grey granite-faced brick, it is a solid, workmanlike tower that has guided generations of vessels into one of Europe's busiest canal ports. Its light, Fl(4) W 20s with a range of 29 nautical miles, is supplemented by a lower companion tower that together create a leading-light pair for vessels approaching the canal entrance. The lighthouse was automated in 1989 and is listed as a national monument. The dunes around IJmuiden offer straightforward access; the outer harbour breakwaters provide a dramatic foreground for photography on stormy days.

## 3. Paard van Marken, Marken

The Paard van Marken — Horse of Marken — is a charming yellow-painted wooden lighthouse on the tip of the Marken peninsula in the IJsselmeer. First established in 1839, the current structure dates from 1914 and is among the few wooden lighthouses surviving in the Netherlands. Its location marks the shallow approaches to the former Zuiderzee, now the freshwater IJsselmeer since the Afsluitdijk closed in 1932, changing the lighthouse's context from tidal sea to controlled lake. The light (Fl W 5s, 15 nautical miles) still guides recreational traffic and occasional commercial barges. The lighthouse keeper's cottage beside it was converted into a museum café, making Marken one of the most visitor-friendly lighthouse sites in the country. The peninsula is accessible by road or, more pleasingly, by ferry from Volendam.

## 4. Vuurtoren Urk, Urk

Urk, once an island in the Zuiderzee and now connected to the reclaimed Flevoland polder, retains a lighthouse that predates its transformation from island to peninsula. The current round brick tower, painted white with a red lantern house, was built in 1844 on the rocky outcrop at the village's northern tip. Standing 15 metres tall, it served the fishing fleets of the old Zuiderzee before the Afsluitdijk sealed the sea. Its light (Fl(3) W 12s, range 12 nautical miles) now guides vessels on the IJsselmeer and into the small harbour below. The lighthouse is embedded in the old fishermen's quarter of Urk, surrounded by traditional cottages and a village that still fishes seriously. Access is on foot through the old town; the lighthouse is not open to the interior but is easily circumnavigated and photographed from the rocky foreshore.

## 5. Westhoofd, Ouddorp

Standing at the southwestern tip of the Goeree peninsula, the Westhoofd lighthouse guards the approach to the Rotterdam waterway and the outer Haringvliet estuary. Built in 1947 to replace an earlier structure destroyed in the Second World War, the octagonal concrete tower rises 55 metres, making it one of the tallest in the Netherlands. Its light — Fl(4+1) W 15s, range 30 nautical miles — is a distinctive pattern that identifies it unambiguously to deep-water shipping approaching from the North Sea. The tower is painted white with a black band and red lantern. The surrounding dune landscape is part of a nature reserve, and the lighthouse can be reached by a walk through the dunes from the village of Ouddorp. Summer opening days allow access to the viewing gallery.

## 6. Leekerhoek, North Holland

The Leekerhoek lighthouse near Den Helder marks the northern entrance to the North Holland Canal, where busy commercial traffic once transited between the Zuiderzee and the Texel Roads. Built in 1877, the octagonal iron tower stands just 11 metres tall but occupies a strategically commanding position at a channel junction subject to strong tidal currents and shifting sandbanks. The lighthouse represents a generation of smaller Dutch channel markers that complemented the major coastal towers, guiding vessels through the intricate inland waterway system for which the Netherlands is famous. The harbour surroundings of Den Helder offer a broader collection of maritime heritage, including the historic naval drydocks and the ferry terminal for Texel island.

## 7. Texel (Eierlandse lighthouse), Texel

The northernmost lighthouse on Texel island, known as the Eierlandse vuurtoren, sits at the island's northwestern tip and dates from 1864. Its red-brick tower, 34 metres tall, marks the dangerous shoals between Texel and the open North Sea to the northwest, a passage notorious for strong currents and unpredictable sandbar migration. The light (Oc W 10s, range 20 nautical miles) uses occulting character to distinguish it from the neighbouring Kijkduin light to the south. The tower stands in a remote stretch of dunes with no year-round vehicular access; visitors walk or cycle from the village of De Cocksdorp. In summer, organised open days allow the public inside. The isolation of the location — sea on three sides, dunes sweeping to the horizon — gives it a striking desolate beauty.

## 8. Kijkduin (Huisduinen), Den Helder

The Kijkduin lighthouse at Huisduinen, near Den Helder, is one of the tallest in the Netherlands at 65 metres. Built in 1877 and designed by P.J. Ouwerkerk, this red-brick tower is tapered elegantly and topped with a white lantern room. Its light — Fl(4) W 20s, range 30 nautical miles — covers the Marsdiep, the tidal channel between the North Holland coast and Texel island, one of the busiest and most hazardous passages on the Dutch coast. The power of the tidal stream in the Marsdiep is formidable; vessels approaching in poor visibility have always depended heavily on this light. The lighthouse is administered by the Rijkswaterstaat, and guided climbs are offered periodically. The surrounding Kijkduin beach resort makes access straightforward by public transport from Den Helder.

## 9. Nieuwe Waterweg, Hook of Holland

The two lighthouses at the entrance of the Nieuwe Waterweg — the engineered waterway connecting Rotterdam to the North Sea — work as a leading-light pair, their alignment guiding supertankers and container ships into the world's busiest port complex. The rear high tower, red-banded white brick, and the front low iron structure, both date from the late nineteenth century when the Nieuwe Waterweg was first completed. The light characters Iso W and Oc W, both with ranges of 21 nautical miles, must be brought into vertical alignment to confirm a vessel is on the correct track. This system, mundane to describe, is the critical safety architecture for Rotterdam's enormous shipping throughput. The Hook of Holland harbourfront gives excellent views of the pair and of the constant procession of deep-draft vessels.

## 10. Maasmond, Maasvlakte

The Maasmond lighthouse on the Maasvlakte — the enormous land reclamation project that extended Rotterdam's port into the North Sea — is the most recently established major lighthouse in the Netherlands, sited during the 1970s expansion. Its white tower, 52 metres tall, marks the seaward face of the Maasvlakte and the outer approaches to Europoort. The light (Fl W 5s, 24 nautical miles) operates in conjunction with an elaborate system of buoys and leading lights for the deep-dredged Euro Channel. The Maasvlakte itself is a remarkable industrial landscape, part container terminal and part nature reserve, and access to the lighthouse's immediate surroundings is possible during daylight hours via the public beach and dune reserve on the seaward side of the port.

The best time to explore the Dutch lighthouses is late spring or early summer, when the Wadden Islands are less crowded, the migratory birds are present in enormous numbers, and the low northern light makes for outstanding photography. Many of the island towers — Brandaris, Texel, Ameland — offer guided climbs in July and August. Ferries to the Wadden Islands run year-round from Harlingen, Den Helder and Holwerd, though winter crossings can be rough.
