Pakistan's coast runs for roughly a thousand kilometres along the northern edge of the Arabian Sea, from the silt-choked mouths of the Indus delta in the east to the Iranian border at Gwadar and Jiwani in the far west. It is a low, hot, and in places almost roadless shore, where the great prize for any navigator is the entrance to Karachi Harbour. Guarding that entrance, on the rocky tip of Manora Island, stands the one true heritage lighthouse of the country: the Manora Lighthouse, a colonial-era tower whose beam has marked the approach to South Asia's busiest cargo port for well over a century. Around it stretches a coast where lights are sparse, distances are long, and the southwest monsoon dictates the rhythm of the sea.

## Manora Lighthouse, the Flagship Beacon

The Manora Lighthouse is the structure most people mean when they speak of a lighthouse in Pakistan. It stands on the seaward end of the Manora peninsula, a low spit of land that with neighbouring Sandspit and Oyster Rocks forms the natural breakwater sheltering Karachi Harbour. A light has marked this point since the British developed Karachi as a port in the mid-nineteenth century; the present masonry tower, painted in bold bands and rising above the surrounding cantonment, dates from the late nineteenth or early twentieth century and replaced earlier, simpler arrangements. From its gallery a visitor looks out over the harbour mouth, the long line of the Karachi waterfront, and the open Arabian Sea where ships queue for a pilot. The tower sits within a short walk of Manora Fort, an old Hindu temple, a colonial church, and the historic observatory buildings, making the headland a compact concentration of the city's maritime past.

## Visiting Manora as a Day Trip

Reaching Manora from central Karachi is itself part of the appeal, and the island makes an easy and rewarding day trip. The traditional route is the passenger ferry that crosses from the Kemari boat basin near the Karachi Port Trust, a short, cheap, and atmospheric voyage among fishing launches and harbour craft. By road, the peninsula is connected to the mainland via the Sandspit causeway, the same narrow neck of sand and mangrove that separates the harbour from the open sea. Once ashore, the light, the fort, the beach, and the old buildings are all within walking distance. The best time to go is the cooler, drier season from November to February; the summer months bring fierce heat and the monsoon swell, and visitors should treat the lighthouse compound itself as a working naval and port installation where access to the tower may be restricted.

## The Approaches to Karachi Port

Karachi Harbour is the reason the Manora light exists, and understanding the port explains the coast. The natural harbour lies behind the Manora breakwater, with a dredged channel leading vessels in from the open sea past Oyster Rocks and the sandspits. The approach is marked not only by the Manora tower but by a working system of channel buoys, leading lights, and minor beacons that guide deep-draught container ships and tankers through water that the Indus and local currents are forever trying to silt up. The Karachi Port Trust maintains this marking, and the modern reality is that a navigator relies far more on these buoys, ranges, and electronic aids than on any single dramatic tower. The harbour entrance is busy day and night, and the interplay of the historic light and the contemporary navigation system is the living heritage of the place.

## Port Qasim and the Indus Delta

East of Karachi lies Port Qasim, Pakistan's second major seaport, built from the 1970s on a creek of the Indus delta to relieve pressure on Karachi Harbour. Its long, winding approach channel — the Phitti Creek and its continuation — threads through tidal flats and mangrove islands that shift with every season. This is a coast almost without conventional lighthouses, because there is little solid rock or headland to build on; instead, navigation depends on lit buoys, leading marks, and channel beacons maintained for the steady traffic of bulk carriers and tankers. Beyond the port the Indus delta proper spreads across a vast fan of creeks, mudbanks, and disappearing channels, one of the largest arid-zone deltas in the world. Its mariners have always relied on local knowledge as much as on charted lights, for the seabed here is never the same from one year to the next.

## The Southwest Monsoon

No account of this coast makes sense without the monsoon. From roughly June to September the southwest monsoon drives moisture-laden winds and a heavy swell straight onto the Arabian Sea shore of Pakistan, raising surf, reducing visibility, and making the open coast dangerous for small craft. Fishing fleets from Karachi, Ibrahim Hyderi, and the Balochistan ports time their seasons around it, and the calmer months from late autumn to spring are when the sea is most workable. For the lights and beacons of the coast, the monsoon is the great test: salt, spray, and humidity attack every metal fitting, and the value of a reliable beam is never clearer than on a grey monsoon night when the Manora light cuts through the haze at the harbour mouth.

## Cape Monze and Mubarak Village

West of Karachi the shore turns wild within an hour's drive. Cape Monze — known locally as Ras Muari — is a bold rocky headland of pale cliffs marking the point where the city's developed coast gives way to the open Arabian Sea. It is the most prominent natural seamark on the approach to Karachi from the west and carries a navigation light to warn vessels off its rocks. Nearby Mubarak Village, a working fishing settlement of brightly painted boats at the foot of the headland, is the jumping-off point for the cliffs, the small islands offshore, and some of the cleanest water near the city. The drive out past Hawke's Bay and Sandspit to Cape Monze is a favourite escape for Karachi residents, and the headland's light and the harbour's tower together frame the entrance to the metropolitan coast.

## The Makran Coast: Ormara, Pasni, Gwadar and Jiwani

Beyond Cape Monze the Makran coast of Balochistan runs westward for hundreds of kilometres in a sequence of arid headlands, fishing harbours, and astonishing geology. The principal points are Ormara, with its great flat-topped hammerhead peninsula; Pasni, a fishing and minor port town; Gwadar, whose twin-bayed peninsula and deep natural harbour have made it Pakistan's strategic deep-water port; and Jiwani, the last town before the Iranian border, with a sheltered bay long valued by mariners. Each of these ports needs its harbour lights and approach beacons rather than tall heritage towers, for the Makran coast was historically remote and lightly settled. The fishing communities here have navigated by headland, star, and hard-won local knowledge for generations, and the modern lights at Gwadar's expanding port mark a new chapter in a very old seafaring tradition.

## The Hingol Coast and Its Strange Geology

Midway along the Makran shore, the Hingol region offers some of the most dramatic coastal scenery in Pakistan, much of it within Hingol National Park. Here the desert meets the sea in carved cliffs, the wind-sculpted rock formations of the Buzi Pass, and — most remarkably — active mud volcanoes that bubble and occasionally erupt cones of grey mud near the shore. The coastal highway that now links Karachi to Gwadar runs through this country, opening a once-inaccessible coast to travellers. For the navigator offshore, this is a long stretch of bold, unlit, or lightly marked coast where the natural landmarks of the Hingol cliffs and the Kund Malir bay serve as seamarks in their own right, and where the discipline of careful coastal piloting still matters as much as any beacon.
