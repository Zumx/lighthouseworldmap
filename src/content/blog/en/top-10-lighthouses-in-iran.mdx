Few maritime nations are split between two seas as different as Iran's. To the south lies the Persian Gulf and the Gulf of Oman, shallow, scorching, and among the most heavily trafficked waters on the planet, where a third of the world's seaborne oil passes through the narrow Strait of Hormuz. To the north lies the Caspian, the largest enclosed body of water on earth — a sea by courtesy only, with no connection to any ocean, no real tide, and a surface that lies far below global sea level. Iran's lighthouses serve both, and the contrast between them is the key to understanding the country's navigation.

## Two Seas, One Country

The geography could hardly be more divided. The Zagros and Alborz mountain ranges separate the two coasts by the whole width of the Iranian plateau, and the seas themselves answer to entirely different rules. The Persian Gulf is salt, hot, and tidal, a marginal sea of the Indian Ocean rimmed by the oil states of Arabia and Iran. The Caspian is brackish, landlocked, and fed chiefly by the Volga from the north; its level has risen and fallen by metres over the centuries, drowning and exposing shorelines in turn. A lighthouse keeper on the Gulf and one on the Caspian work in different worlds, and the two coasts have developed largely separate maritime traditions.

## The Strait of Hormuz and the Southern Approaches

The Strait of Hormuz is the most strategically important waterway in the world, the only sea route in or out of the Persian Gulf, and at its narrowest only about 33 kilometres wide, with the navigable channels narrower still. Through it passes the oil of Iran, Iraq, Kuwait, Saudi Arabia, Qatar, Bahrain, and the Emirates. The Iranian shore here is mountainous and indented, with islands such as Qeshm — the largest island in the Gulf — Hormuz, and Larak commanding the strait. The navigation aids that guide tanker traffic through this bottleneck carry an importance out of all proportion to their modest size, marking a channel where a single mishap can disrupt a significant share of global energy supply.

## Bandar Abbas and the Gulf Ports

Bandar Abbas, on the Strait of Hormuz, is Iran's principal southern port and the headquarters of much of its maritime activity. The approaches to Bandar Abbas and the nearby island of Qeshm are marked for the heavy traffic of tankers, container ships, and naval vessels that use these waters. Further up the Gulf lie the great oil terminals — Kharg Island, the largest crude-export facility, and Bandar-e Mahshahr near the head of the Gulf — each demanding reliable marking of approaches that are often shallow and complicated by offshore structures. The lights of the Iranian Gulf coast serve one of the busiest and most consequential industrial seaways on earth.

## Farur and the Gulf Islands

The Iranian Gulf is studded with islands, and several carry important lights. Farur (فارور), a small island in the central Gulf, marks a position on the routes between the Strait of Hormuz and the upper Gulf ports. The Gulf islands range from the strategically famous — Abu Musa and the Tunbs, long disputed — to quiet outposts whose chief function is to carry a navigation light. Many Iranian Gulf beacons are described simply by the generic فانوس دریایی, "sea light," reflecting a network built more for function than for monument. In a sea this shallow and this busy, every island light helps thread tankers between hazards.

## The Gulf of Oman and the Outer Coast

East of the Strait of Hormuz, beyond the choke point, the Iranian coast opens onto the Gulf of Oman, a deeper and more open water that connects to the Arabian Sea and the Indian Ocean. The port of Chabahar, on this outer coast, is Iran's only ocean port outside the Gulf, free of the bottleneck at Hormuz, and it has been developed as a strategic gateway to the wider Indian Ocean. The lights of this stretch mark a coast that is more exposed and oceanic than the sheltered Gulf, and they guide the traffic that prefers, or requires, an approach that does not depend on passing through the strait.

## The Caspian: A Sea Apart

Turn north, and everything changes. The Iranian Caspian coast runs along the foot of the Alborz mountains through the green, humid provinces of Gilan and Mazandaran — a landscape of rice paddies, tea gardens, and forest utterly unlike the arid Gulf shore. The Caspian has no tide worth the name and no link to any ocean, but it is a real working sea, carrying trade between Iran, Russia, Azerbaijan, Kazakhstan, and Turkmenistan. Its level has fluctuated markedly, which complicates harbour engineering and the siting of lights. The ports of Anzali, Nowshahr, and Amirabad anchor Iranian shipping on this enclosed sea.

## Anzali, Nowshahr, and the Caspian Ports

Bandar-e Anzali, with its lagoon and its long association with Russian trade, is the historic heart of Iranian Caspian shipping. Nowshahr, further east, is marked at its harbour entrance by the light on the east mole (Nowshahr east mole), the kind of pier-head beacon that guides vessels through a narrow artificial entrance. At Amirabad, the harbour control tower (برج مراقبت بندر امیرآباد) oversees a modern port developed to handle the growing Caspian trade. Lights such as Hasan Bekandeh mark points along this northern coast. Caspian harbour engineering must contend with the sea's changing level and with the silt carried down from the mountains, and the lights here serve a trade that looks north to Russia and Central Asia rather than south to the oceans.

## Two Maritime Worlds

To travel Iran's coasts is to experience two maritime worlds that happen to belong to one country. The Gulf is hot, salt, oil-driven, and globally strategic, its lights threading supertankers through the world's most important strait. The Caspian is cool, green-backed, enclosed, and oriented toward its inland neighbours, its lights marking harbours on a sea that touches no ocean. The mountains of the plateau keep the two apart, and their beacons reflect the difference at every turn — in climate, in traffic, in the very nature of the water they mark. Together they make Iran one of the most geographically divided maritime nations in the world.
