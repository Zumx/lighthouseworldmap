## The Logic of Small Lights

The mental image of a lighthouse is almost always a tall one: a soaring masonry column, a lantern balanced on a rock-stack above crashing surf. But the vast majority of the world's lighthouse structures are short, modest, and unassuming. A harbour entrance a few miles inside a breakwater does not need its light visible for twenty miles offshore; it needs it reliable, clearly sited, and readable from a moving deck at close range. A river bend needs a light that picks out the safe channel, not one that dazzles pilots miles away. Shortness in a lighthouse is rarely a deficiency; it is almost always an engineering choice matched precisely to the task.

Understanding the smallest and shortest lighthouses also means understanding how lighthouse authorities classified structures. In the United States system, a first-order light ran a massive first-order Fresnel lens in a tower designed to be seen from twenty miles or more, often on exposed headlands. A sixth-order light, by contrast, used a tiny lens barely six inches in diameter, adequate for a range of five to seven miles, and sat in a cast-iron lantern on a post or a stubby concrete column. Many of these sixth-order structures, built in their thousands along American rivers, lake shores, and harbour approaches from the 1850s onward, stand between three and six metres tall — barely taller than a garden gate.

## Latimer Reef Light, New York

One of the most photographed small lighthouses in North America is Latimer Reef Light, constructed in 1884 on a shoal between Fishers Island and the Connecticut shore at the eastern end of Long Island Sound. The structure is a screwpile-and-caisson type: a cast-iron cylinder was driven into the seabed and the lighthouse erected on top of it. Total height from water level to the top of the lantern is about 13 metres, but the focal plane of the light is only about 10 metres above the sea, and the tower itself — a squat white cylinder with a red iron lantern — stands barely 7 metres above its foundation deck.

Latimer Reef's fog signal and occulting green light (Oc G 6s) mark the reef that runs south-west from Fishers Island. It was automated in 1983 and remains active, tended by the Coast Guard.

## Pemaquid Point and Burned Island, Maine

Maine's Burned Island Light, positioned at the entrance to Boothbay Harbour, is often cited as one of the shortest traditional lighthouse towers on the Maine coast. Built in 1821 and rebuilt in 1857, the white rubblestone tower stands roughly 7 metres tall. Its low height was not a limitation: the site elevation on the island promontory gave the focal plane of the light sufficient height above the water to be seen from the required distance. The keeper's house, unusually, was attached directly to the tower, forming a single compact structure that remained in use until 1988.

This design logic — using the natural elevation of the site rather than tower height to achieve the required focal plane — accounts for a large proportion of the world's shortest lighthouse towers. Rocky coastal headlands, cliff edges, and raised promontories allow a tower of six or eight metres to display a light at a focal plane thirty or forty metres above the sea.

## Tom Point and Low Island Lights, Great Lakes

The American Great Lakes contain a remarkable collection of small lighthouse structures because lake traffic, while heavy, operates in confined and well-charted waters where visibility requirements differ from ocean coasts. Tom Point Light on Lake Michigan, a cast-iron post light with a square lantern barely visible above the treeline, is typical: the entire structure from base to ventilator ball stands around 5 metres. The lights that marked the mouths of river tributaries in Michigan, Wisconsin, and Ohio were often even simpler — a square wooden post with a small lens lantern at the top, looking from a distance more like a traffic signal than a lighthouse.

Several of these minor lake lights were demolished when their stations were discontinued, but a number survive in good repair as aids to navigation or as part of local maritime heritage collections.

## Mukae-jima, Japan

The Japanese coast is home to some of the world's most numerous lighthouse structures, maintained by the Japan Coast Guard. Among the smallest is the post light on Mukae-jima, a tiny uninhabited island in the Seto Inland Sea, where a concrete pillar supporting a small LED lantern stands barely four metres above the rock it crowns. The functional simplicity of structures like this one is in sharp aesthetic contrast to the more monumental lights that Japan constructed from the 1870s onward under the influence of British engineer Richard Henry Brunton, who designed more than thirty of the country's major lighthouse towers.

## Newburyport Harbor Range Lights, Massachusetts

Range lights — pairs of lights positioned one behind the other to mark a leading line into harbour — are among the shortest lighthouse structures on record, because the crucial variable is not height but the vertical and horizontal separation between the front and rear light. Newburyport Harbor on the Merrimack River in Massachusetts has a front range light that stands barely 5 metres above its foundation, a simple white daymark tower with a fixed white light. The rear light, set back on higher ground, brings the pair's angular geometry to bear on guiding vessels through the shallow bar at the river mouth.

Range lights across the world — on the St Lawrence, the Gironde, the Elbe, the Mississippi delta, and dozens of Baltic and North Sea approaches — follow this logic, making precise horizontal and vertical positioning more important than physical height.

## Havre de Grace Lighthouse, Maryland

At the northern tip of the Chesapeake Bay, the Concord Point Lighthouse at Havre de Grace, completed in 1827, stands just 9.5 metres tall. It is one of the oldest lighthouses in Maryland still in its original form. The tower was built of granite blocks and lit by an oil lamp under a fixed sixth-order Fresnel lens; the keeper's house stands separately a few metres away. Its modest height was entirely appropriate to its task: marking the mouth of the Susquehanna River for the shallow-draft vessels that used the upper Chesapeake trade.

The lighthouse was automated in 1975 and is now owned by the City of Havre de Grace, which opens it to the public on weekend afternoons from May through October.

## The Smallest Physical Structures

When lighthouse authorities in the late nineteenth century needed to place a light on a shoal that barely breached the surface, they sometimes used a structure that consisted of little more than an iron post, a small lantern, and a fog bell. The Craighill Channel Lower Range Front Light in Maryland, built in 1873, takes the form of a caisson-mounted screwpile tower barely 12 metres tall including its lantern. The Cove Island Light in Canada's Georgian Bay, erected in 1858 and listed at 18 metres, is a relative giant compared with some of its lake contemporaries.

Several nineteenth-century American harbour lights were recorded as having overall heights below 4 metres — essentially a lantern mounted on a post cemented into a concrete block, with no accommodation or ancillary structures at all.

## Why Small Lighthouses Matter

The smallest lighthouses are often the ones in most danger of being demolished. Their scale makes them seem insignificant, unworthy of the preservation investment given to the grand rock towers or the tall Cape lights. Yet the community and harbour contexts of small lighthouse structures frequently carry as much historical weight as their more photogenic relatives. The Concord Point tower marked the Susquehanna trade. The Burned Island light guided fishing boats home to Boothbay. The tiny Seto Inland Sea post light keeps ferries and fishing vessels off the rocks of Mukae-jima every night.
