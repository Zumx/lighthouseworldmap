Virginia's coast wraps around the broad mouth of the Chesapeake Bay, the largest estuary in the United States, where the open Atlantic gives way to a maze of shoals, reefs, and river entrances. To mark this soft-bottomed, ship-snaring bay, engineers turned to two ingenious designs: the screwpile light, a cottage perched on iron legs screwed into the mud, and the caisson light, a cast iron cylinder sunk and filled with concrete to stand against ice and storm. Alongside these sit the great Atlantic sentinels of Cape Henry and Assateague. Together they tell the story of how a nation learned to light its busiest waters. Nowhere else in America did the screwpile cottage flourish as it did on the Chesapeake, and nowhere were so many lost to the grinding winter ice that gave the caisson its eventual victory.

## 1. Old Cape Henry Lighthouse

At the southern mouth of the Chesapeake on the grounds of Fort Story in Virginia Beach stands the Old Cape Henry Lighthouse, completed in 1792. It was the first lighthouse authorised and funded by the new United States federal government, the inaugural public works project of the young republic. The octagonal sandstone tower rises about 27 metres (90 feet). Cracks in the masonry retired it in 1881, but it survives as a National Historic Landmark, open for climbing with sweeping views over the bay entrance.

## 2. New Cape Henry Lighthouse

Just 110 metres from its predecessor, the New Cape Henry Lighthouse was lit in 1881 when fractures threatened the old tower. Built of cast iron plates in distinctive black-and-white vertical bands, it stands about 50 metres (164 feet) tall, making it one of the tallest cast iron lighthouses in the country. Still an active US Coast Guard aid to navigation, it guides traffic through the Chesapeake Bay entrance and can be climbed by visitors with access to the military base.

## 3. Old Point Comfort Lighthouse

On the grounds of Fort Monroe at the tip of the Virginia Peninsula, this small octagonal sandstone tower dates to 1802, making it among the oldest in the bay. Roughly 16 metres (54 feet) tall, it marks the entrance to Hampton Roads, the great natural harbour where the James and Elizabeth Rivers meet the bay. It served as a watchtower in the War of 1812 and remains an active aid to navigation, viewable within the historic fort grounds at Fort Monroe.

## 4. New Point Comfort Lighthouse

Not to be confused with Old Point Comfort, this 1804 octagonal stone tower stands at the tip of a Mathews County peninsula at the mouth of Mobjack Bay. About 17 metres tall, it is the third-oldest lighthouse on the Chesapeake. Erosion has left it stranded on a tiny islet, cut off from the mainland, and it was decommissioned in 1963. Restored and stabilised, it is best seen by boat or from the New Point Comfort Natural Area Preserve observation platform.

## 5. Assateague Lighthouse

On the Atlantic side of Virginia's Eastern Shore, near Chincoteague, the Assateague Lighthouse was first lit in 1867. Its bold red-and-white horizontal bands make it one of the most recognisable lights on the coast. The brick tower rises about 43 metres (142 feet) and still shows an active light over the dangerous shoals offshore. Set within the Chincoteague National Wildlife Refuge, it is open for climbing in season and reached by a short woodland trail through the famous pony country.

## 6. Thimble Shoal Lighthouse

Out in the open water of Hampton Roads near the Chesapeake Bay Bridge-Tunnel stands Thimble Shoal Light, a cast iron caisson tower. The present structure dates to 1914, replacing earlier screwpile lights repeatedly destroyed by fire and ship collisions. The brown cylinder, sunk into the bay floor, marks the shoal on the busy approach to Norfolk and Newport News. Now privately owned and automated, it is a familiar sight to anyone crossing the bridge-tunnel and is viewable only from the water.

## 7. Wolf Trap Lighthouse

Marking a notorious shoal off Mathews County where a British warship, HMS Wolfe, supposedly ran aground, Wolf Trap Light is a brick-and-iron caisson tower built in 1894. It replaced a screwpile light carried away by ice in the harsh winter of 1893. The octagonal brick house sits atop a cast iron caisson, about 16 metres above the water. Decommissioned and privately owned, it stands alone in the bay and is best appreciated from a boat out of Mathews or Deltaville.

## 8. Smith Point Lighthouse

Guarding the mouth of the Potomac River where it empties into the Chesapeake, Smith Point Light is a caisson tower built in 1897, the latest of several lighthouses on this exposed site dating back to 1802. The brown-and-white caisson supports a brick dwelling and lantern. Ice and storms repeatedly destroyed its predecessors, which is why the durable caisson design prevailed. Still an active Coast Guard light, it stands offshore near Reedville and is reached only by water.

## 9. Cape Charles Lighthouse

At the northern entrance to the Chesapeake on Smith Island off the Eastern Shore, the present Cape Charles Lighthouse is a tall iron skeletal tower lit in 1895. Rising about 58 metres (191 feet), it is among the tallest lighthouses in the United States. The open framework was chosen to withstand the shifting sands and storm winds of this exposed barrier island. Still active, it is remote and difficult to reach, visited mainly by boat-borne lighthouse enthusiasts.

## 10. Newport News Middle Ground Lighthouse

In the channel off Newport News in Hampton Roads, this caisson light was built in 1891 to mark a shoal in the middle of the busy harbour approach. The brown cast iron caisson supports a white dwelling and lantern, a classic spark-plug profile. It replaced lightships once moored on the spot. Privately owned and restored, it remains an active aid to navigation and a landmark for the warships and container vessels that crowd the Hampton Roads anchorage. The spark-plug name comes from its silhouette, a squat cylinder topped by a tapering gallery and lantern that genuinely resembles an automobile spark plug rising from the water. Of the dozens of screwpile cottages that once dotted the Chesapeake, almost none survive, swept away by ice floes and replaced by exactly these sturdier caissons through the 1890s and early 1900s.
