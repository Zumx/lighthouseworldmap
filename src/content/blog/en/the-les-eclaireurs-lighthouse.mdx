East of Ushuaia, where the Beagle Channel opens out towards the islands of the far south, a small conical tower of red brick banded with white stands on a low rocky islet barely larger than its own base. This is the Les Eclaireurs Lighthouse, almost universally called el faro del fin del mundo — the lighthouse at the end of the world. The name is poetic and misleading in equal measure, and the truth behind it is one of the more interesting tangles in lighthouse history, involving a French novelist, a different lighthouse altogether, and one of the remotest navigable waterways on the planet.

## The Channel and Its Dangers

The Beagle Channel runs east to west between the main island of Tierra del Fuego and the chain of islands to its south, providing a sheltered alternative to the open passage around Cape Horn. For ships approaching Ushuaia from the Atlantic, the eastern reaches of the channel are studded with rocks and small islands, and the approach to the port required marking. The Les Eclaireurs group is a cluster of islets roughly five nautical miles east-north-east of Ushuaia, and the largest of them carries the lighthouse.

The waters here are cold, the weather changeable, and the wind frequently strong. The channel takes its name from HMS Beagle, the survey ship that charted these waters in the 1830s with the young Charles Darwin aboard. The hydrography Darwin's shipmates produced underpinned the navigation of the channel for generations, and the eventual placement of lights like Les Eclaireurs followed the dangers their charts had identified.

## The Tower Itself

The Les Eclaireurs Lighthouse was built and first lit in 1920. It is a modest structure: a cylindrical tower of red brick around eleven metres tall, with a single white band, standing on the bare rock of the islet with no land around it. It was never a manned station in the romantic sense — it has always been automated, its light maintained by the Argentine naval hydrographic service on periodic visits rather than by a resident keeper. Its light flashes white at intervals, marking the eastern approach to Ushuaia for the vessels that now include a steady traffic of Antarctic cruise ships.

The setting does the work that the modest architecture cannot. The tower stands against a backdrop of the snow-streaked mountains of Tierra del Fuego, with cormorants and sea lions sharing its rock, and the cold green water of the channel all around. It is one of the most photographed lighthouses in South America precisely because of where it stands, not because of what it is.

## The Legend at the End of the World

The phrase lighthouse at the end of the world comes from Jules Verne's posthumous novel of that name, published in 1905. But Verne did not write about Les Eclaireurs — his novel was inspired by a different and far more remote light, the Faro de San Juan de Salvamento on Isla de los Estados (Staten Island), well to the east at the very tip of the Argentine archipelago. That lighthouse, built in 1884, was a genuinely isolated and dangerous posting, and it is the true original of Verne's tale.

Over the decades the literary fame attached itself to the more accessible and more photogenic Les Eclaireurs, which sits within easy reach of Ushuaia's tourist boats, while the real San Juan de Salvamento fell into ruin on its distant island. A replica of the San Juan de Salvamento light was eventually built and the original site recognised, but in popular imagination the title belongs to the little red tower in the Beagle Channel. It is a rare case of a legend migrating from one lighthouse to another for reasons of pure convenience.

## A Coast of Faros

Argentina's coastline is one of the longest in the southern hemisphere, and its lighthouses — faros — march down it from the temperate north to the sub-Antarctic south. In the far south, near Les Eclaireurs, the Faro Le Maire and the Faro San Diego mark the strait of Le Maire and the approaches to Isla de los Estados, while the Faro Cabo San Pio stands at the south-eastern extremity of the Tierra del Fuego main island. The Cabo San Pio light marks one of the loneliest points on the whole Argentine coast.

Moving north along the Atlantic shore, the Faro Cabo Domingo and the Faro Península Páramo guard the approaches to Bahía San Sebastián on the Tierra del Fuego coast, and the Faro Cabo Espíritu Santo marks the northern entrance to the Strait of Magellan. On the Patagonian mainland the Faro Cabo Blanco, the Faro San Jorge, and the Faro Isla Pingüino mark the long, windswept coast of Santa Cruz and Chubut, where the desert runs almost to the water's edge.

## The Patagonian Lights

Further north still, the rhythm of the lights follows the great bays and river mouths of Patagonia. The Faro Isla Leones stands at the entrance to the Golfo San Jorge, and the Faro Punta Delgada marks the dramatic cliffs of the Península Valdés, the peninsula famous for its southern right whales and elephant seals. The Faro de Escollera Sur at Puerto Rawson and the Faro Río Negro guide vessels into the river ports where Patagonia's rivers meet the Atlantic.

The Faro Recalada near Bahía Blanca and the Faro Claromecó on the Buenos Aires province coast mark the approaches to the busier waters of the temperate north, where the long beaches and shallow approaches demanded landfall lights of considerable range. Each of these lights belongs to a national system built up over more than a century, much of it by the same naval hydrographic service that still maintains Les Eclaireurs today.

## Seeing Les Eclaireurs

For the visitor, Les Eclaireurs is the easiest of all these lights to reach. The catamarans that leave Ushuaia's waterfront for half-day tours of the Beagle Channel make the lighthouse their turning point, circling the islet so that passengers can photograph the tower against the mountains. The boats also pass the sea lion colony on Isla de los Lobos and the cormorant rookeries on the nearby rocks, so the lighthouse is the centrepiece of a wider wildlife excursion.

Those who want the true end of the world must travel much further, to Isla de los Estados, reachable only by expedition vessels and rarely visited. But for most travellers the little red tower in the channel, with the Fuegian mountains behind it and the cold light of the far south on the water, is end-of-the-world enough. It is a place where the geography genuinely lives up to the legend, even if the legend originally belonged elsewhere.
