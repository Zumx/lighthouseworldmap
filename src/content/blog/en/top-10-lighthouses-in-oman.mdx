Few coastlines work as hard as Oman's. The country wraps around the southeastern corner of the Arabian Peninsula for roughly 3,165 kilometres, and along that arc it controls the southern shore of the Strait of Hormuz, the single busiest oil passage on earth, before sweeping down past Muscat and the long, empty Arabian Sea coast toward the monsoon-soaked hills of Dhofar. The lights of Oman are scattered across this huge distance, from the sheer cliffs of the Musandam fjords to the coral-fringed islands off Muscat and the surf-battered headlands near Salalah. There are no Victorian masonry giants here; Oman's beacons are mostly modern steel and concrete towers, but they stand in some of the most spectacular and strategically vital seascapes anywhere.

## The Strait of Hormuz and Musandam

The northern tip of Oman, the Musandam Peninsula, is geographically separated from the rest of the country by the United Arab Emirates, and it juts directly into the Strait of Hormuz. Here the land falls in vertical limestone walls into deep blue fjords, the so-called Khors, and every tanker bound for or leaving the Gulf passes within sight of these cliffs. Navigation lights mark the headlands around Khasab, the regional capital, and the islands that stud the strait. The light on the cliffs near Kumzar and the beacons guarding the approaches to Khasab port are working aids in one of the most closely watched waterways in the world. From a dhow cruise through the Khor ash Sham you can see how essential these markers are amid the rock and the relentless shipping traffic.

## Muscat and Mutrah Harbour

The capital, Muscat, sits in a series of rocky coves backed by the Hajar Mountains, and its old harbour at Mutrah is one of the most atmospheric in Arabia. The entrance is guarded by Portuguese-era forts, Jalali and Mirani, and modern navigation lights mark the breakwaters and the cruise terminal that now receives large ships. The headlands east and west of the city carry beacons that guide vessels along an otherwise harbourless stretch of coast. Standing on the Mutrah Corniche at dusk, you watch the channel lights blink as fishing boats and naval vessels move in the dark water below the floodlit forts.

## Ras al Hadd and the Turtle Coast

Where the Gulf of Oman meets the Arabian Sea, the coast bends sharply at Ras al Hadd, the easternmost point of the Arabian Peninsula. This headland is famous for the green turtles that nest on its beaches at Ras al Jinz, but it is also a critical navigation corner. A light here marks the turn that ships make as they round the cape from the strait into the open Indian Ocean. The low, sandy point is unremarkable to look at, but its position makes it one of the most important beacons on the whole Omani coast.

## The Daymaniyat Islands

Offshore from the Batinah coast northwest of Muscat lies the Daymaniyat archipelago, a chain of nine small islands that form Oman's only marine nature reserve. The reefs around them are a hazard to navigation and a magnet for divers, and minor lights mark the group for the dhows and dive boats that work these waters. The islands are protected, with access controlled during the seabird nesting and turtle seasons, so the beacons here serve a quiet, careful traffic rather than heavy commercial shipping.

## Sur and the Shipbuilding Coast

The old town of Sur, on the coast south of Ras al Hadd, was once one of the great dhow-building and trading ports of the western Indian Ocean, with links running to Zanzibar and India. Its lagoon entrance and the headland at Ayjah are marked by lights, and a slender white tower near the Ayjah watchtowers overlooks the channel where the wooden ghanjah dhows were once launched. Sur remains a working fishing and boatbuilding town, and the lights guide the modern fleet in and out past the sandbars at the lagoon mouth.

## Masirah Island

Far down the Arabian Sea coast lies Masirah, a long, flat desert island separated from the mainland by a shallow channel. It is exposed to the full force of the summer monsoon, the Khareef winds that whip the sea into a fury from June to September, and its shores are littered with shipwrecks. Lights mark the ferry channel from Shannah on the mainland and the approaches around the island. Masirah is one of the loneliest and most weather-beaten lit coasts in the region, a place where the sea genuinely dictates the rhythm of life.

## Salalah and the Dhofar Coast

In the far south, the city of Salalah anchors the Dhofar region, a place transformed each summer when the Indian monsoon brushes the hills and turns them green. Salalah's deep-water port is now one of the largest container transshipment hubs in the world, and its breakwaters and approach channels are heavily lit with modern beacons. West toward the Yemeni border the coast grows wild again, with the dramatic blowholes at Mughsail and headlands marked by lights that guide ships along the edge of the Arabian Sea. The contrast between the industrial port lights and the empty monsoon coast is striking.

## Modern Beacons in an Ancient Sea

Oman's lights, including the headland beacon known on regional charts simply as أخضر, the green light, are nearly all modern. The reason is partly historical: while Oman was a great maritime power in the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries, ruling an empire that stretched to Zanzibar, its navigation aids were rudimentary, relying on local knowledge, fort fires and the skill of dhow captains who read the stars and the coast. The systematic lighting of the coast came late, much of it built during the rapid modernisation that followed 1970. Today solar-powered LED towers do the work that masonry lighthouses never had the chance to do here.

## Visiting the Omani Coast

For the traveller, Oman rewards a coast-following journey. Fly into Muscat, walk the Mutrah Corniche, then drive the dramatic road to Sur and on to the turtle beaches at Ras al Jinz. A separate trip north to Musandam, reached through the UAE, gives you the fjords and the strait. The far south, around Salalah, is best in the Khareef season from July to September, when the monsoon greens the hills, though the sea is too rough for boating then. Elsewhere the cool months from October to April are ideal. Throughout, the lights are modest and modern, but they stand in landscapes of rare drama.
