Algeria's Mediterranean coast runs for some 1,200 kilometres along the northern edge of Africa, from the Moroccan border in the west to Tunisia in the east. It is a coast of bold capes and deep bays, of the Tell Atlas mountains crowding close to the sea, of ancient Phoenician and Roman ports overlaid by centuries of Ottoman and then French rule. Most of Algeria's lighthouses were built during the French colonial period of the nineteenth and early twentieth centuries, when the maritime authorities established a comprehensive system of lights to serve the busy traffic between France and its North African territories. These towers, many still in service, mark the headlands of one of the most dramatic shores of the Mediterranean.

## 1. Cap Caxine, Algiers

The lighthouse at Cap Caxine, west of the capital, is among the most important on the Algerian coast, marking the approaches to Algiers, the principal port of the country. The headland rises from the sea west of the city, and the powerful light guides the heavy commercial and ferry traffic that connects Algiers with Marseille and the wider Mediterranean. The setting looks out over the Bay of Algiers toward the white-walled casbah climbing its hillside, the historic heart of the city and a UNESCO World Heritage site whose maze of Ottoman houses and mosques tumbles down to the harbour.

## 2. Phare de Cap de Garde, Annaba

At Cap de Garde, the headland north of Annaba in the far east of Algeria, a lighthouse marks the approach to one of the country's major eastern ports. Annaba, the ancient Hippo Regius where Saint Augustine served as bishop, sits on a bay backed by the green mountains of the Edough massif. The cape's light guides the iron-ore and phosphate carriers that load at Annaba, a centre of Algerian heavy industry. The headland offers sweeping views over the Gulf of Annaba, and the surrounding coast, with its pine-clad slopes and coves, is among the most attractive in the east.

## 3. Phare de Cap Carbon, Béjaïa

The Phare de Cap Carbon, near Béjaïa in Kabylia, occupies one of the most spectacular lighthouse sites in the Mediterranean. The cape rises in sheer limestone cliffs above the sea, and the light stands at the top of a natural rock arch, reached by a path along the clifftop. Below, the deep blue water of the Gulf of Béjaïa stretches toward the city and the slopes of Mount Gouraya, a national park. Cap Carbon is claimed by some to be among the highest natural lighthouse sites on the Mediterranean, and the approach along the cliffs, with the sea far below, is unforgettable.

## 4. Phare de Cap Ténès, Ténès

The lighthouse at Cap Ténès, west of Algiers on the Chlef coast, marks a prominent headland on the central Algerian shore. Ténès is an ancient port, founded by Phoenician traders and later a Roman settlement, and the cape has guided mariners along this coast for millennia. The French-era light stands on the rocky point above the modern fishing harbour, guiding the coastal traffic and the fishing fleet. The old town of Ténès, with its remnants of successive civilisations, sits behind the cape, and the coast here alternates between rocky headland and sandy bay.

## 5. Phare de Cherchell, Cherchell

Cherchell, west of Algiers, was the ancient Caesarea, capital of the Roman client kingdom of Mauretania and one of the most important cities of Roman North Africa. The lighthouse marks the approach to this historic harbour, which still functions as a fishing port. The town preserves remarkable Roman remains — a theatre, baths, and a celebrated archaeological museum holding mosaics and statuary from the ancient city. The light stands above a coast where the layers of Phoenician, Roman, Ottoman and French history are unusually visible, and the fishing boats work the same waters that served Caesarea two thousand years ago.

## 6. Phare de Sidi Fredj, Algiers

The lighthouse at Sidi Fredj, on the peninsula west of Algiers, marks a headland of considerable historical significance: it was here, in 1830, that the French expeditionary force landed to begin the conquest of Algeria. Today the peninsula is a resort and marina, and the light guides the pleasure craft and coastal traffic of the Algiers region. The setting combines the historical weight of the landing site with the modern leisure development that has grown around the sheltered bay, an easy excursion from the capital along the western coast.

## 7. Phare de Tipaza, Tipaza

At Tipaza, west of Algiers, the lighthouse stands beside one of the great archaeological sites of the Mediterranean. The Roman ruins of Tipasa, a UNESCO World Heritage site, spread along the shore: a forum, basilicas, a theatre, and an extensive necropolis, all set among pines above the blue sea. The light marks the approach to the small harbour and the rocky coast where the ancient town met the water. The combination of the lighthouse, the Roman ruins on their headland, and the backdrop of the Chenoua mountain makes Tipaza one of the most evocative coastal sites in all of Algeria.

## 8. Phare de Cap Falcon, Oran

Cap Falcon, the headland west of Oran, carries a lighthouse marking the approach to Algeria's second city and its great western port. Oran, the city of rai music and a long Spanish and French heritage, sits on a bay overlooked by the fortress of Santa Cruz. The cape's light guides the ferry and commercial traffic between Oran and Spain, just across the western Mediterranean. The coast around Cap Falcon, with its beaches and the resort of Aïn El Turk, is among the most popular in western Algeria, and the headland offers fine views back toward the city and its fortress-crowned mountain.

## 9. Phare de l'île Habibas, Habibas Islands

The Habibas Islands, a small volcanic archipelago off the western Algerian coast near Oran, carry a lighthouse marking these isolated rocks. The islands are a protected marine reserve, home to seabird colonies and one of the last Mediterranean refuges of the monk seal, and their waters are renowned for their clarity and marine life. The French-era light stands on the main island, guiding vessels along this offshore hazard. The Habibas are reached only by boat and remain among the wildest and least visited places on the Algerian coast, a fragment of unspoiled Mediterranean nature.

## 10. Phare de Cap Bengut, Dellys

The Phare de Cap Bengut, near the old town of Dellys in Kabylia east of Algiers, marks a headland on the coast where the mountains of Greater Kabylia reach the sea. Dellys is an ancient port with Ottoman walls and a long maritime tradition, and the cape's light guides the fishing fleet and coastal traffic. The coast here is rugged and green, with the Djurdjura mountains rising inland, and the town's old quarter, damaged in the 2003 earthquake, preserves the character of an Ottoman-era harbour. The light stands at one of the most scenic points of the Kabylian shore.
