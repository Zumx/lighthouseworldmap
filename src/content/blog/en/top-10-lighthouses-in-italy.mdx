Italy wraps around the central Mediterranean in a long peninsula flanked by the Tyrrhenian Sea, the Adriatic, and in the south the Ionian, with major islands — Sicily, Sardinia, Elba, Capri — adding further hundreds of kilometres of coastline to navigate. For centuries the maritime states of Genoa, Venice, and later unified Italy invested heavily in the infrastructure of navigation, and the lighthouse tradition here is correspondingly old and architecturally rich. The Ministero delle Infrastrutture e dei Trasporti, through the Marina Militare, maintains the modern network of aides to navigation, but many of the towers themselves carry histories stretching back to the medieval commune period or beyond. From La Lanterna of Genoa, one of the oldest working lighthouses in the world, to the cliff-top drama of Capri's Punta Carena, Italian lighthouses combine maritime function with some of the most spectacular settings in Europe. ## 1. Faro di Genova (La Lanterna), Liguria

La Lanterna is one of the oldest active lighthouses in the world, with a documented history stretching to 1128 when records first mention a fire tower at the entrance to Genoa's harbour. The present structure was rebuilt in its current form in 1543 after the original tower was damaged during the wars of the Italian city-states. It stands 77 metres tall — two square masonry stages tapering upward, painted in distinctive grey stone — and the light is now roughly 117 metres above sea level when the full elevation of the headland is included, giving a range of about 33 nautical miles. La Lanterna is a working lighthouse and a symbol of Genoa: the city's coat of arms includes the tower. A small museum at its base traces the history of the light; the access path along the old city walls is open to visitors daily.

## 2. Faro di Punta Carena, Capri, Campania

Punta Carena at the southwestern tip of Capri is one of the most powerful lights in the Tyrrhenian Sea. The lighthouse was first established in 1867 and the current white cylindrical tower stands about 30 metres tall on the cliffs above the water, with an elevation of approximately 73 metres above sea level. Its character is a white flash every three seconds with a range of around 25 nautical miles, guiding ships through the Strait of Capri and around the rocky southern tip of the island. The lighthouse is active and owned by the Marina Militare; access to the compound is restricted, but the cliff road from Anacapri descends to a small rocky beach immediately below the tower that is popular with swimmers. The view from the water looking up at the lighthouse against Capri's sheer cliffs is one of the most dramatic in the Mediterranean.

## 3. Faro della Vittoria, Trieste, Friuli Venezia Giulia

The Faro della Vittoria stands on the hill of Gretta above Trieste, built between 1923 and 1927 as a memorial to the sailors who died in the First World War. Designed by Attilio Maguolo, the tower is 68 metres tall from ground to the tip of a bronze winged victory figure that crowns the lantern — the combined structure dominates the skyline of Trieste and the northern Adriatic. The light itself flashes white every five seconds and is visible for about 30 nautical miles. The tower is both a functioning lighthouse and a war memorial; the interior contains a hall of remembrance and a chapel. The lighthouse grounds on the hill are freely accessible, and the view over the Gulf of Trieste, with Slovenia's Karst plateau behind, is exceptional. Interior visits are available on scheduled open days.

## 4. Faro di Portofino, Liguria

The lighthouse at Portofino occupies the rocky tip of the Portofino promontory, below the church of San Giorgio that has stood on the point since the twelfth century. The present white cylindrical tower was built in 1870 and stands about 14 metres tall on a platform just above the sea; its group-flashing white light is visible for about 24 nautical miles across the Ligurian Sea. The path from Portofino village to the lighthouse is one of the most celebrated coastal walks on the Italian Riviera, passing through Mediterranean maquis above a succession of rocky coves. The lighthouse itself is active and access to the tower is restricted, but the broad terrace in front of the church and lighthouse is open and commands superb views of the Riviera di Levante. The promontory is within the Portofino Marine Protected Area.

## 5. Faro di Murano, Venice, Veneto

The Faro di Murano is the lighthouse at the entrance to the Murano channel in the Venetian Lagoon, marking the approach to the island famous worldwide for its glass-making traditions. The tower is a slender white masonry structure about 36 metres tall, built in the nineteenth century to guide vessels through the shallow lagoon system north of Venice. Its light marks the narrow navigable channel between extensive mud-flat shoals that have always required careful piloting. The lighthouse is active and functional, a modest but historically resonant structure in one of Europe's most unusual maritime landscapes. Murano is easily reached by vaporetto from Venice; the lighthouse is visible from the waterfront of the island's northern shore and is best appreciated from the water rather than from land, where it stands behind harbour walls.

## 6. Faro del Gianicolo, Rome, Lazio

The Faro del Gianicolo on the Janiculum Hill above Rome is not a maritime lighthouse at all, but deserves mention as the most architecturally striking lighthouse-form structure in the country. Built in 1911 as a gift from Italian emigrants in Argentina and designed by Manfredo Manfredi, the tower stands 13 metres tall on the hilltop and projects a green light over the city — the three colours of the Italian flag are represented in the light, the tower base, and the surrounding marble. It is a working lighthouse in form but functions as a civic monument, its beam visible over Rome after dark. The Gianicolo park is open daily; the combination of the lighthouse, the equestrian statue of Garibaldi, and the panorama over Rome's rooftops makes this one of the city's finest viewpoints.

## 7. Faro di Fano, Marche

The lighthouse at Fano stands on the mole at the mouth of the old Roman harbour on the Adriatic coast of Marche. Fano was the Roman town of Fanum Fortunae, and the harbour has been in use since antiquity. The current lighthouse tower, a white cylindrical structure about 21 metres tall, was built in the nineteenth century and guides vessels along the relatively low-relief Adriatic coast where the shallow waters and offshore sandbanks create navigational hazards. Its light character is a group flash, visible for about 12 nautical miles. The lighthouse sits at the end of the northern mole protecting the harbour, accessible on foot from the old town; the surrounding area includes a beach resort that is very active during the summer season. The lighthouse is an active aid to navigation and not open to the interior.

## 8. Faro di Punta Vagno, Genoa, Liguria

Punta Vagno lighthouse sits on the western outskirts of Genoa, on a rocky point between the suburb of Albaro and the district of Sturla. Built in the mid-nineteenth century, the squat white tower rises about 14 metres above the rocky promontory; combined with the cliff elevation, its light is some 28 metres above sea level with a range of about 13 nautical miles. It marks a section of the Ligurian coast where rocky headlands alternate with small pebble beaches, and where sea fog from the Ligurian Sea can reduce visibility dramatically in autumn and spring. The lighthouse is active and within a fenced Marina Militare compound. The coastal path from Genoa's eastern suburbs passes near the promontory, and the tower is visible from the Via Aurelia coast road above.

## 9. Faro di San Venerio, Tino Island, Liguria

The tiny island of Tino, off the tip of the Portovenere peninsula near La Spezia, is one of the most restricted in Italy — the island is a military zone and access is generally permitted only on the feast day of San Venerio in September. The lighthouse, a white tower roughly 17 metres tall, stands on the island's highest point alongside the ruins of a Benedictine monastery abandoned in the Middle Ages. The light character is a white flash visible for about 16 nautical miles across the Gulf of La Spezia, one of the deepest natural harbours on the Italian coast and an important Italian Navy base. The annual pilgrimage by boat from Portovenere on 13 September is the principal opportunity for visitors to see the island's ruins and the lighthouse at close quarters.

## 10. Faro di Capo d'Anzio, Lazio

The Capo d'Anzio Lighthouse stands on the ancient harbour mole of Anzio on the Tyrrhenian coast south of Rome. Anzio was the Roman resort town of Antium, and the harbour has been in use since ancient times; it was also the site of the Allied landings in January 1944. The lighthouse tower, a white cylindrical structure about 18 metres tall, was built in the mid-nineteenth century on the end of the ancient mole and guides vessels along this relatively featureless section of coast into the small marina. Its white light, with a range of about 12 nautical miles, marks a hazard-free approach in calm conditions but serves as a key position marker for vessels on the busy Rome-Naples coastal route. The harbour area is accessible; the lighthouse is active and owned by the Marina Militare.

## Planning Your Visit

Italian lighthouses are spread across an enormous length of coastline, from Trieste in the northeast to the Strait of Sicily in the south. The best time to visit is from April to June or September to October, when summer crowds have not yet peaked or have subsided, temperatures are comfortable, and sea conditions suitable for coastal driving or boat trips. Most active Italian lighthouses are managed by the Marina Militare and are not routinely open to the public, though some — La Lanterna in Genoa, the Faro della Vittoria in Trieste — have established visitor programmes. Many towers are best seen from the water on a boat excursion, particularly on Capri and in the Ligurian coast.
