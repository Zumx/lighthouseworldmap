The Great Barrier Reef is more than two thousand kilometres of coral that turns one of the busiest shipping coasts in the world into a maze. Between the reef and the Queensland mainland runs the inner route, a sheltered but treacherous channel that ships have used since the nineteenth century to avoid the open Coral Sea. To mark it, colonial engineers raised a chain of lights that is unlike any other on earth: squat timber-framed towers clad in galvanised iron, cast-iron columns shipped in pieces from Britain, and improbable cay lighthouses standing on sandbanks barely above the tide. These ten are the ones worth knowing.

## 1. Lady Elliot Island Lighthouse, Capricornia Cays

Lady Elliot is the southernmost coral cay of the entire reef, a flat green disc of vegetated sand ringed by coral perhaps forty hectares across. Its lighthouse, first lit in 1873, is a Queensland classic: a timber frame clad in galvanised iron, painted white, low and broad against the wind. The cay had been stripped almost bare by guano miners, and the keepers spent decades planting the casuarinas that now shade the island. The light guided traffic past the dangerous southern approaches where the reef first rises from deep water. Today the cay is an eco-resort and a famous manta-ray dive site, and the restored tower remains its quiet landmark.

## 2. North Reef Lighthouse, Capricornia Cays

North Reef is the most audacious lighthouse on the reef. Completed in 1878, it stands on a tiny sand cay that barely exists at high water, and the engineers solved the problem of an unstable foundation by sinking a great iron caisson into the coral and filling it with concrete and sand to form an artificial island. The tower itself rises from this drum, with the keepers' quarters wrapped around its base. For decades the keepers lived on what was effectively a man-made islet in the open sea, resupplied by boat and exposed to every cyclone that tracked down the Capricorn coast. It is the finest surviving example of a caisson cay light in Australia.

## 3. Bustard Head Lighthouse, near Gladstone

Bustard Head holds a special place: lit in 1868, it was the first coastal lighthouse built by the newly self-governing colony of Queensland. The tower is prefabricated cast iron, its curved plates bolted together and shipped from England, standing on a headland north of the Town of 1770. Its history is also the reef's most haunted — the small cemetery near the light records the deaths and tragedies of generations of keeping families, isolated on a remote point with no road. Decommissioned and vandalised, it was rescued by volunteers and restored, and now stands as a memorial to the people who kept the coastal lights.

## 4. Cape Capricorn Lighthouse, Curtis Island

Standing on the high northern tip of Curtis Island, Cape Capricorn marks the point where the Tropic of Capricorn meets the coast and where ships turn for Gladstone and the inner route north. The original light dated from 1875; the present concrete tower replaced it in the twentieth century. From its bluff the keeper's view ran out over the southern reef and the Coral Sea, and the station guarded the busy approaches to one of Queensland's principal ports. The headland setting, high above sheer cliffs, is typical of the mainland and large-island lights as opposed to the low cays further out.

## 5. Dent Island Lighthouse, Whitsundays

In the heart of the Whitsundays, opposite the resort tower-blocks of Hamilton Island, the little Dent Island light has burned since 1879. It is a timber and iron tower of modest height, perched on a steep green island in the middle of the most beautiful cruising ground on the reef. It marks the Whitsunday Passage, the narrow run of deep water threading between dozens of continental islands where the trade winds funnel and the tides run hard. Reachable today by boat tour from Hamilton Island, it is among the most visited heritage lights on the coast.

## 6. Pine Islet Lighthouse, relocated to Mackay

Pine Islet, off the Percy Isles south of Mackay, carried a cast-iron tower first lit in 1885. Its fame rests on a single distinction: it was the last manned lighthouse in Australia to burn kerosene, its mantle lit by hand each night until automation finally came in the 1980s. When the station was abandoned, the entire tower was dismantled and re-erected on the Mackay harbour foreshore, where it was restored to working order. On special occasions its original kerosene burner is lit again, the last living flame of the old reef-keeping era.

## 7. Low Isles Lighthouse, near Port Douglas

Low Isles is a pair of tiny cays inside the reef off Port Douglas: one a sandy coral cay, the other a mangrove island. The lighthouse, a white timber-and-iron tower lit in 1878, has become one of the most photographed images of the tropical reef, surrounded by turquoise shallows and reachable on a day sailing trip from Port Douglas. Beyond its navigational role, Low Isles is famous in science as the base of a landmark 1928 British expedition that produced some of the earliest detailed studies of a living coral reef.

## 8. Cape Cleveland Lighthouse, near Townsville

Guarding the southern approach to Townsville, Cape Cleveland is a timber-framed, iron-clad tower of the true Queensland pattern, lit in 1879 on a rugged granite headland at the end of a long peninsula. The station was notoriously isolated, accessible only by sea or a punishing overland track, and its keepers endured the full force of the wet-season cyclones that batter the central coast. The light marks the entrance to Cleveland Bay and the busy port of Townsville, gateway to the central section of the reef.

## 9. Cape Moreton Lighthouse, Moreton Island

At the southern gateway to the reef coast, Cape Moreton is the oldest lighthouse in Queensland, first lit in 1857 while the region was still part of New South Wales. Unusually, it is built of local sandstone rather than iron, quarried on the island itself, and its tower is banded in red and white. It marks the northern entrance to Moreton Bay and the approaches to Brisbane, where ships leaving the bay begin the long run up the inner reef route. As the senior light of the coast, it set the pattern for everything that followed.

## 10. Grassy Hill Lighthouse, Cooktown

Far up the coast at Cooktown, where Captain Cook beached the damaged Endeavour in 1770, a small iron lighthouse stands on Grassy Hill overlooking the Endeavour River. Prefabricated and lit in 1886, it is more a harbour and landfall light than a great coastal beacon, but its position is unforgettable: from the hilltop you look out over the maze of reefs that nearly destroyed Cook, the same labyrinth he climbed the hill to study. It marks the northern reach of European reef navigation in the colonial era, close to the Torres Strait beyond.

## Visiting the Reef Lights

Reaching these lights ranges from easy to nearly impossible. Cape Moreton, Grassy Hill and the relocated Pine Islet tower are accessible by road or short ferry. Low Isles and Dent Island are standard day trips from Port Douglas and Hamilton Island. The cay lights — Lady Elliot, North Reef — and the remote headlands such as Bustard Head and Cape Cleveland require a chartered boat, a flight, or a serious expedition, and many sit within national parks or marine protected zones with access rules. Plan around the weather: the cyclone season runs from November to April, when the reef is hot, wet and dangerous, and the calmer, drier months from May to October are far better for travel. Nearly all are now automated and managed by AMSA, but their heritage value is enormous, and the restored towers reward the effort of reaching them.
