Singapore sits at the junction of two of the most heavily trafficked waterways in the world: the Strait of Malacca to the west and the Singapore Strait to the east, together forming the primary sea lane between the Indian Ocean and the South China Sea. Through these waters passes approximately one-third of the world's maritime trade by value. The navigation of these straits has required careful marking since the founding of the British settlement in 1819, and the lighthouses built under the authority of the Straits Settlements government — and later the British colonial administration — were among the most technically ambitious in Asia. The Horsburgh Lighthouse on Pedra Branca, completed in 1851, was the product of the same engineering tradition that produced the great wave-swept towers of the British Isles, applied to a granite outcrop in the open South China Sea. Singapore's lighthouse system today is maintained by the Maritime and Port Authority of Singapore (MPA), which operates one of the most efficient and technologically advanced port management systems in the world.

## 1. Raffles Lighthouse, Pulau Satumu

Raffles Lighthouse, named after Singapore's founder Sir Stamford Raffles, stands on the small island of Pulau Satumu — the southernmost point of Singapore's territorial waters — approximately 8 kilometres southwest of Sentosa. The white cylindrical masonry tower, completed in 1855 and rebuilt in its current form in 1994, stands about 29 metres tall and produces a group-flashing white light with a range of around 24 nautical miles. It marks the western approach to the Singapore Strait for vessels coming from the Strait of Malacca and is one of the most important navigation marks in the entire region. The lighthouse is maintained by the MPA and is not open to the public, though it can be seen from ferry services passing through the southern waters and from the waters off Sentosa on a clear day.

## 2. Horsburgh Lighthouse, Pedra Branca

The Horsburgh Lighthouse is the most remote and arguably the most historically significant lighthouse in Singapore's administration. It stands on the bare granite rock of Pedra Branca — 'White Rock' in Portuguese — in the South China Sea, approximately 45 kilometres east of the main island of Singapore, marking the eastern approach to the Singapore Strait. The tower, completed in 1851 after seven years of construction, is a 29-metre pink granite cylinder designed by James Thomson of the Surveyor-General's Department. It was named after Captain James Horsburgh, hydrographer to the East India Company. The rock was the subject of a 32-year territorial dispute between Singapore and Malaysia, resolved by the International Court of Justice in 2008 in Singapore's favour, making the lighthouse's story inseparable from the modern geopolitics of the region. The structure is active and automated; the light shows a flashing white character visible for about 25 nautical miles.

## 3. Sultan Shoal Lighthouse, Singapore Strait

Sultan Shoal Lighthouse, built in 1896 on a coral shoal in the western Singapore Strait, is the most ornate lighthouse in Singapore's waters. The tower is a slender white cylinder rising from an octagonal foundation building with arched colonnades, the whole structure decorated in a style that blends Victorian and Malay architectural elements. The focal plane of about 21 metres above sea level gives a light range of around 20 nautical miles for the group-flashing white character. The structure was designed by the colonial Public Works Department. Sultan Shoal is built on a concrete raft over the original shoal and the entire structure — tower and dwelling — is ringed by a decorative iron railing. The lighthouse can be seen from the western Jurong coast and from ferry services to Batam. Like the other offshore lighthouses, it is a restricted facility.

## 4. Bedok Lighthouse, Changi

The Bedok Lighthouse (sometimes called the Changi Point Lighthouse) marks the eastern approach to Changi Passage — the narrow, strongly tidal channel between Singapore's northeastern tip and the Malaysian island of Pulau Ubin. The existing automated tower stands on Changi Point, a low headland a short walk from the Changi Village hawker centre, giving it the rare distinction among Singapore's lighthouses of being easily accessible on foot from public transport. The light marks the entrance to the passage for vessels transiting between the Singapore Strait and the Johor Strait. The area is also famous for the Changi Memorial, associated with the Second World War prisoner-of-war camp.

## 5. Pulau Pisang Lighthouse, Malaysia (operated by Singapore)

Pulau Pisang — 'Banana Island' in Malay — is a small island off the southwestern tip of the Malay Peninsula in Malaysian territorial waters, but under a unique bilateral agreement the lighthouse has been operated by Singapore since 1900. The island was ceded to British Straits Settlements use, specifically for lighthouse purposes, and the agreement has continued through Malaysian independence and into the present. The white tower, rebuilt in 1914, stands 32 metres tall with a focal plane giving a light range of about 22 nautical miles. It marks the approach to the southern end of the Strait of Malacca. The treaty governing Singapore's lighthouse operation at Pulau Pisang is a remarkable example of colonial-era maritime cooperation that has outlasted the colonial period itself.

## 6. St John's Island Lighthouse

St John's Island, one of Singapore's southern islands, was the site of a quarantine station, a drug rehabilitation centre, and is now partly a recreational island accessible by ferry from Marina South Pier. The small lighthouse on the island marks the approach channel between St John's and Lazarus Island and has been active since the early colonial period. The current structure is an automated beacon. The island's ferry service runs regularly and the lighthouse grounds — combined with the island's beaches, the aquaculture research facilities, and the remnant secondary forest — make for an accessible half-day excursion within Singapore's southern island zone.

## 7. Coney Island (Pulau Serangoon) Light

The automated navigational mark at Coney Island (Pulau Serangoon) in the Serangoon Harbour area of northeastern Singapore marks the approach to the Serangoon Harbour anchorage zone. Coney Island was converted into a public park in 2015 and is now accessible by bridge from Punggol. The navigational light on the island is a minor mark serving the recreational and commercial traffic of the area rather than the major shipping lanes, but its setting within a nature park — with resident monitor lizards, migratory birds, and secondary coastal forest — makes it the most accessible 'lighthouse walk' available within the city-state.

## 8. Eastern Fairway and Approach Buoys

The eastern approaches to the Singapore Strait through the Horsburgh sector are marked by a dense system of major light buoys that function as seamarks of equivalent navigational importance to a lighthouse, despite being unmanned floating structures. The MPA's buoy tender vessels maintain these marks as part of an integrated vessel traffic management system centred on the Singapore Strait Traffic Separation Scheme — one of the busiest in the world. Understanding these marks as part of the lighthouse heritage of Singapore is important: the distinction between a manned tower and an automated buoy has largely dissolved in the age of GPS, and the buoys mark the same hazards and channels that the original lighthouse engineers identified.

## 9. Western Brani Terminal Light

The Port of Singapore's Tanjong Pagar and Brani terminal complex, for many years the world's busiest container port, is marked at its seaward approaches by a series of breakwater-end and pier-head lights. The light at the western end of the Brani terminal mole — a simple elevated green light on a steel tower — marks the edge of the terminal berths and the deep-water channel used by mega-container vessels. These port facility lights, though not architecturally distinguished, are among the highest-traffic navigational marks in the world, guiding vessels valued in the hundreds of millions of dollars past within metres of concrete quay faces.

## 10. Johor Strait Northern Lights

The Johor Strait, separating Singapore from the Malaysian state of Johor, is one of the most intensely used inland waterways in Southeast Asia, serving the two causeway crossings, the Johor Bahru port, and the Pasir Gudang industrial complex. A series of minor lights marks the navigable channel through the strait for the coastal and riverine vessels, ferries, and industrial craft that use it. The combination of dense urban development, a major industrial port, and the narrow, shoal-prone channel creates a demanding navigation environment that the strait's lights have served since the early colonial period.

## Visiting Singapore's Lighthouses

The Bedok/Changi Point light is the only major Singapore lighthouse reachable on foot without a boat, accessible from Changi Village by bus and a short walk. St John's Island and the other southern islands are served by the Singapore Island Cruise ferry from Marina South Pier, with return services on weekends and public holidays — booking online is recommended. No inland light is open for tower climbing. The MPA operates the VTS and lighthouse infrastructure as working safety systems and does not have a public visitor programme. The best views of Raffles Lighthouse and Sultan Shoal are from pleasure craft, dive trips, and the ferry to Batam, Indonesia.
