Off the coast of Trondelag in central Norway, where the Bjugnfjord opens toward the open sea, a single red tower rises straight out of the water. There is no island under it, no green ground, no shore. Kjeungskjaer fyr stands on a low skerry that the waves wash clean, an eight-sided cast-iron tower painted a deep red that glows against the grey northern sea. It is one of the most striking and unusual lighthouses on the entire Norwegian coast, and for nearly a century whole families lived inside it, perched alone above the cold Atlantic swell.

## A light for a difficult coast

The Norwegian coast is among the most demanding in the world for navigation, a labyrinth of fjords, islands, skerries and sounds stretching thousands of kilometres north toward the Arctic. The waters off Trondelag are no exception. Vessels working their way along the leads, the sheltered inner passages between the islands and the mainland, needed marks to find the safe channels and avoid the countless rocks lying just below the surface. The approach to the Bjugnfjord and the wider Orland district demanded a light precisely where there was nothing to build on but bare rock.

Kjeungskjaer was first lit in 1880, a date that places it among the wave of lights built as Norway systematically marked its coast through the nineteenth century. The choice of an octagonal cast-iron tower reflected both the engineering of the age and the brutal exposure of the site. Cast iron could be cast in plates, shipped to a remote rock and bolted together, producing a strong tower without the mass of stone that a masonry light would need on such a foundation.

## The red iron tower

What makes Kjeungskjaer instantly recognisable is its form. The tower is octagonal, eight straight red sides rising to the lantern, and the keeper's dwelling is built into the tower itself rather than standing as a separate house. This integration was a necessity: there was no room on the skerry for a separate home, so the family lived within the same iron structure that carried the light. The deep red colour, set against the often grey and stormy sea, served as a daymark, making the tower visible to mariners in daylight as well as by its beam at night.

The lantern at the top originally held a lens-and-lamp apparatus in the tradition of the era, throwing its characteristic flashes out across the fjord mouth. Standing directly on the rock, the tower took the full force of storms, with spray and even green water striking its lower walls during the worst weather. The iron construction, sealed and painted, was built to endure exactly this.

## Families on a bare rock

For decades Kjeungskjaer was a family station. The keeper, his wife and their children lived together on the skerry, their world bounded by the iron walls of the tower and the water all around. Children were raised here, with the sea as their constant horizon and the nearest land reachable only by boat in fair weather. Supplies, mail and contact with the mainland depended entirely on the conditions, and in winter storms the family could be cut off for long stretches.

Life in such a station shaped a particular kind of existence. The routines of tending the light, keeping the lamp burning and the lens clean, recording the weather and maintaining the iron structure against the corroding salt air, filled the days. The isolation was profound, yet stations like Kjeungskjaer were also communities in miniature, self-contained households clinging to a rock in the sea. The human story of the light is as remarkable as its engineering.

## Automation and afterlife

Like nearly every lighthouse in Norway, Kjeungskjaer was eventually automated, its keepers withdrawn and its light run by machinery and remote control. Automation ended the era of resident families but ensured the light itself continued to guide vessels through the fjord mouth. The tower, no longer a home, became a monument to the age of the manned coastal light.

Today Kjeungskjaer is recognised as a protected cultural heritage site, valued both for its unusual architecture and for the social history it embodies. It can be visited by boat in the calmer months, and standing on the skerry beside the red iron tower, with the open sea on one side and the fjord on the other, gives a vivid sense of what it meant to live here. The tower endures as one of the most photographed and beloved lights of the Trondelag coast.

## Norway's wider constellation of lights

Kjeungskjaer is one star in a vast constellation of Norwegian coastal lights. Far to the south, at the country's southernmost tip, stands Lindesnes fyr, the oldest lighthouse site in Norway, its light marking the corner where the North Sea meets the Skagerrak. Nearby along the Sorlandet coast lie famous lights such as Oksoy fyr off Kristiansand, Gronningen fyr, Ryvingen fyr and Songvar fyr, each guarding a stretch of the rocky southern shore.

Other distinctive towers punctuate this coast: Lyngor fyr among its sheltered islands, the twin lights of Torungen fyr off Arendal, Jomfruland fyr on its long flat island, Faerder fyr at the mouth of the Oslofjord, and Eigeroy fyr on the exposed western coast. Smaller skerry lights such as Homlungen fyr, Torbjornskjaer fyr and Stavernsodden fyr continue the chain. Together they form a system that turned one of the world's most dangerous coasts into a navigable highway, and within it Kjeungskjaer holds its own as the lonely red octagon of the Trondelag fjords.
