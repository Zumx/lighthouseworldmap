Tasmania sits squarely in the path of the Roaring Forties, the band of relentless westerly wind that circles the Southern Ocean without a single landmass to slow it before it strikes the island's western shore. Spray drives horizontally across exposed headlands, dolerite cliffs fall sheer into the sea, and the shipping lanes through Bass Strait have swallowed more than a thousand vessels. Into this weather the colonial authorities and, later, the Commonwealth threw a chain of lighthouses, some of them the oldest in Australia, others the most remote light stations the country ever attempted to keep. These are ten of the finest.

## 1. Cape Bruny Lighthouse, Bruny Island

Standing on the southern tip of Bruny Island since 1838, Cape Bruny is one of the oldest lighthouses in Australia and was built by convict labour from local stone. The white-painted rubble tower rises about 13 m on a headland already 100 m above the sea, giving its old light an enormous geographic range across the entrance to the D'Entrecasteaux Channel. It guided ships approaching Hobart from the south for more than 150 years before being deactivated in 1996 in favour of an automatic light nearby. Today it is the centrepiece of South Bruny National Park, and visitors can climb to the lantern on guided tours to look out over the same grey swell that wrecked the early traders.

## 2. Tasman Island Lighthouse, Tasman Peninsula

Few light stations on earth occupy a more dramatic position. Tasman Island is a slab of dolerite rearing out of the sea off Cape Pillar, its cliffs among the tallest in the Southern Hemisphere, and the lighthouse stands near the summit roughly 250 m above the breaking waves. Completed in 1906, the cast-iron tower had to be supplied by a flying fox hauling stores up the cliff face, and keepers and their families lived in genuine isolation, with children schooled on the rock. The light was automated in 1976 and the keepers withdrawn; access now is effectively by helicopter only, which makes the island a prize for the few who reach it.

## 3. Maatsuyker Island Lighthouse

Maatsuyker is the southernmost lighthouse in Australia, a windswept island off the wild south coast where the next stop south is Antarctica. Lit in 1891, the round masonry tower endures some of the most ferocious weather recorded anywhere on the continent, with gales for a large share of the year. The station is no longer permanently staffed by professional keepers, but the Parks and Wildlife Service Tasmania famously installs volunteer caretakers who live there for months at a time, recording weather observations and minding the buildings in exchange for solitude at the bottom of the world. The waiting list for the post runs for years.

## 4. Low Head Lighthouse, Tamar River

At the mouth of the Tamar River near George Town stands Low Head, marking the approach to Launceston up one of Tasmania's busiest historic waterways. A light has guided ships here since 1833, with the present tower dating from 1888, its broad red horizontal band painted on white masonry as a daymark. Low Head is celebrated for its foghorn, a compressed-air diaphone whose mournful note still sounds for visitors on the first Sunday of each month, one of very few working foghorns left in the country. The pilot station and keepers' cottages survive alongside it as a maritime precinct.

## 5. Cape Wickham Lighthouse, King Island

On the storm-battered northern tip of King Island, in the western entrance to Bass Strait, Cape Wickham is the tallest lighthouse in Australia at about 48 m. It was built in 1861 of local granite after a catastrophic run of wrecks on the island's coast, including the emigrant ship Cataraqui in 1845, which drowned more than 400 people in the worst civil disaster in Australian history. The unpainted grey tower is a stark, beautiful thing against the green of the headland, and its commanding height was a direct answer to the reputation of these waters as a graveyard for ships running the strait.

## 6. Currie Lighthouse, King Island

Also on King Island, on the western coast above the small port of Currie, this round cast-iron tower was prefabricated and erected in 1880 to mark the harbour and warn vessels off the rocky shore. Cast-iron construction allowed the components to be shipped in pieces and bolted together on a site where transporting masonry would have been near impossible. Currie remains an active light and a focal point of the little town, its red-and-white scheme standing over a coast where the swell rolling in from the Southern Ocean has had thousands of miles to build.

## 7. Eddystone Point Lighthouse, Bay of Fires

At the northeastern corner of mainland Tasmania, above the famous orange-lichened boulders of the Bay of Fires, Eddystone Point rises in pink granite quarried close at hand. First lit in 1889, the elegant tapered tower marks the approach to Banks Strait and the northeast coast, its warm stone glowing at sunset against turquoise water and white sand. Reached by a long gravel road through Mount William National Park, it is one of the most photogenic stations in the state and a reward for travellers willing to drive to the edge of the map.

## 8. Table Cape Lighthouse, Wynyard

Perched on the rim of an extinct volcanic plug near Wynyard on the north coast, Table Cape commands a flat-topped headland that drops vertically to the sea. The tower was first lit in 1888 and still operates, its position about 180 m above the water giving the light a long reach across the approaches to Burnie and the northwest. The surrounding farmland blazes with commercial tulip fields in spring, and guided tours climb the tower for a view that takes in the whole sweep of coast and, on clear days, the distant outline of the strait.

## 9. Iron Pot, Derwent Estuary

The Iron Pot is a tiny rock at the mouth of the Derwent estuary below Hobart, and it carries the oldest light station site in Tasmania and one of the oldest in Australia, with a light shown here from 1832. The squat tower marks the channel that every vessel bound for Hobart must thread, where the broad river meets the open water of Storm Bay. Small, low and unglamorous beside the cliff-top giants, the Iron Pot is historically the most important of them all, the point from which the colony's lighthouse service effectively began.

## 10. Cape Sorell Lighthouse, Macquarie Harbour

Guarding the notorious entrance to Macquarie Harbour near Strahan on the wild west coast, Cape Sorell marks the narrow, dangerous bar known as Hells Gates. First lit in 1899, the tower stands on a remote peninsula exposed to the full force of the Roaring Forties, with nothing between it and South America but open ocean. The harbour beyond was the site of one of the colony's harshest penal stations, and the light made the treacherous passage across the bar at least navigable for the timber and supply ships that served the coast.

## Planning a Visit

Tasmanian lighthouses range from drive-up and climb to all but unreachable. Cape Bruny, Table Cape and Low Head offer guided tower tours and are accessible by road, the best starting points for most travellers. Eddystone Point and the King Island lights reward a longer journey, while Tasman Island and Maatsuyker demand a helicopter, a guided expedition cruise, or in Maatsuyker's case a successful application as a volunteer caretaker. Check the Parks and Wildlife Service Tasmania for access, weather and seasonal closures, dress for sudden squalls in any season, and treat the cliff edges with the respect that the Southern Ocean has taught every keeper who ever lived out here.
