No other coastline in the world ever carried so many screwpile cottage lighthouses as Chesapeake Bay. Because the bay is broad, shallow, and floored with soft mud rather than rock, nineteenth-century engineers could not build masonry towers offshore; instead they screwed wrought-iron piles into the bottom and perched a hexagonal or square keeper's cottage on top, with the lantern rising from its roof. At their peak some forty-odd of these distinctive lights guarded the shoals between the Susquehanna and the Potomac. Ice floes, neglect, and automation destroyed most of them, but Maryland still keeps the finest survivors, three of them moved ashore to museums and one improbably clinging to its original screwpile foundation off Annapolis.

## 1. Thomas Point Shoal Lighthouse

The most famous lighthouse on the bay, and arguably in America, Thomas Point Shoal stands a few miles south of Annapolis on the original screwpile foundation it was given in 1875. It is the last screwpile cottage light in the United States surviving in its natural setting, a white hexagonal cottage with a red roof balanced on iron legs over the open water. Its light still shows automatically, and it is a National Historic Landmark jointly cared for by the U.S. Lighthouse Society, the city of Annapolis, and Anne Arundel County. Seasonal boat tours from Annapolis land visitors directly onto the platform in summer, weather permitting.

## 2. Drum Point Lighthouse

Built in 1883 to mark the entrance to the Patuxent River, Drum Point is one of only three Chesapeake screwpile cottages to survive intact. When erosion and the end of its working life threatened it, the hexagonal cottage was floated and moved in 1975 to the Calvert Marine Museum at Solomons, where it now sits restored and furnished as a keeper's family would have known it. Visitors can climb into the cottage and stand beneath the lantern. It is one of the easiest screwpile lights anywhere to explore at close quarters.

## 3. Hooper Strait Lighthouse

The third surviving screwpile cottage, Hooper Strait dates from 1879 and once marked the narrow strait between Tangier Sound and the Honga River on the Eastern Shore. Threatened with demolition, it was cut in half, barged up the bay in 1966, and reassembled at the Chesapeake Bay Maritime Museum in St Michaels. Octagonal rather than hexagonal, it is the centrepiece of the museum's waterfront and is open for climbing through most of the year. Together with Drum Point it lets a visitor compare two cottage designs within a single weekend.

## 4. Cove Point Lighthouse

At Lusby, on the western shore below the mouth of the Patuxent, Cove Point is the oldest continuously operating tower light in Maryland, first lit in 1828. Unlike the screwpile cottages, it is a conventional whitewashed brick tower, about 15 metres tall, with attached keeper's dwellings now let as holiday cottages. It marks a shoal that pushes out into the main shipping channel and remains an active aid managed in partnership with the Calvert Marine Museum. The grounds and tower are open seasonally.

## 5. Concord Point Lighthouse

Standing at Havre de Grace where the Susquehanna River empties into the head of the bay, Concord Point was first lit in 1827 and is among the oldest continuously operated lighthouses on the East Coast. The squat granite tower, less than 10 metres tall, was tended for generations by a single family, the O'Neills. Restored and open to the public with the keeper's house across the street, it is free to climb and sits in a pleasant riverside park, an easy stop at the northern tip of the Chesapeake.

## 6. Seven Foot Knoll Lighthouse

Seven Foot Knoll is a screwpile light of a different kind: a circular cast-iron cottage built in 1856 to mark the approach to the Patapsco River and Baltimore. Round rather than hexagonal, painted red, it was moved in 1988 to the Inner Harbor in Baltimore, where it now anchors the Historic Ships collection at Pier 5. Its keeper famously rescued the crew of a sinking tug during a 1933 storm. Set among historic vessels, it is one of the most accessible lighthouses in the state.

## 7. Sandy Point Shoal Lighthouse

Just north of the Bay Bridge near Annapolis, Sandy Point Shoal is a caisson lighthouse of 1883, a brick dwelling built atop a cast-iron caisson sunk into the shoal. Its Victorian brickwork and mansard roof give it the look of a small townhouse improbably set out in the open water. Now privately owned and automated, it is best seen from Sandy Point State Park or from the deck of a passing boat, and it makes a striking foreground to the bridge.

## 8. Sharps Island Lighthouse

Sharps Island is the bay's leaning tower: a caisson light of 1882 whose island has long since washed away beneath it. Ice damage in the late 1970s shoved the iron tower off the vertical, and it has tilted ever since at an angle that startles first-time visitors. It stands off the mouth of the Choptank River, automated and unmanned, accessible only by boat. It is a vivid monument to the erosion and ice that destroyed so many Chesapeake lights.

## 9. Bloody Point Bar Lighthouse

Off the southern tip of Kent Island, Bloody Point Bar is a second leaning caisson light, also built in 1882. A fire gutted its interior in 1960, and like Sharps Island it now tilts noticeably from settling and ice. Privately owned today, it marks the deep channel off Kent Island and is a familiar sight to sailors crossing from the Eastern Shore. It can only be approached by water, and its rust-streaked, tilted cylinder is unmistakable.

## 10. Point Lookout Lighthouse

At the very tip of the western shore, where the Potomac River meets the bay, Point Lookout was first lit in 1830. The plain white dwelling-style lighthouse sits within Point Lookout State Park, the site of a vast Civil War prison camp, and is reputed to be among the most haunted lighthouses in the country. Decommissioned in 1966, it is now restored and opened on selected days by the state park and a preservation society, at the end of a long peninsula well worth the drive.

## Explore on the map

Maryland's lighthouses fall into two natural circuits: the museum cottages at Solomons, St Michaels, and Baltimore, which can be toured comfortably in a long weekend, and the offshore caissons and screwpiles best reached by boat from Annapolis.
