## The Forgotten Fleet

The lighthouse dominates the coastline and the imagination, but it could not have existed without the vessel that served it. For every lighthouse that stood on a wave-swept rock, a remote island, or a sandbar miles offshore, there was a tender — a small ship, usually purpose-built, that arrived several times a year with oil for the lamp, food and fresh water for the keepers, replacement parts for the clockwork drive mechanism, and, most importantly, the relief keeper who allowed the resident keeper to go ashore. The lighthouse tender fleet was substantial: at its peak the United States operated more than thirty dedicated tenders, Trinity House ran a comparable fleet from ports along the English and Welsh coast, and the Northern Lighthouse Board maintained several vessels from its Leith and Oban depots.

## Design and Purpose

A lighthouse tender had to perform a demanding set of conflicting tasks. It needed enough cargo capacity to carry several tons of lamp oil, building materials for ongoing maintenance, coal for heating, and provisions for a station that might not be visited for two or three months. It needed the sea-keeping ability to operate in the waters around offshore rock stations — exposed positions that are, almost by definition, among the most dangerous on the coast. And it needed a shallow enough draught and sufficient maneuverability to approach small landing jetties in surge conditions, or to lower a boat in open water when no landing was possible at all.

Most tenders in the nineteenth and early twentieth centuries were steam-powered screw vessels of between 400 and 1,200 tons displacement. They were fitted with derricks and cargo winches for lifting heavy loads — a replacement lens assembly, a cast-iron lantern pane, a fog signal compressor — over the side and into a small boat or directly onto a station jetty. Some carried diving equipment for underwater inspection of lighthouse foundations. The better-appointed ones had modest passenger accommodation for a lighthouse inspector making his annual round of the district's stations.

## The US Lighthouse Service Tenders

American lighthouse tenders were among the most systematically organised in the world. The Lighthouse Board, established in 1852, divided the coastline and inland waterways into twelve districts, each served by at least one tender. Vessels were named after flowering plants and trees: Arbutus, Columbine, Azalea, Madrona, Wisteria. The naming convention, introduced around 1870, persisted through the transition to the US Coast Guard in 1939 and continues today — the current Coast Guard tender fleet still carries botanical names.

The tender Columbine, built in 1893 and operating on the 7th District out of Charleston, South Carolina, was typical of the era: 160 feet overall, 380 tons, a compound steam engine giving her about 10 knots. Her log books, preserved at the National Archives, record relief runs to offshore lights including Hunting Island, Cape Romain, and the screwpile lighthouses of the South Carolina sounds. A round trip to a remote screwpile station might take three days, depending on weather and the number of stops. If a keeper was ill or the light had suffered storm damage, the tender might linger for a week.

## Trinity House Tenders

Trinity House operated its tender fleet from depots at Harwich, Swansea, Penzance, and later East Cowes, with vessels responsible not only for lighthouse supply but for the placement and maintenance of buoys — a far more frequent and labour-intensive operation. The THV Alert, built in 1911 and serving until the 1950s, was for many years the primary vessel serving the offshore lights of the Bristol Channel and southern Irish Sea, including the Smalls, the Bishops and Clerks, and the South Bishop. Her successor, the THV Winston Churchill, built in 1963, represented the shift to diesel propulsion and larger, more capable vessels that could carry modular lantern assemblies and handle the increasingly automated equipment being installed in decommissioned keeper stations.

The current Trinity House fleet includes THV Patricia, which entered service in 1982 and acts as the flagship, carrying the depot stocks and the helicopter now used for rapid access to rock stations, and a buoy tender fleet that handles the several hundred Trinity House buoys in English and Welsh waters. The shift to helicopter access fundamentally changed the tender's role: relief of keepers, where it still applied before full automation was completed in 1998 at South Foreland, could be done far more quickly by air, leaving the tender to focus on heavy cargo movements.

## The Northern Lighthouse Board Fleet

The Northern Lighthouse Board's tenders, operating from Leith and later Oban, served a coastline of exceptional difficulty — the exposed Atlantic shores of the Hebrides, Orkney, and Shetland, with their strong tides, variable weather, and absence of harbours. The NLV Pharos has been the Board's flagship name since 1799; the current vessel, the NLV Pharos built in 2006, is a modern multi-role ship with a helideck, a buoy deck capable of handling very large fairway buoys, and accommodation for engineering teams undertaking extended offshore work. Her predecessor undertook the last major buoy-laying work before GPS-era navigation reduced the number of lit buoys required in Scottish waters.

## Commissioners of Irish Lights

The Commissioners of Irish Lights operated tenders from Dublin and the vessel ILV Granuaile — named for the sixteenth-century pirate queen Grace O'Malley — remains one of the most recognisable ships on the Irish coast. She carries out buoy maintenance, lighthouse inspection, and emergency works on stations from Malin Head in the north to Mizen Head in the south. Like her British counterparts, she incorporates a helideck used for the rapid deployment of maintenance engineers to offshore stations.

## Surviving Tenders as Museum Ships

Several retired tenders have been preserved as museum vessels. The steam tender Colfax — originally built for the US Lighthouse Service in 1881 and serving on the Columbia River — is displayed ashore in Astoria, Oregon, close to the Cape Disappointment lighthouse she served. In Norway, the lighthouse tender Christiansand is preserved afloat in Bergen. These vessels allow visitors to understand the scale of the logistical operation that kept any given lighthouse running: the oil barrels in the hold, the spare lens panels lashed on deck, the tiny chart room from which a master navigated in the days before satellite positioning to the exact rock that needed to be resupplied.

## The End of an Era

Full automation of lighthouse stations — completed in Britain by 1998, in the United States progressively through the 1970s and 1980s, in Ireland and Scandinavia on similar timetables — transformed the tender's purpose. Without keepers to relieve, the tender lost its most humanly significant function. The vessels that remain in service today are primarily buoy tenders and engineering support ships. The relief run, with its mixture of practical supply work, medical emergencies, personal drama, and the keeper's long-anticipated sight of the tender's hull rounding the headland, belongs to the past.
