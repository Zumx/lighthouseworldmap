When Japan emerged from more than two centuries of self-imposed seclusion in the 1860s, it had almost no lighthouses. The treaties forced on the country by the Western powers required it to mark its dangerous coasts so that foreign ships could trade safely, and the young Meiji government turned to Britain for help. The man who answered was a thirty-year-old Scottish railway engineer named Richard Henry Brunton, who arrived at Yokohama in 1868 with no experience of lighthouses whatsoever, and who would spend the next eight years building the towers that opened modern Japan to the sea.

## The Engineer Who Knew Nothing of Lights

Brunton was recommended for the post by the firm of the Stevenson brothers, the celebrated Edinburgh lighthouse engineers whose family had built the lights of Scotland. He was not himself a lighthouse man, and the Stevensons gave him a crash course before he sailed: a few months learning the principles of lighthouse construction, optics, and the management of lights. Armed with little more than this and the formidable self-confidence of a Victorian engineer, he set out for a country he had never seen and whose language he did not speak.

What he found was a coastline of great danger and almost no aids to navigation. Japan's waters are studded with reefs, swept by powerful currents, and frequently shrouded in fog, and the approaches to the new treaty ports of Yokohama, Kobe, Nagasaki, and Hakodate were treacherous. Brunton's task was to build, from nothing, a national lighthouse system to Western standards, training Japanese workers and overcoming the challenges of an unfamiliar country as he went.

## Building to Last in an Earthquake Land

The greatest engineering problem Brunton faced was one the Scottish lights had never had to consider: earthquakes. Japan is one of the most seismically active places on earth, and a tall masonry tower of the kind the Stevensons built on the rocks of Scotland would simply shake apart. Brunton studied the problem and developed methods suited to the ground, including towers of brick and iron and careful attention to foundations, producing lights that could ride out the tremors that regularly shook the country.

He also had to import nearly everything. Lenses came from France and Britain, ironwork and machinery from European foundries, and even the skilled labour and materials for the early towers had to be brought in or developed locally under his direction. He established a lighthouse depot and workshops, laid the foundations of a Japanese lighthouse service, and trained the engineers and keepers who would carry the work forward after he left.

## Twenty-Six Lights Around a Coast

Over eight years Brunton built around twenty-six lighthouses and numerous lesser lights and buoys, ringing the coasts of the Japanese islands with a chain of navigation aids where almost none had existed. Among the most important were the lights guarding the approaches to the treaty ports and the great shipping channels. The Kannonzaki Lighthouse at the entrance to Tokyo Bay, near Yokohama, is generally counted as the first Western-style lighthouse in Japan, and stands as a symbol of the whole enterprise even though the present tower is a later rebuilding.

The lights spread far beyond the ports. On the southern tip of the Kii Peninsula, the Kashinozaki Lighthouse marks a notorious headland on the route into the Inland Sea. Around the shores of that great enclosed sea, and along the exposed Pacific coast, Brunton's towers gave masters of foreign and Japanese vessels alike the fixes they needed to navigate safely. The Shirahama and Hyuga coasts, the approaches to Osaka and Kobe, and the northern routes towards Hokkaido were all brought within a marked and lighted system for the first time.

## The Lights and the Opening of Japan

Brunton's lighthouses were far more than navigation aids. They were among the first and most visible works of Western engineering in Japan, physical symbols of the country's decision to engage with the modern industrial world. The treaty ports they served — Yokohama, Kobe, Nagasaki — became the gateways through which foreign trade, ideas, and technology flowed into Meiji Japan, and the lights that guarded those gateways were part of the infrastructure of the country's astonishingly rapid modernisation.

Brunton himself did much more than build lights during his eight years. He laid out the foreign settlement at Yokohama, advised on harbours, drainage, and the first telegraph and railway works, and helped establish the engineering profession in Japan. He was a difficult and opinionated man who quarrelled with his employers, but his contribution was immense, and he left Japan in 1876 having transformed its relationship with its own coasts.

## A Network That Endures

Many of Brunton's original towers still stand and several remain in service, among the oldest working lighthouses in Japan and now treasured as cultural properties. The chain he began was extended over the following decades by Japanese engineers he had helped to train, who built lights at headlands like Sata Misaki at the southern tip of Kyushu, Tsurumisaki on the same island, and countless harbour and breakwater lights around the long indented coast. The breakwater lights of ports such as Hachinohe in the north are the descendants of the system Brunton founded.

Today Japan maintains one of the densest and most reliable lighthouse networks in the world, the direct heir of the work begun by a young Scot who knew nothing of lights when he arrived. The phrase commonly applied to him, the father of Japanese lighthouses, is no exaggeration: the marked and lighted coast that makes Japanese waters navigable today grew directly from the towers he raised in the first years of the Meiji era.

## Visiting Brunton's Legacy

Several of Brunton's lights can be visited, and they make a rewarding way to trace the opening of Japan. The Kannonzaki Lighthouse stands in a park at the mouth of Tokyo Bay, easily reached from Yokohama, and is one of the small number of Japanese lighthouses that visitors can climb. The Kashinozaki Lighthouse on the Kii Peninsula and others around the Inland Sea sit in spectacular coastal settings, their solid Victorian towers a reminder of the moment when Japan turned to face the sea.

The lights also feature in Japan's celebration of its own modernisation, and the role of foreign engineers like Brunton is increasingly recognised in the museums and heritage sites of the former treaty ports. To stand at the foot of one of his towers, watching the shipping pass into a great Japanese harbour, is to see the working result of a remarkable meeting of Scottish engineering and Japanese ambition.
