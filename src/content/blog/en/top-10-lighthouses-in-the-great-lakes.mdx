The Great Lakes form the largest body of fresh water on Earth by surface area, and their lighthouse heritage is one of the richest and least understood in North America. Lake Superior alone is larger than all of England; the five lakes combined contain roughly twenty percent of all surface fresh water on the planet. They are also ferociously dangerous. November gales on Lake Superior have produced waves exceeding ten metres; the lake does not surrender its victims easily — cold water temperatures mean that a person overboard rarely survives more than minutes. The Edmund Fitzgerald, lost in a Lake Superior gale in November 1975 with all twenty-nine crew, is only the most famous of the 6,000 or so wrecks that lie on the lake floors. The United States Lighthouse Board, established in 1852 to bring professional organisation to what had been a chaotic and underfunded system, commissioned a generation of stone, brick and iron towers that still stand at nearly every strategic point: harbour mouths, reefs, peninsulas, and the lonely outer shoals far from shore. These are lighthouses built not for the poetry of the sea but for the practical necessity of moving iron ore, grain, and timber through waters that could kill without warning.

## 1. Split Rock Lighthouse, Lake Superior

Split Rock lighthouse, built in 1910 on a 30-metre diabase cliff above the Minnesota shore of Lake Superior, is the most photographed lighthouse in the United States and one of the most dramatically sited in the world. The octagonal yellow-brick tower is only 17 metres tall, but the cliff gives it a focal plane of 56 metres and a range of 22 nautical miles. It was built after the Mataafa storm of 1905 destroyed or damaged 30 vessels in a single three-day blow, demonstrating that the north shore of Lake Superior was inadequately lit for the iron ore trade. The light guided vessels until decommissioned in 1969, after which Minnesota purchased the station as a state historic park. The lighthouse is now one of the most visited state park destinations in the country; the 1-hour guided tour includes the fog signal building, the keeper's dwellings, and the tower with its original Third Order Fresnel lens intact.

## 2. Whitefish Point Lighthouse, Lake Superior

Whitefish Point marks the shelter at the east end of Lake Superior where vessels caught by storms in the open lake traditionally ran for protection. The current iron skeleton tower, erected in 1861, replaced an 1848 structure that was the first lighthouse on Lake Superior. The tower is 24 metres tall; the light is active, showing a white flash and visible for 17 nautical miles. Whitefish Point is where the Edmund Fitzgerald was heading when she sank; the lighthouse station now houses the Great Lakes Shipwreck Museum, which tells the story of the Fitzgerald and dozens of other wrecks through artefacts including the ship's bell, recovered by divers in 1995. The museum is open May through October. The point is also one of the finest raptor migration watchpoints in North America; hundreds of thousands of hawks pass over during the spring migration corridor that funnels south along the Lake Superior shore.

## 3. Big Sable Point Lighthouse, Lake Michigan

Big Sable Point lighthouse, on the Michigan shore of Lake Michigan north of Ludington, was built in 1867 and stands 34 metres tall. The original brick tower was encased in cast-iron plates in 1900 to arrest deterioration from lake weather; the resulting black-and-white banded tower is one of the most visually striking on the Great Lakes. The light shows a white flash every five seconds with a range of 18 nautical miles, marking the long unindented Michigan coast between Ludington and Frankfort where a lamp failure left vessels with no reference point. The lighthouse is accessible only on foot — a four-kilometre walk through sand dunes from the Ludington State Park trailhead — which gives it the character of a discovery. The station is managed by the Big Sable Point Lighthouse Keepers Association and is open for tours from May through October; volunteers staff the tower and maintain the fog signal building.

## 4. Point Betsie Lighthouse, Lake Michigan

Point Betsie lighthouse, near Frankfort on the eastern Lake Michigan shore, was built in 1858 and is one of the most important waypoints on the lake. The white tower and attached keeper's dwelling are of the characteristic Great Lakes lighthouse form: a compact house-and-tower unit that could be built quickly and maintained efficiently. The point marks the southern entrance to the Manitou Passage, the shipping lane between the Manitou Islands and the Michigan shore, and vessels rounding the point from the south enter the passage heading for the Straits of Mackinac. The light shows a white flash every five seconds and is visible for 15 nautical miles. Point Betsie is an active Coast Guard aid to navigation. The lighthouse grounds are open and the station is managed by a local historic preservation group that offers tours on selected days; the setting on a sandy bluff above the blue lake is among the most picturesque on Lake Michigan.

## 5. Stannard Rock Lighthouse, Lake Superior

Stannard Rock, built between 1877 and 1882 on a shoal 40 kilometres from the nearest land in central Lake Superior, is one of the most isolated lighthouse stations in the United States — called 'the loneliest place in America' by the mariners who served there. The masonry tower is 33 metres tall; it was built on a submerged reef that rises to within a few centimetres of the water surface at low water, using the same stepped-granite caisson technique developed for the offshore Canadian lighthouses. The light marks the most dangerous shoal in Lake Superior for vessels on the ore routes between Duluth, the Soo Locks, and the lower lakes. The lighthouse was automated in 1962. There is no visitor access; the rock can be seen from passing vessels on the Duluth-Sault Ste Marie route.

## 6. Spectacle Reef Lighthouse, Lake Huron

Spectacle Reef lighthouse in northern Lake Huron, completed in 1874, was one of the most technically demanding lighthouse construction projects in nineteenth-century America. The reef lies in the open lake off the northern shore of the Lower Peninsula; the crib foundation had to be constructed during the brief Great Lakes open-water season when winter ice does not shut the lake completely. The engineer Orlando Metcalfe Poe supervised the work. The cylindrical limestone tower is 34 metres tall and showed a fixed white light before conversion to a modern character; it is now automated. The lighthouse was built to guide vessels through the Straits of Mackinac route, one of the busiest passages in the Great Lakes system. It is visible from passing commercial vessels but has no public access.

## 7. Seul Choix Point Lighthouse, Lake Superior

Seul Choix — 'only choice' in French — is the sole natural harbour on the 170-kilometre stretch of Michigan's Upper Peninsula south shore between Manistique and Munising, and it was named by French voyageurs for exactly that reason. The lighthouse was built in 1895 after years of wrecks on the exposed shore; the white masonry tower stands 21 metres tall with a focal plane of 34 metres and a range of 17 nautical miles. The station is one of the best-preserved on the Great Lakes: the fog signal building, keeper's dwelling, and oil house are all intact and restored. The lighthouse is managed as a Michigan state heritage site and is open for guided tours from May to October; access is straightforward via US-2 along the Upper Peninsula shore, and the surrounding Lake Superior State Forest provides context for the isolation that keepers experienced year-round.

## 8. Presque Isle Lighthouse, Lake Huron

The Presque Isle lighthouse on the Presque Isle Peninsula in northern Michigan is actually two lighthouses. The Old Presque Isle lighthouse of 1840 is a squat stone tower now preserved in a county park; the New Presque Isle lighthouse of 1871 is the active station — a 34-metre white tower that marks the approach to Presque Isle Harbour and the northern shore of Lake Huron. The new lighthouse shows a white flash every ten seconds with a range of 24 nautical miles. Both towers are open for tours through Presque Isle Township; the old keeper's dwelling is a small museum. The Presque Isle Peninsula is a rare piece of unmodified Great Lakes shoreline, and the surrounding forests and shoreline are excellent for birding during migration.

## 9. Thunder Bay Island Lighthouse, Lake Huron

Thunder Bay Island lighthouse, on a small island off Alpena in northern Lake Huron, marks the approach to Thunder Bay — the deepwater bay that has been a refuge for Great Lakes mariners for two centuries and is now one of the most important freshwater shipwreck diving sites in North America. The lighthouse was first established in 1832; the current tower dates from 1858 and stands 9 metres tall. The Thunder Bay National Marine Sanctuary encompasses the wrecks of more than 200 vessels in the bay and surrounding waters; the sanctuary visitor centre in Alpena provides detailed information about the wrecks and the lighthouse history. Charter dive boats from Alpena serve the wrecks throughout summer.

## 10. Wind Point Lighthouse, Lake Michigan

Wind Point lighthouse, at the tip of a promontory near Racine, Wisconsin, is the tallest lighthouse on Lake Michigan at 37 metres. Built in 1880 to the design of Orlando Metcalfe Poe — who also engineered Spectacle Reef — the tower is of Cream City brick from Milwaukee, giving it a distinctive pale yellow colour. The character is a white flash every five seconds with a range of 22 nautical miles; it is an active aid to navigation maintained by the Wind Point village government, an arrangement unique among Great Lakes lighthouses. The grounds are a village park, open daily; the lighthouse offers tower tours on selected weekend days in summer. The point marks the northern approach to Racine Harbor, the third largest port on Lake Michigan.

## Visiting the Great Lakes Lighthouses

The Great Lakes contain more than 300 active and historic lighthouses across five states and one Canadian province. The best single concentration of accessible historic stations is the Michigan Lower and Upper Peninsulas, which between them hold over 100 lighthouse sites. Split Rock in Minnesota and Whitefish Point in Michigan offer the most complete visitor experiences, combining exceptional settings with well-developed museums. The Great Lakes lighthouse touring season runs from May through October; November brings the storms that have given the lakes their deadly reputation, but also their most dramatic light.
