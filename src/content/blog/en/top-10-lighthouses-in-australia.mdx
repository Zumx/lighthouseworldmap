Australia's lighthouse heritage reflects the extraordinary demands placed on navigators approaching a continent surrounded by shallow banks, exposed reefs, and notoriously strong coastal currents. The Australian Maritime Safety Authority (AMSA) maintains hundreds of navigational aids across some of the most challenging coastal waters in the world, from the Great Barrier Reef in the north to the Bass Strait — one of the stormiest stretches of water in the Southern Hemisphere — in the south. The earliest colonial lights were modest lanterns on poles, but by the 1850s the goldfields boom and the expansion of shipping had driven the construction of major masonry towers, many of them designed by Colonial Architects and built by convict or free labour with granite, sandstone, and brick. These structures still mark the coasts today.

## 1. Cape Byron Lighthouse, New South Wales

Cape Byron, the most easterly point of the Australian continent, carries a lighthouse completed in 1901 that remains the most powerful navigational light in the country. Designed by Charles Harding under the New South Wales Public Works Department, the white masonry tower stands 22 metres on a headland 94 metres above sea level, giving a combined focal plane height of 116 metres and a range for the flashing white light of 27 nautical miles. The light character is a single white flash every fifteen seconds. The surrounding Cape Byron State Conservation Area is one of the most visited natural reserves in New South Wales; the headland is traversed by the most easterly section of the Byron Bay walking trails. The lighthouse is open to visitors and the grounds provide whale-watching opportunities during the winter migration season.

## 2. Cape Otway Lighthouse, Victoria

Cape Otway in south-western Victoria guards the western entrance to Bass Strait, the treacherous passage between mainland Australia and Tasmania that claimed dozens of vessels in the colonial era. The lighthouse, designed by Henry Ginn and completed in 1848, is the oldest surviving lighthouse on the Australian mainland. The white granite tower stands 20 metres on a cliff 90 metres above sea level; its light character is a group of four white flashes every twenty seconds, visible for 24 nautical miles. The Cape Otway Lightstation, managed by a private operator under a National Parks lease, is one of the most complete historic lighthouse complexes in Australia, with original keepers' quarters, a telegraph station dating from 1859, and interpretive displays in the outbuildings. The lighthouse is open daily and the tower can be climbed.

## 3. Split Point Lighthouse, Victoria

Split Point lighthouse at Aireys Inlet on the Great Ocean Road was completed in 1891 to a design by Compton, Bird and Keating, rising 34 metres as a white octagonal masonry tower. The lighthouse is better known to many Australians as 'Old Barney', and it gained wider recognition as a prominent feature in the children's television series Round the Twist. The light character is a single white flash every ten seconds with a range of about 18 nautical miles, marking the low cliffs of the Surf Coast and warning of offshore shoals between Cape Otway and Port Phillip Heads. The lighthouse is managed by AMSA and open for guided tours; the Great Ocean Road position makes it one of the most accessible lighthouses in Victoria.

## 4. Macquarie Lighthouse, New South Wales

Macquarie Lighthouse on South Head in Sydney marks the entrance to Port Jackson (Sydney Harbour) and is the oldest lighthouse site in Australia. The original tower, a modest structure designed by convict architect Francis Greenway and completed in 1818, was replaced by the current tower in 1883 when it became structurally unsound. The replacement, designed by James Barnet in a remarkably faithful recreation of the original Greenway design, stands 21 metres in white-painted sandstone. Its light character is a group of two white flashes every fifteen seconds, visible for 25 nautical miles over the Pacific approaches to Sydney. Heritage NSW manages the lighthouse; guided tours include the lantern room and the historic watchroom.

## 5. Cape Leeuwin Lighthouse, Western Australia

Cape Leeuwin at the south-western corner of Australia marks the meeting of the Indian Ocean and the Southern Ocean, one of the most meteorologically dramatic coastlines in the country. The lighthouse was completed in 1896 and stands 39 metres tall in local limestone, with a focal plane of 56 metres and a flashing white light visible for 26 nautical miles. The first-order Fresnel lens, manufactured by Chance Brothers of Birmingham, was in service until electrification and is now displayed in the adjacent interpretive centre. Cape Leeuwin Lightstation is operated by a heritage trust and open to visitors; the water wheel near the lighthouse, a curious relic of the original fresh water supply, is also on display.

## 6. Wilsons Promontory Lighthouse, Victoria

Wilsons Promontory, the southernmost point of the Australian mainland, carries a lighthouse completed in 1859 by the Victorian Colonial Architect John Alexander. The granite tower stands 17 metres on a headland 107 metres above sea level, giving a focal plane height sufficient for a range of 21 nautical miles. The light shows a single white flash every four seconds. The Promontory is one of Australia's premier wilderness national parks, and the lighthouse is accessible only by a 19-kilometre walking track from the nearest car park; the lighthouse keepers' cottages are available for overnight accommodation, booked through Parks Victoria, making a stay here one of the most rewarding lighthouse experiences in the country.

## 7. Mersey Bluff Lighthouse, Tasmania

The Mersey Bluff lighthouse at Devonport on the northern Tasmanian coast was completed in 1889 and marks the entrance to the Mersey River from Bass Strait. The white masonry tower stands 22 metres on a basalt bluff and shows a single white flash every seven and a half seconds, with a range of 16 nautical miles. The lighthouse is associated with the nearby Tiagarra cultural centre and the Mersey Bluff Aboriginal petroglyph site, one of the most significant collections of Aboriginal rock carvings in Tasmania. The lighthouse grounds are open to the public; the tower is available for tours and the location provides direct views of Spirit of Tasmania ferries departing for Melbourne.

## 8. Griffiths Island Lighthouse, Victoria

Griffiths Island, connected to Port Fairy by a causeway, carries a lighthouse completed in 1859 on one of the oldest and most historically significant sites in Victoria. A light has guided vessels into Port Fairy harbour since 1839; the current bluestone tower stands 12 metres. The light character is a single white flash every four seconds, with a range of 12 nautical miles. The island is home to a large short-tailed shearwater (muttonbird) colony, and the annual arrival of the birds in late September is a notable local event. The lighthouse is listed on the Victorian Heritage Register; the island and causeway walk is free and open daily.

## 9. Point Lonsdale Lighthouse, Victoria

Point Lonsdale guards the western entrance to Port Phillip Heads, known as 'The Rip', where tidal currents of up to eight knots flow through a narrow channel between two reefs and where dozens of vessels came to grief in the early colonial period. The current lighthouse, completed in 1902, replaced earlier structures on the same headland. The white masonry tower stands 13 metres on a cliff and its group-flashing character is visible for 18 nautical miles. The operational lighthouse is managed by AMSA; the nearby historic lighthouse (1862) is maintained separately and both structures are visible from the public foreshore.

## 10. Port Germein Lighthouse, South Australia

Port Germein on the upper Spencer Gulf carries a lighthouse associated with what was historically the longest jetty in the Southern Hemisphere — the 1.6-kilometre jetty built in 1882 to load grain from the wheat-farming district of the southern Flinders Ranges. The lighthouse at the end of the jetty, a modest iron structure completed in the 1880s, marked the approach to the jetty head across the very shallow and tidal waters of the gulf. The structure is of historical interest as an example of the minor lights that guided small coastal vessels in the South Australian grain trade. Port Germein is a quiet holiday town; the jetty and its lighthouse are open for walking and fishing.

## Visiting Australia's Lighthouses

Australia's lighthouse sites range from the easily accessible — several lie within or adjacent to major cities — to the genuinely remote, accessible only on foot or by boat. The lightstation heritage visitor programmes at Cape Otway, Cape Leeuwin, and Macquarie are well-developed and run year-round. The Bass Strait and southern Victorian lights are best visited in summer (December to March) when weather conditions are most reliable. The whale migration along the New South Wales coast, from May to November, makes the Cape Byron headland especially rewarding.
