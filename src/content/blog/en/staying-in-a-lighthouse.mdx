## Why People Sleep in Lighthouses

The appeal is not hard to understand. A lighthouse keeper's dwelling is, by definition, positioned where the sea meets its most dangerous self — on a headland, a reef approach, or a tidal island. When the keepers left and the authorities began looking for ways to fund the upkeep of listed buildings, conversion to accommodation was a natural answer. Guests get the location and the story; the operators get revenue to maintain the fabric. The arrangement has proved durable, and today dozens of former lighthouse stations across Britain, Ireland, Scandinavia, the United States and elsewhere offer overnight stays that range from self-catering flats in old engine rooms to fully serviced hotel suites inside the tower itself.

What those guests actually experience is a particular kind of solitude. The view from a lighthouse window is, almost by definition, unobstructed. There are no neighbours, no road noise, often no mobile signal. The sound is wind and water. In winter the beam still sweeps — most automated lights are active regardless of who occupies the cottage below — and the rhythm of flash and dark becomes part of the night.

## The Landmark Trust and Trinity House Cottages

In England, two organisations dominate lighthouse holiday letting. The Landmark Trust, founded in 1965 to rescue buildings of architectural and historic interest, manages a small number of lighthouse properties including the Lundy North Light on Lundy Island in the Bristol Channel. Built in 1897 to a design by Trinity House engineer Sir Thomas Matthews, the tower stands 17 metres tall and the fog signal station beside it has been converted into sleeping accommodation for up to four guests. Reaching Lundy requires the MS Oldenburg ferry from Bideford or Ilfracombe, which runs from April to October; in winter the helicopter service is the only option.

Trinity House itself, the General Lighthouse Authority for England, Wales and the Channel Islands, offers holiday lets at several of its former keeper stations through a dedicated lettings programme. The Old Higher Lighthouse at Portland Bill in Dorset — a squat, conspicuous white tower dating from 1716, replaced as the active light in 1906 — is available as a self-catering cottage. The setting on the limestone tip of the Portland peninsula, with the race churning visibly offshore, is exactly the kind of place that makes guests understand why a light was needed here.

## Iconic Stays in Ireland and Scotland

The Irish Lights board manages lighthouse properties through a scheme that has made some of the Republic's and Northern Ireland's most remote stations available to visitors. Fanad Head Lighthouse, on the northern shore of Lough Swilly in County Donegal, was first lit in 1817 following the wreck of the frigate HMS Saldanha on the reef below. The current tower, rebuilt in 1886 and 38 metres tall, exhibits a flashing white light with a range of 18 nautical miles. Self-catering cottages in the former keeper's compound can sleep up to six, and the location — a narrow headland between the Atlantic and the lough — gives views in three directions.

In Scotland, the Northern Lighthouse Board's scheme offers holidays at stations from Arbroath on the east coast to the Mull of Kintyre and beyond. The Mull of Galloway, Scotland's most southerly point, has been available as holiday accommodation since the board's property was no longer required for keepers following full automation in 1988. The tower, built by Robert Stevenson in 1830 and rising 26 metres above the clifftop, still operates with a flashing white character visible for 28 nautical miles. The cottages are metres from the cliff edge, and on clear days the coasts of Northern Ireland, the Isle of Man and Cumbria are simultaneously visible.

## American Lighthouse Bed and Breakfasts

The United States has a tradition of lighthouse bed-and-breakfast operations that developed after the Coast Guard began de-staffing stations from the 1960s onwards. The transfer of many lighthouses to local authorities, non-profit groups and private individuals under the National Historic Lighthouse Preservation Act of 2000 opened the door to a variety of accommodation models.

The Heceta Head Lighthouse in Oregon, completed in 1894 on a dramatic clifftop 56 metres above sea level, operates as a licensed bed and breakfast in the former assistant keeper's duplex. The First Order Fresnel lens, which remains in the lantern room of the 56-foot tower and throws a light visible 21 nautical miles offshore, is one of the strongest still operating on the Oregon coast. Guests share the grounds with an active automated light and can arrange tours of the lens room. Bookings are competitive, particularly through summer.

On the Great Lakes, the Sand Hills Lighthouse on the Keweenaw Peninsula in Michigan, built in 1917 and decommissioned in 1954, became a bed and breakfast in the 1990s. The yellow-brick keeper's house and attached tower overlook Lake Superior from a wooded shore. Further east on Lake Michigan, the Old Presque Isle Lighthouse in northern Michigan, dating to 1840 and among the oldest surviving structures on the lakes, offers period-furnished rooms managed by the township that now owns the property.

## Scandinavia's Lighthouse Hotels

Norway and Sweden have been particularly imaginative in converting lighthouse stations to tourism use. Norway's Kvitsøy Lighthouse, an archipelago south-west of Stavanger, has operated as a hotel since the main keeper's house was adapted in the early 2000s. The station marks the southern approach to the Boknafjord and was built in 1700, making it one of Norway's oldest manned lights; the current tower dates from 1836. Guests arrive by boat from Kvitsøy island and can, in calm conditions, walk the rocky foreshore around the peninsula.

Sweden's Pater Noster lighthouse on the skerry of Hamneskär, 12 nautical miles west of Marstrand on the Bohuslän coast, is one of the most photographed stations in Scandinavia. The tower, built in 1868 to a height of 27 metres with a light character of two flashes every ten seconds, was converted to a boutique hotel with eight rooms. Access is by boat from Tjörn or Marstrand; the crossing takes around forty-five minutes in fair weather. The skerries here are almost entirely flat rock with a few windswept shrubs, and the hotel has made a particular feature of the unbroken 360-degree sea horizon.

## Practical Matters for Lighthouse Guests

Lighthouse accommodation is not resort accommodation. Access is typically by a single road or by sea, and the same exposed position that makes the view spectacular makes the weather variable and sometimes severe. Heating systems in converted keeper's cottages are generally adequate for the climate, but guests should expect draughts in older buildings and should bring appropriate clothing for any season.

The best periods for lighthouse stays depend on geography. On the Atlantic coasts of Britain and Ireland, May and June offer long days, moderate winds and the chance of warm spells. July and August bring the most visitors and peak prices. September and October are atmospheric and often calmer than summer, and the autumn light on the sea is exceptional for photography. Winter stays are possible at many stations and have their own qualities — the sense of isolation is complete when gales keep you inside for a day — but some remote stations close between November and March.

Booking well in advance is essential at the most popular properties. The Landmark Trust, Irish Lights and the Northern Lighthouse Board all use online booking systems, and many popular weeks sell out six months or more ahead. ## The Longer Tradition

Before tourism, lighthouses were lived in by necessity. Principal keepers and their assistants — often with families — occupied the dwellings attached to shore stations for months at a time. The domestic arrangements were prescribed in detail by the lighthouse authority: bed sizes, shelf heights, paint colours, even the allocation of garden plots. The cottages that visitors now occupy were built to a standard that was functional rather than comfortable, designed for people who had no choice about their accommodation. That the buildings now command a premium for exactly the conditions their original occupants endured — wind, salt air, the relentless sound of the sea — is one of the subtler ironies of lighthouse heritage.
