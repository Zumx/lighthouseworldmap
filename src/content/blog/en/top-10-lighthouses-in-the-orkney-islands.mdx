Orkney lies off the very top of mainland Scotland, separated from Caithness by the Pentland Firth, a strait so violent that for centuries it was considered all but impassable. Tides here race at up to sixteen knots, throwing up roosts and overfalls with names that read like a warning, the Swelkie, the Merry Men of May, the Bores of Duncansby. Across the roughly seventy islands of the archipelago, and on the headlands that face this churning water, sits a remarkable family of lighthouses, many of them built by the Stevenson engineering dynasty whose work defined Scottish coastal lighting for over a century. Visiting them means combining seabird cliffs, Neolithic monuments and some of the most powerful tidal scenery in Europe.

## The Stevenson Legacy in the North

The lighthouses of Orkney are inseparable from the Stevensons. Robert Stevenson, his sons and grandsons, including the engineers who were uncles to the novelist Robert Louis Stevenson, designed and built the Northern Lighthouse Board's towers across these waters from the early nineteenth century onward. Their work combined elegant tapering masonry with the practical genius needed to light a coast of ferocious tides and frequent gales. The towers you see today, painted white or in distinctive day-marks, are largely their handiwork, now automated but still maintained by the Northern Lighthouse Board from its base in Edinburgh.

## Start Point, Sanday

One of the most striking lights in Orkney stands on Start Point at the eastern tip of the low, sandy island of Sanday. The tower is unmistakable, painted in bold vertical black and white stripes, a day-mark designed to stand out against the pale sand and sky. The original light here dates to 1806 and was one of the first in Orkney, later rebuilt. Sanday earned a grim reputation as a shipwreck island, its low shore invisible until too late, and Start Point was the answer. Reaching it involves a walk across a tidal causeway, so the timing of your visit depends on the sea itself.

## North Ronaldsay

Beyond Sanday lies North Ronaldsay, the northernmost of the Orkney islands, and home to two towers standing close together. The older, a stone beacon from 1789, was one of the earliest navigation marks in the archipelago but proved an inadequate light. It was replaced in 1854 by a magnificent brick tower, painted with two red bands, that at over thirty metres is the tallest land-based lighthouse in Britain made of brick. The island is also famous for its seaweed-eating sheep, kept on the foreshore by a drystone dyke, and the lighthouse visitor centre tells both stories.

## Brough of Birsay

On the northwestern tip of the Orkney Mainland, the tidal islet known as the Brough of Birsay carries a small lighthouse reached only at low tide across a slippery concrete causeway. The island holds the remains of a Pictish and Norse settlement, and the walk out to the light, with puffins nesting on the cliffs in summer, is one of the finest short excursions in Orkney. The light itself is modest, but the setting, with the Atlantic breaking on three sides, is unforgettable. Check the tide tables carefully; people are regularly caught out by the rising water.

## Cantick Head, Hoy

Guarding the southern approaches to Scapa Flow, the great natural harbour at the heart of Orkney, the lighthouse at Cantick Head stands on the island of South Walls, joined to Hoy by a causeway. Built by the Stevensons in 1858, it marks the eastern entrance used by shipping bound for the anchorage that sheltered the British Grand Fleet in both world wars. The former keepers' cottages here are now let to visitors, making it one of the places where you can actually sleep beside a working Orkney light.

## Graemsay and the Hoy Sound Lights

The narrow Hoy Sound, the western entrance to Scapa Flow, is guarded by two lights on the little island of Graemsay, the Hoy High and Hoy Low lighthouses, both built by Alan Stevenson in 1851. They work as a pair of leading lights: a vessel keeping them in line can safely thread the fierce tidal stream of the sound. Graemsay is tiny, with a handful of residents, and the two elegant towers are among the most photogenic in Orkney, framed against the dark hills of Hoy beyond.

## The Pentland Skerries

Out in the Pentland Firth itself, between Orkney and the Scottish mainland, lie the Pentland Skerries, a cluster of low rocks in the path of the worst of the tides. The lighthouse here, established in 1794 with twin towers and later modernised, was among the most isolated and difficult of all keepers' postings. The skerries sit amid the roost where the firth's tidal streams collide, and the light marks a hazard that has claimed countless ships over the centuries. It is not a place for casual visiting, but it is one of the most important beacons in northern Britain.

## Lighthouses Among the Monuments

What sets Orkney apart is the way its lights sit among extraordinary human history. The archipelago holds the Neolithic village of Skara Brae, the Ring of Brodgar, the chambered tomb of Maeshowe and the great red sandstone cathedral of St Magnus in Kirkwall, all older than or contemporary with the stone-building traditions that produced the lighthouse towers. A lighthouse tour of Orkney naturally weaves through five thousand years of building on these islands, from the prehistoric to the Victorian engineers who lit the sea.

## Visiting Orkney's Lights

Orkney is reached by ferry from Scrabster or Gills Bay near John o' Groats, or by air to Kirkwall. The best season runs from late May to August, when the long northern daylight, the simmer dim, gives almost endless evenings and the seabird colonies are at their busiest. Inter-island ferries and small flights link the outer isles where many lights stand. Tides govern access to the causeway lights at Birsay and Start Point, so a tide table is essential. Pack for wind in any season; even summer brings sudden gales off the Atlantic.
