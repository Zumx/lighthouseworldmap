Tunisia's 1,300-kilometre coastline curves from the Gulf of Tunis in the north, around the Cap Bon Peninsula, past the Gulf of Hammamet and the Gulf of Gabès, to the desert shores near the Libyan border in the south. This coast has been navigated continuously since Phoenician traders established Carthage on the Gulf of Tunis around 814 BC, and the Roman, Arab, and Ottoman empires that followed each recognised the importance of these waters to Mediterranean trade. The hazards are numerous: the shallow Kerkennah Banks and the rocks of the Zembra archipelago have claimed ships for millennia. Tunisia's modern lighthouse network was established primarily during the French Protectorate period between 1881 and 1956, when the Service des Phares et Balises constructed most of the major towers using French lighthouse engineering standards. Today the Organisation Nationale de la Navigation Aérienne et Maritime (ONNAM) maintains the network. [Open the map](/map) to explore Tunisia's complete chain of coastal lights.

## 1. Cap Bon Lighthouse (Ras el-Mar), Cap Bon

Cap Bon, the most prominent headland of Tunisia and the nearest point of the African continent to Sicily, is marked by one of the most important lights in the western Mediterranean. The lighthouse at Ras el-Mar, which occupies a rocky promontory at the cape's very tip, was built during the French Protectorate and its white cylindrical tower stands approximately 28 metres above the sea. The light character is a group flashing white sequence visible around 22 nautical miles — a critical waypoint for vessels transiting the Sicilian Channel, one of the Mediterranean's two main narrows. The cape separates the Gulf of Tunis to the north from the Gulf of Hammamet to the south. The surrounding coast is dramatic rocky limestone; the lighthouse stands in a wind-scoured landscape that has changed little since the Sicilian Channel first carried Phoenician galleys. The cape is accessible by road from the resort town of Kelibia and from Nabeul.

## 2. Cap Bizerte Lighthouse, Bizerte

The port of Bizerte is the northernmost city in Africa, and its lighthouse marks the approach to both the commercial harbour and the entrance of the Bizerte Canal, which links the Mediterranean to Lac de Bizerte. The white tower, of French Protectorate construction, stands on the headland north of the town and carries a flashing white light visible about 20 nautical miles. Bizerte's strategic position made it a critical naval base from the Roman period through to the Second World War, when it was the scene of bitter fighting; the lighthouse was damaged and repaired during this period. The harbour today handles ferry traffic to France and commercial shipping; the old city medina and the kasbah survive from the Ottoman period. The lighthouse headland offers clear views over the Mediterranean toward the Sicilian coast.

## 3. Cani Islands Lighthouse, Iles des Chiens

The Cani Islands — known in French as the Iles des Chiens (Dog Islands) — are a small group of rocky islets off the northern coast of Tunisia, northeast of Bizerte. The lighthouse here marks a significant shipping hazard: the islands lie in the middle of the main lane for vessels navigating between the Gulf of Tunis and the Sicilian Channel. The white tower, built on the principal island, is an isolated offshore station of considerable historical importance, its light character a flashing white signal with a range of about 18 nautical miles. The station was one of the most isolated in the Tunisian network and required supply by tender. The surrounding sea is clear and deep, with good visibility for the ferries and cargo vessels that pass close offshore.

## 4. La Goulette Lighthouse, La Goulette

La Goulette is the port district of Tunis, sitting at the end of the canal linking the capital to the Mediterranean at the southern end of the Gulf of Tunis. Its lighthouse marks the canal entrance and the approach to the commercial port, guiding the large ferries that connect Tunis with Marseille, Genoa, and Palermo. The white structure is a working harbour light rather than a dramatic coastal tower, but its position at the entry to the Tunisian capital gives it considerable logistical importance. La Goulette's port is surrounded by restaurants specialising in Tunisian seafood, and the adjacent Carthage archaeological site — the ruins of the Phoenician and Roman city that dominated Mediterranean trade for centuries — lies within a few kilometres. The combination makes the area one of the most layered in all of North African maritime history.

## 5. Kelibia Lighthouse, Kelibia

Kelibia sits on the eastern coast of Cap Bon where the Sicilian Channel is at its narrowest — barely 140 kilometres of open water separate Tunisia from Sicily here. The lighthouse occupies a position below the old Byzantine and Arab fortress on the headland, and the white tower dates from the French Protectorate period. The light character is a flashing white signal visible about 18 nautical miles, marking the channel passage and warning of the offshore shoals. The harbour below the fort is an active fishing port; the local specialty is grilled grouper and a distinctive Kelibia white wine produced from the sandy soils around the cape. The fortress, occupied by successive powers from the Byzantine period through to the Ottoman era, is open as a heritage site; the lighthouse and fort together make one of the most evocative maritime viewpoints on the Tunisian coast.

## 6. Monastir Lighthouse, Monastir

The coastal city of Monastir, on the Gulf of Hammamet between Sousse and Mahdia, is defined by its ninth-century ribat — a fortified monastery that has served as both place of worship and defensive tower since the Aghlabid period. The ribat's tower functions in a lighthouse capacity in tradition if not in modern practice, but a more recent navigation light marks the harbour approaches to the busy marina and fishing port. Monastir's airport receives significant tourist traffic, and the marina here is among the best-equipped on the Tunisian coast. The old ribat, now a museum, offers views over the harbour and the approaches that have been guarded from this hilltop for over a thousand years.

## 7. Mahdia Lighthouse, Mahdia

Mahdia, on the narrowest part of the Tunisian coast where the Sahel Peninsula juts into the Mediterranean, was the first capital of the Fatimid Caliphate in the tenth century. Its lighthouse marks the harbour approach and the offshore reefs that make navigation around the headland demanding. The white tower is of moderate height with a flashing character covering the main hazards. Mahdia is known for its textile crafts and its fishing fleet, whose boats land swordfish and tuna; the lighthouse overlooks a working harbour of considerable activity. The old city walls and the Skifa el-Kahla gate, a Fatimid-era fortified passage, survive from the medieval city that once rivalled Cairo in ambition.

## 8. Sfax Lighthouse, Sfax

Sfax, Tunisia's second city, commands the approach to the Kerkennah Islands and the Golfe de Gabès, whose enormous tidal range — up to 2 metres at springs, exceptional for the Mediterranean — complicates harbour navigation. The lighthouse at Sfax marks the approach to the commercial harbour, which handles phosphate exports from the Tunisian interior. The white tower is a practical harbour aid; the surrounding industrial infrastructure includes one of North Africa's significant fishing fleets and an olive oil industry that stretches back to Roman times. The Sfax medina, largely intact within its Aghlabid walls, is one of Tunisia's finest.

## 9. Kerkennah Islands Lighthouse, Kerkennah

The Kerkennah Islands are a flat, low archipelago of coral limestone and sandy shallows about 20 kilometres offshore from Sfax. Their reefs and shallows are exceptionally treacherous; the lighthouse here marks the main navigable channel through the islands and warns of the banks extending several kilometres from the shoreline. The light is of moderate height — height is less critical here given the nature of the hazard — and carries a flashing character with red sectors. The islands are accessible by ferry from Sfax and are known for their traditional octopus-trap fishing using palm-frond constructions.

## 10. Djerba Lighthouse, Île de Djerba

Djerba, the largest island in North Africa after the Canaries, is connected to the mainland by a Roman causeway of extraordinary antiquity and by a modern ferry service. The lighthouse at the island's cape marks the approach to the ferry route and the small craft harbour, and the white tower is visible from the mainland shore across the Bou Grara lagoon. Djerba is celebrated for its Jewish heritage — the El Ghriba synagogue, perhaps the oldest in Africa — and for its whitewashed architecture. The island's lighthouse, modest in scale, stands at the end of an approach to an island that has been a navigational destination for Mediterranean mariners since antiquity.

## Visiting Tunisian Lighthouses

Tunisia's lighthouse sites are generally accessible by road, and the coast road along the Cap Bon Peninsula, through the Sahel, and south toward Sfax passes within sight of most of the active towers. The best season for coastal exploration is spring — March to May — when temperatures are mild, tourist crowds are absent, and the light quality for photography is exceptional. Summer is hot and can be hazy; autumn brings occasional violent storms. Tower access for civilian visitors is restricted; most lighthouses operate under maritime authority supervision and cannot be climbed, but the surrounding headlands and harbours are freely accessible.
