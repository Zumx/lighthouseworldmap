On the third full weekend of every August, something unusual happens at lighthouses around the world. Alongside the tourists climbing the towers and the heritage volunteers selling postcards, small groups of people arrive with coaxial cable, portable masts, and radio transceivers. They string wire antennas between railings and lantern galleries, switch on their sets, and spend forty-eight hours calling out across the airwaves: the callsign of their station, the name of their lighthouse, and an invitation to make contact. This is the International Lighthouse and Lightship Weekend, the largest event in the amateur radio calendar built around a single class of structure, and it has quietly become one of the most enduring ways the public encounters both hobbies at once.

## How the Event Began

The origins lie in Scotland. In 1993 the Ayr Amateur Radio Group, on the country's south-west coast, organised an on-air activity to commemorate Scotland's lighthouses and the work of the Northern Lighthouse Board. The first events centred on the Mull of Galloway, Scotland's most southerly point, and on Turnberry. The idea was simple: set up a temporary amateur radio station at or near a lighthouse, and encourage other operators to do the same so that contacts could be exchanged between participating lights.

What began as a national commemoration grew rapidly. By the late 1990s the concept had crossed borders, and the event was renamed to reflect its international scope. The Scottish founders, particularly the late John Forsyth, are still credited each year, and the organising baton has passed between amateur radio groups while the format has remained deliberately informal. There is no entry fee, no league table of winners, and no obligation beyond registering a lighthouse and turning up. That lightness of structure is exactly why it has survived for over three decades.

## What Happens Over the Weekend

Participation is straightforward. A licensed amateur, or more often a club, registers a specific lighthouse or lightship with the event coordinators, who maintain a master list. Over the weekend the operators set up at or beside the structure — sometimes inside the lantern room itself where access is granted, more often in the grounds or an adjacent car park — and make as many contacts as they can with other stations, both lighthouse-based and ordinary home stations seeking to log lighthouse contacts.

The activity is not a contest in the competitive sense. The goal is participation and the exchange of greetings, locations, and signal reports. Many operators aim to work as many other registered lighthouses as possible over the two days, building a personal list of towers contacted. Others simply enjoy operating from a scenic, often remote location, combining a weekend by the sea with their hobby. The atmosphere on the bands is relaxed and conversational, a contrast to the rapid-fire exchanges of competitive contests.

## The Scale It Reached

From two Scottish lights, the event has grown to involve several hundred registered structures in a typical year, spread across more than forty countries. Lighthouses on every inhabited continent take part: the towers of the British and Irish coasts that started it all, the tall brick sentinels of the United States eastern seaboard, the Australian and New Zealand coastal lights, the Scandinavian and Baltic stations, and many more. In strong years the registration list has exceeded five hundred entries worldwide.

This scale makes it one of the most geographically distributed amateur radio activities anywhere. A single operator working from a headland in Cornwall might, over a weekend, exchange greetings with stations at lighthouses in Germany, Sweden, the United States, Argentina, and Japan. Each contact is a small confirmation that on this particular weekend, the same idea — celebrating these structures by putting them on the air — is being acted out simultaneously around the planet.

## A Related but Separate Award

It is worth untangling a common confusion. The International Lighthouse and Lightship Weekend is an activity weekend, not an awards programme. Running alongside it, and sometimes conflated with it, is the Amateur Radio Lighthouse Society, an American-based organisation that maintains a permanent numbered registry of lighthouses and runs awards for operators who make contacts from or with listed lights at any time of year, not only during the August weekend.

The two complement each other. The August weekend provides the concentrated burst of activity and public visibility; the year-round society provides the catalogue, the reference numbers, and the long-term incentive to keep operating from lighthouses. An operator might activate a tower during the August weekend and log the same contacts toward a society award. For newcomers, the distinction matters mainly when reading the rules: the weekend itself asks nothing more than that you take part.

## Why Lighthouses and Radio Suit Each Other

The pairing is less arbitrary than it first appears. Lighthouses are, by their nature, sited on prominent headlands, islands, and points — exactly the locations that radio operators prize for clear take-off toward the horizon and minimal obstruction. A tower built to throw light far out to sea stands where radio waves, too, travel well. Operating from such a site, especially on the higher-frequency bands that depend on a clear path, can produce notably good results.

There is also a shared heritage. Both lighthouses and amateur radio are, in different ways, about communication across distance and the safety of those at sea. Many coastal radio enthusiasts feel a natural kinship with the keepers and engineers who maintained the lights, and a number of preserved lighthouses now host permanent or semi-permanent amateur stations run by heritage groups. For these volunteers, a weekend on the air is both a hobby and a way of keeping a working purpose alive in a structure whose original navigational role has often been automated away.

## Taking Part or Listening In

You do not need a licence to enjoy the weekend. Anyone with a basic shortwave receiver can listen across the amateur bands during the third August weekend and hear lighthouse stations identifying themselves and exchanging contacts. For the licensed, taking part is a matter of registering a local light, gathering some portable equipment, and committing a weekend. Many clubs welcome visitors at their lighthouse setups, and it is one of the friendliest entry points into the hobby.

For the lighthouse enthusiast who has never touched a radio, the weekend offers a different angle on familiar structures: a chance to see a tower you may have only photographed brought briefly back into active service, humming with signals reaching across oceans. It is a reminder that these buildings, even decommissioned and dark, can still be the focus of a living, connecting activity.
