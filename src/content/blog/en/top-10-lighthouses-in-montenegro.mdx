Montenegro's Adriatic coastline runs barely 300 kilometres, yet it contains one of the most varied sets of navigational challenges in the eastern Mediterranean: the dramatic rocky cliffs of the open coast south of Bar, the narrow mouth of the Boka Kotorska — one of the deepest fjord-like inlets in southern Europe — and the outlying island reefs that have wrecked vessels since antiquity. The lighthouse network here was primarily developed under Austro-Hungarian administration in the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries, when the port of Kotor and the naval base at Tivat formed part of the imperial maritime frontier. Many towers follow the characteristic Austro-Hungarian design language: a compact cylindrical white tower attached to a two-storey keeper's dwelling of ochre-coloured render, built from local limestone. After the First World War the lights passed to the Kingdom of Yugoslavia and later the Socialist Federal Republic; they are today maintained by the Port Authority of Montenegro. Few are open to visitors in any formal sense, but the coastal road and the network of footpaths give access to most.

## 1. Cape Mendra Lighthouse, near Petrovac

Cape Mendra is the most prominent headland on Montenegro's open southern coast, jutting into the Adriatic between the small resort towns of Petrovac and Buljarica. The existing tower, completed in 1872 under Austro-Hungarian supervision, is a white cylindrical stone structure approximately 12 metres tall, set on a low limestone cape with excellent sea clearance. Its light — now automated and powered by solar panels — shows a group-flashing white character visible for around 10 nautical miles, warning vessels off the cape and the scattered rocks to its south. The keeper's house beside the tower is disused but structurally sound. The cape is reached by a footpath from the Petrovac road and offers some of the finest views on the Montenegrin coast, looking south toward the Albanian border and north toward the cliffs of Budva.

## 2. Platamuni Lighthouse, near Petrovac

Platamuni stands at one of the most photogenic points on the entire Adriatic: a near-vertical limestone cliff some 120 metres above the sea, with the tower perched at the very edge. The original light at this position dates from the Austro-Hungarian period, though the current installation is a replacement automated beacon. The cliff-top location gives an effective range well beyond what the small structure would suggest — the light can be seen for approximately 14 nautical miles in clear conditions. Access is by an unmarked path from the main coastal road south of Petrovac, and the site rewards the walk: the sea colour changes from turquoise to deep navy directly below the cliff face. The nearest permanent hazard is the rock known as Hrid Platamuni, which breaks the surface in rough conditions and has damaged several small craft over the decades.

## 3. Cape Ostro Lighthouse, Tivat Peninsula

Cape Ostro (Rt Oštra) marks the southern entrance to the Boka Kotorska, one of the most navigated passages in Montenegro's waters. The tower here is a white masonry structure of late nineteenth-century construction, approximately 18 metres tall, and its light — a flashing white and red sector arrangement — serves to guide vessels into the fjord while warning of the rocks on the Prevlaka Peninsula across the narrow entrance. The cape is part of a Montenegrin-Croatian maritime boundary area with some historical complexity, and access has at times been limited by military considerations, though the cape itself is currently reachable from the Tivat side via a headland walk. In calm weather the view encompasses the mountains of Orjen to the north and the open sea south to Ulcinj.

## 4. Žukovica Lighthouse, Herceg Novi

The entrance to the inner bay of Boka Kotorska is marked on its northern side by the Žukovica light, a simple white cylindrical tower on a low rocky point near the historic town of Herceg Novi. The tower was built in the late nineteenth century and its light provides a sector guide for vessels entering and leaving the busy bay, which contains the Port of Bar's inner roadstead and several marinas. The automated light shows a group-flashing character with a nominal range of about 8 nautical miles. The tower is visible from the seafront promenade of Herceg Novi and from ferry services crossing the bay entrance. It is a working navigation aid and not open to visitors, but the combination of the Ottoman-era fortress above, the medieval town walls, and the lighthouse below makes the whole headland a rewarding area to explore on foot.

## 5. Bar Harbour Lighthouse, Bar

The port of Bar, Montenegro's only commercial deep-water harbor capable of handling cargo vessels of significant size, is marked by a pier-head light on the outer breakwater. The existing metal tower, modernised in the latter twentieth century, is primarily functional rather than historic, but it occupies an important position in the national lighthouse system: the port handles fuel imports and bulk cargo, and the approach from the south requires careful navigation past a series of shoals. The light is active and automated. The historic old town of Stari Bar, destroyed in part during the 1979 earthquake, lies several kilometres inland; the modern port and its lights are a visible expression of how the town's commercial centre shifted toward the coast during the Yugoslav period.

## 6. Ulcinj Harbour Light, Ulcinj

The southernmost of Montenegro's coastal towns, Ulcinj, sits close to the Albanian border and the Bojana River delta, a shallow and shifting system that has challenged navigators for centuries. The harbour light at Ulcinj marks the small-boat port used by fishing vessels and tourist craft, and was upgraded in the 1970s with a steel framework tower. Though of limited architectural interest, it marks a coast with a long historical resonance: Ulcinj was one of the last pirate strongholds of the Adriatic and the harbour has been in continuous use since the medieval period. The long Ada Bojana sandbar to the south, formed by the river delta, is navigable only by shallow-draft vessels and is not lit at its extremities.

## 7. Mamula Island Lighthouse, Boka Kotorska entrance

The small volcanic island of Mamula, placed precisely in the mouth of Boka Kotorska, carries a lighthouse that has operated since the Austro-Hungarian fortification of the island in the 1850s. The island is better known for the circular fortress built by Austrian general Lazar Mamula in 1853, which served as a concentration camp during the Second World War. The lighthouse itself is a modest installation on the seaward face of the island, providing a mid-channel mark for vessels entering the bay alongside the Cape Ostro and Žukovica sector lights. The island was for many years inaccessible but has recently been developed as a boutique hotel within the fortress walls, making it the most unusual lighthouse-adjacent accommodation in Montenegro.

## 8. Rose Peninsula Light, Boka Kotorska

Within the inner reaches of Boka Kotorska, the passage narrows dramatically at the Verige straits near Perast, and a series of small range and sector lights guide vessels through. The light at Rose (Ružica) marks the eastern shore of the passage and, together with the Dobreč point light on the opposite bank, forms a pair that shepherds vessels through the tightest section of the navigable channel. The Rose light is a simple lantern-on-post structure that was upgraded in the 1990s, lacking the architectural interest of the open-coast towers, but its position within the enclosed bay — surrounded by grey karst mountains plunging directly into the water and the baroque church towers of Perast visible to the north — gives it a setting of exceptional beauty.

## 9. Sv. Nedjelja Reef Beacon, off the Lustica Peninsula

The Lustica Peninsula divides the southern part of Boka Kotorska and the open sea approaches carry several submerged and partially submerged rock hazards. The Sv. Nedjelja reef beacon, a metal framework structure on a concrete base, marks one of the more dangerous of these, a shoal that extends westward from the peninsula tip. These offshore marks are maintained by the Port Authority and are not accessible, but they represent the functional core of the safe-water marking system for the approaches to Tivat and Kotor. The reef has claimed at least two vessels in the post-Second World War period and the beacon was installed as a direct response to these incidents.

## 10. Stari Bar Area Historical Beacons

The coast between the modern port of Bar and the historical anchorage below Stari Bar was once marked by a series of coloured-sector lanterns on the breakwater heads. Several of these remain in various states of decommission and represent the pre-Yugoslav lighthouse infrastructure of the Albanian coast transition zone. The original Austrian surveys of this coast, published in the 1880s, show a comprehensive system of small lights and beacons that was maintained with varying attention through the Balkan Wars, the two world wars, and the socialist period. Several original cast-iron lantern housings survive on the old harbour breakwaters and could form part of a future heritage interpretation project.

## Visiting Montenegro's Lighthouses

Most of Montenegro's open-coast lighthouses are reached on foot from the Jadranska magistrala (Adriatic highway), which runs the full length of the coast. Cape Mendra and Platamuni are accessible from Petrovac, with walks of 20 to 40 minutes. The Boka Kotorska lighthouses are best combined with boat trips on the bay: regular scheduled ferries cross from Kamenari to Lepetane and taxi boats operate from Kotor and Herceg Novi. Summer (June to September) brings excellent weather and long daylight hours but heavy tourist traffic. May and early October offer quieter conditions with reliable sun.
