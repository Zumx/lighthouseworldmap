Mozambique's coast stretches nearly 2,500 kilometres along the western shore of the Indian Ocean, from the Tanzanian border in the north to the South African frontier near Ponta do Ouro in the south. This immense coastline encompasses one of the most complex navigational environments in the southern hemisphere: coral reefs, shifting sandbars at the mouths of great rivers like the Zambezi and the Limpopo, the treacherous shoals of the Mozambique Channel, and some of the most remote and least-surveyed waters in the world. Portuguese navigators charted these waters from the late fifteenth century and established the first navigation marks — fires on hilltops and painted rocks — to guide the carracks of the India trade through the channel. Permanent lighthouse construction began under Portuguese colonial administration in the late nineteenth century, accelerating after the opening of the Suez Canal in 1869 transformed the Mozambique Channel from a primary route to a secondary one, but one still essential for vessels calling at East African ports. Many of these colonial-era towers survive, some maintained by the Instituto Nacional de Hidrografia e Navegação, others derelict since the 1975 independence and subsequent civil war.

## 1. Ilha de Moçambique Lighthouse (Goa Island), Ilha de Moçambique

The lighthouse on Goa Island — a small coral-reef islet immediately north of Ilha de Moçambique — is inextricably connected with one of Africa's most extraordinary pieces of maritime heritage. Ilha de Moçambique itself was the capital of Portuguese East Africa from the sixteenth until the late nineteenth century, and the stone fort of São Sebastião, built in 1558, is one of the oldest European fortifications in sub-Saharan Africa. The lighthouse on the adjacent Goa Island was constructed in the nineteenth century and stands as a white masonry tower visible for about 14 nautical miles, marking the northern approach to the island's anchorage and the reef systems that make arrival dangerous without guidance. Ilha de Moçambique is a UNESCO World Heritage Site and the lighthouse can be visited in combination with the fort and the historic town. Access is by the single causeway connecting the island to the mainland.

## 2. Inhaca Island Lighthouse, Inhaca

Inhaca Island guards the northern side of Maputo Bay, the large natural harbour at the mouth of which the Mozambican capital stands. The lighthouse on the northern tip of Inhaca is a significant navigational mark for vessels entering or leaving Maputo — one of southern Africa's major deep-water ports — and for dhow traffic navigating the complex channel between Inhaca and the mainland. The Portuguese-built tower, a white masonry cylinder of late nineteenth or early twentieth century construction, stands on a headland above the coral-fringed shore. The island is a marine research centre and national reserve, and access is by regular ferry from Maputo's waterfront, making the Inhaca lighthouse one of the more accessible on this coast. The surrounding reef and marine environment is considered among the best-preserved close to a major African city.

## 3. Bazaruto Island Lighthouse, Bazaruto

The Bazaruto Archipelago, a chain of sand islands about 30 kilometres offshore between the towns of Inhambane and Vilankulo, is protected as a national marine park and contains some of the finest coral reefs in the western Indian Ocean. The lighthouse on the southern tip of Bazaruto Island marks the dangerous shoals and submerged reefs that make navigation through the archipelago hazardous for any vessel drawing more than a metre or two. The original tower was a modest Portuguese installation; the structure has been maintained and modernised since independence. The light is critical for vessels servicing the archipelago's luxury island lodges and for the dive boats and fishing charters that operate in these waters. Dugong and manta rays are regularly sighted near the lighthouse point.

## 4. Pebane Lighthouse, Pebane

The small coastal town of Pebane, roughly midway along the Mozambique coast in Zambezia Province, was a minor Portuguese administrative post with a small harbour used by coastal dhow traders. The lighthouse here, built in the colonial period, marks a stretch of coast that is otherwise largely unlit — the central Mozambican coast between the Zambezi delta and the towns of Nacala and Mozambique Island is one of the least-lit coastlines in the world, and the Pebane light provides a critical reference for vessels on the Mozambique Channel passage. The tower is a white masonry structure that has suffered from decades of deferred maintenance but remains identifiable on navigation charts. Access to Pebane requires a significant overland journey on dirt roads, and the town has very limited visitor facilities.

## 5. Beira Harbour Lighthouse, Beira

The port of Beira, at the southern end of the Mozambique Channel, is the country's second city and one of the principal transit ports for landlocked Zambia, Zimbabwe, and Malawi. The approaches to Beira are notoriously difficult: the harbour entrance channel runs through an extensive intertidal sandflat system, and the combination of strong tidal currents, shifting bars, and the sediment load of the Pungwe River means the channel requires constant dredging and its lit marks frequent repositioning. The pier-head lighthouse at Beira is a functional modern tower, but the channel buoys and leading lights that guide large vessels in are the real operational challenge. The historic lighthouse structure of the Portuguese colonial port district survives in the city centre, a square tower incorporated into the waterfront infrastructure.

## 6. Sofala Bank Navigation Marks

The Sofala Bank, a broad shallow shelf extending offshore from the coast between Beira and the Zambezi delta, is one of the most extensive shoal areas in southern African waters. It is marked by a series of buoys and minor lights rather than manned towers, and represents the scale of the navigational challenge facing mariners on this coast. Portuguese navigators named this bank after the medieval gold-trading settlement of Sofala, and it appears on charts from the sixteenth century. The offshore platforms and floating marks maintaining safe-water guidance here are serviced by vessels of the Instituto Nacional de Hidrografia e Navegação from Maputo. The bank is a significant fishing ground and its navigational marks serve the trawler fleet operating from Beira.

## 7. Angoche Lighthouse, Angoche

Angoche (formerly António Enes) is a coastal town in Nampula Province with a history as an Arab trading settlement predating the Portuguese arrival. The lighthouse marking the approaches to Angoche's small harbour and the reef-strewn coast nearby is a colonial-period tower, now in uncertain condition. The Angoche reef system extends several kilometres offshore and was responsible for numerous wrecks during the dhow trading era. The surrounding coast forms part of the Primeiras and Segundas Archipelago environmental protection area, a chain of coral islands with some of the least-disturbed marine ecosystems in Mozambique. Access to Angoche is by road from Nampula or by light aircraft.

## 8. Pemba Harbour Light, Pemba

Pemba (formerly Porto Amélia) in the far north of Mozambique is the capital of Cabo Delgado Province and the main service port for the offshore natural gas developments that have transformed the regional economy since the 2010s. The harbour light at Pemba marks the entrance to Pemba Bay, one of the finest natural harbours on the East African coast. The colonial-period lighthouse was supplemented and in part replaced during the port expansion associated with the gas industry, but the position remains one of the most significant navigational marks on the northern coast. The waters around Pemba are known for excellent diving on relatively pristine coral reefs, despite the industrial development offshore.

## 9. Ibo Island Light, Quirimbas Archipelago

The Quirimbas Archipelago, a chain of coral islands stretching along the northern Mozambican coast toward Tanzania, contains some of the oldest Portuguese settlement sites in sub-Saharan Africa. Ibo Island's fortified town, with its eighteenth-century fort of São João Baptista, is a heritage site of considerable significance. The navigational marks in the Quirimbas were originally managed from Ibo, which served as the regional administrative centre for much of the colonial period. The narrow reef-girt channels between the islands require local pilotage knowledge and the minor lights installed during the Portuguese period provide orientation rather than complete safe-water guidance. Ibo is accessible by light aircraft from Pemba or by dhow from the mainland.

## 10. Maputo Port Lighthouse, Maputo

The commercial harbour of Maputo, formerly Lourenço Marques, is the country's principal port and gateway. The lighthouse at the port entrance, a steel tower on the breakwater, marks the deep-water channel that runs through Maputo Bay. The bay itself is one of the largest natural harbours in Africa, and the lighthouse serves the container ships, bulk carriers, and tankers that call at Mozambique's most modern port. The Maputo waterfront has undergone significant renovation and the lighthouse area is accessible from the harbour promenade. Nearby is the old colonial customs house and the broad Avenida Marginal, giving the lighthouse a setting that combines working port infrastructure with the elegant if faded architecture of the Portuguese colonial capital.

## Visiting Mozambique's Lighthouses

Mozambique's lighthouse circuit requires a combination of internal flights, boat transfers, and long road journeys on often difficult surfaces. Inhaca Island is the most accessible, reached by a 90-minute ferry from Maputo. Ilha de Moçambique can be reached by bus or car from Nampula. The Bazaruto lighthouses are most practically visited as part of a resort stay on the archipelago. The best time to visit most of the coast is the dry season between May and October, avoiding both the cyclone season and the heaviest rains. Northern Mozambique's security situation in Cabo Delgado Province should be checked before travel.
