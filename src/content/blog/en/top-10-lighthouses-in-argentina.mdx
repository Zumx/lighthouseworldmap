Argentina's coastline sweeps nearly 5,000 kilometres from the Río de la Plata estuary to the archipelagos of Tierra del Fuego, taking in the Patagonian steppe, the Valdés Peninsula, and the windswept approaches to the Drake Passage. This coast has shaped a substantial lighthouse heritage: the Argentine Navy's Servicio de Hidrografía Naval has maintained navigational lights here since the nineteenth century, many of them installed to protect the growing wool and cattle trade routes between Patagonian ports and Buenos Aires or Montevideo. The southernmost stations, built at the threshold of Antarctic waters, are among the most dramatic lighthouse settings in the world.

## 1. Faro del Fin del Mundo, Isla de los Estados

Completed in 1884 on the eastern end of Isla de los Estados (Staten Island) in the Beagle Channel approaches, the Faro de San Juan de Salvamento earned international fame through Jules Verne's novel 'The Lighthouse at the End of the World', published in 1905 and set at this very station. The original iron tower stood 11 metres tall and showed a fixed white light visible for about 10 nautical miles over the Paso del Mar channel, where ships rounding the Horn had to negotiate treacherous currents and rocks. The lighthouse was decommissioned in 1902 and the island became a nature reserve. A replica was constructed near the original site in 1998, maintaining the historical character of the tower. Visiting requires an expedition by boat; sea conditions around Isla de los Estados are severe, and organised tours depart from Ushuaia seasonally.

## 2. Faro Les Éclaireurs, Canal Beagle

Les Éclaireurs lighthouse sits on a small rocky islet in the Beagle Channel east of Ushuaia, completed in 1920 to mark the narrow navigable passage between the numerous islands of the Tierra del Fuego archipelago. The red-and-white banded tower stands 11 metres and shows a flashing white light every five seconds, with a range of approximately 9 nautical miles. It is frequently misidentified as the 'lighthouse at the end of the world' by tourists, a confusion that annoys maritime historians but does nothing to diminish the appeal of the setting: the tower rises from a bare rock surrounded by the cold blue-green water of the Beagle Channel, with the snowy peaks of the Chilean Andes visible to the south. The lighthouse is active and automated but accessible only by boat; day cruises from Ushuaia pass it regularly.

## 3. Faro Querandí, Buenos Aires Province

Built in 1922 on the Atlantic coast of Buenos Aires Province north of Mar del Plata, the Faro Querandí is a slender white masonry tower rising 54 metres — one of the tallest lighthouses in Argentina. It marks a featureless stretch of sandy coastline where the main hazard is shoaling rather than rocks, and its height compensates for the complete absence of natural elevation. The light character is a white flash every fifteen seconds, visible for 22 nautical miles. The lighthouse stands within a protected coastal area now designated as the Faro Querandí nature reserve, which shelters a remnant coastal scrub habitat. Visits are possible; the reserve has a ranger station and the surroundings can be explored on foot, though the tower interior is not generally open.

## 4. Faro Cabo San Antonio, Buenos Aires Province

Cabo San Antonio marks the northern entrance to the Bahía Samborombón and the approach to the Río de la Plata from the south. The current tower, completed in 1904, stands 42 metres and exhibits a group-flashing white light with a range of 23 nautical miles, marking the shoal-prone transition from the open Atlantic into the estuary. The cape itself is a low sandy promontory — there is no cliff to add height — and the tower's design accounts for this with substantial masonry construction. The area around the cape is a natural reserve protecting nesting sea turtles and shore birds. The lighthouse is active and maintained by the Argentine Navy; access requires prior arrangement.

## 5. Faro Punta Mogotes, Mar del Plata

Punta Mogotes lighthouse, completed in 1890 and standing 28 metres tall in the coastal zone south of Mar del Plata, is the lighthouse most accessible to Argentina's largest recreational boating community. It marks a rocky point at the southern entrance to the Mar del Plata harbour area and shows a flashing white light visible for 20 nautical miles. The lighthouse has been integrated into a small tourist complex; the tower can be climbed for views over the Plata coast, and the surrounding area is busy with visitors in the summer season. The original Fresnel lens, now displayed rather than operational, is a second-order apparatus of Belgian manufacture installed in 1921.

## 6. Faro Punta Delgada, Chubut

Punta Delgada occupies the south-eastern tip of the Valdés Peninsula, one of the most biologically significant coastal reserves in South America. The lighthouse, built in 1905, stands 23 metres on a cliff that adds considerable height to its focal plane, and its flashing white character carries about 22 nautical miles over the Golfo San Jorge approach. The peninsula below the lighthouse is one of the primary elephant seal haul-out beaches in Patagonia. A historic estancia building near the lighthouse has been converted to accommodation, and staying here provides one of the most unusual lighthouse lodging experiences in South America: the sound of the seals and the flash of the light through the window form a particular atmosphere unique to this coast.

## 7. Faro San Gregorio, Santa Cruz Province

The San Gregorio lighthouse on the Patagonian coast of Santa Cruz Province marks an isolated headland on the western shore of the Golfo San Jorge. Completed in 1916 on a cliff above a pebbled beach, the 18-metre tower shows a white flash every ten seconds with a range of 18 nautical miles. Its setting is characteristic Patagonian steppe: guanacos graze the dry grassland around the station, the wind off the South Atlantic is persistent and cold even in summer, and the coast gives way to colonies of Magellanic penguins at nearby beaches. The lighthouse is maintained by the Armada Argentina and staffed; access is by unpaved road from the nearest town.

## 8. Faro Isla Pingüino, Santa Cruz

The lighthouse on Isla Pingüino (Penguin Island), a small island off Puerto Deseado, was completed in 1941 to mark the approaches to the natural harbour. The tower stands 15 metres and shows a group-flashing white character visible for 16 nautical miles. The island takes its name from the large colony of rockhopper penguins that nest there from October to April, and the lighthouse station occupies the same island as this spectacular wildlife concentration. Boat excursions from Puerto Deseado visit the island; landing is permitted in designated areas and the penguins are accustomed to visitors.

## 9. Faro Cabo Vírgenes, Santa Cruz

Cabo Vírgenes, at the entrance to the Strait of Magellan, carries one of the most historically significant lights on the Argentine coast. The cape was named by Magellan himself in 1520. The lighthouse, completed in 1904 and standing 14 metres on a flat coastal plain, marks the Atlantic entrance to the Strait — one of the most important navigational waypoints in the Southern Ocean. Its flashing white light has a range of 22 nautical miles. The cape is also home to the largest Magellanic penguin colony in the world, estimated at over 100,000 breeding pairs. A provincial nature reserve protects the area and receives visitors throughout the penguin season; the lighthouse grounds are accessible and the tower is sometimes open.

## 10. Faro de Ushuaia, Tierra del Fuego

The lighthouse at Ushuaia, the southernmost city in the world, was completed in 1920 to guide vessels into the harbour from the Beagle Channel. The tower is modest in scale — about 11 metres — but its position on the waterfront of Ushuaia gives it a prominence that larger, more remote stations lack. It marks the entrance to the inner harbour used by Antarctic expedition vessels, cruise ships, and the Argentine naval base. The light character is a white flash every five seconds, visible for 7 nautical miles in the sheltered channel. The lighthouse is one of the most photographed in Argentina; the snowcapped mountains of the Andes form the backdrop to the south, and the channel waters reflect the lights of the city after dark.

## Visiting Argentina's Lighthouses

Argentina's lighthouse heritage spans from the subtropical Río de la Plata to the sub-Antarctic waters of Tierra del Fuego. The most accessible stations are in Buenos Aires Province and near Ushuaia; the Patagonian lights require more effort, typically combining coastal road travel with boat excursions. The summer season (November to March) offers the best weather, and coincides with penguin and elephant seal activity near many southern stations. The Servicio de Hidrografía Naval maintains the active lights; some are open to organised visits while others require advance arrangement.
