Tanzania's Indian Ocean coastline and the island of Zanzibar have been maritime crossroads for over a thousand years, at the heart of the Swahili trading world that connected East Africa to the Persian Gulf, India and China. The dhow routes that brought ivory, gold and later enslaved people to Arab merchants also demanded coastal navigation aids, and from the late nineteenth century the German colonial administration and then the British systematically equipped the coast with lighthouses. The islands of Zanzibar, Mafia and Pemba, together with the offshore reefs of the Tanzanian continental shelf, create a navigation challenge of considerable complexity: coral reefs, seasonal monsoon-driven sea states, and the tidal currents that funnel through narrow inter-island passages all demand reliable lights. Tanzania's lighthouse heritage bridges Swahili maritime tradition, German Kaiserliche Admiralität engineering, and British colonial construction.

## 1. Chumbe Island Lighthouse, Zanzibar

The Chumbe Island Lighthouse, standing on a small coral island about 12 kilometres south of Zanzibar Town, was built by the British administration in 1904 and stands 22 metres tall on the island's highest coral ridge, displaying a flashing white light with a range of about 15 nautical miles. It guided vessels working the passage between Zanzibar and the Tanzanian mainland coast through the shallow and reef-studded Zanzibar Channel. After Tanzania's independence, Chumbe Island fell into neglect, but in the 1990s it became the centrepiece of a pioneering private marine conservation project. The surrounding reef was established as the Chumbe Island Coral Park, one of Africa's first privately managed marine reserves, and the lighthouse was restored as a heritage structure. Today Chumbe Island is accessible by arranged day-trip or overnight stay from Zanzibar Town, and the combination of the historic lighthouse, the intact coral reef and the forest reserve makes it one of the finest environmental tourism destinations in East Africa.

## 2. Ras Nungwi Lighthouse, Zanzibar

At the northern tip of Zanzibar Island, Ras Nungwi is the point that vessels must round when moving between the open Indian Ocean and the sheltered Zanzibar Channel. The lighthouse at Ras Nungwi was established in the colonial period and marks the rocky northern headland and the shallow ledges extending offshore from the cape. Nungwi village is now one of the most visited beach resorts in East Africa, and the lighthouse stands somewhat incongruously amid a landscape of tourist bungalows and beach bars. Despite its setting, the lighthouse retains its operational function: the passage around Ras Nungwi in the northeast monsoon, when strong winds funnel against the island's northern tip, remains genuinely demanding for traditional dhows and small coastal craft. The lighthouse is visible from the beach and can be approached on foot from the village.

## 3. Dar es Salaam Lighthouse, Dar es Salaam

The lighthouse at the entrance to Dar es Salaam's natural harbour is integral to the operation of Tanzania's principal commercial port. The city's harbour, one of the finest natural anchorages on the East African coast, was developed by the German colonial administration from the 1880s and the British from 1919, and the lighthouse at the harbour mouth has guided the container ships, tankers and ferry vessels that make Dar es Salaam the hub of Tanzania's maritime trade. The current lighthouse structure reflects post-independence Tanzanian maintenance, and while it lacks the heritage character of the island lighthouses, it performs an essential function for one of East Africa's busiest ports. The lighthouse can be seen from the Kigamboni ferry crossing and from the sea approach to the harbour.

## 4. Kilwa Lighthouse, Kilwa Kisiwani

Kilwa Kisiwani — 'Kilwa on the Island' — was the greatest trading city of the medieval Swahili coast, its merchants controlling the gold trade from Great Zimbabwe to the Indian Ocean world. The ruins of the palace of Husuni Kubwa, the Great Mosque and the Portuguese fort on the island have been designated a UNESCO World Heritage Site. A lighthouse was established on Kilwa Kisiwani in the colonial period to mark the approach to the Kilwa Channel, a treacherous passage through coral reefs that has tested navigators since the first Arab dhows arrived here in the ninth century. The lighthouse is a modest structure compared to the ancient monuments surrounding it, but its position within the UNESCO site gives it an archaeological and cultural resonance shared by few navigational marks anywhere in the world. Kilwa Kisiwani is accessible by boat from Kilwa Masoko on the mainland.

## 5. Mafia Island Lighthouse, Mafia Island

Mafia Island, south of Zanzibar and Dar es Salaam, is the least developed of Tanzania's principal islands and the centre of a marine park protecting the large coral reef system on the island's southeastern side. The lighthouse on Mafia was established during the British period to guide vessels navigating the Mafia Channel between the island and the mainland, a passage carrying coastal cargo traffic and dhows bound for the ports of southern Tanzania and Mozambique. The Mafia Channel is subject to strong tidal currents and has extensive shoal areas on its mainland side, making the lighthouse essential for night passages. Mafia Island is reached by small aircraft from Dar es Salaam and is known principally for its diving, whale shark encounters and traditional dhow-building communities.

## 6. Ras Kipingwe Lighthouse, Kilifi area

The lighthouse at Ras Kipingwe marks a headland along Tanzania's northern coast near the Kenyan border, guiding coastal traffic through the passages between the mainland shore and the offshore coral reefs. This stretch of coast was historically active with dhow traffic between the Swahili port towns of Mombasa (just over the Kenyan border), Malindi and the Tanzanian coast, and the lights marking the headlands played a central role in the night navigation of these shallow inshore routes.

## 7. Pemba Island Lighthouse, Pemba

Pemba Island, north of Zanzibar and known for its clove plantations and exceptional scuba diving, has a lighthouse that guides vessels through the Pemba Channel — a deep and often turbulent passage between the island and the mainland. The Pemba Channel is noted for strong southerly swells during the southeast monsoon and is a significant obstacle for small vessels. The lighthouse was established in the British colonial period and is maintained by the Tanzania Ports Authority. Pemba is less visited than Zanzibar but offers a more authentic picture of the traditional Swahili island economy, and the lighthouse can be incorporated into an island circuit for visitors arriving by ferry or light aircraft.

## 8. Mtwara Harbour Light, Mtwara

The port of Mtwara in southern Tanzania, close to the Mozambique border, was developed by the British as a groundnut export terminal in the 1950s and has subsequently grown into an important commercial harbour serving the southern mainland region. The lighthouse at the Mtwara harbour mouth marks the entry to Mikindani Bay and guides coastal cargo vessels and dhows making the passage along this relatively isolated stretch of coast. Mtwara has gained strategic importance with the discovery of offshore natural gas reserves in the southern Tanzania basin, and the harbour's navigational aids are increasingly important for the supply and survey vessels that service the offshore industry.

## 9. Tanga Lighthouse, Tanga

Tanga in northeastern Tanzania was the German colonial railway terminus, the landing point for the central Uganda Railway that was built in the early twentieth century to open the interior. The port developed a lighthouse to guide vessels into the sheltered harbour of Tanga Creek, a long tidal inlet that provides good protection but requires careful navigation through shoal water. The lighthouse at Tanga combines German colonial history with the sisal and cargo trade that sustained the town through the twentieth century. Tanga is now also a departure point for Pemba Island and the northern Zanzibar Archipelago, and its harbour light continues to serve this inter-island traffic.

## 10. Latham Island Light, offshore Tanzania

Latham Island, a low coral and sand island about 70 kilometres south of Zanzibar, sits in the middle of the Zanzibar Channel and carries a light that marks this isolated mid-channel hazard for vessels crossing between the Tanzanian mainland ports and the Mozambique Channel. The island is little more than a sand cay with a seabird colony, significant for nesting boobies and other tropical seabirds; the lighthouse is an unattended automated structure that has been maintained as an important aid to navigation for the busy Zanzibar Channel traffic. Latham Island is occasionally visited by research vessels and dive charters but has no regular public access.

## Practical Information

Tanzania's lighthouse circuit is best approached as part of a broader East African coastal itinerary. The Zanzibar lighthouses (Chumbe, Ras Nungwi) are the most accessible and combine naturally with the island's historical and beach tourism. Kilwa Kisiwani requires a specific effort but rewards with one of the finest combinations of medieval heritage and maritime history in Africa. The southeast monsoon (June to September) brings rough seas to the southern coast; the northeast monsoon (December to March) is calmer on the northern coast and is the preferred season for crossing to Pemba. Dhow safaris from Zanzibar Town offer an atmospheric way to reach several of the island lights.
