Uruguay's coastline is short by South American standards — roughly 660 kilometres from the Argentine border on the Río de la Plata to the Brazilian frontier near Chuy — but it encompasses two quite distinct maritime environments. The western coast faces the Río de la Plata, one of the widest river estuaries on earth; its shallow, fast-changing sandbars and strong currents in a body of water that is technically a river but behaves like a sea demanded early navigational marking. The eastern coast, exposed to the South Atlantic from roughly Maldonado onward, faces open ocean swells and is characterised by rocky headlands, offshore islands, and seal colonies. Uruguay's lighthouse network was established in the nineteenth century, primarily during the period of political consolidation under Presidents Batlle, with the Armada Nacional (the Uruguayan Navy) taking responsibility for construction and maintenance. The Uruguayan lights, modest in scale compared with those of Argentina or Brazil, carry considerable individuality. ## 1. Isla de Lobos Lighthouse, Punta del Este Department

Isla de Lobos is a small rocky island about 10 kilometres offshore from Punta del Este, best known as the site of one of the world's largest South American sea lion colonies — up to 200,000 animals at peak season. The lighthouse here is the tallest in Uruguay, a white cylindrical iron tower completed in 1874 and standing approximately 47 metres above sea level. Its light character is a group flashing white sequence visible around 26 nautical miles — the most powerful light on the Uruguayan coast. The tower was engineered to mark the outer approaches to the Río de la Plata from the Atlantic, a waypoint where vessels choosing the southern deep-water channel must alter course. The island is administered by the Armada Nacional; it is accessible only by boat and only with prior permission through authorised tour operators. The combination of a working lighthouse and a massive seal colony makes it one of the most remarkable accessible wildlife sites in South America.

## 2. José Ignacio Lighthouse, José Ignacio

José Ignacio is a small promontory on the eastern Uruguayan coast between Punta del Este and La Paloma, the site of a lighthouse that has become as famous as an architectural and cultural landmark as for its navigational function. The white cylindrical tower, built in 1877 and standing 32 metres tall, marks the rocky headland and the approach to the Laguna José Ignacio. Its light character is a flashing white with a range of about 20 nautical miles. José Ignacio has transformed in recent decades from a fishing village into one of South America's most fashionable resort destinations; the lighthouse stands at the end of the peninsula surrounded by boutique hotels, celebrated restaurants, and the residences of artists and writers who have been drawn to the quality of the Atlantic light. The tower is open to visitors in summer, and the view from the gallery over the beaches and the offshore seal rocks is exceptional.

## 3. Cabo Polonio Lighthouse, Cabo Polonio

Cabo Polonio occupies a dramatic rocky headland on the Atlantic coast of the Rocha department, a place of such natural integrity that motor vehicle access is prohibited — visitors arrive by tracked all-terrain vehicle or on foot across the dunes. The lighthouse here, a white cylindrical tower built in 1881 and standing 26 metres tall, marks one of the most dangerous stretches of the Uruguayan coast: the rocky promontory extends into the sea and offshore banks add to the hazard. The light character is a flashing white sequence visible about 22 nautical miles. The village of Cabo Polonio — perhaps 100 permanent inhabitants, with hundreds more summer visitors — clusters below the lighthouse in a landscape of sand dunes, sea lions sunbathing on the rocks, and Atlantic swells breaking over the offshore reefs. The lighthouse is the physical and psychological centre of this extraordinary community, visible from all parts of the settlement and from far offshore.

## 4. Punta Brava Lighthouse, Montevideo

Punta Brava on the western outskirts of Montevideo marks the outer limit of the Río de la Plata approach to the Uruguayan capital. The lighthouse, a white and red banded tower standing 31 metres tall and first lit in 1876, carries a flashing white and red sector light with a range of about 23 nautical miles. The red sectors cover the shoal water extending from the Punta Brava headland and from the Banco Inglés, a notorious sandbank in the estuary. Montevideo's harbour receives some of the largest container vessels and cruise ships operating on the South American east coast, and Punta Brava is the first lighthouse they pass when approaching from the open Atlantic. The tower is situated in the Punta Brava neighborhood on the Rambla, Montevideo's celebrated waterfront promenade, and can be viewed easily from the rambla road.

## 5. Punta Carretas Lighthouse, Montevideo

Within Montevideo itself, the Punta Carretas lighthouse marks a rocky point at the southern extremity of the city, dividing the Playa Pocitos to the east from the older residential waterfront. The octagonal white tower, completed in 1874 and reaching 22 metres, carries a flashing white and red sector light with a range of about 15 nautical miles. The neighbourhood of Punta Carretas is named for the lighthouse area and is one of Montevideo's most elegant residential districts, known for the conversion of its former prison — the Cárcel de Punta Carretas — into a shopping centre in the 1990s. The lighthouse stands on a small rocky promontory maintained as a green space, easily accessible on foot from the Rambla.

## 6. Punta del Este Lighthouse, Maldonado

The famous resort peninsula of Punta del Este carries its own lighthouse, a white cylindrical tower that has marked this conspicuous land feature since 1860. The tower stands 27 metres tall at the end of the peninsula where the Río de la Plata meets the Atlantic — a geographical transition point well understood by mariners since Portuguese and Spanish explorers first mapped the coast in the sixteenth century. The light character is a flashing white visible about 20 nautical miles. The lighthouse stands among the marina and luxury apartment towers of modern Punta del Este, a juxtaposition of nineteenth-century navigational infrastructure and twenty-first-century resort development. It is one of the few Uruguayan lighthouses that is formally open to visitors year-round, as its position in the resort centre ensures maintenance funding and tourist interest.

## 7. La Paloma Lighthouse, Rocha

La Paloma, on the Rocha department coast, is one of Uruguay's most active fishing ports and surf destinations. The lighthouse marks the harbour entrance and the Cape Santa María headland, which extends into the sea creating a significant hazard for vessels navigating south from the Río de la Plata. The white tower, approximately 20 metres tall, dates from the early twentieth century; its light character is a flashing white with range around 17 nautical miles. La Paloma's harbour is also the departure point for excursions to the Cabo Polonio coast, and the fishing fleet here targets weakfish, corvina, and squid in the productive Atlantic waters offshore.

## 8. Colonia del Sacramento Lighthouse, Colonia del Sacramento

Colonia del Sacramento is a UNESCO World Heritage historic quarter, the oldest colonial town in Uruguay, founded by the Portuguese in 1680 on the Río de la Plata opposite Buenos Aires. Its lighthouse, a working tower built within the town's historic fortifications, dates from 1857 and occupies a converted bastion of the original Portuguese defences. The white cylindrical tower stands about 19 metres tall and carries a flashing white light visible about 15 nautical miles, serving the ferry traffic that crosses the 50-kilometre estuary to and from Buenos Aires. The lighthouse is one of only a handful of working lights built into a UNESCO World Heritage Site, and it can be climbed for views over the Plata and toward the Buenos Aires skyline on clear days.

## 9. Punta Negra Lighthouse, Maldonado

Between Montevideo and Punta del Este, the rocky headland of Punta Negra marks a transition in coastal character from the sandy Plata shores to the rockier Atlantic coast. The lighthouse here, a modest white structure, dates from the early twentieth century and carries a flashing character covering the inshore rocks. Punta Negra has developed as a residential coast since the mid-twentieth century; the lighthouse stands in a residential area above the rocky shore that gives the headland its name.

## 10. Isla de Flores Lighthouse, Río de la Plata

Isla de Flores is a small rocky island in the Río de la Plata about 20 kilometres southwest of Montevideo, historically used as a quarantine station for vessels arriving at the Uruguayan capital. The lighthouse, a white tower on the island's highest point, marks this isolated landmass in the middle of the estuary, warning vessels on the deep-water channel of the island's position. The island is administered as a nature reserve; its colonial quarantine buildings survive in varying states of preservation, and the surrounding waters are shallow enough that the island's rocky base is visible from a considerable distance. Access is by private boat arrangement.

## Visiting Uruguay's Lighthouses

Uruguay's lighthouse circuit is one of the more accessible in South America. The Rambla in Montevideo connects several urban lights within walking or cycling distance. Punta del Este and José Ignacio are straightforward summer destinations, though prices and crowds peak in January and February; the shoulder season of November-December and March offers better conditions. Cabo Polonio requires the tracked vehicle shuttle from the highway and a willingness to accept that facilities are minimal — no mains electricity, no paved roads. The Isla de Lobos excursion requires advance booking through authorised operators in Punta del Este.
