The Texas coast is a low, sandy edge of barrier islands, shallow lagoons, and shifting passes where the Gulf of Mexico meets a string of broad, brackish bays. There are no rocky headlands here, so the lights were raised on cast iron cylinders and skeletal frames rather than masonry crags. They marked the narrow inlets where vessels crossed from the open Gulf into Galveston Bay, Matagorda Bay, Aransas Pass, and the Laguna Madre. Hurricanes, erosion, and the silting of channels have erased some and stranded others far from the water, but the surviving towers trace the whole sweep of the coast from the Sabine to the Rio Grande.

## 1. Bolivar Point Lighthouse

Standing on the Bolivar Peninsula across the channel from Galveston, this 35-metre (116-foot) cast iron tower dates to 1872, replacing an earlier 1852 light dismantled for its iron during the Civil War. Its black-painted plates make it unmistakable. During the catastrophic 1900 Galveston hurricane and again in 1915, residents climbed the spiral stair and clung to the lantern while the storm surge drowned the land below. Decommissioned in 1933, it is privately owned and not open to the public, but it is easily viewed from the Bolivar ferry.

## 2. Galveston Bay Entrance / Galveston Jetty Light

The deep-water entrance to Galveston Bay, the busiest pass on the Texas coast, is guarded today by a modern light on the South Jetty rather than a classic tower. The jetties themselves, built of granite in the late nineteenth century, channel the tide and keep the bar scoured. The light marks the inbound track for the ship channel that serves Galveston and Houston, one of the largest petrochemical ports in the world. It is best seen from the Galveston seawall or from the deck of the Bolivar ferry.

## 3. Matagorda Island Lighthouse

This handsome 24-metre (79-foot) cast iron tower was first lit in 1852 at the entrance to Pass Cavallo, the historic gateway into Matagorda Bay. Damaged in the Civil War and threatened by erosion, the whole tower was dismantled and relocated about three kilometres inland in 1873. Painted black, it still stands within the Matagorda Island State Park and Wildlife Management Area, reachable only by boat from Port O'Connor. The remote setting, all marsh grass and shorebirds, gives a vivid sense of how isolated these keepers once were.

## 4. Lydia Ann Lighthouse (Aransas Pass)

Also called the Aransas Pass Light, this brick tower near Port Aransas was first established in 1857 to mark the pass between Mustang and San José Islands. Confederate forces damaged it during the war; it was rebuilt and relit in 1867. The 12-metre tower, with its distinctive buff-and-white daymark, was restored privately and still shows a light. It sits on private land but is one of the most photographed sights on the central coast, viewed from the Lydia Ann Channel as boats run between Port Aransas and the bay.

## 5. Half Moon Reef Lighthouse

A rare survivor of the screwpile era on the Gulf, Half Moon Reef Light was built in 1858 on iron piles screwed into the soft bottom of Matagorda Bay. The hexagonal cottage-style structure carried its keeper above the open water of the bay. After decommissioning and decades of decay, it was moved ashore and beautifully restored at Port Lavaca, where it now stands beside the harbour as a museum piece, open to visitors and a fine example of the bay-light design once common along the coast.

## 6. Point Isabel Lighthouse

Near the mouth of the Rio Grande at Port Isabel, this brick tower of 1852 is the only Texas lighthouse open to the public for climbing. At about 17 metres (57 feet), it guided shipping into the Brazos Santiago Pass and was a landmark during the Mexican-American War. Now a Texas state historic site, its lantern offers a sweeping view over the Laguna Madre and out to South Padre Island. The whitewashed tower and adjacent keeper's cottage make it the best single lighthouse visit in the state.

## 7. Sabine Bank Lighthouse

Far offshore at the eastern end of the Texas coast, Sabine Bank Light was completed in 1906 as a cast iron caisson tower planted on a submerged shoal in the Gulf. Roughly 22 metres tall, it warned ships off the dangerous bank as they approached the Sabine Pass and the ports of Port Arthur and Beaumont. Its original lantern was removed to a museum in Port Arthur, and the stub of the caisson still stands offshore, automated and visible only from the water or air.

## 8. Sabine Pass Lighthouse

On the Louisiana side of the Sabine River mouth but long serving Texas shipping, this unusual 1856 brick tower has six buttressed fins flaring from its base, a design meant to resist the soft marshland and storm winds. It marked the entrance to Sabine Lake and the inland ports. Decommissioned in 1952 and now privately owned, the weathered tower remains a striking sight across the marshes, a reminder of how the Texas-Louisiana border lights worked the same Gulf approaches.

## 9. Brazos Santiago / South Padre Approaches

The Brazos Santiago Pass at the southern tip of Texas separates South Padre Island from Brazos Island and forms the seaward entrance to the Port of Brownsville. Lit by minor lights and beacons today, the pass was historically marked in concert with the Point Isabel tower inland. Jetties built in the twentieth century stabilised the inlet. The modern entrance lights here mark the southernmost navigable Gulf entrance in the United States, just a few kilometres from the Rio Grande.

## 10. Redfish Bar and the Houston Ship Channel Lights

Inside Galveston Bay, the long Houston Ship Channel is studded with range lights and beacons that march across the shallow water toward the refineries upstream. The historic Redfish Bar once forced a dogleg in the channel where dredges cut a passage through the oyster reef dividing the upper and lower bay. These working lights lack the romance of a tall tower, but they carry more tonnage past their beams than any classic Texas lighthouse ever did, and they show how the coast's navigation has shifted inland.
