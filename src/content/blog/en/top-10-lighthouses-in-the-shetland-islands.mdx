Shetland is the end of Britain. The archipelago sits roughly 160 kilometres north of the Scottish mainland, closer to Bergen in Norway than to Edinburgh, a place of treeless moorland, deep sea inlets called voes, and cliffs that drop sheer into a grey and restless ocean. Its lighthouses are the northernmost in the British Isles, built to mark a coast where the North Sea, the Atlantic and the Norwegian Sea converge and where winter storms can blow for days. Many are the work of the Stevenson engineering family, and the greatest of them, Muckle Flugga, stands on a sea-battered rock that seemed impossible to build on. A lighthouse tour of Shetland is a journey to the literal edge of the country.

## Sumburgh Head

The southern tip of the Shetland Mainland ends in the dramatic headland of Sumburgh Head, where cliffs rise more than a hundred metres above the sea. The lighthouse here was built by Robert Stevenson in 1821, the first in Shetland, and its white tower and keepers' buildings now house a celebrated visitor centre and nature reserve. The cliffs below teem with puffins, guillemots and razorbills in summer, and the surrounding waters are a noted spot for watching whales and dolphins. Sumburgh is the most accessible of Shetland's great lights, close to the airport, and it makes the ideal introduction to the archipelago.

## Muckle Flugga

North of Unst, the northernmost inhabited island, lies the rock of Muckle Flugga, and on it stands the most famous lighthouse in Shetland. Built by the Stevensons in 1854 and made permanent in 1858, it rises from a wave-lashed stack where seas in storms break clean over the rock fifty metres up. The young Robert Louis Stevenson visited his family's works here, and the island of Unst is sometimes claimed as an inspiration for the map in Treasure Island. Muckle Flugga is the most northerly lighthouse in the British Isles, beyond which there is nothing but the open sea toward the Arctic.

## Eshaness

On the wild northwest coast of the Mainland, the Eshaness lighthouse stands atop sheer volcanic cliffs sculpted into stacks, blowholes and natural arches by the full force of the Atlantic. Built in 1929 by David A. Stevenson, it is a square tower of a later style than the classic round towers, and it guards one of the most spectacular stretches of coast in Britain. The cliffs here show the remains of an ancient volcano, and storms drive spray and even boulders far inland. It is a place to feel the raw power of the northern sea.

## Bressay

Guarding the southern approach to Lerwick, Shetland's only town and its busy harbour, the lighthouse on the island of Bressay was built by David and Thomas Stevenson in 1858. It marks the entrance to Bressay Sound, the sheltered channel that has made Lerwick a haven for fishing fleets, ferries and the great oil-industry support vessels that now use the port. The keepers' cottages have been used as accommodation, and the light remains a key mark for the constant traffic in and out of the capital.

## Out Skerries

Far to the east of the Mainland lie the Out Skerries, a tiny cluster of inhabited islands that form the easternmost point of Shetland. The lighthouse on Bound Skerry, built by David and Thomas Stevenson in 1858, stands on the outermost rock, the first British light a vessel meets crossing from Scandinavia. The Skerries are remote even by Shetland standards, reached by a small ferry or flight, and the light marks a perilous scatter of reefs that have wrecked many ships over the centuries.

## Fair Isle

Midway between Shetland and Orkney sits Fair Isle, one of the most isolated inhabited islands in Britain, famous for its knitting patterns and its bird observatory. The island carries two lighthouses, Fair Isle South and Fair Isle North, both built by the Stevensons in 1892. They were the last lighthouses in Scotland to be automated, in 1998, ending a long tradition of resident keepers. The South light has a poignant history: its keepers' cottages were bombed during the Second World War, and lives were lost. The island lies on a major bird migration route, drawing ornithologists from around the world.

## Esha and the Stevenson Method

Building lighthouses in Shetland tested the Stevensons to the limit. The remoteness, the ferocious seas and the short building seasons forced ingenious solutions. At Muckle Flugga, materials had to be hauled up the rock by hand and the men sometimes marooned for weeks. The engineers refined techniques of dovetailed masonry, learned on the Bell Rock far to the south, and adapted them to stacks that no earlier builder would have attempted. The result is a set of towers that have stood for over a century and a half against some of the worst weather in Europe.

## Lights at the Top of Britain

What makes Shetland's lights distinctive is their sheer northerliness and the quality of the light around them. In high summer the sun barely sets, the famous simmer dim, and the towers stand against skies that never fully darken. In winter the nights are long and the lights burn for most of the day. The Norse heritage is everywhere, in place names, in the fire festival of Up Helly Aa at Lerwick in January, and in the sense that this is a Scandinavian as much as a Scottish place. The lighthouses anchor that identity, marking the seaward edge of a culture poised between two worlds.

## Visiting Shetland's Lights

Shetland is reached by overnight ferry from Aberdeen or by air to Sumburgh. The best season is from late May to August, when the long daylight and calmer seas make the outer islands accessible and the seabird colonies are at their peak. Sumburgh Head and Eshaness are easily reached by road; Muckle Flugga is best seen from a boat trip or from the cliffs of Hermaness on Unst, a fine walk through a national nature reserve. Fair Isle and the Out Skerries need careful planning around ferries and weather. Dress for wind and rain even in midsummer.
