There is a tension at the heart of every lighthouse trip. The conditions that make a lighthouse most beautiful — wild seas, low dramatic light, storm skies, empty headlands — are precisely the conditions in which the tower is closed, the ferry cancelled and the keeper's cottage shuttered for winter. The conditions that guarantee an open door, a working café and a smooth boat crossing are the calm summer days when the light is flat, the car park is full and the magic is hardest to find. Choosing when to visit a lighthouse is really about deciding which of these you are travelling for, because almost no single month delivers both.

## Summer: Open Doors, Flat Light and Crowds

For most travellers, summer is the default, and for good reason. The overwhelming majority of climbable lighthouses, museum stations and keeper's-cottage visitor centres open only for a defined season, typically late spring through early autumn, with the fullest hours in July and August. If your goal is to climb the tower, see the lens up close and tour the living quarters, summer is often the only time it is possible at all.

The trade-offs are real. High summer brings the largest crowds, and a popular tower with a narrow spiral stair can mean queues and timed tickets. The midday light that dominates a summer visit is the least flattering for photography, washing out the sea and flattening the tower against a hazy sky. And calm settled weather, pleasant as it is to walk in, robs the coast of the drama that makes a lighthouse feel necessary. Summer is the season of access, not of atmosphere.

## Spring and Autumn: The Quiet Sweet Spot

The shoulder seasons are, for many seasoned lighthouse visitors, the genuine sweet spot. Late spring and early autumn often keep enough of the season open — many stations run from around Easter to October — while shedding the peak crowds. The light is better, lower and warmer in the morning and evening, and the weather is lively enough to give the sea some character without routinely closing everything down.

Spring brings the seabird colonies that nest on the cliffs around so many lighthouses to noisy, wheeling life, and the longer northern days stretch the usable hours. Autumn offers the first real swells of the season, golden low light and a coast emptying of summer visitors, with stations often still open into October before they close for winter. For a visitor who wants to climb the tower and still feel the wildness of the coast, the shoulder months are the most reliable compromise.

## Winter: Drama, Storms and Locked Doors

Winter is when a lighthouse is most itself. The great storms roll in, seas break white over reefs the tower was built to mark, and the low sun, when it appears, throws long theatrical light across an empty coast. Storm-watching has become a draw in its own right at exposed stations, where waves can burst dozens of metres up a tower's side. For photographers and for anyone who wants to understand why these structures exist, winter is unmatched.

It is also when almost everything is closed. The tower will be locked, the cottage dark, the café shuttered, the ferry to any island light suspended until spring. Winter lighthouse visiting is an outdoor, from-the-base affair, and it demands respect: exposed headlands and harbour walls become genuinely dangerous in heavy weather, with people swept from piers most years by waves they underestimated. Admire the drama from a safe distance, well back from the edge and never on a sea wall in a big swell.

## Reading Light and Tide, Not Just the Calendar

Beyond the broad seasons, the finest lighthouse moments are governed by the daily rhythms of light and water. The golden hours after sunrise and before sunset transform any tower, and a lighthouse that looks ordinary at noon becomes extraordinary when the low sun sets its white tower glowing against a dark sea. Photographers also prize the blue hour after sunset, when the beam itself begins to show against the deepening sky — the one time you can capture the light doing its actual job.

Tide matters as much as time of day at many sites. Causeway and tidal-island lights such as those reached only at low water dictate their own timetable entirely, and a misread tide table means either a missed visit or a genuinely dangerous crossing. Even where access is not tidal, the state of the sea shapes the scene: a spring tide with an onshore wind throws the spray that makes a dramatic image, while a flat neap tide leaves the same tower looking tame.

## Matching the Season to Your Trip

The practical answer, then, is to be honest about what you want. To climb towers, tour museums and travel comfortably, plan for the open season and accept the crowds and the flat midday light, but chase the golden hours morning and evening to rescue the photography. To find atmosphere and solitude, travel in the shoulder months and check each station's published dates carefully, because closures creep earlier in autumn than the calendar suggests.

To witness a lighthouse in its element, come in winter, expect every door to be locked, dress for the worst, keep well back from the water, and accept that you are there for the sea and the sky rather than the stair. The best time of year to visit lighthouses is not a fixed date but a decision about which version of the lighthouse you have come to meet — the accessible museum piece of summer, the balanced compromise of the shoulder seasons, or the wild, shuttered, magnificent tower of a winter gale.
