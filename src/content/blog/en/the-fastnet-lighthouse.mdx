Four and a half miles off the south-west tip of County Cork, a single pillar of grey Cornish granite rises from a wave-battered rock in the Atlantic. The Fastnet is the most southerly point of Ireland, the turning mark for ships leaving Cork Harbour and the open ocean beyond, and for more than a century it carried a name that says everything about its place in the national memory: An Charraig Aonair, the lonely rock, and to the emigrants who passed it, the Teardrop of Ireland. For the millions who sailed from Cobh to Boston, New York and beyond, this tower was the last fragment of home they would ever see.

## The Lonely Rock

The Fastnet Rock is a small, almost vertical-sided outcrop of clay-slate, breaking the surface in some of the roughest water on the Irish coast. It sits at the mouth of the Celtic Sea where Atlantic swells, funnelled by the continental shelf, can reach extraordinary heights. The rock has no natural harbour, no shelter and no flat ground; everything that was ever built there had to be cut into or bolted onto the stone itself.

Its position made it indispensable. Vessels rounding the south of Ireland to enter Cork or to make for the Bristol Channel had to clear this point, and the waters around it had wrecked countless ships. The most infamous loss was the American packet Stephen Whitney, driven onto nearby West Calf Island in 1847 with the death of around ninety people. That disaster was the direct spur to building a light on the Fastnet itself rather than the dimmer, ill-placed beacon that then stood on Cape Clear Island a few miles inshore.

## The First Tower

The original Fastnet light was a cast-iron tower, designed by George Halpin of the Ballast Board and first lit in 1854. Cast iron was quick to erect and well suited to a site where landing stone was almost impossible, and for a time the tower did its work. But the Fastnet is one of the most exposed lighthouse stations in the world, and iron proved a poor match for the Atlantic.

Storms drove water clean over the lantern. The fixed structure flexed and rang in heavy seas, crockery was smashed inside the keepers' quarters by the shock of waves, and over the decades the iron casing showed alarming signs of fatigue. By the 1890s the Commissioners of Irish Lights had concluded that the tower could not be relied upon indefinitely. A wave in 1881 had even carried away a section of rock and damaged the building. The decision was taken to replace it entirely with a granite tower built to the most demanding standards of the age.

## Cornish Granite and the Work of William Douglass

The new tower was the work of William Douglass, engineer to the Irish Lights, and it stands among the finest pieces of rock-lighthouse engineering ever completed. Construction ran from 1896 to 1904. Every one of the more than two thousand granite blocks was quarried and dressed in Cornwall, at the De Lank quarries on Bodmin Moor, then shipped to a depot on Rock Island near Crookhaven and finally ferried out to the Fastnet.

The blocks were dovetailed together, each one keyed into its neighbours so that the whole tower behaved as a single mass of stone that the sea could not prise apart. The lowest courses were set into a base solid almost to the level of the entrance door, giving the structure enormous weight low down to resist the impact of breaking waves. The completed tower rises about 54 metres above the rock, making it the tallest lighthouse in Ireland and Great Britain, and its light sits roughly 49 metres above the sea. It was a triumph of the same tradition that had raised the Bell Rock and the Eddystone, and it has stood unmarked by serious damage ever since.

## The Teardrop of Ireland

The name that clings to the Fastnet has nothing to do with its engineering. Between the Famine years and the mid-twentieth century, emigration was the central fact of Irish life. Ships left Cobh, then called Queenstown, carrying families who knew they would almost certainly never return. As the liners cleared the harbour and turned west into the Atlantic, the Fastnet was the last point of Irish land to drop below the horizon.

Passengers crowded the rails to watch it fade. For many it was a moment of unbearable finality, the literal last sight of home, and out of that grief came the name: the Teardrop of Ireland. The image recurs in songs, letters and family memory across the Irish diaspora. To understand the Fastnet only as a navigation mark is to miss why it matters; it is woven into the story of a nation that scattered itself across the world, and the tower stood as the final witness to every departure.

## Keepers, Relief and Automation

Life on the Fastnet was among the hardest of any lighthouse posting. Keepers worked turns of several weeks at a stretch, and relief depended entirely on the sea. In bad weather the relief boat simply could not come, and men were sometimes stranded weeks beyond their due date with dwindling supplies. Landing was made by a derrick that swung keepers and stores from a heaving boat onto the rock, a manoeuvre that demanded perfect timing and nerve.

In the era before helicopters, the isolation was total. Keepers kept the optic turning, the clockwork wound, the fog signal charged and the brasswork bright, and they recorded the weather four times a day. The Fastnet was finally automated by the Commissioners of Irish Lights in 1989, its last keepers withdrawn, and it is now monitored remotely from the depot at Dún Laoghaire. The great hyper-radial optic, one of the largest lenses ever made, still revolves on its bath of mercury.

## A Coast of Lights

The Fastnet does not stand alone. The southern and western coasts of Ireland are studded with lights that together tell the story of a seafaring nation, and many can still be visited or seen from the shore. To the east, near Cork, stand the Charlesfort Lighthouse guarding Kinsale and the Youghal Lighthouse on its harbour wall. Westward along the Cork and Kerry coast lie the Crookhaven Lighthouse, Sheep's Head Lighthouse, Copper Point Lighthouse and the dramatic Mizen Head Lighthouse, perched on Ireland's most south-westerly cliff and now a celebrated visitor centre reached by an arched footbridge over a chasm.

Beyond Mizen the lights continue around the Beara Peninsula and Bantry Bay, where Ardnakinna Point Lighthouse, Roancarrigmore Lighthouse, Bull Rock Lighthouse and Castletownbere Lighthouse mark the approaches to sheltered harbours. Off the Kerry coast the Inishtearaght Lighthouse on the outermost Blasket and the Cromwell Point Lighthouse on Valentia, near the Valentia Front Leading Light, guard the wild Atlantic seaboard. Up in Donegal the elegant Fanad Head Lighthouse and Saint John's Point now offer holiday accommodation in the former keepers' cottages, while on the east coast the Howth Lighthouse, the twin Dun Laoghaire East Lighthouse and Dun Laoghaire West Lighthouse, the Muglins Lighthouse, the Kish Lighthouse off Dublin Bay and the Tuskar Lighthouse off Wexford keep watch over the Irish Sea.

## Visiting the Fastnet Today

You cannot land on the Fastnet, but you can come close. Charter boats run out of Baltimore, Schull and Crookhaven in the summer months, circling the rock so that visitors can take in the full height of the tower against the swell. On a calm day the granite glows almost pink in the low light, and the sheer scale of Douglass's work becomes clear only when a boat sits in its shadow. The voyage out passes Cape Clear Island and the smaller islands of Roaringwater Bay, themselves a haven for seabirds, seals and the occasional whale.

The Fastnet has also lent its name to one of the most demanding offshore yacht races in the world, the biennial Fastnet Race, which rounds the rock as its turning point. The disastrous storm that struck the 1979 race, killing fifteen sailors, fixed the name once again in the public mind as a place of both beauty and danger. For most visitors, though, the pull is gentler and older: the simple wish to see the last thing that generations of departing Irish ever saw of their own country.
