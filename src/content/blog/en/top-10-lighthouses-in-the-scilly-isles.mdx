The Isles of Scilly lie some 28 nautical miles beyond Land's End, the first land an Atlantic crossing meets and, for centuries, the last thing many ships ever struck. The Western Rocks alone have wrecked hundreds of vessels, most infamously Sir Cloudesley Shovell's fleet in 1707, when around two thousand sailors drowned in a single night. The lights that now guard these waters are among the most exposed in Trinity House's care, several of them built on bare ledges barely clear of the sea. Together they mark the approaches to the granite archipelago and the deep-water lanes of the Western Approaches beyond.

## 1. Bishop Rock

Bishop Rock is the great prize of Scilly's lights and the tallest lighthouse in the British Isles at nearly 49 metres. The first attempt, an iron screw-pile design, was swept away in 1850 before it ever lit. The granite tower that replaced it, engineered by James Walker and built by Nicholas Douglass and his son James, first showed its light in 1858; it was later strengthened and raised in the 1880s. The light flashes white twice every fifteen seconds with a range of around 20 nautical miles, and a helipad now crowns the lantern. Automated in 1992, it stands on a ledge at the very western edge of the archipelago, often the first British light seen from a westbound ocean crossing.

## 2. Round Island

Round Island lighthouse sits on a steep granite islet off the northern edge of the Scilly group, marking the approaches from the north and west. Built by William Tregarthen Douglass and first lit in 1887, the white tower stands 19 metres but its clifftop perch lifts the light to around 55 metres above the sea, giving a range of some 18 nautical miles. The character is a red flash every ten seconds. The station was once noted for its powerful fog signal, and the island, now a seabird haven, is passed by the pleasure boats that run from St Mary's in summer.

## 3. St Agnes (old lighthouse)

The old lighthouse on St Agnes is one of the oldest surviving tower lights in Britain, completed in 1680 and originally lit by a coal fire in an open brazier on its top. The squat white tower, around 23 metres, served for nearly two centuries until the lighting of Bishop Rock made it redundant in 1858, after which it became a daymark. It still dominates the centre of the island and is among the most accessible historic lighthouse structures in Scilly, an easy walk from the quay at Porth Conger.

## 4. Peninnis Head, St Mary's

Peninnis Head marks the southern tip of St Mary's, the main island, guarding the approach to Hugh Town and St Mary's Sound. The present light is a functional white-painted lattice structure that replaced an earlier 1911 lantern; it shows a flashing white character to mark the headland and its surrounding reefs. The headland is famous for its weathered granite formations, sculpted into strange shapes by the Atlantic weather, and the coast path from Hugh Town makes it one of the easiest lights to reach on foot.

## 5. Wolf Rock

Wolf Rock lies roughly midway between Land's End and Scilly, on an isolated reef long dreaded by mariners on the approach from the south-west. The granite tower, completed in 1869 to a design by James Walker and finished under James Douglass, stands 41 metres on a rock awash at high water. It shows a white flash every fifteen seconds with a range of around 16 nautical miles, and was the first British lighthouse to be fitted with a helideck, in 1973. Automated in 1988, it remains a key mark for vessels rounding the south-western tip of England.

## 6. Seven Stones Lightvessel

The Seven Stones is not a tower but a manned-then-automated lightvessel, stationed over the notorious Seven Stones reef between Scilly and Land's End. The reef was the scene of the Torrey Canyon disaster in 1967, when the tanker grounded there and spilled its cargo across the coasts of Cornwall and Brittany. The lightvessel shows a group of three white flashes every thirty seconds and sounds a fog signal, marking a hazard that no fixed tower has ever been built upon. It is one of the few lightvessel stations still maintained by Trinity House.

## 7. St Agnes Day Beacon

Above the old St Agnes tower, the wider island carries a network of unlit daymarks, but the great white-painted Day Beacon on the neighbouring island of St Martin's is the most prominent navigational mark of all. This conical stone tower, built in 1683 and one of the oldest daymarks in Britain, stands on the highest point of St Martin's, painted red and white, and has guided vessels into the islands by day for over three centuries. It is a short walk from Higher Town across heath and bracken.

## 8. Crebawethan and the Western Rocks Beacons

The Western Rocks south-west of St Agnes are guarded not only by Bishop Rock but by a scatter of minor beacons and marks among reefs such as Crebawethan, Rosevean and Gorregan. These low, unmanned marks warn small craft of the drying ledges that have claimed so many vessels over the centuries, including ships of the 1707 fleet disaster. They are best appreciated from the dedicated wildlife and wreck-tour boats that run out from St Mary's, weaving among the reefs in settled summer weather.

## 9. Hanjague and the Eastern Isles Marks

At the eastern edge of the archipelago, the Eastern Isles and the outlying rock of Hanjague mark the approach from the east. The minor beacons and buoyed marks here guide vessels clear of the half-tide rocks that fringe the group. The Eastern Isles are uninhabited and a noted grey-seal haul-out, and the same pleasure boats that visit the seals pass these eastern marks, completing the ring of navigation around the islands.

## 10. Hugh Town Harbour Lights

The working heart of Scilly is Hugh Town on St Mary's, and its harbour is marked by the small fixed and flashing lights at the quay head and pier that guide the inter-island launches and the Scillonian ferry from Penzance. Modest as they are beside Bishop Rock, these harbour lights are the ones most travellers actually use, and the quay is the natural starting point for any tour of the islands' more dramatic seaward towers.
