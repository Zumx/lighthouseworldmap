## The Problem the Lightship Solved

There are places at sea where the water is too deep for a conventional lighthouse foundation, too exposed for a fixed beacon, and too dangerous for shipping to navigate without a light. Where a shoal lies many miles from shore, or where a submerged sandbank shifts over time, the practical answer for two centuries was the lightship — a purpose-built vessel anchored at a fixed position, carrying a mast-mounted light, a fog signal, and a crew whose sole duty was to ensure the light was kept burning and the position was maintained.

The first lightship in the world was stationed at the Nore — the sandbank at the mouth of the Thames estuary — in 1732. Its operator, Robert Hamblin, obtained a patent from the Crown to maintain a floating light on a converted coal tender, paying a levy on passing ships in exchange for the service. The concept spread rapidly: by the early nineteenth century, lightships marked the major offshore dangers of the North Sea, the English Channel and the American east coast, each vessel anchored with a heavy mushroom anchor and mooring chain, rolling in the swells while its crew kept the light burning throughout the night.

## Life on a Lightship

The conditions aboard a lightship were among the most confined and monotonous of any seafaring employment. The vessel was small — typically 30 to 50 metres long — and never moved from its station except for periodic relief. The crew, usually eight to twelve men, lived in the vessel's cramped quarters for rotations of four to eight weeks at a time. The ship rolled constantly in the offshore swell, often quite violently in bad weather. There was no going ashore, no variation in the horizon, and limited contact with the outside world before the era of radio.

The duties were simple and relentless. The main lamp — carried on the mast or on a framework at the masthead, and fuelled successively by oil, kerosene and finally electricity — had to be kept burning between sunset and sunrise without interruption. The fog signal, usually a steam horn or an air siren, had to be operated whenever visibility fell below the prescribed limit. The vessel's anchor chain had to be checked for wear. The hull had to be maintained against rust, the pumps tested, the bilges inspected.

In exchange for these conditions, lightship crewmen received wages somewhat above those of ordinary merchant sailors, plus the knowledge that they were performing a service of genuine importance to other mariners. The casualty lists of the pre-lightship era — when the Goodwin Sands, the Varne Bank and the Nore were all unmarked — testified to what was at stake.

## The Outer Gabbard and the North Sea

The North Sea has always been one of the busiest and most hazardous stretches of water in the world. The shallows of the Thames estuary, the sandbanks running southward from Norfolk, and the approaches to the major continental ports all demanded marking, and for much of the nineteenth and twentieth centuries this was done by a combination of fixed lights on the English and Dutch coasts with lightships stationed at the more distant dangers.

The Outer Gabbard lightship, stationed approximately 25 miles east-northeast of Harwich, marked the eastern edge of the Gabbard shoal on the approach to the Dutch and Belgian ports. The station was established in 1838 and remained in service, with successive replacements of the vessel, until the 1970s. During both world wars, lightships in the North Sea were removed or painted over to deny navigational assistance to hostile forces, a measure that caused casualties among neutral shipping unfamiliar with the now-unmarked hazards.

The Trinity House lightship fleet at its peak in the nineteenth century comprised approximately forty vessels, covering stations from the Channel approaches to the North Sea and around into the Irish Sea. Managing and supplying this fleet required an administrative and operational infrastructure comparable to a small naval force.

## American Lightships: East Coast and Great Lakes

In the United States, the Lighthouse Board and its successor the Bureau of Lighthouses maintained a lightship service that at its height included more than fifty vessels stationed along the Atlantic coast, the Gulf of Mexico and the Great Lakes. Some of these vessels became celebrated landmarks. The Ambrose lightship, stationed at the entrance to New York Harbour from 1908 and now preserved at the South Street Seaport Museum in Manhattan, was the first sight of America for millions of transatlantic immigrants. The Nantucket Shoals lightship, stationed in the open Atlantic about 50 miles south of Nantucket island, marked the outer end of the shoals and was the first light seen by eastbound ships making landfall in North America. It was also the last: the Nantucket lightship was rammed and sunk by the liner RMS Olympic in fog in 1934, with the loss of seven of the eleven crew members.

The Great Lakes lightships were adapted for a freshwater environment and the particular hazard of winter ice. Several Great Lakes lightships were armoured or reinforced at the waterline to resist ice pressure, and they were typically withdrawn from station before the ice season and returned in spring.

## The Sandettie and Its Replacement

The Sandettie lightship, positioned over the Sandettie Bank in the southern North Sea about 25 miles southwest of the Belgian coast, is one of the most familiar to users of the BBC Shipping Forecast, which has given the name to this stretch of the forecast area. The vessel that occupied the station for much of the twentieth century was a distinctive red-hulled ship with the station name painted in large white letters on her hull — a necessary identification measure because lightships were sometimes dragged off station by extreme weather and a displaced lightship was more dangerous than no lightship at all.

The Sandettie automatic buoy, which replaced the crewed lightship in 1988, provides the same optical and electronic signals from a large automated light float. The buoy transmits an AIS signal identifying itself and its position, maintains a flashing light character visible for several miles, and monitors its own position against its approved station — alarming the control centre if it drags anchor. It requires only periodic servicing from a maintenance vessel rather than a continuous crew.

## Why Lightships Disappeared

The disappearance of the crewed lightship from most of the world's coasts between 1960 and 2000 had three principal causes. First, the development of large automatic buoys with reliable power supplies and adequate light output made it possible to mark offshore dangers without a crew. Second, the Lanby — Large Automatic Navigation Buoy — was developed in the 1960s as a specific replacement for lightships, offering a taller, more stable platform for the light and fog signal than a conventional buoy. Third, the construction of offshore platform technology, originally developed for the oil industry, made it possible to erect fixed structures in deeper water than had previously been feasible, reducing the number of positions that could only be marked by a floating light.

The economic argument was decisive. A crewed lightship cost more annually to operate than almost any other single aid to navigation. The crew, their food and water, the fuel for the ship's engines and auxiliary generators, the relief vessel needed to rotate the crew, the maintenance drydocking required every few years — the total was very large. An automatic large buoy at the same station cost a fraction as much and, once the technology was proven reliable, carried the same essential navigational information.

## Preserved Vessels

A substantial number of former lightships survive as museum ships and heritage attractions. The Ambrose (LV-87) at South Street Seaport, New York, is among the most visited. The Helwick lightvessel, formerly stationed at the Helwick Shoal in the Bristol Channel, is preserved at Swansea Maritime Quarter. The Planet lightvessel from the Mersey Approach is on display at the Merseyside Maritime Museum in Liverpool. The Hubert, built in 1954 and the last relief lightvessel in the Trinity House fleet, was retired in 2011 and has attracted interest from preservation groups.

## The End of an Era

The last crewed Trinity House lightship, the Albatross, left her station on the East Goodwin in 1994, ending 262 years of crewed floating lights in English waters. The last US Coast Guard lightship was retired in 1983. The crewed lightship is now a purely historical object — but the offshore dangers it marked are as real as they ever were, and the automatic buoys and lights that replaced it continue the same essential function. The name 'Nantucket' still appears on charts at the point where the shoals begin; the Sandettie still appears in the shipping forecast. The vessels are gone, but the hazards and the need to mark them remain.
