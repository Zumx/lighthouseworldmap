## Estonia's Lighthouse Archipelago

Estonia's western coast opens onto a sea of islands: Hiiumaa and Saaremaa, the two large islands of the West Estonian Archipelago, together with dozens of smaller ones, form a labyrinth of channels, shoals and restricted passages that has challenged mariners entering or leaving the Gulf of Finland and the Gulf of Riga for centuries. The Baltic here is shallow and island-fringed, with no tides but with ice that can seal the passages from December to April in a hard winter. Estonia's lighthouses carry the layered history of the region: built or designed under the Russian Imperial lighthouse service from the early nineteenth century, some of the towers are among the finest examples of Baltic lighthouse architecture. Soviet-era military use closed many sites for decades, and since independence in 1991 the Maritime Administration of Estonia has managed the network while a growing heritage tourism movement has opened many towers to visitors.

## 1. Kõpu Lighthouse, Hiiumaa

Kõpu is the third-oldest lighthouse still in operation in the world, its stone tower completed in 1531 on the highest point of Hiiumaa island at an elevation of 102 metres above sea level. It was built by the Hanseatic League to guide ships through the dangerous passage between Hiiumaa and the open sea, which was responsible for numerous losses of merchant vessels carrying the trade of the Baltic ports. The massive stone tower, approximately 36 metres tall, has a markedly different character from the slender round towers of later centuries: it is a broad, tapering structure with massive supporting buttresses, built to withstand both Atlantic storms and the passage of time. The light character today is a white flash every fifteen seconds with a range of about 26 nautical miles. Kõpu is a national heritage monument and is open to visitors; the tower can be climbed, and the view over the forested plateau of Hiiumaa and out to sea is extraordinary. A small museum at the base tells the history of the light and the island.

## 2. Tahkuna Lighthouse, Hiiumaa

Tahkuna stands on the northernmost tip of Hiiumaa, a slender cast-iron tower completed in 1875, manufactured in France by the Sautter firm and assembled on the Estonian coast. It is 42.6 metres tall, making it the tallest lighthouse in Estonia, and its light — a white flash every four seconds — is visible for 22 nautical miles across the approach to the Gulf of Finland. The Tahkuna headland marks the separation between the Soela Sound (the passage between Hiiumaa and Saaremaa) and the outer Baltic. The prefabricated iron tower design reflects the period's industrial approach to lighthouse construction — similar towers were shipped from French foundries to locations across the world. Tahkuna is now a memorial site as well as a lighthouse: a monument near the tower commemorates the victims of the Estonia ferry disaster of 1994, the worst maritime accident in the Baltic since the Second World War. The lighthouse is open to visitors and the headland is accessible by car.

## 3. Ristna Lighthouse, Hiiumaa

Ristna marks the westernmost point of Hiiumaa and of Estonia, a headland that juts into the open Baltic south of the main Hiiumaa coastline. The lighthouse is a red-painted octagonal cast-iron tower of the same French Sautter construction as Tahkuna, 29 metres tall, built in 1874 and first lit in 1875. The light character is a white flash every four seconds, range 16 nautical miles. The cape marks the southern approach to the Soela Sound for vessels coming from the west. Ristna is now within the Hiiumaa Island Biosphere Reserve, and the surrounding coastal meadows and juniper heathland are among the finest undisturbed coastal landscapes in Estonia. The lighthouse compound is accessible by road through the Kõpu peninsula, and the tower is open to visitors in summer. The sunsets at Ristna, facing due west over the open sea, are outstanding.

## 4. Sõrve Lighthouse, Saaremaa

The Sõrve Peninsula extends southward from Saaremaa into the Gulf of Riga, a long, narrow spit of land at the southern extreme of the West Estonian Archipelago. The lighthouse at the tip of Sõrve marks the division between the Gulf of Riga to the east and the open Baltic to the west. The current tower is a concrete structure built in 1960 — the wartime original was destroyed in the Second World War fighting on the peninsula, which was among the most prolonged in the Baltic — and stands 52 metres tall. The light character is a white flash every three seconds with a range of approximately 22 nautical miles. The Sõrve tip has strong wartime associations; the battle of Sõrve in 1944 involved some of the last German forces in Estonia, and the landscape retains bunkers and fortifications. The lighthouse is accessible at the end of the Sõrve road and is open to visitors.

## 5. Kihnu Lighthouse, Kihnu Island

Kihnu is a small island in the Gulf of Riga off the southern Estonian coast, famous as the home of a traditional community whose culture — particularly its matriarchal social structure, music and handicrafts — was inscribed on the UNESCO Intangible Cultural Heritage list in 2008. The lighthouse on the island's western coast was completed in 1864 and rebuilt in its current form later; it is a red-painted iron tower of about 30 metres, its light character a white flash every six seconds visible at 15 nautical miles. Kihnu's identity as both a lighthouse island and a living cultural heritage site makes it one of the most distinctive destinations in the Estonian islands. Regular ferry service connects Kihnu to Pärnu, and the island is small enough to be explored by bicycle. The lighthouse keeper's cottage and the island's church, school and cultural centre together tell a story that goes far beyond navigation.

## 6. Kajakarahu Range Light (Lower), Saaremaa

The Kajakarahu lower range light is one of several pairs of leading lights on the coast of Saaremaa that guide vessels through the shallow inner passages of the West Estonian Archipelago. Range lights — a near light and a far light, aligned to create a safe leading line — are a characteristic feature of the Estonian island navigation system, where the channels between islands and shoals can be only a few hundred metres wide. The lower light is a modest daymark-style structure, white with a directional beam. These range lights are less architecturally spectacular than the major island towers but represent the practical heart of navigation in these intricate waters.

## 7. Pirita Range Light (Lower), Tallinn

The Pirita lower range light stands near the mouth of the Pirita River on the southern shore of Tallinn Bay, where it forms part of the approach leading into the Tallinn yacht harbour. The Pirita marina was constructed for the 1980 Moscow Olympics sailing events, and the range lights here were upgraded as part of that infrastructure. The lower light is a white lattice tower of modest height. Tallinn Bay is the busiest navigational area in Estonia, with the ferry services to Helsinki and Stockholm, the commercial port and the yacht harbour all generating continuous traffic. The Pirita area is a popular recreational area close to the city, and the lighthouse is easily accessible.

## 8. Pirita South Mole Light, Tallinn

The Pirita lõunamuuli light marks the southern breakwater entrance to Pirita harbour, a low red-painted metal tower at the tip of the mole. Mole-head lights are the most numerous class of lighthouse in Estonia, as in most Baltic countries — small structures marking harbour entrances across the island coast. This particular light takes on added significance from the context of the Pirita marina's Olympic history and its ongoing role as the main sailing base in Estonia. The sight lines across Tallinn Bay toward the medieval towers and spires of the Old Town give the area a visual setting unmatched elsewhere in the Estonian lighthouse network.

## 9. Pärnu East Mole Light

The Pärnu idamuuli light marks the eastern breakwater of the entrance to Pärnu harbour, the largest harbour on the eastern shore of the Gulf of Riga and an important fishing and small-craft port. Pärnu is Estonia's summer capital — the beach resort that draws Estonians from across the country in July and August — and the harbour entrance is busy throughout the season. The east mole light is a red cylindrical structure at the end of the stone breakwater. The walk along the Pärnu mole, past the light at its end, is a popular summer evening stroll, and the light's reflection in the calm Gulf of Riga is a familiar image of this resort town.

## 10. Moldova Buoy Light, West Estonian Channels

The Moldova tulepaak is one of the navigational marks in the inner channels of the West Estonian Archipelago, near the coast of Muhu Island. It represents the dense network of lights and marks that the Estonian Maritime Administration maintains to guide traffic through the shallow and island-filled inner sea. The Muhu and Saaremaa coast is busy with the ferry traffic between the mainland and the islands, with the primary ferry routes using the Kuivastu ferry landing on Muhu. The inner channel lights, while individually modest, collectively constitute the infrastructure that makes year-round navigation in this complex sea possible.

## Visiting Estonian Lighthouses

Estonia's lighthouse network is exceptionally well-positioned for heritage tourism. The two major islands, Hiiumaa and Saaremaa, are reached by ferry from Rohuküla (for Hiiumaa) and Virtsu (for Saaremaa) respectively; both crossings take under two hours. On Hiiumaa, Kõpu, Tahkuna and Ristna form a natural tour of the island's western and northern coasts, ideally spread over two days with cycling or a hire car. Saaremaa is larger and requires a car; the Sõrve peninsula is a full day's excursion from the island capital Kuressaare. The best visiting season is May through September; in winter the ferry services may be affected by ice. Many towers are managed by local authorities and are open without charge.
