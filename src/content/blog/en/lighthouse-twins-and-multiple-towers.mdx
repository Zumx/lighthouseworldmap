## The Logic of Two Towers

A single lighthouse identifies a coastline. Two lighthouses at the same station tell a navigator something more precise: the line between them, projected seaward, marks a safe channel, a course to steer, or the exact bearing of a rock. This is the founding principle of the range light or leading light pair, in which a taller rear tower and a shorter front tower stand at a measured horizontal distance apart, so that a vessel keeping both lights vertically aligned is following a specific track across the chart. But twin and multiple towers arose from other purposes too — the need to distinguish a cape from adjacent lights, the desire to provide redundancy at a critical location, local politics about whose coast should be marked, and occasionally the sheer complexity of the navigational problem that a particular stretch of water presented.

## Twin Lights as Range Markers

The Navesink Twin Lights in Highlands, New Jersey are the most famous twin lighthouse station in North America, and unusually they were not originally a range pair. Built in 1828 and rebuilt substantially in 1862 to a design by Joseph Lederle, the two towers stand on a Highlands bluff overlooking the entrance to New York Harbour at an elevation of about 70 metres above sea level. The north tower carried a first-order Fresnel lens with a fixed red light; the south tower carried a flashing first-order lens with a characteristic that distinguished it clearly. Together they provided a distinctive landmark that pilots approaching from the Atlantic could not confuse with any single light on the coast. The south tower has been deactivated; the north tower remains active with a modern optic. The keeper's dwelling between them houses the Twin Lights State Historic Site.

In Scotland, the Mull of Kintyre station has operated a single tower since 1788, but the island of Ornsay on the Sleat Sound once carried leading lights that guided vessels through the narrow passage between the mainland and Skye. Leading light pairs like these were the workhorse configuration: a lower front structure — sometimes not a lighthouse at all but a simple painted column or a post carrying a small lantern — aligned with a larger rear tower. The mariner kept them vertically in line and knew the ship was on the cleared track.

## The Needles and the Isle of Wight

The eastern approach to the Solent, the main shipping channel into Southampton, is guarded by the Needles — a chalk stack with a lighthouse at its tip, first lit in 1786 and rebuilt in 1859 to a design by James Walker. For much of the nineteenth century the Needles light worked in conjunction with a high light at St Catherine's Point, the southernmost tip of the Isle of Wight, to bracket the island and allow navigators to judge their position. St Catherine's lighthouse, rebuilt in 1840 at 26 metres height after its predecessor at the headland's summit was too often cloud-bound, shows an occulting white and red light with a range of 30 nautical miles. The two towers are not twins in the formal sense but form a coordinated pair that defines the island.

## Three Towers: Cape Elizabeth, Maine

Cape Elizabeth on the southern Maine coast originally carried two towers, built in 1828 by Captain Decius Wadsworth, standing 300 metres apart on a rocky headland. The twin configuration was chosen to distinguish the cape from Portland Head Light six kilometres to the north. After both towers were rebuilt in 1874 — the west tower to 46 metres, the east tower slightly lower — a third structure, a fog signal building, was added between them. The east tower was decommissioned in 1924 when improved optics gave the west tower sufficient range and distinctiveness. It still stands, and the surviving west tower is active, showing a flashing white light visible at 27 nautical miles.

## Hartland Point and the Redundancy Principle

In exposed Atlantic locations, early lighthouse authorities occasionally built a spare tower that could be quickly converted to use if the primary structure was damaged. Hartland Point in Devon had two towers in the nineteenth century, though only one was operational at any given time — the second was a standby in a stretch of coast that the Bristol Channel pilots knew as one of the most dangerous in Britain. This redundancy logic was more common in the era before reliable steam tenders could deliver replacement equipment in days; if the lamp mechanism failed in November with no relief vessel due for six weeks, a spare tower a hundred metres away was a practical precaution.

## The Great Basses and Little Basses, Sri Lanka

The south-east coast of Sri Lanka is guarded by two entirely separate lighthouse stations on isolated coral reefs, the Great Basses and Little Basses, standing roughly 14 kilometres apart. Both were built by the British colonial authorities in the 1870s — Great Basses in 1873, 32 metres tall, and Little Basses in 1877, 26 metres — and both were constructed in extraordinarily difficult conditions on reefs that submerge in heavy swells. Although they are independent stations rather than true twins, they function together as a pair in the navigation of the southernmost tip of the island, with the channel between them used by vessels rounding the cape. Both remain active lighthouses.

## The Makapuu Lens and the Problem of Identification

Multiple towers sometimes arose not from the need for two lights but from the need for a single light to be unmistakably identified. The Makapuu lighthouse on Oahu, Hawaii, was completed in 1909 and carries the largest lighthouse lens in the United States — a hyper-radial optic nearly three metres in diameter, producing a flashing white light visible at 28 nautical miles. The station was originally intended to work alongside a lighthouse at Diamond Head to give vessels a two-point fix on the island. Diamond Head lighthouse was built in 1899 and remains active, its occulting white and red light marking the approach to Honolulu Harbour. Though structurally independent, the two lighthouses were conceived as a coordinated navigational system for the most commercially important island in the Pacific.

## Preservation and the Twin Light Legacy

Twin and multiple-tower stations have proven disproportionately popular with preservation groups, partly because the two-tower plan gives a station a monumental presence that a single tower cannot match. The Navesink complex in New Jersey, the Cape Elizabeth towers in Maine, and the two Beavertail structures in Rhode Island all attract visitors drawn as much by the architectural drama of two towers on a single headland as by the historical narrative of navigation.
