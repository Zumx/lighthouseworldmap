## Lighthouses of the Aegean and Ionian

Greece has been a maritime civilisation for three thousand years, and the need to navigate safely between its roughly six thousand islands, through seas subject to seasonal meltemi winds and unpredictable local squalls, shaped the development of aids to navigation from antiquity. The Pharos of Alexandria — built by Ptolemaic engineers around 280 BC — was the most celebrated lighthouse of the ancient world, and while it stood in Egypt rather than Greece, it was a product of Hellenistic civilisation. Greece's modern lighthouse service was established after independence in the early nineteenth century, initially with French technical assistance, and the Hellenic Navy today manages a network of lights from the Ionian islands to the Dodecanese. Several of Greece's most celebrated lighthouses combine extraordinary architectural settings with equally extraordinary natural ones — the caldera rim of Santorini, the sea-carved rock of Andros, the rocky Corinthian cape that guards the gulf named after it.

## 1. Faros Akrotiriou (Cape Lighthouse), Santorini

The lighthouse at Akrotiri on the southern tip of Santorini stands on the rim of the ancient caldera, at a point where the volcanic cliffs drop sheer to a sea that fills the collapsed centre of one of the largest volcanic eruptions in recorded history. The white-painted tower, a cylindrical structure of modest height — approximately 9 metres — was erected in 1892 by a French engineering company under contract to the Greek state. The light character is a white flash every five seconds, range 17 nautical miles. The setting is the most visually dramatic of any lighthouse in Greece: the tower perches on red and black volcanic rock above the deep blue of the caldera, with the white villages of Fira and Oia visible on the caldera rim across the water. The lighthouse is operational and managed by the Hellenic Navy. It is accessible by road from Akrotiri village, and the surrounding cape is part of the archaeological zone containing the Minoan city excavated at Akrotiri. Visitors can approach the lighthouse fence.

## 2. Tourlitis Lighthouse, Andros

Tourlitis is one of the most visually striking lighthouses in the Aegean: a white tower perched on a sea-carved rock stack rising from the sea at the entrance to Andros harbour, accessible only by a metal ladder from the seawall at certain states of the tide. The original lighthouse here was built in 1897, allegedly commissioned by the philanthropist Theofanis Empirikos. The current tower was rebuilt in 1994 after the original was destroyed by storm damage, with funding from the Goulandris family whose shipping dynasty is closely associated with Andros — the island has provided officers and crews to Greek merchant shipping for generations. The light character is a white flash every four seconds visible at about 12 nautical miles, marking the harbour entrance at Chora. The lighthouse is visible from the harbour front and from the old Venetian castle ruins above the town. It has become one of the iconic images of Andros and of the Aegean coast.

## 3. Faros Melagavi, Corinth

The Melagavi lighthouse stands on Cape Melagavi (also known as Cape Ireon), the promontory that marks the eastern entrance to the Gulf of Corinth — the long, narrow sea that separates the Peloponnese from mainland Greece and leads to the port of Corinth and the Corinth Canal. The cape is a natural turning mark of the first importance for vessels navigating the gulf, and a lighthouse here has guided traffic for centuries. The current tower is a white cylindrical structure of modest dimensions; the light character is a white flash every ten seconds visible at about 12 nautical miles. The gulf is one of the most sheltered bodies of water in the eastern Mediterranean, bordered by the mountains of the Peloponnese to the south and central Greece to the north, and the lighthouse sees a steady traffic of sailing vessels, ferries and cargo ships. The cape is accessible by road from the Corinthian coast and offers excellent views of the gulf.

## 4. Gourouni Lighthouse, Skopelos

The Gourouni lighthouse occupies a low cape on the northwestern coast of Skopelos, one of the Northern Sporades islands in the Aegean. Skopelos is best known internationally as a filming location for the film 'Mamma Mia!', but its maritime significance is older: the island sits at the junction of the routes through the Northern Sporades and the passages between the mainland and the chain of islands that includes Skiathos, Skopelos and Alonnisos. The lighthouse is a white tower of modest height, its light character covering the northwestern approach to the Skopelos Strait. The surrounding landscape — rocky cape, pines, the blue of the Aegean — is representative of the island setting that gives Greek lighthouses a particular quality that their North Atlantic counterparts cannot match.

## 5. Cape Greco Lighthouse, Cyprus

Cape Greco, on the southeastern peninsula of Cyprus, is technically the lighthouse of an EU member state with strong historical and geographical ties to the Hellenic world. The lighthouse at Cape Greco — a white cylindrical tower about 10 metres tall on a headland of limestone sea caves — marks the eastern approach to the southern coast of Cyprus and the passage toward Limassol and Famagusta Bay. The cape, now within a national forest park, is well visited for its geology and the famous sea caves at the foot of the cliffs. The lighthouse is operational and maintained by the Department of Merchant Shipping. The setting, with clear blue water and dramatic limestone formations, is one of the most beautiful in the eastern Mediterranean.

## 6. Psytalia (Psyttalia) Lighthouse, Piraeus

The Psytalia lighthouse marks the southern tip of Psytalia island in the Saronic Gulf, the island that shelters the main approach channel to the port of Piraeus — the busiest passenger port in Europe. Psytalia has been the site of a sewage treatment facility since the 1990s, significantly reducing its scenic appeal, but the lighthouse remains operationally important for the dense traffic of Greek domestic ferries, cruise ships and cargo vessels entering and leaving Piraeus. The light character is a white flash visible at about 14 nautical miles, guiding vessels through the Piraeus approach channel past the island's southern tip.

## 7. Methoni Lighthouse, Peloponnese

The Methoni lighthouse stands within the walls of the Venetian castle at Methoni — one of the finest medieval fortifications in the Mediterranean, constructed by the Republic of Venice in the fourteenth and fifteenth centuries to guard the sea route from Venice to the eastern Mediterranean. The lighthouse occupies the Bourtzi, a small octagonal tower at the end of a causeway from the castle, originally a Venetian structure later used as a lighthouse. The light marks the southwestern cape of the Peloponnese and the entrance to the Methoni anchorage. The castle at Methoni is one of the most visited historical sites in Greece; the lighthouse setting within the castle walls gives it a unique character entirely different from any other in the country.

## 8. Sapientza Island Lighthouse, Peloponnese

Sapientza Island lies off the southern cape of Messenia in the southwestern Peloponnese, and its lighthouse marks the southern passage through the islands that dot this section of the open Ionian Sea. The island was a Venetian staging post for their eastern trade; the lighthouse occupies the highest point and provides a range of approximately 16 nautical miles for vessels rounding the cape. Sapientza is uninhabited except for the lighthouse keeper rotation and is part of a national marine park. Diving around the island, with its clear water and underwater caves, is regulated and permitted.

## 9. Araxos Lighthouse, Peloponnese

Cape Araxos marks the northwestern tip of the Peloponnese — the entrance to the Gulf of Patras from the Ionian Sea — and a lighthouse here guides the busy traffic between the Ionian islands, the Adriatic ferry routes from Patras and the gulf passages toward Corinth and Athens. The tower is a white cylindrical structure; its light character covers the northern approach to the gulf. The cape is significant historically as the location of an ancient sanctuary of Artemis and as a major navigational waypoint on the Bronze Age and classical sea routes between the western and eastern Mediterranean.

## 10. Plaka (Milos) Lighthouse, Cyclades

The lighthouse on the Plaka headland of Milos, the westernmost of the Cyclades, marks the island's northern cape and the passage through the strait between Milos and the smaller island of Kimolos. Milos is famous for the Venus de Milo, now in the Louvre, discovered in 1820, and for its extraordinary landscape of volcanic rock, white village architecture and turquoise sea. The lighthouse is a white round tower, its light visible across the approach to the island's main harbour at Adamas. The lighthouse headland is accessible on foot from the Plaka village above the capital, and the views north toward Sifnos and south toward Folegandros are among the finest panoramas in the Cyclades.

## Visiting Greek Lighthouses

Greek lighthouses are managed by the Hellenic Navy and are predominantly operational infrastructure. The majority are not open for interior visits, but many are in settings — headlands, island capes, historic forts — that make approach and exterior viewing deeply rewarding. The sailing season from May to October brings the best conditions; the meltemi wind that blows strongly across the Aegean from July to September can complicate boat access to remote capes. Island ferries from Piraeus, Rafina and Thessaloniki provide access to the island lights. The Santorini and Andros lighthouses are the most visited.
