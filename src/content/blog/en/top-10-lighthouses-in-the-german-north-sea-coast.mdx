The German North Sea coast is one of the most demanding stretches of shoreline in the world for a lighthouse builder. The Wadden Sea — the vast intertidal mudflat system that stretches from Den Helder in the Netherlands to Esbjerg in Denmark — means that the true coast lies several kilometres behind a fringe of barrier islands, tidal channels, and sand banks that shift perceptibly from year to year. There is no solid rock here to anchor a tower; the seabed is soft sand and mud, and in a North Sea storm the water can rise five metres above normal tide. The German hydrographic administration responded to this challenge with extraordinary ingenuity: cast-iron towers screwed into the sand on offshore banks, brick towers of exceptional solidity on the island crests, and — most remarkably — a series of offshore wave-washed towers on the outer sandbanks built using techniques borrowed from marine engineering. The resulting network, managed today by Wasserstraßen- und Schifffahrtsverwaltung des Bundes, includes some of the most architecturally distinctive lighthouses in Europe.

## 1. Roter Sand Lighthouse, Weser Estuary

The Roter Sand lighthouse, standing on an offshore sandbank in the outer Weser estuary 45 kilometres northwest of Bremerhaven, was when it was built in 1885 the first offshore wave-washed lighthouse in the world to be founded on an iron caisson sunk into a sandy seabed. The engineer Franz Gustav Hagen designed the 30-metre cast-iron tower and the pneumatic caisson method of its construction; the project took seven years. Roter Sand is circular in plan, painted red, and sits alone on its bank with no land visible in any direction. The light was active from 1885 until decommissioned in 1986 when a light buoy proved sufficient for the modern approach to Bremerhaven. Since then, a preservation society has maintained the tower as a heritage monument; it offers overnight accommodation in extreme isolation, accessible by tender from Bremerhaven. The tower is a designated German cultural monument and one of the most remarkable lighthouse structures in Europe.

## 2. Leuchtturm Westerheversand, Schleswig-Holstein

The Westerheversand lighthouse at Westerhever on the Eiderstedt Peninsula is the most recognisable lighthouse on the German North Sea coast and one of the most photographed in Europe. The cast-iron spiral-painted tower was built in 1908, is 41 metres tall, and stands on a low dyke above the Wadden Sea salt marshes on legs that elevate the tower above storm surge level. The character is a white flash every five seconds with a range of 21 nautical miles, marking the approach to the Hever tidal inlet that gives access to the ports behind the barrier. The two keeper's cottages — connected to the tower base by a raised causeway — remain and can be rented as holiday accommodation through the Schleswig-Holstein Wadden Sea National Park. The surrounding salt marsh landscape, with the tower rising above the flat horizon, has a hypnotic minimalist beauty. The lighthouse is accessible on foot from Westerhever village at low tide.

## 3. Pilsum Lighthouse, East Frisia

The Pilsum lighthouse near Greetsiel in East Frisia is the compact red-and-yellow-striped tower that became famous as the backdrop to several films and advertisements; it is now among the most recognised lighthouses in Germany. Built in 1891 as a leading light for the Ems estuary approach channel, it stands only 11 metres tall but its bold colouring and its solitary position in the flat dyke landscape of East Frisia give it an outsized presence. The light is no longer active as a primary navigation aid — its function has been taken over by modern equipment — but the tower is maintained as a cultural monument by the local municipality. A café and small exhibition occupy the keeper's cottage. The surrounding East Frisian dyke landscape, with its windmills, terpen mounds and channel networks, is one of the most distinctive cultural landscapes in Germany.

## 4. Alte Liebe, Cuxhaven

The Alte Liebe — 'Old Love' — is not a lighthouse in the conventional sense but an elevated platform with a navigation light at the end of the Cuxhaven pier, where the Elbe estuary opens into the Heligoland Bight. The name derives from an old Dutch term for a loading pier; the platform has existed in various forms since 1732. The current structure is an iron jetty extending into the Elbe with a light at its tip that guides vessels into the port. Cuxhaven is the last port before the open North Sea on the German side of the Elbe, and the Alte Liebe was historically where ships took on fresh water and supplies before departing and where pilots embarked or disembarked. The platform is open to the public and is a favourite viewpoint for watching container ships, tankers and car carriers negotiating the Elbe approach, the world's longest approach channel for ocean-going vessels.

## 5. Wangerooge Lighthouse, East Frisian Islands

The lighthouse on Wangerooge, the easternmost of the East Frisian Islands, was built in 1856 to replace an earlier tower that had been destroyed by a catastrophic storm flood. The red-brick tower is 60 metres tall — the tallest on the East Frisian islands — and its range of 21 nautical miles covers the outer approach to the Jade Bay and the naval port of Wilhelmshaven. The island itself is car-free and accessible by ferry from Harlesiel on the mainland; the lighthouse stands in the old village area of the island, now separated from the current settlement by a wide storm-stripped beach. A second, older tower stub remains nearby from the eighteenth century. The lighthouse is managed by Wasserstraßen- und Schifffahrtsverwaltung and is not regularly open to visitors, but it dominates the island skyline and is the natural focal point of any walk through Wangerooge.

## 6. Borkum Grosser Leuchtturm, East Frisian Islands

The Borkum Grosser Leuchtturm — Great Lighthouse — on the westernmost of the East Frisian Islands is one of the oldest and most powerful lighthouses on the German coast, first established in the sixteenth century. The current tower, built in 1879, is 60 metres tall and stands in the residential area of Borkum town. Its character is a white group flash of four every fifteen seconds with a range of 24 nautical miles, marking the outer approach to the Ems estuary from the west. The island of Borkum is the largest of the East Frisian Islands and a major North Sea resort; the lighthouse is accessible on foot from the town centre and the tower is open for guided tours in summer. A second lighthouse, the Kleiner Leuchtturm, serves as a leading light in conjunction with the Grosser. The view from the top extends across the Wadden Sea, the Borkum Riff shoal, and the Dutch coast to the southwest.

## 7. Norderney Lighthouse, East Frisian Islands

The Norderney lighthouse, built in 1874 on the island of Norderney — the oldest of the North Sea resorts, patronised by the Prussian royal family from the early nineteenth century — marks the approach to the Norderney Seegat, the tidal channel separating Norderney from Juist to the west. The red-brick tower is 35 metres tall and shows a white flash every six seconds with a range of 19 nautical miles. The lighthouse stands in a nature reserve at the eastern end of the island, surrounded by dune heath; it has been automated since 1980. Norderney is accessible by regular ferry from Norddeich on the mainland. The lighthouse is open for guided tours in summer and the keeper's cottages have been converted to accommodation and a small café. The surrounding dune landscape is characteristic of the East Frisian barrier islands at their finest.

## 8. Helgoland Unterfeuer, Heligoland

The island of Heligoland — the only German territory in the open North Sea, lying 70 kilometres offshore — has carried lights since the island served as a Danish and then British naval base before passing to Germany in 1890. The current lighthouse installation on the red sandstone island is a relatively modern structure, but the island itself is a critical navigation landmark: it is the sole visible feature in hundreds of square kilometres of otherwise featureless sea. The Helgoland light guides vessels on the approaches to the Elbe, Weser and Jade estuaries and is the primary reference point for the German Bight light structure. The island is a duty-free shopping destination and day trip from Hamburg; the lighthouse is visible from the ferry approach and from the island's walking paths.

## 9. Scharhörn Lighthouse, Elbe Estuary

The Scharhörn lighthouse stands on the Neuwerk Island in the outer Elbe estuary, part of the Hamburg administrative district despite lying 100 kilometres from the city centre. The red-brick tower was built in 1909 to replace an earlier structure and marks the southern approach to the Elbe fairway from the Heligoland Bight. The island is reached on foot across the Wadden Sea mudflats at low tide — the walk from Cuxhaven takes about three hours each way — or by horse-drawn carriage. Neuwerk itself has a medieval tower dating from 1310, built to protect Hamburg's trade routes; the lighthouse was added when the medieval tower proved inadequate for modern navigation. The island is a national park and a bird reserve of the first order; spring and autumn migrations produce extraordinary numbers of waders and wildfowl on the surrounding mudflats.

## 10. Friedrichsort Lighthouse, Kiel Fjord

The Friedrichsort lighthouse at the entrance to Kiel Fjord is the oldest lighthouse in Schleswig-Holstein, built in 1832 on the fortified point that commands the entry to the Kiel Bight. The white tower is 26 metres tall and marks the outer approach to the Kiel Canal, the world's most heavily trafficked artificial waterway, connecting the North Sea to the Baltic Sea. The light shows a white character with red and green sectors delineating the fairway and the shallow flanks; it is one of the few German North Sea/Baltic approach lights that still uses coloured sectors as the primary channel-marking method. The fort below the lighthouse is a military heritage site and the lighthouse grounds are within the Kiel city area, accessible by public transport from the city centre.

## Visiting the German North Sea Coast Lighthouses

The East Frisian Islands and the Eiderstedt Peninsula contain the greatest concentration of accessible lighthouse heritage on the German North Sea coast. The Westerheversand tower and Pilsum are easily reached by car; the island lighthouses on Borkum, Norderney and Wangerooge require a short ferry crossing, which becomes part of the experience. Roter Sand, accessible only by charter tender, is the most extraordinary destination for the dedicated lighthouse traveller. The best season is May through September, before the autumn gale season begins in earnest; the flat Wadden Sea landscape and the wide North Sea skies are at their most dramatic in the low golden light of summer evenings.
