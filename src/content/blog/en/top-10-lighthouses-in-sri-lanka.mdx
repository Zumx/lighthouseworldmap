Sri Lanka sits at one of the great crossroads of the Indian Ocean, positioned where the sea lanes from the Persian Gulf and Red Sea converge with the routes between the Bay of Bengal and the Strait of Malacca. This strategic centrality made the island's lights indispensable to European colonial trade from the seventeenth century onward, and it was British Trinity House engineers who, from the 1830s, built the systematic network of lighthouses that still defines the island's coast. Sri Lanka's lighthouses stand on wave-washed offshore reefs, atop dramatic southern capes, and in the shadow of colonial forts — a circuit of towers that ranges from the gleaming white finger of Dondra Head, the tallest masonry lighthouse in South Asia, to the iron-screw towers planted by James Douglass on the exposed reefs of the Great and Little Basses. The island's maritime heritage is inseparable from its lighthouse history.

## 1. Galle Lighthouse (Pointe de Galle), Galle

Within the seventeenth-century Dutch-and-British fortified harbour of Galle, the lighthouse on Point Utrecht bastion is among the oldest operational light stations in the Indian Ocean. The current white masonry tower dates from 1939, replacing an earlier colonial structure, but a light has guided ships around the rocky point at the southern end of Galle harbour since the Dutch colonial period. The tower stands 18 metres tall and displays a flashing white light with a range of around 12 nautical miles, marking the eastern entrance to Galle Bay. The setting is extraordinary: the lighthouse stands within the Galle Fort UNESCO World Heritage Site, surrounded by eighteenth-century ramparts, colonial villas and the towers of Dutch Reformed and British churches. Access is straightforward — the fort is pedestrian-friendly and the lighthouse headland is a public promenade popular at sunset.

## 2. Dondra Head Lighthouse, Dondra

At the southernmost tip of Sri Lanka and of the entire Indian subcontinent, Dondra Head Lighthouse is the tallest lighthouse in South Asia, its white octagonal granite tower rising 49 metres above sea level. It was built between 1889 and 1890 by James Nicholas Douglass, the engineer who had completed Eddystone and Wolf Rock in England, and it displays a group flashing white light with a range of 28 nautical miles. The Dondra headland projects into the convergence of the Bay of Bengal and the Indian Ocean, and the hazards it marks are the submerged ledges and strong seasonal currents around the cape that have wrecked vessels working the South Asian trade routes for centuries. The lighthouse is maintained by Sri Lanka Ports Authority and the tower is occasionally open to the public; the surrounding grounds are freely accessible. Dondra village has a famous Buddhist temple nearby, adding cultural depth to any visit.

## 3. Barberyn Lighthouse (Beruwala), Beruwala

Barberyn Island, just offshore from the beach resort of Beruwala on Sri Lanka's southwest coast, holds one of the Indian Ocean's most photographed lighthouses. The white masonry tower was completed in 1889 and stands about 30 metres tall on the island's highest point, displaying an occulting white light visible for some 20 nautical miles across the approaches to the Palk Strait and the shallow coastal waters north of Colombo. Barberyn has long been a landmark for Arab traders — the Arabic name Barbar translates roughly as 'sea gate' — reflecting the island's role as a waypoint on the dhow routes long before British lighthouse engineers arrived. The lighthouse is operated by Sri Lanka Ports Authority and boatmen from Beruwala beach will ferry visitors to the island; the crossing takes about fifteen minutes and the island, apart from the lighthouse keepers' quarters, is compact and walkable.

## 4. Little Basses Lighthouse, Southern Province offshore

The Little Basses reef lies roughly 10 kilometres off the southeast coast of Sri Lanka, a submerged ledge of coral and rock that has claimed numerous vessels navigating the passage between India and the Bay of Bengal. The cast-iron screw-pile lighthouse erected on the reef in the 1870s under the direction of James Douglass — who simultaneously built the Great Basses lighthouse further east — represents a remarkable feat of offshore engineering. The tower screws directly into the reef rock and stands about 30 metres above the sea, displaying a flashing white character visible around 15 nautical miles. It is entirely wave-swept, accessible only by boat in calm conditions, and unmanned since automation. The Little Basses reef is also celebrated among divers for its extraordinary marine biodiversity, and the lighthouse is frequently photographed from dive boats working the area.

## 5. Great Basses Lighthouse, Southern Province offshore

Seventeen kilometres further east along the reef chain lies Great Basses, considered by many maritime historians to be one of the finest offshore lighthouse achievements in the British Empire. The granite tower was built between 1871 and 1873 on the submerged Great Basses reef, with materials landed from a supply tender in a narrow window of calm weather each season. The tower stands 33 metres above the sea on its rock foundation and displays a group flashing white light with a range of approximately 18 nautical miles, marking the extreme eastern boundary of the southern reef chain that extends across Sri Lanka's continental shelf. Arthur C. Clarke, who lived in Sri Lanka for decades, dived the Great Basses with friends in the 1960s and subsequently wrote about the shipwrecks it had failed to prevent. The lighthouse is unmanned and accessible only in calm conditions by chartered vessel.

## 6. Point Pedro Lighthouse, Jaffna Peninsula

At the northernmost point of Sri Lanka's Jaffna Peninsula, Point Pedro Lighthouse marks the tip of land that ships must round when entering or leaving the Gulf of Mannar and the Palk Strait. The tower is a slender white column of about 24 metres, built in the nineteenth century and substantially maintained through the civil war period that severely affected the north of the island. Point Pedro is the most northerly settlement in Sri Lanka, and the lighthouse stands amid a landscape of palmyra palms and fishing lagoons characteristic of the peninsula. Access has improved significantly since the end of the civil conflict in 2009, and the lighthouse is now accessible to visitors making the journey to Sri Lanka's northern extremity.

## 7. Colombo Lighthouse, Colombo

The lighthouse at the entrance to Colombo harbour is integral to the operations of the country's principal commercial port, guiding the enormous container ships and tankers that call at Colombo — one of the fastest-growing transshipment hubs in the Indian Ocean. The current structure is a modern tower reflecting post-independence construction, but lights have marked the Colombo anchorage since the Portuguese and Dutch colonial periods when the roadstead outside the reef was the primary anchorage for Indian Ocean trade. The lighthouse is not accessible to the public, being within the active port area, but it is visible from the Galle Face Green promenade and from the upper decks of ships entering or leaving.

## 8. Trincomalee Lighthouse, Eastern Province

Trincomalee's magnificent natural harbour on the northeast coast is one of the finest deepwater anchorages in Asia, and its lighthouse marks the entrance channel between the rocky headlands of Swami Rock — where the Koneswaram Hindu temple stands dramatically above the sea — and the northern entrance point. The light has guided warships and merchant vessels into Trincomalee since the British established their Indian Ocean naval base here in the early nineteenth century. The lighthouse stands within an area of considerable natural beauty where the hills meet the sea, and the harbour is surrounded by the beaches of the eastern coast that remain relatively undeveloped compared to the southwest.

## 9. Kankesanthurai Lighthouse, Jaffna

Near the Kankesanthurai port on the northern coast of the Jaffna Peninsula, this lighthouse served the ferry routes between Sri Lanka and southern India, a crossing of the Palk Strait that was a principal link between the two countries before air travel. The tower marks a coast where the shallow and shifting sands of the Palk Strait demand careful pilotage, particularly for the ferry vessels and fishing boats that work this passage year-round. Kankesanthurai was an important port for the Tamil community of the north, and the lighthouse carries with it the complex history of that community's relationship with the sea.

## 10. Batticaloa Lighthouse, Eastern Province

The lighthouse at Batticaloa on the east coast serves the entrance to the Batticaloa lagoon, a large brackish water body separated from the Indian Ocean by a narrow barrier beach. The town of Batticaloa is famous for 'singing fish' — an acoustic phenomenon of the lagoon disputed in its origin but celebrated in local lore — and the lighthouse marking the sea entrance is a modest structure appropriate to the relatively sheltered nature of the lagoon mouth. The east coast's lighthouse network was disrupted during the civil conflict, and the Batticaloa lighthouse is part of a broader restoration of maritime infrastructure that has accompanied the region's post-war recovery and the growth of coastal tourism.

## Practical Information

Sri Lanka's lighthouse circuit rewards a peripatetic approach: the southwestern coast (Galle, Beruwala, Dondra) is accessible by road from Colombo along the well-maintained coastal highway, while the offshore towers at Little Basses and Great Basses require chartering a boat from Kirinda, best done through Yala area operators between March and May when seas are calmest. The northern lighthouses (Point Pedro, Kankesanthurai) are now freely accessible since the end of the civil conflict. The southwest monsoon from June to October brings heavy swells to the southern and western coasts, making the dry season from November to April the most rewarding time for lighthouse visits.
