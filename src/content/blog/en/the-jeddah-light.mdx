At the entrance to the Islamic Port of Jeddah, on the eastern shore of the Red Sea, a slim white concrete tower rises far above the cranes and container stacks of one of the busiest harbours in the Middle East. Crowned by a steel observation sphere and topped by its light, the Jeddah Light is widely cited as the tallest lighthouse in the world, reaching well over 130 metres. It is a structure most travellers never notice and few can name, yet it stands sentinel over a coast that has carried pilgrims and trade for well over a thousand years.

## A Gateway to Mecca

Jeddah is not an ordinary port. For centuries it has been the maritime gateway to the holy city of Mecca, which lies inland a short distance to the east. Pilgrims performing the Hajj and the Umrah have arrived by sea at Jeddah for generation upon generation, disembarking here before completing the journey overland. In the modern era the great majority arrive by air, but the sea route shaped the city, and the port remains central to the kingdom's commerce and to the movement of goods up and down the Red Sea.

The Red Sea itself is a difficult and historically dangerous body of water, narrow, hot, and strewn with coral reefs that have wrecked countless ships. The approaches to Jeddah are guarded by reefs that demand careful navigation, and the need for a powerful, high light marking the harbour entrance is plain. The Jeddah Light answers that need on a monumental scale.

## The Tallest Tower

The lighthouse was built in the late twentieth century as part of the modernisation of the port, and its height is its most remarkable feature. Figures cited for the tower commonly place it at around 133 metres, which would make it the tallest lighthouse in the world, exceeding the famous towers of Europe and the Americas by a wide margin. It is a work of reinforced concrete rather than the brick or cut stone of older lights, a slender shaft engineered for an age of large ships and electronic navigation.

Near the summit sits a steel sphere, an observation level reached by a lift within the shaft, and above that the lantern and its light. The whole structure doubles as a control tower for the port, overseeing the movement of vessels in and out of the harbour. It is less a romantic isolated beacon than a piece of major port infrastructure, but its sheer height places it in a category of its own among the lighthouses of the world.

## Light and Function

Unlike the historic lighthouses that draw tourists in Europe and North America, the Jeddah Light is a working part of a secure commercial and industrial port, not a visitor attraction. Its purpose is strictly practical: to mark the harbour entrance, to provide a vantage point for port control, and to carry the light that guides shipping through the reef-strewn approaches at night. The tower is not open to the public, and access to the port area is restricted, as is normal for a major modern harbour.

This functional character is worth dwelling on. Many of the world's most celebrated lighthouses have outlived their original purpose and survive as museums and heritage sites; the Jeddah Light, by contrast, is a thoroughly contemporary structure doing a contemporary job. It represents a different chapter in the long story of coastal navigation, one in which the lighthouse is integrated into a vast logistical machine rather than standing alone on a wild headland.

## The Red Sea Coast

The Saudi coast of the Red Sea runs for well over a thousand kilometres, from the Gulf of Aqaba in the north to the border with Yemen in the south, and it is marked by lights, beacons and range lights that guide shipping along a shore fringed almost continuously by coral. Far to the south, near the city of Jazan close to the Yemeni frontier, stands a port control tower of its own, while along the coast a network of smaller beacons and breakwater lights marks harbours, anchorages and the channels through the reefs.

This is a coast undergoing rapid change. Vast development projects along the northern Red Sea shore are reshaping a coastline that was, until recently, sparsely settled, and tourism is being actively developed along reefs that have long been prized by divers for their clarity and the richness of their marine life. The lights of the Saudi Red Sea, the towering Jeddah beacon among them, mark a shore that is both ancient in its history and strikingly modern in its present.

## A Lighthouse Among Giants

The claim to be the tallest lighthouse in the world invites comparison. The traditional record-holders of the lighthouse world, the great brick towers of the American coast and the granite columns of Europe, rarely exceed 60 to 70 metres. By rising to roughly twice that height, the Jeddah Light sits in a class apart, more comparable to a tall building than to a conventional coastal tower. Whether it is counted as a true lighthouse or as a port control tower carrying a light is partly a matter of definition, but by the usual measure of height it has no equal.

Its scale reflects the scale of the port it serves. The Islamic Port of Jeddah handles an enormous volume of containers and bulk cargo, and the great tower marking its entrance is sized to match. There is a certain symmetry in the tallest lighthouse in the world standing over one of the oldest and most storied harbours of the Red Sea, a gateway through which faith and commerce have flowed for more than a millennium.

## The Pilgrim Sea

It is impossible to think about Jeddah without thinking about the Hajj. For centuries the sea voyage across the Red Sea or down from the Mediterranean and the Suez route was the great pilgrimage road, and the harbour of Jeddah was its terminus. Travellers wrote of the dangers of the reefs, the heat, and the crowds of the season, and of the relief of reaching the port at last. The modern light that towers over the harbour stands in that long tradition, the latest and tallest in a line of beacons that have guided travellers toward the holy cities.

For the visitor today, Jeddah offers far more than its port. The old town of Al-Balad, with its tall coral-stone houses and their carved wooden balconies, is a UNESCO World Heritage Site and one of the most atmospheric historic quarters on the Red Sea, a reminder of the merchant city that grew up around the pilgrim trade. The great concrete light at the harbour mouth is the modern bookend to that history.

## Visiting Jeddah

Because the Jeddah Light is part of a secure working port, it cannot be visited in the way that a heritage lighthouse can, and travellers should not expect to climb it or approach it closely. What they can do is appreciate it from the city, where its slender shaft is visible rising above the harbour, and set it in the context of a fascinating coastal city. Saudi Arabia has opened to tourism in recent years, and Jeddah, with its corniche along the Red Sea, its historic old town and its position as the gateway to Mecca, has become one of the country's principal destinations.

A visit might combine the corniche and its modern landmarks, the coral-stone alleys of Al-Balad, and a boat trip out over the reefs that have made these waters both beautiful and dangerous. From the sea, the tallest lighthouse in the world rises over the port, a beacon for a coast where the very oldest patterns of pilgrimage and trade meet the newest scale of global shipping.
