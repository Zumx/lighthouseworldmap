The Mediterranean Sea has been a highway of human civilisation for four thousand years, and its lighthouses reflect the full depth of that history. The Pharos of Alexandria — one of the Seven Wonders of the Ancient World, standing perhaps 120 metres tall on the island of Pharos at the entrance to Alexandria's harbour — established the very word for lighthouse in a dozen European languages. The Romans maintained fire-towers at the entrances to their major ports from Gibraltar to the Bosphorus. The medieval trading republics of Venice, Genoa, and Ragusa built towers at the entries to their harbours whose masonry still stands beneath modern lighthouse structures. The sea itself is not as benign as its reputation suggests: the Mistral and the Tramontane drive violent seas against the French and Ligurian coasts; the Bora howls from the north into the Adriatic; the Meltemi raises whitecaps across the Aegean for weeks at a time in summer. The lighthouse network that developed in response to these conditions spans Roman ruins, medieval towers, Baroque structures, and the iron-and-glass engineering of the nineteenth century, distributed across the coasts of twenty-two countries.

## 1. Faro di Genova (La Lanterna), Italy

The Lanterna of Genoa is the oldest lighthouse in continuous operation in the world that has functioned as a lighthouse in the modern sense — a light visible from sea, on an elevated tower, marking a major harbour entrance. The first tower was built on the promontory of Capo di Faro around 1128; the current structure, 77 metres tall in two octagonal tiers, dates substantially from 1543, with the lantern room rebuilt in the nineteenth century. The white flash every five seconds is visible for 26 nautical miles and guides vessels into the busy port of Genoa, Italy's largest seaport. Cristoforo Colombo's uncle was keeper here in the late fifteenth century; family tradition held that the young Columbus climbed the tower and first conceived his idea of a westward route to Asia watching ships depart. The lighthouse is open to visitors on weekend afternoons; the small museum at the base documents its medieval and Renaissance history.

## 2. Faro de Formentor, Mallorca, Spain

The Faro de Formentor, at the northern tip of Mallorca, combines dramatic geography with a long operational history. The lighthouse was completed in 1863 on a 210-metre cliff at the end of the Serra de Tramuntana; the white tower is 15 metres tall and the character is a white flash every four seconds visible for 25 nautical miles. The cape divides the Bay of Pollença from the open Mediterranean and marks the most exposed point of the Balearic Islands to the northerly Tramontane wind. The access road, the Ma-2210, was carved into the cliff face in the 1920s and is one of the most spectacular drives in the western Mediterranean, passing through tunnels and along corniche sections above sheer drops to the sea. The lighthouse grounds are freely accessible and the cape is a popular destination for the yachts that fill Pollença Bay in summer.

## 3. Phare du Planier, Marseille, France

The Phare du Planier stands on the island of Planier 14 kilometres southwest of Marseille, the outermost navigation mark for the approach to France's largest seaport and the principal lighthouse for the western approaches to the Gulf of Lion. Built in 1829, the tower was substantially rebuilt in 1959 after wartime damage; the current white tower is 35 metres tall with a focal plane of 59 metres. The character is a white flash every ten seconds visible for 30 nautical miles. The island is bare limestone rock, barely large enough for the lighthouse buildings; it carries a population of zero year-round. The approaches to Marseille from the west are complicated by the Planier reef and associated shoals, making the lighthouse indispensable to the container ships, ferries, and tankers that use Marseille daily. Access is by private boat only; the island can be seen from the coastal road at Callelongue.

## 4. Faros Akrotiriou (Santorini), Greece

The lighthouse at Akrotiri on Santorini stands on the southwestern cape of the island above the caldera that forms the most dramatic landscape in the Aegean. The white-and-red-striped tower, built in 1892 during the period of French involvement in Greek lighthouse administration, is 9 metres tall on a cliff 138 metres above the sea. The character is a white flash every five seconds with a range of 24 nautical miles, guiding vessels around the southern approach to the Santorini anchorage from the southwest. The caldera of Santorini is the collapsed remnant of a volcanic eruption around 1628 BC that may have contributed to the decline of the Minoan civilisation; the lighthouse sits near the edge of this geological monument. The cape is accessible by road from the village of Akrotiri and the walk from the excavation site of the ancient Minoan city is one of the finest half-days in the Cyclades.

## 5. Faro della Vittoria, Trieste, Italy

The Faro della Vittoria, built in 1927 on the hill of Gretta above Trieste, is both a working lighthouse and a First World War memorial to Italian sailors lost in the Adriatic. The tower is 60 metres tall — the highest lighthouse building in Italy — surmounted by a bronze statue of Victory and ringed by a colonnade at its base. The light shows a white flash every five seconds with a range of 31 nautical miles, visible across the Gulf of Trieste to Slovenia and Croatia. The lighthouse marks the entrance to the port of Trieste, historically the principal seaport of the Habsburg Empire and still one of the most important container ports in the Adriatic. The structure was designed by architect Arduino Berlam in a mixture of Roman triumphal and Art Deco styles. The lighthouse can be viewed from the road below the hill and from the Trieste waterfront promenade; the surrounding park provides access to the base.

## 6. Capo San Vito Lighthouse, Sicily

The lighthouse at Capo San Vito on the northwestern tip of Sicily marks the transition between the Tyrrhenian Sea and the Strait of Sicily, the narrow passage between Sicily and the North African coast. Built in 1859, the white tower stands on a low cape above one of the most beautiful stretches of Sicilian coastline: clear turquoise water, limestone promontories, and the distant profile of the Egadi Islands to the west. The light shows a group of two white flashes every fifteen seconds with a range of 20 nautical miles. The cape is within the San Vito Lo Capo nature reserve and is accessible from the beach resort of San Vito Lo Capo, one of Sicily's most popular summer destinations. The lighthouse grounds are enclosed but the tower is clearly visible from the beach promenade.

## 7. Cape Greco Lighthouse, Cyprus

Cape Greco, the southeastern tip of Cyprus between Ayia Napa and Protaras, carries a lighthouse that marks the transition between the eastern Mediterranean and the approaches to the Levant coast — historically one of the busiest sea lanes in the ancient world, linking Alexandria, Beirut, and the ports of the Syrian coast. The current white tower was built in 1957 and is 10 metres tall on a cliff 98 metres above the sea; the flashing white light carries 16 nautical miles. The surrounding Cape Greco National Forest Park protects limestone cliffs, sea caves, and maquis scrub; the area is popular for diving, sea kayaking, and cliff walking. The lighthouse is visible from the coastal paths that run through the park from both Ayia Napa and Protaras; the setting — white tower, blue sea, and the rocky headland of the eastern Mediterranean — is one of the most distinctly Mediterranean lighthouse vistas.

## 8. Porer Lighthouse, Croatia

The Porer lighthouse stands on a small islet at the southern tip of the Istrian Peninsula in Croatia, marking the approaches to the Kvarner Gulf from the Adriatic. Built in 1833 during the Austrian administration of Istria, the square stone tower is 35 metres tall on a rock barely large enough for the lighthouse buildings; the character is a white flash every ten seconds with a range of 21 nautical miles. The islet is tiny and exposed to the Bora wind that periodically sweeps down from the Dinaric Alps into the Adriatic with speeds exceeding 100 kilometres per hour. The lighthouse is available as holiday accommodation through the Plovput Croatian lighthouse authority, making it possible to stay on the isolated rock in the summer; the experience of watching dawn break over the Adriatic from a stone tower on a bare rock in the open sea is one that few lighthouse visitors anywhere can match.

## 9. Capo Spartivento Lighthouse, Sardinia

The Faro di Capo Spartivento at the southern tip of Sardinia marks the division between the Tyrrhenian Sea to the east and the western Mediterranean. Built in 1856 on a dramatic granite headland, the white tower is 13 metres tall with a focal plane of 30 metres; it flashes white every three seconds with a range of 22 nautical miles. The cape is the southernmost point of Sardinia and the headland immediately to the west is a nature reserve protecting the nesting grounds of Eleonora's falcon, a raptor that breeds throughout the Mediterranean islands and migrates to Madagascar in winter. The lighthouse buildings have been converted to exclusive accommodation; the surrounding headland, accessible from Chia beach on the south coast, offers walking through maquis above the turquoise sea.

## 10. Punta Carena Lighthouse, Capri

The Faro di Punta Carena at the southwestern tip of Capri is the second most powerful lighthouse in Italy after the Lanterna of Genova; built in 1867, the white tower stands 24 metres tall on a cliff above one of the most famous swimming spots in the Mediterranean. The character is a white flash every three seconds with a range of 25 nautical miles, guiding vessels around the western tip of Capri in the Gulf of Naples approach. The lighthouse marks the boundary between the Tyrrhenian open sea and the shelter of the Capri anchorage; vessels approaching Naples from the south use it as the final waypoint before bearing northeast for the port. The lighthouse grounds are not open to the public, but the lighthouse is visible from the boat tours that circumnavigate the island, and from the Faro di Punta Carena sea terrace beach immediately below the cliff.

## Visiting the Mediterranean Lighthouses

The Mediterranean's lighthouse heritage spans three continents and twenty-two countries; even a focused tour by region offers extraordinary variety. The Italian and Croatian coasts have the best combination of lighthouse access and accommodation options: the Porer and Savudrija lighthouses in Croatia and several Sicilian stations offer holiday lets directly on the lighthouse islets. For architectural history, the Lanterna of Genova is unrivalled. The Greek Aegean islands are best explored by ferry, which brings several lighthouse headlands within view. Summer is the practical season across the Mediterranean, though the Aegean winds and Adriatic Bora mean that some headland sites are most dramatic in spring or autumn.
