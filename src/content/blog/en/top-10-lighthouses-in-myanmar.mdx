Myanmar's coast runs for some 2,000 kilometres down the eastern flank of the Bay of Bengal and the Andaman Sea, from the border with Bangladesh in the north to the long tail of Tanintharyi reaching toward the Malay Peninsula in the south. It is a coast of contrasts: the vast, silt-laden delta of the Irrawaddy in the centre; the rocky, island-strewn Rakhine shore to the north; and the Myeik Archipelago, hundreds of largely uninhabited islands, in the far south. This was Burma, one of the great rice-exporting lands of the British Empire, and many of its navigation aids date from that era of intense maritime trade.

## The Irrawaddy Delta: A River Building Land

The defining feature of Myanmar's coast is the Irrawaddy delta. The river, the country's great artery, descends from the Himalayan margins and spreads at its mouth into a vast fan of distributaries, carrying an enormous load of silt that builds new land into the Bay of Bengal year by year. Navigation across this delta is governed by the same logic that rules every great river mouth: shifting channels, moving bars, and the constant work of dredging and re-marking. The approaches to the delta ports demand range lights and buoys that can be moved as the channels migrate, rather than fixed monuments, and the whole system reflects a coast where the land itself is provisional.

## Yangon and the Rangoon River

Yangon, the former Rangoon, is Myanmar's largest city and principal port, lying not on the open coast but some distance up the Rangoon River, one of the eastern channels of the delta. For ships, reaching Yangon means crossing the bar at the river mouth and ascending a tidal channel through the delta flats. Under British rule Rangoon was one of the busiest rice ports in the world, and the navigation aids of the river approach were developed to handle that traffic. The lights and beacons marking the bar and the channel guide vessels from the open Andaman Sea up to the city wharves, and they remain essential to the country's seaborne trade.

## Rakhine and the Northern Coast

North of the delta, the coast of Rakhine State faces the Bay of Bengal and is a different world: rockier, more indented, fringed with islands, and backed by the Arakan mountains that cut it off from the interior. The old port of Sittwe (Akyab), at the mouth of the Kaladan River, was historically important to trade with Bengal, and it remains the principal town of the northern coast. The waters here are exposed to the full force of the south-west monsoon, and the lights of this stretch mark a coast of headlands and offshore islands rather than the low delta flats to the south. This is also some of the most beautiful and least developed coastline in the country.

## St Martin's Island and the Northern Frontier

At the very northern tip of Myanmar's coast, the waters meet those of Bangladesh near St Martin's Island — a small coral island that lies on the Bangladeshi side of a maritime border that has at times been contested. The island, known in Bengali as সেন্ট মার্টিন দ্বীপ বাতিঘর for its lighthouse, sits in shallow, reef-fringed water at the mouth of the Naf River, which divides the two countries. The lights along this frontier serve both the local fishing traffic and the wider coastal trade, and they mark a sensitive corner where the deltas of two nations meet in the north-eastern Bay of Bengal.

## The Andaman Coast and Tanintharyi

South of the delta, the coast turns and runs down the long, narrow region of Tanintharyi (Tenasserim) toward the Malay Peninsula, facing the Andaman Sea. This is a tropical coast of clear water, mangrove, and an increasingly island-strewn sea as one moves south. The port of Mawlamyine (Moulmein), at the mouth of the Salween (Thanlwin) River, was another major centre of the British teak and rice trade, immortalised in the opening of Kipling's verse about the road to Mandalay. The lights of this coast mark river mouths and headlands along a shore that grows wilder and more remote toward the south.

## The Myeik Archipelago: Hundreds of Islands

The far south of Myanmar holds one of the great island groups of Southeast Asia: the Myeik (Mergui) Archipelago, some 800 islands scattered across the Andaman Sea off the Tanintharyi coast. Largely uninhabited apart from the sea-nomad Moken people, these islands are fringed with coral reefs and rise from clear tropical water, and they have remained among the least-visited places in the region. For navigation, the archipelago is a labyrinth: a scatter of islands, reefs, and channels through which the coastal trade and a growing dive- and cruise-tourism traffic must pick their way. The lights here are sparse, and local knowledge counts for as much as any chart.

## The British Legacy and the Monsoon

To understand Myanmar's lights one must remember Burma's role in the British Empire. Rangoon was a great imperial port, and the navigation network of the delta and the coast was built to serve the export of rice, teak, and oil on a vast scale. Many of the older beacons and the survey work behind them date from that period. Over it all rules the monsoon: the south-west monsoon from roughly May to October brings heavy rain, swollen rivers, and rough seas, while the dry season offers calmer conditions. The lights serve a coast where the rhythm of the year, the load of the rivers, and the legacy of empire all shape the way ships move.

## A Coast Between River and Sea

Myanmar's coast is, above all, a meeting of river and sea on a grand scale. The Irrawaddy builds land into the bay; the monsoon governs the seasons; the British built the ports and many of the lights; and the islands of the far south remain among the wildest in Asia. From the rocky Rakhine shore in the north, through the great delta in the centre, to the scattered jewels of the Myeik Archipelago in the south, the lights of old Burma mark one of the longest and most varied coastlines on the Bay of Bengal — a shore still finding its place in the modern maritime world.
