Canada's lighthouse heritage is among the most extensive in the world, shaped by a coastline of extraordinary length — over 202,000 kilometres including island shorelines — and by the navigational demands of Atlantic fishing grounds, Pacific approaches, the Gulf of St Lawrence, and the thousands of kilometres of British Columbia's fractured inland sea. The first Canadian lighthouse, the Louisbourg tower on Cape Breton Island, was lit in 1734 under French colonial administration. By the time of Confederation in 1867, hundreds of lights guided vessels through the approaches to Halifax, Quebec, and the Great Lakes. The Canadian Coast Guard now maintains approximately 1,000 active lighthouses across the country, with many hundreds more decommissioned, transferred to heritage organisations, or demolished. The surviving towers range from the beloved granite sentinel of Peggy's Cove to the working lights of the outer British Columbia coast, staffed by keepers who remain an anomaly among modern lighthouse services.

## 1. Peggy's Cove Lighthouse, Nova Scotia

The lighthouse at Peggy's Cove, completed in 1914 near the entrance to St Margaret's Bay on the Nova Scotia south shore, is the most visited and most photographed lighthouse in Canada. The white-painted concrete octagonal tower stands 15 metres on a smooth, wave-washed granite outcrop at the edge of the village. The light character is a single white flash every ten seconds, with a range of 14 nautical miles over the approaches to St Margaret's Bay. The lighthouse serves as an active Canada Post outlet in summer, reportedly the most unusual postal address in the country. The site is managed by Parks Canada as a National Historic Site. Warning signs around the low coastal rocks are required reading: rogue waves periodically sweep the granite and have killed visitors who ignored safety instructions. The best photographic light is in the early morning, before the tour buses arrive.

## 2. Cape Spear Lighthouse, Newfoundland

Cape Spear, the most easterly point of North America, carries two lighthouses: the historic 1836 tower — the oldest surviving lighthouse in Newfoundland — and the 1955 automated tower that replaced it as the active light. The 1836 structure is a rare example of a keeper's dwelling with the lantern room built into the roof, a design used occasionally in eastern Canada in the early nineteenth century. It has been restored to its appearance in the 1830s-1840s period and is open to visitors as a National Historic Site. The active light shows a white flash every five seconds, range 23 nautical miles. Cape Spear is also a significant military heritage site: gun batteries built in the Second World War to protect St John's harbour remain intact and are accessible. The site is open year-round; the combination of the historic lighthouse and the dramatic clifftop setting overlooking the Labrador Current makes this one of the essential heritage sites in Atlantic Canada.

## 3. Point Atkinson Lighthouse, British Columbia

Point Atkinson lighthouse at the northern entrance to Burrard Inlet in West Vancouver, completed in 1875 and rebuilt in 1912, marks the approach to the port of Vancouver from the Strait of Georgia. The current hexagonal reinforced concrete tower with its distinctive wide lantern gallery was designed in a style unusual in Canadian lighthouse construction and is a National Historic Site. The light character is a white flash every four seconds with a range of 20 nautical miles; a fog signal, one of the most powerful remaining on the BC coast, sounds in reduced visibility. The lighthouse is the last staffed lighthouse in the Strait of Georgia and one of a small number of staffed stations remaining on the Canadian Pacific coast. The surrounding Lighthouse Park in West Vancouver is a popular hiking destination with old-growth forest close to the city; the lighthouse grounds are accessible on foot from the park trails.

## 4. Cove Island Lighthouse, Ontario

Cove Island at the north entrance to Georgian Bay in Lake Huron carries a lighthouse that has guided vessels through the shoal-strewn approaches to the Bruce Peninsula since 1858. The imperial tower — a 25-metre white masonry structure with a red lantern — was built to a design used at several Lake Huron stations and marks the gap between the main channel and the Bruce Shoals. The light character is a white flash every ten seconds, range 16 nautical miles. The Georgian Bay route was the main passage for vessels carrying grain and lumber from the north shore ports to Lake Ontario, and the Cove Island light served this traffic for over a century. The lighthouse is currently staffed by the Canadian Coast Guard as one of the remaining monitored stations on the Great Lakes; access is by chartered boat from Tobermory.

## 5. Long Point Lighthouse, Ontario

Long Point, the sand spit extending 30 kilometres into Lake Erie from the northern shore of Norfolk County, is one of the most dangerous navigational hazards on the Great Lakes and has been called the 'Graveyard of the Great Lakes' along with sections of Lake Superior and Lake Huron. The lighthouse, completed in 1879, is a red brick imperial tower of 27 metres on the tip of the spit, accessible only by boat. The light character is a white flash every four seconds, range 18 nautical miles. Long Point is a UNESCO World Biosphere Reserve for its significant wetland and migratory bird habitat; the lighthouse stands within the biosphere area. The Long Point Company, which owns the inner portions of the spit, controls access; organised bird-watching and heritage tours visit the lighthouse in season.

## 6. Peggy's Point to Cape Forchu: Nova Scotia's South Shore

The stretch of Nova Scotia coastline between Lunenburg and Yarmouth contains more historic lighthouses per kilometre than any comparable section of the Atlantic Canadian coast. Cape Forchu lighthouse at Yarmouth, a reinforced concrete tower of distinctive 'apple core' design completed in 1962 to replace an 1840 wooden structure, rises 23 metres above the rocky cape that marks the western entrance to the Bay of Fundy approaches. The light character is a group of three white flashes every fifteen seconds, range 23 nautical miles. The cape is accessible by road and the Friends of the Yarmouth Light operate a visitor centre in the restored keepers' dwelling.

## 7. Boat Bluff Lighthouse, British Columbia

Boat Bluff on Sarah Island in the narrow Sarah Passage of Milbanke Sound on the north-central BC coast is one of the most remote staffed lighthouses in Canada. Completed in 1907 and rebuilt several times, the current white tower stands 9 metres on a rocky bluff. The station marks the narrow channel between the outer coast and the inside passage, used by the BC ferry fleet, commercial fishing vessels, and the small-craft marine highway serving isolated coastal communities. Boat Bluff is one of approximately 27 staffed lighthouses remaining on the BC coast, maintained by the Canadian Coast Guard. Access is by private boat or floatplane. The light character is a white and red sector light visible for 14 nautical miles.

## 8. Nootka Lighthouse, British Columbia

The Nootka lighthouse on San Rafael Island at the entrance to Nootka Sound was built in 1911 to mark one of the historically significant passages on the BC coast — the site of Spanish and British contact in the 1770s and the subject of the Nootka Sound Convention of 1790. The lighthouse station has been staffed continuously since its construction. The current tower shows a white flash every five seconds, range 17 nautical miles. The lighthouse keepers at Nootka are a primary source of weather observations for the outer Pacific coast; the station is one of several that contribute meteorological data collected manually and transmitted to Environment and Climate Change Canada. Access is by floatplane from Gold River or by private vessel.

## 9. Cape Beale Lighthouse, British Columbia

Cape Beale on the western coast of Vancouver Island marks the southern entrance to Barkley Sound and one of the most notorious stretches of coastline in Canada: the 'Graveyard of the Pacific', where the prevailing south-westerly swells, offshore rocks, and persistent fog have caused hundreds of shipwrecks. The lighthouse, first established in 1874, is a white wooden tower of 15 metres on a headland overlooking the Pacific approaches. It has been staffed continuously and the Cape Beale keepers have assisted in numerous rescues along the West Coast Trail, the hiking route established partly as a lifesaving trail following the wreck of the Valencia in 1906 with the loss of 136 lives. The light character is a white flash every five seconds, range 21 nautical miles. Access is by trail from Bamfield or by boat.

## 10. Brockton Point Lighthouse, British Columbia

Brockton Point lighthouse in Stanley Park, Vancouver, is the most-visited lighthouse in Canada in terms of foot traffic — though few of the millions of Stanley Park visitors who pass it each year recognise it as a working navigational light. The small white cast-iron tower, completed in 1914 on the rocky point guarding the First Narrows approach to Burrard Inlet, shows a fixed red light that warns vessels of the shoal water between the lighthouse and the north shore. The structure is diminutive compared to the major coastal lights, but it marks a point used by every vessel entering or leaving the port of Vancouver on the inner route. The lighthouse is surrounded by the park and accessible on foot; the totem poles a few hundred metres away draw most of the visitors' attention.

## Visiting Canada's Lighthouses

Canada's lighthouse heritage is enormous and geographically dispersed. Atlantic Canada — Nova Scotia, Newfoundland, Prince Edward Island, New Brunswick — offers the densest concentration of historic towers and the best heritage visitor infrastructure. British Columbia's outer coast lighthouses are world-class for scenery and historical significance but require considerable effort to reach; the staffed stations are not open to casual visitors but can be seen from the water. The Canadian summer (July to September) is the prime visiting season for most stations; the fall colour in Nova Scotia in October adds to the appeal for lighthouse itineraries.
