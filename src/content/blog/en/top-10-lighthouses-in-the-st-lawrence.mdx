The St Lawrence River and its estuary form one of the great maritime corridors of North American history. From the open Atlantic at Cabot Strait, vessels have followed the widening funnel of the Gulf and then the increasingly narrow, increasingly treacherous river channel for five centuries, first in search of furs and fish, later to supply the colonies of New France, and eventually to reach the ports of Quebec City and Montreal that lie more than 1,000 kilometres from the open sea. The challenges the route imposes are formidable: winter ice that closes the river entirely, summer fogs that can persist for days, shifting sandbars at river mouths, and a tidal range that at certain points in the estuary exceeds four metres. The Canadian lighthouse network on the St Lawrence is among the most extensive and historically significant in North America, maintained today by the Canadian Coast Guard under the auspices of Transport Canada. ## 1. Pointe-au-Père Lighthouse, Rimouski

Pointe-au-Père, on the south shore of the St Lawrence estuary near Rimouski, is home to one of the most visited lighthouse complexes in Canada. The principal tower, completed in 1909 and standing 33 metres tall, is the third lighthouse on this site — the original was established in 1859. The light's importance derives from Pointe-au-Père's role as the compulsory pilotage station for the St Lawrence: pilots boarded ocean-going vessels here to guide them upriver through the difficult navigation above Rimouski. The light character is a flashing white sequence with a range of 22 nautical miles. The station is now a National Historic Site of Canada and includes a museum centred on the wreck of the RMS Empress of Ireland, which sank in 1914 in the estuary below with the loss of 1,012 lives — Canada's worst peacetime maritime disaster. The submarine wreck lies visible in the museum as an exhibit; the lighthouse tower is open for climbing in the summer season.

## 2. Cap-des-Rosiers Lighthouse, Gaspésie

Cap-des-Rosiers, at the northeastern tip of the Gaspé Peninsula where it meets the Gulf of St Lawrence, is home to the tallest lighthouse in Canada. The white stone tower, completed in 1858 to the design of Théodore Lambart, rises 37 metres above the headland with a light character of group flashing white visible 30 nautical miles — one of the most powerful lights in eastern Canada. The cape marks the transition between the Gulf and the estuary and was historically one of the most dangerous points on the St Lawrence approach, with strong currents and frequent fog. The lighthouse was designated a National Historic Site in 1966. The keeper's quarters and outbuildings have been preserved, and the tower is accessible to visitors in summer. Cap-des-Rosiers village sits below the headland; the surrounding Forillon National Park offers exceptional hiking and whale watching.

## 3. Île Verte Lighthouse, Île Verte

Île Verte (Green Island) holds the distinction of being the site of the oldest surviving lighthouse in Canada, established in 1809 on the south shore of the St Lawrence estuary opposite Rivière-du-Loup. The original stone tower, 18 metres tall, still stands and still operates, making it a living artefact of the early Canadian lighthouse programme. The light character is a flashing white with a range of about 16 nautical miles. The island itself is inhabited by a small permanent community and is accessible by ferry from Notre-Dame-des-Portages; visitors can stay in heritage accommodation in the lighthouse cottages. The island's combination of historic agriculture, endangered river beluga whale habitat, and lighthouse heritage makes it one of the most distinctive destinations in the St Lawrence. The keeper's house is now managed as a heritage inn.

## 4. La Martre Lighthouse, La Martre

On the north shore of the Gaspé Peninsula, La Martre lighthouse is perhaps the most aesthetically distinctive on the entire St Lawrence. The octagonal red wooden tower, completed in 1906, rises 22 metres from its clifftop site on the southern shore of the Gulf. The wooden construction and octagonal plan are unusual — most Canadian lighthouses of this era used either circular masonry or iron-skeleton structures — and the red-painted exterior set against the dark boreal forest makes it immediately recognisable. The light character is a flashing red sequence visible around 12 nautical miles. The station is managed by the municipality as a heritage attraction; the lighthouse and adjacent keeper's house are open in summer. The village of La Martre, with its traditional Gaspesian character, complements the maritime heritage of the site.

## 5. Cap-Chat Lighthouse, Cap-Chat

Cap-Chat (Cat's Head Cape) sits on the south shore of the St Lawrence estuary, where the river narrows perceptibly and the tidal currents begin to intensify. The lighthouse here was established in 1871; the current white tower is a cylindrical structure of moderate height, perhaps 14 metres, with a light visible around 14 nautical miles. The cape takes its name from a rock formation said to resemble a crouching cat, visible at low tide. Cap-Chat is better known today for its wind farm — among the first commercial wind energy installations in Quebec — which includes an experimental vertical-axis turbine of record height. The lighthouse stands as a quieter counterpoint to this energy infrastructure, a continuity of the cape's long relationship with the wind that drives both weather and turbines. The site is accessible by road along the south shore route.

## 6. Pointe-des-Monts Lighthouse, Côte-Nord

Pointe-des-Monts marks the transition between the St Lawrence River and the wider St Lawrence Gulf on the north shore; many navigators consider it the true limit between the two bodies of water. The stone tower, completed in 1830 and standing 26 metres tall, is one of the oldest lighthouses in Quebec and operated continuously for over 150 years before being automated. The character is flashing white with a range of about 22 nautical miles. The station now operates as a heritage bed and breakfast, with rooms available in the former keeper's house; overnight guests can experience the lighthouse from the keeper's perspective. The cape's position on the north shore, where beluga whales congregate in summer and seabirds are abundant, makes it a destination beyond its lighthouse heritage alone.

## 7. Matane Lighthouse, Matane

The town of Matane on the south shore operates a ferry service to the north shore and Anticosti Island, and the lighthouse at Matane's harbour entrance has guided ferry traffic and commercial vessels for many decades. The present red and white tower, of steel construction, dates from the mid-twentieth century and is functional rather than picturesque; but it marks the ferry route that is a lifeline for north shore communities. The town itself has a salmon river — the Matane River passes through its centre — and combines lighthouse watching with a strong culinary tradition centred on river fish.

## 8. Anticosti Island Lighthouse (Heath Point), Anticosti

Heath Point, at the eastern end of Anticosti Island, is the guardian of the Gulf's most dangerous graveyard. The island, 222 kilometres long but sparsely inhabited, lies across the shipping lane between the Cabot Strait and the river; its shallow shores and powerful currents have claimed more than 400 vessels. The lighthouse at Heath Point, first lit in 1835 and the current white cylindrical tower dating from 1900, stands 26 metres tall with a light of group flashing white visible 23 nautical miles. The station is extremely remote; access is by small aircraft to Port-Menier, the island's only village, followed by a long drive on gravel roads. The surrounding boreal forest holds one of the world's densest white-tailed deer populations and occasional sightings of St Lawrence beluga and fin whales from the cape.

## 9. Sept-Îles Lighthouse, Sept-Îles

The natural harbour of Sept-Îles (Seven Islands) on the north shore is guarded by an archipelago of islands that shelter the port but complicate navigation. The main lighthouse marks the approach channel between the islands, guiding the ore carriers and bulk vessels that use Sept-Îles as a major iron ore export terminal. The white tower serves a large commercial harbour; the surrounding landscape of boreal forest, rocky islands, and St Lawrence belugas contrasts sharply with the industrial scale of the mining infrastructure.

## 10. Île Bicquette Lighthouse, Rimouski

Île Bicquette is a small rocky island in the middle of the St Lawrence estuary, roughly 20 kilometres offshore from Rimouski. Its lighthouse, first established in 1844 and the present white cylindrical tower dating from around 1906, marks one of the critical waypoints for vessels navigating the estuary between the gulf and Quebec City. The light character is a flashing white sequence visible about 22 nautical miles. The station stands on an island without permanent inhabitants; it was one of the last in the Canadian lighthouse system to retain keepers, finally automated in 2012. The tower and station buildings are preserved as a heritage site, though access for casual visitors is limited to occasional charter arrangements.

## Visiting the St Lawrence Lighthouses

The St Lawrence lighthouse route divides naturally between the north shore (Route 138 and its extensions, occasionally requiring ferries), the south shore (Route 132 runs most of the length of the Gaspésie), and the islands — Île Verte and Anticosti — that require boat or air access. The summer season from mid-June to early September is optimal: tower openings are in effect, ferry services run at full frequency, and whale activity in the estuary is at its peak. The spring freshet closes some roads and can strand island stations; autumn brings fog and the first intimations of winter.
