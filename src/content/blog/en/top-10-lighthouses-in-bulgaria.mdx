Bulgaria's Black Sea coastline runs for about 378 kilometres between the Danube delta to the north and the Turkish border near Rezovo to the south. Historically it was a working coast rather than a dramatic one — the Black Sea is enclosed, its tides negligible — but the combination of shallow nearshore areas, rocky capes, seasonal fog, and heavy traffic between the Bosphorus and the Danube ports demanded a full chain of lighthouse aids. The Ottoman Empire built several of the early structures; Russia and later the independent Bulgarian state inherited and rebuilt them after 1878. The resulting collection of towers, ranging from substantial masonry columns to modest painted-concrete posts, tells the story of the coast's transition from Ottoman province to independent nation.

## 1. Cape Kaliakra Lighthouse

At the tip of the Kaliakra headland, a dramatic limestone promontory that juts 12 kilometres into the Black Sea north of Kavarna, the lighthouse stands at one of the most striking positions on the Bulgarian coast. The current tower, a white cylindrical concrete structure, rises about 33 metres above the clifftop and marks the northern approaches to the Varna shipping corridor. Its light character is Fl(3) W 9s with a range of approximately 20 nautical miles. The cape has been inhabited since antiquity — a medieval fortress wall crosses the neck of the headland — and the lighthouse site encompasses archaeological remains from several periods. A nature reserve surrounds the promontory; dolphins are frequently visible from the cliff edge.

## 2. Cape Emine Lighthouse

Cape Emine, the easternmost point of the Balkan mountain range where it reaches the sea, is one of the most important navigational waypoints on the Bulgarian coast. The lighthouse, a white-painted round tower with a red lantern gallery, marks the cape where vessels must alter course when navigating the coastal traffic lane. Built during the Bulgarian maritime administration's modernisation programme in the late nineteenth century and substantially rebuilt in the twentieth, the tower stands about 19 metres tall. Its light is visible for approximately 18 nautical miles. The cape is within the Emine-Irakli protected area; the footpath from Irakli village provides fine views of the lighthouse.

## 3. Shabla Lighthouse

The Shabla lighthouse, near the northernmost inhabited point of the Bulgarian coast, holds the distinction of being the tallest lighthouse in Bulgaria, rising approximately 33 metres above sea level and standing on flat coastal terrain that gives it a sweeping range of visibility in all seaward directions. Built in 1856 under Ottoman administration and substantially renovated by the Bulgarian state in 1908 and again in the postwar period, the tower is a round masonry column with a gallery and red lantern. Its Fl W 4s character provides a range of about 20 nautical miles. The lighthouse is an active Bulgarian Border Police station; public access to the interior is restricted, but the tower and its white-washed outbuildings are visible from the roadway.

## 4. Cape Galata Lighthouse

Cape Galata, on the headland immediately north of Varna Harbour, is the principal approach light for Bulgaria's largest commercial port. The current tower dates from the early Soviet-era renovation of the Bulgarian lighthouse system; it is a white cylindrical concrete structure with a range of about 16 nautical miles. The cape is named after the Genoese trading settlement that occupied this coast in the medieval period, and the lighthouse continues a centuries-long function of marking Varna Bay for approaching vessels. A small maritime cemetery near the lighthouse contains graves of mariners lost in the waters off Cape Galata.

## 5. Cape Atia Lighthouse

Cape Atia, south of Burgas, marks the southern edge of Burgas Bay and the beginning of the more rugged southern Bulgarian coastline. The lighthouse here is a modest white tower on a low cliff, operational and automated, marking the transition from the bay approaches to the open coast. The cape is within the Southern Black Sea coast protected area; the lighthouse can be reached on foot from the resort of Chernoморец in about forty minutes along the cliff path.

## 6. Varna Port Pier Lights

At the entrance to Varna commercial harbour, a pair of range lights mounted on the breakwater piers guides vessels through the dredged channel. The lights are functional concrete-and-steel structures rather than architecturally significant towers, but their role in managing the traffic of one of the largest ports on the Black Sea — handling bulk grain, containers, and industrial cargo — makes them important components of the coastal navigation system.

## 7. Emona Lighthouse

North of Cape Emine, near the village of Emona, a small white lighthouse marks a shoaling area where the nearshore bathymetry becomes complex during south-easterly weather. The tower is modest, standing about 12 metres above the coastal terrace, but its sector light distinguishes the safe water from the shallow ground. The Emona area is popular with camping tourists in summer, and the lighthouse is visible from several of the cove beaches.

## 8. Nessebar Pier Light

Nessebar, the UNESCO-listed medieval town on a rocky peninsula south of Varna, has a small pier light at the entrance to its harbour. The structure is plain and functional, but it marks an approach to one of the most historically significant sites on the Bulgarian coast — a Thracian and later Byzantine settlement whose medieval churches survive largely intact. The harbour serves small fishing boats and tourist vessels; the light operates nightly.

## 9. Sozopol Pier Light

The ancient Greek colony of Apollonia, now the town of Sozopol on the southern Bulgarian coast, has a breakwater light that marks the harbour of one of the most visited medieval towns in the country. The light is a steel post with a small lantern; its navigational significance is modest, but it is photographed frequently by visitors to Sozopol's old quarter. The harbour is used by fishing boats and dive charter vessels.

## 10. Rezovo Border Lighthouse

At the southernmost point of Bulgaria's coast, near the Turkish border at the mouth of the Rezovo River, a small lighthouse marks the coastal boundary and guides vessels into the narrow estuary. The tower is white-painted concrete, barely 10 metres tall, and its light character provides coastal vessels navigating close inshore with the precise position of the border. The reserve of Strandzha Natural Park surrounds the area; access is by permit, and the lighthouse is not open to visitors.

Most Bulgarian lighthouses are maintained by the Executive Agency 'Maritime Administration' and are not open to the public, but the approach roads and coastal paths offer clear views of several towers. The coast is most accessible between April and October; spring visits avoid summer crowds while the wildflowers along the Strandja and Dobruja clifftops are at their best.
