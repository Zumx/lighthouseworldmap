Saudi Arabia is bracketed by two seas of very different temperament. To the west runs the Red Sea, a long, narrow, coral-fringed rift over 2,000 kilometres of Saudi coastline, the historic approach to the holy cities by way of the pilgrim port of Jeddah. To the east lies the Arabian Gulf, shallow and hot, lined with the oil terminals and industrial harbours that have made the kingdom one of the world's great energy exporters. Between them stretches the desert interior. The lights of Saudi Arabia serve both coasts, and they mark waters where pilgrimage and petroleum, ancient trade and modern industry, meet the sea.

## The Red Sea: Coral, Reefs, and the Pilgrim Coast

The Red Sea is one of the most demanding bodies of water in the world for navigation, not because of storms but because of coral. Fringing reefs and offshore shoals line much of the Saudi coast, and the approaches to its ports thread between reef and reef where a misjudgement of a few hundred metres can put a ship on the coral. The sea is also one of the saltiest and warmest in the world, with almost no freshwater inflow and very high evaporation. For centuries this was the maritime highway of the hajj, carrying pilgrims from across the Muslim world toward the Hijaz, and the lights of the Saudi Red Sea coast still serve a traffic of pilgrims as well as cargo.

## Jeddah and the Gateway to Mecca

Jeddah is the great port of the Red Sea and the maritime gateway to Mecca, about 70 kilometres inland. For most of Islamic history the pilgrims who could not cross the desert by caravan came by sea to Jeddah, and the port remains one of the busiest on the Red Sea, handling both the modern hajj and a major share of the kingdom's imports. The Jeddah Light — variously recorded as Jiddah Light or Jeddah Light — marks the approach through the reefs to this historic harbour. Modern Jeddah is also famous for its towering port control structures and the elaborate beacons that mark the dredged channels through the coral to the container and pilgrim terminals.

## Yanbu and the Northern Red Sea

North of Jeddah, Yanbu is the kingdom's second great Red Sea port and an industrial city built around oil refining and petrochemicals, the western terminus of a pipeline carrying crude across the peninsula from the Gulf fields. The approaches to Yanbu, like those to Jeddah, run through reef-strewn water, and the port's beacons and channel marks guide tankers and cargo vessels to the terminals. This northern stretch of the Saudi Red Sea coast, running up toward the Gulf of Aqaba and the new megaprojects of the north-west, is among the most rapidly developing maritime regions in the country.

## Jazan and the Southern Red Sea

At the southern end of the Saudi Red Sea coast, near the border with Yemen, lies Jazan, a port and province set among a scatter of islands — the Farasan archipelago — and shallow, reef-strewn waters. The port control tower at Jazan (برج مراقبة ميناء جازان) oversees a harbour developed to serve the agricultural south and a growing industrial zone. The Farasan Islands, low and coral-built, are a protected area known for their wildlife and their traditional fishing communities, and the navigation through these waters is among the most intricate on the coast, threading between low islands and submerged reefs.

## The Arabian Gulf: Oil and Industry

Cross the peninsula to the east, and the maritime world changes entirely. The Arabian Gulf coast of Saudi Arabia is the heart of the kingdom's oil industry, home to the largest crude-export facilities on earth. The Gulf here is shallow, hot, and studded with offshore oil fields, platforms, and the loading terminals that ship Saudi crude to the world. Navigation is dominated by the needs of the tanker trade and by the dense industrial infrastructure offshore. The lights, beacons, and structures such as the Breakwater Beacon that mark the harbours and approaches here serve one of the most concentrated energy-export regions anywhere.

## Ras Tanura and Dammam: The Oil Terminals

Ras Tanura, on the Gulf coast, is one of the largest oil-shipping ports in the world, with a refinery and a sea-island terminal capable of loading the biggest tankers afloat. Nearby Dammam is the kingdom's principal Gulf commercial port and the centre of the Eastern Province. The approaches to these ports across the shallow Gulf are marked by an elaborate system of lights, buoys, and beacons, because the margin for error among the offshore structures and the shipping lanes is small. The traffic here — supertankers, gas carriers, supply vessels — represents an enormous share of global energy movement, and reliable marking is a matter of real economic consequence.

## Islands, Beacons, and a Working Sea

Both Saudi coasts are studded with islands and shoals that carry navigation marks. The historic buildings of the old ports, such as the Sayed Reza house (بيت سيد رضى) in old Jeddah, recall the era when these were merchant harbours of the wider Indian Ocean trade, long before oil. Many of the working aids today are not grand towers but practical structures — breakwater beacons, range lights, and channel buoys — suited to a coast where the hazards are reefs and shoals rather than rock cliffs. The kingdom's navigation network reflects a maritime nation that is at once ancient, in its pilgrim and trading past, and intensely modern, in its industrial present.

## Two Coasts, Two Vocations

To consider Saudi Arabia's lights is to see two maritime vocations meeting at one country. The Red Sea coast carries the deep history of the hajj and the Indian Ocean trade, its lights threading ships through coral toward Jeddah and the holy cities. The Gulf coast carries the modern weight of the oil economy, its beacons guiding supertankers among the offshore fields. Between them lies the great peninsula, and the contrast between the reef-bound pilgrim sea in the west and the oil-laden shallow Gulf in the east defines the kingdom's relationship with the water on either side.
