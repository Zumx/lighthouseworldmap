The Gulf of Bothnia is the northern arm of the Baltic Sea, a long body of water reaching almost to the Arctic Circle between Sweden and Finland. It is a sea unlike the open ocean: brackish, nearly tideless, and frozen solid for months each winter, so that its lighthouses guard a coast shaped as much by ice as by water. The land here is still rising, rebounding from the weight of the ice age glaciers at a measurable rate, so that islands grow and harbours silt up within a human lifetime. The lights of the Gulf, Swedish on the western shore and Finnish on the eastern, reflect a maritime world of timber ships, iron ore, and long winter darkness.

## Bjuröklubb on the Swedish Coast

On the Västerbotten coast of northern Sweden, the lighthouse of Bjuröklubb stands on a high rocky headland, one of the few genuine cliffs on this otherwise low and rounded shore. Built in 1859, the white tower marks a point where the coast turns and the shipping lanes pass close inshore. The surrounding nature reserve, with its glacier-scoured rocks and ancient labyrinth of stones laid by fishermen for luck, is among the most atmospheric coastal sites in northern Sweden. The land here is rising so steadily that the shoreline of a few centuries ago now lies well inland.

## Holmögadd in the Kvarken

Where the Gulf of Bothnia narrows to its waist at the Kvarken, the lighthouse of Holmögadd stands on a remote island marking the Swedish side of the strait. The Kvarken archipelago, a UNESCO World Heritage Site shared with Finland, is the place where the land rebound after the ice age is most dramatically visible, with new land emerging from the sea at nearly a centimetre a year. The light guards the narrowest crossing of the Gulf, where in hard winters an ice road has historically connected the two countries.

## Valassaaret on the Finnish Side of the Kvarken

Across the Kvarken on the Finnish side, the iron lighthouse of Valassaaret was designed in the workshop associated with Gustave Eiffel and erected in 1886. The slender riveted-iron tower stands on a low island in the heart of the Kvarken archipelago, marking the eastern approach to the narrowest part of the Gulf. The surrounding waters, shallow and studded with emerging skerries, are a paradise for birds and a navigational challenge that demanded careful lighting.

## Märket, the Lighthouse on the Border

In the southern reaches of the Gulf, between the Åland archipelago and the Swedish coast, the lighthouse of Märket stands on a tiny skerry split by the Finnish-Swedish border. Built in 1885 after numerous wrecks on the low rock, it is famous for the zigzag border that was redrawn around it so that the lighthouse would stand entirely on Finnish soil while preserving each country's share of the islet. It is one of the most isolated and storied lights in the entire Baltic.

## Stora Fjäderägg

On a low island off the Västerbotten coast, the lighthouse of Stora Fjäderägg marks the approach to the Holmöarna islands and the city of Umeå beyond. The wooden-and-iron light, with its keeper's cottages now serving as a youth hostel and bird observatory, stands among grazed meadows and shingle beaches typical of this rising coast. The island is a noted spot for observing the spring and autumn bird migration along the Gulf.

## Ratan and the Old Harbour

The lighthouse at Ratan marks a small harbour on the Swedish coast that was, in 1809, the scene of the last battle ever fought on Swedish soil, when Russian and Swedish forces clashed during the war that lost Finland to Russia. The light marks an old staple port where timber and tar were once shipped south, and the surrounding waters preserve a tide gauge that has measured the steady rise of the land for two centuries, one of the longest such records in the world.

## Sydostbrotten, the Caisson Light

In the open waters of the southern Gulf, the lighthouse of Sydostbrotten is a caisson tower, built on a sunken concrete base on a shoal far from any land. These offshore caisson lights, common in the Baltic, were designed to withstand the crushing pressure of winter ice driven against them by the wind. Sydostbrotten marks a dangerous shoal on the approaches to the Swedish ports, a lonely red tower rising directly from the sea.

## Sälgrund off Kaskinen

On the Finnish coast, the wooden lighthouse of Sälgrund marks the approach to the small port of Kaskinen, the smallest town in Finland. The octagonal wooden tower, painted red and white, is a fine example of the timber lighthouse construction once common around the Gulf, where wood was abundant and the building of stone or iron towers expensive. The light guards a low, island-strewn coast where the shipping channels wind among countless skerries.

## Tankar off Kokkola

The island of Tankar, off the Finnish port of Kokkola, carries a lighthouse, a chapel, and a former fishing village, making it one of the most complete historic lighthouse communities on the Gulf. The tall light, built in the 19th century, guards the approach to a port that grew rich on the tar trade, when Ostrobothnia was the world's leading producer of the pine tar that waterproofed the wooden ships of the age. The island is reachable by summer boat and its old village is carefully preserved.

## Marjaniemi on Hailuoto

At the northern end of the Gulf, the lighthouse of Marjaniemi stands on the island of Hailuoto off the Finnish city of Oulu, near the very head of the Gulf where the Arctic comes close. The light marks an approach across some of the shallowest and most ice-bound waters in the Baltic, where the winter freeze is total and long. The island, reached by ferry, preserves a traditional fishing and farming landscape, and its lighthouse looks out over a sea that for half the year is a plain of ice.
