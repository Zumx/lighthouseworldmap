The Ghanaian coast is one of the most historically charged shorelines in Africa. For some 540 kilometres along the Gulf of Guinea, the Atlantic breaks in heavy surf against a low, hot shore that European powers fortified more densely than almost anywhere else on the continent. The Portuguese, Dutch, Danish, Swedish, Brandenburg and British all built castles and forts here, more than thirty of them, to control the trade in gold, ivory and enslaved people. The lighthouses of Ghana grew out of that same coast, often standing within sight of the forts, and several were built on or beside them. To trace Ghana's lights is to read the layered history of the Gold Coast itself.

## Jamestown Lighthouse, Accra

The best-known light in the country stands in Jamestown, the oldest district of Accra. The present red-and-white banded tower dates from around 1930, replacing an earlier light of the 1870s, and rises about 28 metres above the crowded fishing harbour below. It overlooks the surf-boat beach where Ga fishermen still launch through the breakers, and stands a short walk from the British-built Ussher Fort and James Fort. The lighthouse is open to visitors who pay a small fee to the keeper, and the climb up the spiral stair is rewarded with a sweeping view over Jamestown's tin roofs, the harbour and the long Atlantic horizon.

## Cape Coast and the Castle

Cape Coast, west of Accra, is dominated by its vast whitewashed castle, a UNESCO World Heritage Site and one of the most important monuments of the Atlantic slave trade. A light has long marked this stretch of dangerous surf-bound coast, guiding vessels toward the roadstead off the castle where, for centuries, ships anchored offshore and goods and people were ferried out through the breakers. The combination of fortress and beacon at Cape Coast captures the essential character of the Ghanaian shore: navigation and trade, light and shadow, bound together on the same headland.

## Elmina and Fort São Jorge

A few kilometres west of Cape Coast lies Elmina, site of the oldest European building in sub-Saharan Africa, the Portuguese castle of São Jorge da Mina, begun in 1482. Elmina sits on a rocky point between a lagoon and the sea, with the dense fishing harbour crowded with brightly painted pirogues. The point and its forts, including the hilltop Fort São Tiago, have guided mariners toward Elmina for five centuries. The coast here is treacherous, with rocks close inshore, and the elevated forts served as daymarks long before any purpose-built lighthouse.

## Fort Orange, Sekondi

Further west, at the twin city of Sekondi-Takoradi, stands Fort Orange, a Dutch fort founded in 1640 that for many years carried a navigational light on its ramparts. Sekondi-Takoradi grew into Ghana's principal deep-water port, opened in 1928 at Takoradi, and the approaches to the harbour are marked by breakwater and pier lights guiding the bulk carriers that load manganese, bauxite and cocoa. Fort Orange itself, on its bluff above the old harbour, is a reminder of how the colonial forts doubled as the country's first lighthouses.

## Fort William Lighthouse, Cape Coast

One of the most striking marriages of fort and light in Ghana is Fort William, a former British fort in Cape Coast converted into a lighthouse in the nineteenth century. The squat fortified structure, with a light mounted above its old walls, served the busy roadstead and remained an active aid to navigation well into the twentieth century. It is a vivid example of the reuse that defines so much of the Ghanaian coast, where the architecture of trade and conquest was repurposed to keep ships safe.

## Cape St. Paul Lighthouse, Woe

East of Accra, beyond the Volta River, the coast runs out toward the Togolese border in a long sandy spit. Near the village of Woe, close to the town of Keta, stands Cape St. Paul Lighthouse, one of the tallest in West Africa at around 35 metres. Built by the British in the late nineteenth century and rebuilt in the 1960s, it marks a low, featureless coast where the only landmark for many miles is the tower itself. The surrounding district has suffered severe coastal erosion, and the sea has swallowed much of old Keta, making the lighthouse a poignant survivor on a retreating shore.

## The Volta Estuary and Ada

Where the great Volta River meets the Atlantic at Ada Foah, the coast is a maze of sandbars, lagoons and shifting channels. Beacons and minor lights mark the treacherous bar at the river mouth, where the freshwater current meets the Atlantic swell in a line of breaking surf. This is one of the most beautiful stretches of the Ghanaian coast, a place of estuary islands and palm-fringed spits, but also one of the most hazardous to small craft, and the lights here are working aids rather than tourist attractions.

## Why the Forts Came First

Ghana's lighthouse history is inseparable from its forts because, for three centuries, the forts were the lights. Built on every prominent point along the Gold Coast, whitewashed and elevated, they served as unmistakable daymarks, and many carried lanterns or fires on their walls to guide ships toward the roadsteads. Purpose-built lighthouses arrived only in the colonial nineteenth century, and even then they were frequently grafted onto existing fortifications. The result is a coast where maritime heritage and the dark history of the Atlantic trade are physically fused.

## Visiting the Gold Coast Lights

The Ghanaian coast is easily travelled along the main road from Accra westward through Cape Coast and Elmina to Sekondi-Takoradi, or eastward across the Volta to Ada and Keta. Jamestown Lighthouse in Accra is the most accessible to climb, while Cape Coast and Elmina castles are essential, sobering stops. The dry season from November to March offers the calmest seas and clearest air, with the dusty Harmattan wind sometimes hazing the horizon. Pair the lights with the forts, and you experience the full weight of this extraordinary shore.
