Greenland has no tall, romantic lighthouse towers of the kind that crown the capes of Brittany or Maine. Its coast — the longest and most indented of any island on earth, fringed by thousands of skerries, fjords and drifting ice — is marked instead by a network of modest harbour lights, range markers and lit beacons, most of them small steel or concrete structures perched on rock. They serve a navigation problem unlike any other: a sea cluttered with icebergs calved from the inland ice, fjords that freeze in winter, fog that rolls off the cold water, and a polar night during which, in the far north, the sun does not rise for weeks. The lights described here are the beacons that guide the supply ships and coastal ferries of the Royal Arctic Line through this formidable shore, from Cape Farewell in the south to the disko Bay settlements in the west.

## 1. Nuuk Harbour Lights

Nuuk, the capital and largest town, sits on a peninsula at the mouth of a great fjord system on the southwest coast. Its harbour, the busiest in Greenland, is marked by a set of lights and range markers that guide the container ships and the coastal ferry through the rocky approaches. Behind the town rises the distinctive flat-topped mountain Sermitsiaq, often capped with snow, and the harbour looks out across the fjord toward open water dotted with ice. Nuuk's lights are working aids for the country's main port, where almost all imported goods enter Greenland.

## 2. Julianehåb (Qaqortoq) Range Lights

Qaqortoq, known by its Danish name Julianehåb, is the largest town in southern Greenland, set on a sheltered bay among the rounded hills of the south. The range lights here — a front and rear light aligned to mark the safe channel — guide vessels through the islands and skerries that crowd the approach. Qaqortoq is the prettiest of Greenlandic towns, its painted houses rising in tiers around the old colonial harbour and its central square with a fountain, the only one in Greenland. The setting, among fjords and the green slopes of the south, is the mildest in the country.

## 3. Cape Farewell (Kap Farvel) Approaches

The southern tip of Greenland, Kap Farvel, is one of the most dangerous corners of the North Atlantic, where the cold East Greenland Current sweeps drift ice and bergs around the point into the path of ships rounding the continent. The lights and beacons in the channels behind the cape guide vessels through the inner passages that avoid the worst of the open-water ice. Cape Farewell is not a single headland but a maze of islands at the end of the world, and the approaches are among the hardest pilotage in the Arctic, demanding constant vigilance against ice in all seasons.

## 4. Sisimiut Harbour Light

Sisimiut, the second-largest town, lies just north of the Arctic Circle on the west coast, a centre of fishing and the southern end of the famous Arctic Circle Trail. Its harbour, ice-free for most of the year thanks to the warmer water here, is marked by lights guiding the fishing fleet and the coastal ferry. The town climbs the rocky slopes above its busy port, backed by mountains, and the surrounding waters teem with the cold-water shrimp and halibut on which the local economy depends. The harbour light is a working aid for one of Greenland's most active fishing ports.

## 5. Maniitsoq (Sukkertoppen) Lights

Maniitsoq, the old Danish Sukkertoppen, sits on an island in a labyrinth of fjords and skerries on the west coast, in a region of jagged peaks that has earned the name the Greenlandic Alps. The harbour and range lights guide vessels through the intricate channels that thread the islands. The setting, among steep mountains rising straight from the sea, is among the most dramatic in Greenland, and the deep fjords behind the town reach far into a landscape of glaciers and rock. The lights serve the fishing town and the coastal traffic threading this maze.

## 6. Aasiaat (Egedesminde) Lights

Aasiaat lies at the southern edge of Disko Bay, on a flat island among hundreds of skerries on the west coast. The harbour lights guide vessels through the scattered islets into one of the busier ports of the bay. Aasiaat is a fishing and transport hub, the southern gateway to the disko Bay region, and its waters, rich in fish and visited by whales, are among the most productive in Greenland. The flat, island-strewn setting demands careful navigation, and the lights mark the safe routes among the low rocks.

## 7. Ilulissat (Jakobshavn) Approaches

Ilulissat, the third-largest town, sits beside the mouth of the Ilulissat Icefjord, a UNESCO World Heritage site where the Sermeq Kujalleq glacier — one of the fastest-moving in the world — calves the icebergs that crowd Disko Bay. The harbour lights guide vessels through waters perpetually full of drifting ice, some bergs the size of buildings. Navigating here means threading between giants of ice that ground on the shallow bar at the fjord mouth. Ilulissat is Greenland's tourism capital, and the spectacle of the ice, combined with the demands it places on pilotage, makes its approaches unique.

## 8. Vester Ejland Light

The Vester Ejland, an island in Disko Bay west of Aasiaat, carries a light marking the approaches to the bay from the open sea of Davis Strait. The beacon guides vessels into the productive waters of Disko Bay, past the scattered islands toward the settlements of the eastern shore. The island light is one of the outer markers of this great embayment, standing at the edge of the open strait where the supply ships turn in toward the bay's towns. The setting, among low Arctic islands and drifting ice, is characteristic of the west Greenland coast.

## 9. Paamiut (Frederikshåb) Lights

Paamiut, the old Frederikshåb, sits between Nuuk and Qaqortoq on the southwest coast, at the mouth of a fjord in a region once famous for its cod fishery. The harbour lights guide the fishing boats and the coastal ferry through the rocky approach. The town, with its old wooden church and colonial buildings, looks out over waters where the warm and cold currents meet, and the surrounding fjords reach back into a landscape of mountains and ice. The lights serve a quieter port on the long stretch of coast between the two largest southern towns.

## 10. Upernavik District Beacons

Far up the northwest coast, beyond Disko Bay, the Upernavik archipelago is a scatter of islands and skerries marking the start of the high Arctic, where the polar night is long and the sea ice locks the coast for much of the year. The beacons and harbour lights here guide the supply ships during the short open-water season to one of the northernmost permanently inhabited regions of Greenland. The setting, among icebergs and the rocky islands of the far north, under the midnight sun in summer and weeks of darkness in winter, marks the edge of the navigable world.
