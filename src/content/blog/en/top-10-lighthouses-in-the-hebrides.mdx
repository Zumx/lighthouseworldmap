The Hebrides catch the full weight of the North Atlantic, and their lighthouses were built to match it. Strung along the exposed western fringe of Scotland, from the granite stacks south of Barra to the gneiss cliffs at the head of Lewis, these towers were nearly all raised by the Stevenson family of engineers for the Northern Lighthouse Board, the body that still maintains them today. Many guard reefs that lie miles offshore, drowned at high water and ringed by Atlantic swell; others crown headlands where the rock falls sheer into the sea. To visit them is to read a century of dogged engineering against one of the most punishing coastlines in Europe.

## 1. Skerryvore, off Tiree

Skerryvore is the masterpiece of Alan Stevenson, completed in 1844 on a half-tide reef eleven nautical miles south-west of Tiree. At 48 metres it is the tallest lighthouse in Scotland, built from interlocking blocks of Mull granite that took five summers to land and set on a rock awash in any sea. The light, automated in 1994, shows a white flash every ten seconds with a range of around 18 nautical miles. There is no public access to the tower, but the Skerryvore exhibition at Hynish on Tiree, in the buildings that served the shore station, tells the story of its construction.

## 2. Dubh Artach, off Colonsay

Dubh Artach, the dark rock, stands isolated on a reef some 15 nautical miles south-west of the Ross of Mull, completed in 1872 by David and Thomas Stevenson. The 38-metre granite tower carries a distinctive broad red band, a daymark against the grey of sea and sky. It was among the most difficult builds of the era, the reef so exposed that the workers' barrack was repeatedly battered by waves breaking far above it. The light is automated and shows a white flash every thirty seconds. It can be seen from the Iona and Colonsay ferries in clear weather.

## 3. Barra Head (Berneray)

At the very southern tip of the Outer Hebrides, on the island of Berneray, Barra Head lighthouse occupies the highest sea-cliff position of any light in Britain. Built by Robert Stevenson in 1833, the tower itself is modest at 18 metres, but it sits 208 metres above the Atlantic on the cliff edge, giving the white flashing light a range of more than 18 nautical miles. The island is now uninhabited and access is by private boat in settled weather only, a genuine expedition to one of the loneliest lights in the country.

## 4. Butt of Lewis

The Butt of Lewis marks the northernmost point of the Outer Hebrides, where the island runs out into a chaos of broken seas. Robert Stevenson built the tower in 1862 in unpainted red brick, an unusual choice that makes it instantly recognisable. It stands 37 metres and shows a group of three white flashes every fifteen seconds, with a range of around 25 nautical miles. The headland is famous for some of the heaviest seas recorded around Britain, and the road from Port of Ness brings visitors right to the boundary wall.

## 5. Eilean Glas, Scalpay

Eilean Glas, on the island of Scalpay off Harris, carries one of the four original Scottish lighthouses commissioned in 1789, though the present 30-metre tower with its bold red and white bands dates from a Robert Stevenson rebuild of 1824. The light flashes white every twenty seconds. The walk out across the moor from the Scalpay road end is a fine couple of miles, ending at a cluster of weathered keepers' buildings above the rocks of the Minch.

## 6. Neist Point, Skye

Neist Point reaches further west than anywhere else on Skye, a spit of basalt running out below the cliffs of Waterstein. The lighthouse, built in 1909 by David Alan Stevenson, stands 19 metres and shows a white flash every five seconds across the Little Minch towards the Outer Hebrides, with a range of about 16 nautical miles. The footpath down from the car park, with its concrete steps and steep final descent, is among the most photographed walks in the islands, especially at sunset.

## 7. Rubha Reidh, Wester Ross

Rubha Reidh, on the mainland coast near Gairloch but firmly part of the Hebridean searoad, marks the approach to Loch Ewe and the inner sound. Built in 1912 by David Alan Stevenson, the 25-metre tower shows a group of four white flashes every fifteen seconds. The former keepers' accommodation has at times operated as a guest house, and the long single-track road out from Melvaig is an adventure in itself, ending where the land gives way to the open sea.

## 8. Hyskeir (Oigh-sgeir)

Hyskeir, properly Oigh-sgeir, is a low reef of columnar basalt south-west of Canna, marked by a 39-metre tower built by David Alan Stevenson in 1904. The white light flashes three times every thirty seconds with a range of around 24 nautical miles. The rock is so flat and exposed that the keepers once laid out a small grass football pitch between the buildings. Automated in 1997, it is visible from the Small Isles ferry and from the Rum hills on a clear day.

## 9. Rinns of Islay, Orsay

On the tidal island of Orsay off Portnahaven, the Rinns of Islay lighthouse was built by Robert Stevenson in 1825 to guard the southern approach from the Atlantic into the North Channel. The 29-metre tower shows a white flash every five seconds. In calm conditions local boatmen will ferry visitors across the short, fierce tidal gap from Portnahaven, a village built around its sheltered cove on the western edge of Islay.

## 10. Ardnamurchan

Ardnamurchan lighthouse stands on the most westerly point of the British mainland, a granite tower of 36 metres designed by Alan Stevenson and completed in 1849 in an unusual Egyptian style. The white light flashes twice every twenty seconds with a range of around 18 nautical miles. Of all the lights on this list it is the easiest to reach by road, and the visitor centre in the old engine room and stables makes it the best introduction to Hebridean lighthouse history, with the tower open to climb in season.
