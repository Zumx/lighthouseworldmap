The black-and-white spiral of Cape Hatteras, the red-and-white bands of Assateague, the bold chequers of the older West Quoddy Head tower: these are among the most recognisable images in the whole subject of lighthouses, and they are routinely admired as decoration. They are nothing of the sort. Every stripe, band, spiral, and chequered square on a lighthouse is a navigational instrument, designed and assigned according to a logic as deliberate as the flash sequence the same tower shows at night. The painted pattern is called a daymark, and understanding it transforms the way you look at any lit coast.

## The Problem a Daymark Solves

A lighthouse at night is identified by its character: the rhythm and colour of its flashes, unique to each station within a stretch of coast. By day, that character is invisible. The light may not even be lit, and against a bright sky a distant tower is simply a pale vertical shape. Yet a navigator approaching a coast in daylight needs to know which tower he is looking at just as urgently as at night, because the safe course past one headland may be lethal past the next.

The daymark solves this by giving each tower a distinct daytime appearance. Where several lighthouses stand within sight of one another, or along a stretch of coast a mariner might traverse in a single passage, no two are painted alike. The choice of pattern is recorded in the same official light lists that publish the night-time characters, so a navigator can match the tower in view against the book and fix his position with certainty. The painting is, in the strictest sense, part of the aid to navigation.

## Contrast Against the Background

The first principle of daymark design is contrast with the surroundings. A tower must stand out against whatever lies behind it from the sea, and that background varies enormously. Against a dark cliff, woodland, or a built-up shore, a white tower shows clearly. Against a bright sky, sand dunes, or pale rock, a white tower vanishes, and a dark one is needed. Many of the boldest schemes exist precisely because a single colour failed against a particular background.

This is why so many famous towers combine light and dark in the same structure. A black-and-white banded or spiralled tower is visible against almost any background, because some part of the pattern will always contrast with whatever lies behind it. The candy-stripe and chequer schemes are not chosen for drama but because broken patterns of strong opposing tones are the most reliably visible across the widest range of lighting and backdrop conditions a mariner might encounter.

## Stripes, Bands, Spirals and Chequers

The vocabulary of daymarks is precise. Horizontal bands divide the tower into stacked rings of alternating colour. Vertical stripes run from base to lantern. A spiral, or barber-pole, winds a single helical band around the tower, the scheme made famous by Cape Hatteras in North Carolina. Chequers cover the surface in a grid of alternating squares, as on some Scottish and English towers. Diamonds, diagonal stripes, and single contrasting bands near the lantern all appear in the catalogue of recognised patterns.

The pattern itself carries information beyond mere identification. A tall tower with widely spaced horizontal bands reads differently at distance than one with many narrow bands, and the eye can estimate relative height and proportion from the banding even before the whole structure is clear. Where two nearby towers might otherwise be confused, assigning one horizontal bands and the other vertical stripes makes them instantly distinguishable even when colour is hard to judge in haze or low light.

## Famous Schemes and Their Reasons

Cape Hatteras, at over sixty metres the tallest brick lighthouse in the United States, carries its black-and-white spiral so that it cannot be mistaken for the other towers of the treacherous Outer Banks, where the Diamond Shoals have wrecked countless vessels. Bodie Island, further north on the same chain, wears horizontal black-and-white bands; Currituck Beach is left as bare red brick. Three neighbouring lights, three deliberately different daymarks, so that a mariner running the coast always knows his position.

In England, the chequered tower and the broad single bands of various Trinity House lights serve the same end along busy approaches. Sweden's tall coastal towers and the iron lights of the Baltic often carry red-and-white banding for visibility against snow, pale sky, and water. Across the world the same reasoning produces a family resemblance: bold, simple, high-contrast schemes, each unique within its own stretch of coast, none chosen for ornament.

## When the Tower Itself Is the Mark

Not every daymark is painted. The shape, height, and material of a tower are themselves recognition features, and at some stations the structure was left in its natural stone or brick precisely because its form and colour were already distinctive enough. A squat granite tower on a bare rock, a tall white-rendered column on a green headland, a skeletal steel framework against the sky — each has a characteristic profile that a practised eye reads at once.

In remote locations where no other lighthouse stands within many miles, the need for an elaborate painted scheme falls away, and many such towers are simply whitewashed for maximum visibility or left unpainted. The daymark becomes elaborate only where confusion is possible, which is why the boldest, most photographed patterns cluster along the busiest and most hazardous coasts, where many towers compete for a mariner's recognition.

## Reading Daymarks Today

For the modern visitor, the daymark is a key that unlocks the meaning of a tower's appearance. A banded or spiralled scheme tells you the light stood among rivals and needed to declare itself; a plain whitewashed tower suggests an isolated station that could rely on its position alone. The colours hint at the background the engineers were fighting: dark schemes for bright coasts, light schemes for dark ones.

Although electronic navigation has reduced the practical reliance on visual daymarks, lighthouse authorities still maintain the painted schemes, both because small craft and traditional navigators still use them and because the patterns have become part of each tower's identity. To repaint Cape Hatteras in plain white would be unthinkable, not only on heritage grounds but because the spiral remains, after a century and a half, exactly what it was designed to be: an unmistakable daytime signature.
