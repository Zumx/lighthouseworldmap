Jamaica's lighthouses are products of a particular moment in Victorian industrial history: the development of prefabricated cast-iron towers that could be cast in Britain, shipped in numbered sections, and bolted together on a remote tropical headland. The island's principal lights — Morant Point in the east, Negril in the west, Plumb Point guarding Kingston Harbour, and the smaller stations around the coast — were almost all built to guide the sugar and shipping trade that made Jamaica one of the most valuable colonies of the British Empire. Several of these cast-iron towers, including the very oldest, still stand and still show their lights, painted in bold daymark colours against the Caribbean sky. The result is a coastline marked by some of the finest surviving examples of nineteenth-century iron lighthouse engineering anywhere in the tropics.

## Morant Point Lighthouse

At the easternmost tip of Jamaica, the Morant Point Lighthouse is the island's oldest and most historically significant light. The cast-iron tower was manufactured in England, shipped to Jamaica in sections, and erected in 1841 — making it one of the oldest cast-iron lighthouses surviving anywhere. It was assembled, according to long tradition, with the labour of recently emancipated workers. The red-and-white banded tower stands amid sugar-cane country and coconut groves at Morant Point, marking the dangerous eastern approaches where ships rounding the island from the Atlantic first make their landfall. It remains a working light and a national landmark.

## Negril Lighthouse

At the opposite, western end of the island, the Negril Lighthouse rises above the cliffs at the South Negril Point. Built in 1894 by a French firm, the concrete tower is among the tallest lighthouses in the Caribbean, standing around 20 metres on a base raised well above sea level, so that its focal plane commands a long approach. The light guards the western point of the island and the approaches along the famous Negril cliffs and beaches. It is one of the most accessible and most visited lights in Jamaica, set in landscaped grounds with a keeper's cottage, and the climb to the gallery is a popular excursion.

## Plumb Point Lighthouse

Guarding the entrance to Kingston Harbour — one of the largest natural harbours in the world — the Plumb Point Lighthouse stands on the Palisadoes, the long sand spit that shelters the harbour and carries the road out to Port Royal. The light marks the critical turn into the harbour channel past the shoals off the spit. Kingston's commercial traffic depends on this approach, and the Plumb Point light, with its distinctive banded tower, is the key beacon for vessels entering the port.

## Folly Point Lighthouse

At Port Antonio on the north-east coast, the Folly Point Lighthouse marks the entrance to the twin harbours that made the town a centre of the banana trade. The white tower stands on a point near the ruins of the Folly great house, and the light guides shipping along the lush north-eastern coast below the Blue Mountains. Port Antonio's harbours, sheltered by Navy Island, were among the busiest on the island during the banana boom, and the light served the steamers that carried the fruit to North America and Europe.

## Galina Point Lighthouse

On the north coast near Port Maria, the Galina Lighthouse marks a prominent headland along the route between Ocho Rios and Port Antonio. The light guides coastal traffic along the north shore, a stretch of dramatic cliffs and small bays. Galina Point is a well-known landmark on the northern coastal road and offers wide views along the shore.

## Portland Point Lighthouse

At the southernmost point of Jamaica, the Portland Lighthouse marks the low, arid Portland Point on the south coast near the Portland Bight. This is one of the driest and most remote corners of the island, and the light guards the southern approaches and the entrance to the wide bight that shelters the fishing grounds and the Portland Ridge. The contrast between this scrubby southern point and the lush north coast lights is one of the geographical surprises of the Jamaican shore.

## Lover's Leap

On the south coast near Treasure Beach, the light at Lover's Leap stands atop a sheer 500-metre cliff in the Santa Cruz Mountains, where the land drops almost vertically to the sea. The site is famous for its dramatic precipice and the legend that gives it its name, and the small light marks the high southern coast. The combination of a navigation light and one of the highest sea cliffs on the island makes Lover's Leap a notable visitor destination as well as a working beacon.

## Galleon and Breezy Point Lights

Smaller beacons complete the system around the island. The Breezy Point Lighthouse and the minor lights along the south and east coasts mark harbour entrances and hazards for the local fishing and coastal trade. These lesser lights, though modest, form the working detail of the navigation network between the major landfall towers.

## Rose Hall and the North Coast Lights

Near Montego Bay, the area around the famous Rose Hall great house lies along the busy north-west coast, where tourist and commercial traffic crowds the approaches to Jamaica's second city. The minor lights and harbour beacons along this stretch — including those associated with the Rose Hall area — mark the reefs and channels that fringe the resort coast between Montego Bay and Falmouth. Falmouth itself, a Georgian port town, was once a major sugar-shipping harbour, and its approaches were marked accordingly.

## Lazaretto and the Kingston Approaches

Near the Kingston approaches, the Lazaretto Cairn Lighthouse marks a hazard on the way into the great harbour, supplementing the Plumb Point light. The Lazaretto — named for a former quarantine station — and the other minor lights of the Kingston approaches together handle the dense traffic entering and leaving the island's principal port, completing the chain of beacons that ring Jamaica from Morant Point in the east to Negril in the west.
