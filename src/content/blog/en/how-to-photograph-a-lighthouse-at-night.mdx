A lighthouse at night is one of the hardest subjects a landscape photographer can attempt, and one of the most rewarding. The problem is brutal contrast: the lantern room may be throwing a beam visible twenty nautical miles out to sea, while the tower itself, the rocks and the surrounding sky sit in near-total darkness. Get the exposure wrong and you either blow the lamp into a featureless white blob or lose everything else to black. The reward, when it works, is an image that no daytime photograph can match — a blade of light sweeping across a star-strewn sky, anchored by a tower that has stood against the same horizon for a century or more.

## Why night is different

During the day a lighthouse is simply architecture. At night it becomes what it was built to be: a source of light in the dark. That changes everything about how you meter the scene. Your camera's automatic modes will be fooled by the lamp and will badly underexpose the surroundings, so you will be working in full manual from the first frame. The good news is that lighthouses are predictable. Each one has a published light characteristic — a rhythm of flashes and eclipses that repeats on a fixed cycle. Once you know that a particular tower shows, say, a single white flash every ten seconds, you can plan your shutter speed around it rather than gambling.

## Gear that actually matters

You need very little, but what you need is non-negotiable. A sturdy tripod is the single most important item; any exposure longer than a fraction of a second will smear without one, and night work means exposures of seconds or minutes. A camera that allows full manual control of aperture, shutter and ISO, plus a Bulb mode for very long frames, is essential. A remote release or the camera's two-second self-timer prevents the shake that comes from pressing the shutter by hand. Bring a head torch with a red mode so you can see your controls without destroying your night vision, spare batteries (cold drains them fast), and a lens cloth, because salt spray and dew are constant enemies on any coast.

## Reading and using the beam

The rotating beam is your signature element, and you control how it appears entirely through exposure length. A short exposure of one or two seconds may catch a single, frozen finger of light. A thirty-second frame will record several full rotations, building up a soft halo or a fan of overlapping beams around the lantern. To photograph the lamp without burning it out, stop the lens down to around f8 or f11, keep ISO low at 100 or 200, and let the shutter stay open for the surroundings. The lamp will still be bright, but the small aperture and low ISO give it room to breathe. If a single sweep still overexposes the lantern, drape a hat or card over the lens during the moment the beam points straight at you, then uncover for the rest of the exposure.

## Star trails behind the tower

Pointing your camera so that the celestial pole sits behind the lighthouse turns the sky into concentric arcs of light. In the northern hemisphere, frame Polaris above or beside the tower; in the south, aim toward the south celestial pole near the Southern Cross. Star trails are built either from one enormous exposure of twenty or thirty minutes or, far more reliably, from a stack of dozens of two-minute frames combined later in software. Stacking avoids the heat noise and the single ruined frame that a passing car or cloud can cause. Set the lens to manual focus on infinity, lock the aperture around f4 to gather light, and shoot continuously with an intervalometer for an hour or more.

## Twilight, moonlight and the blue hour

The best lighthouse photographs are rarely taken in pitch dark. The blue hour — the half hour after sunset and before sunrise — gives you a sky that still holds colour and detail while the lamp has already been lit. This solves the contrast problem almost entirely, because the gap between the lamp and the sky is at its smallest. A bright moon behind you acts as a giant soft light, painting the tower white and revealing the texture of rock and surf that a moonless night would swallow. Check the lunar calendar before you travel; a full moon rising over the sea behind a working light is a gift, but it also flattens the stars, so a thin crescent is better for trail work.

## Composition and foreground

A lighthouse needs a foreground to give the beam somewhere to travel and the eye somewhere to enter. Wet rocks catching the lamp's glow, a curve of breaking surf rendered to silk by a long exposure, a path of tide pools reflecting the sky — all of these lead the viewer toward the tower. Avoid placing the lighthouse dead centre; the rule of thirds usually serves it better, with the beam sweeping across the empty space. Watch the horizon line and keep it level, because nothing ruins a sea image faster than a tilted ocean. Scout the location in daylight so you know where the edges, cliffs and slippery rocks are before you are stumbling around them in the dark.

## Staying safe and being patient

Coastal night photography demands respect. Cliff edges, rising tides and slick weed have claimed careless photographers. Tell someone where you are going, check the tide tables so you are not cut off, and never set up below a high-water line. Patience is the real skill: you may wait an hour for a cloud to clear from behind the lantern, or for the surf to build to the right rhythm. Bring something warm to drink, accept that most frames will be discarded, and remember that the few that work will be worth every cold, salt-sprayed minute spent waiting for the beam to swing your way.
