## A World of Artefacts

Lighthouse collecting is one of the more specialised corners of maritime heritage, and also one of the most varied. At one extreme sit the great Fresnel lenses — enormous glass and bronze assemblies worth hundreds of thousands of dollars that turn up in museum auctions and specialist sales. At the other, a collector might spend an afternoon at a flea market and come away with a tin keeper's oil can, a log book page, or a postcard showing a long-demolished tower. In between lie lanterns, lamp mechanisms, fog signal parts, keeper uniforms, navigational instruments, lighthouse service china, architectural salvage, models, paintings, and the vast paper trail — charts, official notices, correspondence, photographs — that the lighthouse bureaucracies of the nineteenth and twentieth centuries generated in extraordinary volume.

## Fresnel Lenses

The definitive prize in lighthouse collecting is an original Fresnel lens. Auguste-Jean Fresnel designed the compound lens system that bears his name in the 1820s, and French glassmakers and instrument-makers — principally the firms Barbier, Bénard et Turenne (later Barbier and Turenne) and Henry-Lepaute in Paris — produced lenses of exceptional quality throughout the nineteenth and early twentieth centuries. British production centred on Chance Brothers of Smethwick near Birmingham, and American lenses were made by various manufacturers and imported from France, with the Lighthouse Board making extensive French purchases from the 1850s onward.

Fresnel lenses are classified by order, from first order (the largest, used in major ocean-facing lights) down to sixth order (the smallest, used in harbour and minor channel lights). A first-order lens weighs several tons, stands two to three metres tall, and is composed of hundreds of individually ground prisms and bull's-eye panels assembled in a cast bronze or iron frame. When the United States Coast Guard decommissioned these lenses as lighthouses were automated from the 1960s onward, many were donated to maritime museums, state parks, and lighthouse preservation societies. Others entered the collector market, initially at low prices that reflected the cost of transport and storage. As awareness of their historical significance grew, so did their value.

A first-order Fresnel lens in good condition is now a serious museum acquisition, with examples selling at auction for over $100,000. Fourth-order lenses — still substantial objects roughly the size of a large barrel — are more attainable, and individual lens panels from decommissioned optics occasionally appear at maritime auctions. The challenge with any Fresnel lens is provenance: documentation tracing the lens from a specific lighthouse through its decommissioning is essential, both for authenticity and because the historical record of which light carried which optic is genuinely interesting to curators and researchers.

## Lamp Mechanisms and Fog Signal Equipment

Below the lens sat the lamp mechanism: the clockwork rotation drive, the oil reservoir and wicking assembly, the later kerosene vapour burners and electric arc lamps. These components are far more portable than the lens itself and form a coherent sub-collection in their own right. The weight-driven clockwork mechanisms that rotated the lens assembly are finely made objects, their gears cut to engineering tolerances that rival domestic clockmaking. A keeper wound the driving weight every few hours throughout the night; some towers required winding every ninety minutes. Complete mechanisms with their weights and chains are rare but collectable.

Fog signal equipment — fog bells, striking mechanisms, compressed-air siren components, reed horns — represents another collecting category. The large cast bronze fog bells used at British and Irish stations in the nineteenth century are handsome objects, typically 60 to 90 centimetres in diameter and stamped with the lighthouse authority's mark and foundry date. Trinity House bells are marked accordingly; the Commissioners of Irish Lights used their own foundry marks. These bells occasionally appear at specialist maritime auctions or through the lighthouse heritage organisations that acquired them when signals were modernised.

## Models and Architectural Reproductions

Lighthouse models range from the lovingly detailed handmade constructions built by keepers during long night watches to mass-produced tourist souvenirs. The former are genuinely collectable: a keeper-made scale model of his own station, built from materials at hand, is a direct artefact of lighthouse life. Some were gifts to officials or family; others were entered in craft exhibitions. Documentation that places a model in a specific keeper's hands significantly increases its interest.

Architectural salvage from decommissioned or demolished lighthouses — ironwork, glazing bars, gallery railings, door hardware stamped with authority marks — is collected both decoratively and as heritage material. The cast-iron sections of screwpile lighthouses, which were assembled like construction kits on exposed offshore piles, occasionally appear when derelict structures are cleared. Doorplates, stencilled oil cans, and brass navigational instruments from the lighthouse service all carry the institutional identity of the authority that made and used them.

## Paper Ephemera

The paper record of lighthouse operation is vast and undervalued. Official notices to mariners announcing light changes, fog signal modifications, and buoy shifts were printed in small quantities and circulated to port authorities, harbourmasters, and ship owners. Nineteenth-century examples are now uncommon, particularly those relating to significant changes — the first announcement of electric illumination at a major light, or the notice altering a well-known light character after a lens change.

Keeper log books, which recorded weather observations, light-keeping hours, ship sightings, and equipment faults in the keeper's own handwriting, have significant historical interest. Many remain in official archives — the National Records of Scotland holds extensive Northern Lighthouse Board records, Trinity House archives are held at the Guildhall Library in London — but individual volumes sometimes surface at auction when estate sales are held. A log book from a famous station (Bell Rock, Flannan Isles, Longships) commands considerably more attention than one from an obscure harbour light, but all provide primary evidence of conditions and practice that no secondary source can replicate.

Postcards, stereographs, and early photographs of lighthouses form a broad and accessible market. Some of the most evocative nineteenth-century images show lighthouse stations in their active working state — keepers visible in the doorway, attendant cottages populated, tender vessels at anchor — and these carry both documentary and aesthetic value. The lighthouse postcard market has its own specialist dealers and annual price guides.

## Keeper Memorabilia and Uniforms

The keepers themselves left material traces. Trinity House issued uniforms from the early nineteenth century onward, and surviving examples in good condition are collected by maritime heritage museums and private collectors alike. American Lighthouse Board and later Coast Guard uniforms, kepis, and the distinctive lighthouse service buttons are sought by military and maritime collectors. Personal effects — diaries, family photographs taken at the station, tools of the trade — have begun to attract serious interest as the last generations of working keepers pass.

Finding and verifying this material is part of the pleasure of the pursuit. The Lighthouse Heritage Trust in the United Kingdom, the United States Lighthouse Society, and equivalent bodies in Ireland, Australia and Norway maintain networks through which collectors, curators, and descendants of keepers exchange information and artefacts. ## Where to Buy and Sell

Specialist maritime auctions are the primary venue for serious lighthouse material. Eldred's in East Dennis, Massachusetts, holds dedicated maritime sales that regularly include lighthouse equipment. Christie's South Kensington held notable lighthouse lens sales in the early 2000s. In the United Kingdom, Bonhams and specialist regional auctioneers handle Trinity House disposals. The annual Great Lakes Lighthouse Festival in Alpena, Michigan, and equivalent gatherings across the United States and Canada combine heritage events with dealer tables where smaller items change hands. Online platforms have expanded the accessible market enormously, though condition verification and provenance documentation remain as important in the digital marketplace as in any auction room.
