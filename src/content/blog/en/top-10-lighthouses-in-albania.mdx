Albania's coastline runs for some 450 kilometres down the eastern edge of the Adriatic and into the Ionian Sea, and for most of the twentieth century it was one of the least-known shores in Europe. Under the long isolation of the communist period, the coast was a fortified frontier studded with concrete bunkers, and its lighthouses served a country that admitted almost no foreign shipping and fewer foreign visitors. Today that has changed utterly. The same beaches and headlands now draw growing numbers of travellers, and the lights that once watched a closed sea now guide ferries, yachts, and a reviving merchant trade. This is a coast in transition, and its beacons tell that story.

## Two Seas, One Coast

The Albanian shore divides naturally into two very different halves. The northern and central portion faces the Adriatic: a lower, often marshy coast of lagoons, river mouths, and the broad bay at Durrës, the country's principal port. South of Vlorë the character changes abruptly. The Karaburun Peninsula and the island of Sazan guard the mouth of the Bay of Vlorë, and beyond them the coast becomes the Albanian Riviera — the Ionian shore, where the Ceraunian Mountains plunge almost vertically into clear, deep water. The Strait of Otranto, where Albania and Italy's heel approach within about 70 kilometres of each other, funnels all the traffic between the Adriatic and the wider Mediterranean past these lights.

## Durrës and the Adriatic Approach

Durrës, ancient Dyrrhachium, has been a port since classical antiquity, the western terminus of the Roman Via Egnatia that ran across the Balkans to Constantinople. Its modern harbour is the busiest in Albania, handling ferries to Italy and a steady flow of cargo. The lights marking the approach to Durrës guide vessels across the comparatively shallow Adriatic shelf into the protected bay. The port's importance has only grown as Albania has opened to trade, and the navigation aids here have been progressively modernised to match the increase in traffic and the larger vessels now calling.

## Cape Rodon and the Northern Shore

North of Durrës the coast runs up toward the Montenegrin border past Cape Rodon (Kepi i Rodonit), a long low headland crowned by the ruins of a fifteenth-century castle associated with the national hero Skanderbeg. The cape projects into the Adriatic and is an important turning point for coastal traffic. Further north lie the lagoons and the mouth of the Drin and Bojana rivers, where Albania meets Montenegro near the resort of Velipojë. This stretch is low and shifting, and the lights here mark a coast of sandbars and river outflow rather than rock.

## Vlorë, Sazan, and the Gateway South

The Bay of Vlorë is one of the great natural harbours of the Adriatic, sheltered by the Karaburun Peninsula and by Sazan, Albania's largest island. Sazan held strategic importance out of all proportion to its size: it commands the entrance to the bay and the southern approach to the whole Adriatic, and it was heavily militarised for most of the twentieth century. The lights on and around Sazan and the Karaburun headlands mark the transition from the Adriatic to the Ionian and the entrance to a harbour that has served navies as well as merchants. Vlorë itself is historically significant as the city where Albania declared its independence in 1912.

## The Ceraunian Coast and the Riviera Lights

South of the Llogara Pass, where the road climbs over the Ceraunian Mountains before dropping in dramatic switchbacks to the sea, begins the Albanian Riviera. Villages such as Dhërmi, Himarë, and Qeparo cling to steep slopes above small coves, and the water is among the clearest in the Mediterranean. Navigation along this Ionian shore is governed by the steep-to coast and the headlands that separate one bay from the next. The lights here are fewer and smaller than on the busier Adriatic, but they matter to the growing fleet of yachts and tour boats now working these waters in summer, as well as to the coastal trade.

## Saranda and the Corfu Channel

At the southern end of the country, Saranda faces the Greek island of Corfu across a narrow channel only a few kilometres wide. This is one of the most heavily transited stretches of the Ionian, with ferries shuttling constantly between Saranda and Corfu Town. The Corfu Channel was the scene of a famous international incident in 1946, when British warships struck mines here, an episode that went to the International Court of Justice. The lights of the channel — Greek beacons on the Othonoi islands such as Φάρος των Οθωνών to the west, and the light on the old fortress at Φάρος Παλαιού Φρουρίου on Corfu itself — work alongside the Albanian lights to mark this busy and historically contested water.

## Bunkers, Borders, and a Sealed Sea

To understand Albania's lights one must understand the bunkers. The regime of Enver Hoxha built hundreds of thousands of concrete pillboxes across the country, and the coast was among the most heavily fortified zones of all. The sea was treated as a hostile frontier, and the lighthouses operated within a closed system that served a navy and a coast guard rather than international shipping. Place names along this coast — Kep i Treporteve, Grames, Akra Kosteri — mark headlands and points that were, for decades, watched far more carefully for who might come ashore than for ships needing guidance. The opening of the country after 1991 transformed the function of every one of these lights.

## A Coast Opening to the World

The pace of change on the Albanian coast is now among the fastest in the Mediterranean. New marinas, ferry links, and resorts are appearing along shores that a generation ago saw almost no civilian sea traffic, and the navigation network is being modernised accordingly. For the lighthouse traveller, this is a place to see history in transition: structures built for a sealed border now serving an opening one, and a coast whose dramatic scenery — from the lagoons of the north to the mountain-backed coves of the Riviera — is finally accessible to those who come by sea. The lights are no longer guarding a closed country; they are welcoming visitors to it.
