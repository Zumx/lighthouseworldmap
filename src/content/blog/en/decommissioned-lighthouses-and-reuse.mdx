## After the Light Goes Out

Automation removed the keeper from the lighthouse decades ago. When a station's light, fog signal and radio beacon can be monitored by computer from a regional control centre, the attendant cottages, outbuildings, and ancillary structures — and sometimes the tower itself — become surplus to operational requirements. What happens next depends on jurisdiction, heritage designation, physical condition, and the initiative of local communities, preservation groups, and commercial developers. The range of outcomes is extraordinary: working artist retreats, holiday rentals, international youth hostels, boutique hotels, private homes, bird observatories, museums, and, in the least fortunate cases, progressive collapse through neglect.

## The Scale of Surplus

The United States alone decommissioned hundreds of lighthouses during the twentieth century as the Coast Guard rationalised its aids to navigation. The National Historic Lighthouse Preservation Act of 2000 created a federal mechanism for transferring surplus historic lighthouses to non-federal entities — state and local governments, non-profit organisations, educational institutions, and in some cases private individuals — at no cost, on the condition that the recipient preserved the structure and made it accessible to the public to a reasonable degree. Over 120 lighthouses had been transferred under this programme by the mid-2020s. Recipients have ranged from the city of Detroit, which manages the Grosse Ile North Channel Front Range Light, to small preservation societies operating on volunteer labour.

In the United Kingdom, Trinity House retains responsibility for the active light apparatus within a tower even when the surrounding property has been transferred or sold. This creates an unusual situation where a keeper's cottage may be a private holiday let while the adjacent tower remains technically in lighthouse service, its automated LED flasher and solar panel the legal responsibility of Trinity House. The buildings and land of many English and Welsh lighthouse stations have been sold freehold, subject to Trinity House retaining access rights to the operational equipment.

## Keeper's Cottages as Holiday Lets

The keeper's cottage is typically the most straightforwardly reusable element of a lighthouse station. Built to serve a family or a roster of keepers, these stone or brick structures are often well-built, in dramatic coastal locations, and connected — eventually — to mains water and electricity. The Irish Landmark Trust has pioneered their renovation as self-catering holiday properties in Ireland, restoring keepers' houses at stations including Hook Head, County Wexford, and Inishtearaght on the Blasket Islands to a standard that combines historic character with modern comfort. The Norwegian coastal authority Kystverket has done the same along the Norwegian coast, where many of the small island stations formerly operated by the State Lighthouse Service have become popular summer rentals.

In Scotland, the Northern Lighthouse Board has transferred a number of its former keeper cottages to rural housing trusts or into private use. Rubha Mòr on the Kintyre peninsula and Rua Reidh in Wester Ross are among those that have become guesthouses. The buildings are generally listed at Category B under Historic Scotland designation, requiring consent for any significant alterations, which both protects their character and can complicate sensitive renovation work.

## Hotels and Commercial Accommodation

A minority of lighthouse stations have been converted to commercial hotels, ranging from modest bed-and-breakfast operations to genuinely upmarket retreats. The Lighthouse Inn at New London, Connecticut, operated in the buildings adjacent to the New London Ledge Lighthouse for many years. In South Africa, the Cape Columbine lighthouse near Paternoster has lighthouse cottages available through SANParks. The most celebrated lighthouse-hotel conversions tend to be those where the location is spectacular and the tower itself forms part of the guest experience — where visitors can climb to the gallery, sleep within earshot of the sea, and feel the building move in wind.

Commercial conversion is subject to tensions that self-catering lets avoid. Hotel use requires fire escapes, accessibility provisions, and planning permissions that heritage structures do not always accommodate gracefully. The curved walls of a round tower defeat most standard kitchen and bathroom fittings; the absence of insulation in nineteenth-century stone walls creates heating challenges that running costs must account for. Developers who underestimate these difficulties are frequently undone by them.

## Artist Residencies and Cultural Uses

Several decommissioned lighthouse stations have found a natural home as artist residencies or cultural centres. The remoteness that made them challenging to staff is the very quality that makes them attractive to artists, writers, and composers seeking sustained concentration. The Monhegan Island lighthouse in Maine, whose station buildings were transferred to the island's historical society, now houses the Monhegan Museum of Art and History. The isolation of island and headland stations has supported residency programmes in Norway, Iceland, and Scotland, where participants live and work in former keeper accommodation for weeks at a time.

The lighthouse as a setting for cultural production has a long literary precedent — Virginia Woolf set To the Lighthouse in a fictional station based on Godrevy in Cornwall — and this association lends some residency programmes a self-consciously literary identity. Others are simply practical: a building available at modest cost, in a location that eliminates urban distractions, within a landscape that rewards attention.

## Museums and Heritage Centres

Towers sufficiently famous to attract visitor traffic are natural candidates for museum conversion. Split Rock Lighthouse on Lake Superior in Minnesota, built in 1910 following the catastrophic Mataafa storm of 1905 which wrecked twenty-nine ships, is now a Minnesota Historical Society historic site that receives well over 100,000 visitors per year. The light apparatus remains in the tower and the station is carefully interpreted.

Smaller heritage centres are often operated by local lighthouse preservation societies on thin budgets and with heavy dependence on volunteer guides. They provide irreplaceable local interpretation and serve as repositories for the keeper records, photographs and artefacts that would otherwise disperse. Some have proved financially fragile; others, particularly those attached to popular walking or cycling routes, have demonstrated surprising sustainability. ## Ruination and Loss

Not every decommissioned lighthouse finds a use. Where the structure is physically isolated — on a rock accessible only by helicopter, or at the end of a tidal causeway that vehicles cannot cross — conversion costs may simply exceed any achievable return. Timber structures, less durable than masonry towers, are particularly vulnerable. The United States lost a significant number of timber-frame and wood-sheathed lighthouse buildings on the Great Lakes and Gulf Coast to fire, rot, and storm damage during the twentieth century. In West Africa and parts of South Asia, colonial-era lighthouse buildings have deteriorated rapidly after lighthouse authorities moved to automated LED buoys, leaving structures without any maintenance funding.

Heritage listing provides a degree of protection but is not always sufficient without the resources to match. The tension between the cost of conserving a deliberately isolated building and the limited revenue its use can generate remains the central challenge of lighthouse preservation, wherever in the world the tower stands.
