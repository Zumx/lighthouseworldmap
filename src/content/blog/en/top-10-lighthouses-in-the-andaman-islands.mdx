Few archipelagoes in the world are harder to reach and stranger to imagine than the Andaman and Nicobar Islands, scattered across more than 700 kilometres of the Bay of Bengal between mainland India and the coast of Myanmar. Dense tropical jungle runs down to white beaches and coral reefs, ships approach through channels that have wrecked vessels for two centuries, and many of the islands remain restricted to outsiders. The lights that mark these waters are tended by the Directorate of Lighthouses and Lightships (DGLL), and they carry a history that runs from British penal colonies to the catastrophe of the 2004 Indian Ocean tsunami. This is a tour of ten of them.

## 1. Ross Island Light (Netaji Subhas Chandra Bose Dweep)

Ross Island, renamed Netaji Subhas Chandra Bose Dweep in 2018, sits just east of Port Blair and was once the administrative headquarters of the entire penal settlement. The British built churches, ballrooms and a bakery here, and a beacon to guide vessels into the harbour. Today the island is a haunting ruin, its colonial buildings strangled by the roots of banyan and ficus trees, with deer wandering among the broken walls. A small light still marks the position, and the contrast between the manicured imperial past and the jungle that has reclaimed it makes Ross the most atmospheric stop in the islands.

## 2. North Bay Lighthouse

If you have ever held an Indian 20-rupee note, you have seen North Bay Lighthouse. This slender tower stands on North Bay, a short boat ride from Port Blair near the mouth of the harbour, and its image was chosen to represent the Andamans on the currency. The light guides craft through the approaches to the capital and sits amid some of the best snorkelling and glass-bottom-boat reefs near the city. Reaching it means a short crossing from Wandoor or the Aberdeen jetty, and the tower is among the most photographed structures in the territory.

## 3. Indira Point Lighthouse (Great Nicobar)

At the southern tip of Great Nicobar stands Indira Point, the southernmost point of India, lying only around 150 kilometres from the Indonesian island of Sumatra across the open ocean. The lighthouse here is the loneliest beacon in the country. The 2004 tsunami struck this coast with terrible force, the land subsided by several metres, and the base of the tower was left standing in the sea where dry ground had been before. Repaired and back in service, Indira Point marks both a navigational waypoint and the very edge of the nation. Great Nicobar lies far south across the Ten Degree Channel, and access is heavily restricted.

## 4. Hut Bay Lighthouse (Little Andaman)

Little Andaman lies south of the main Andaman group, separated by open water, and its principal settlement is Hut Bay on the eastern coast. The lighthouse here guides the ferries that connect the island to Port Blair across a passage that can turn rough in the monsoon. Little Andaman is known for its waterfalls, surf and red oil-palm plantations, and the Hut Bay light is the mariner's confirmation of landfall after several hours of open sea with no other mark in sight.

## 5. Cinque Island Light

The two islets known as Cinque, joined by a spectacular sandbar that emerges at low tide, lie south of Port Blair and are ringed by coral. The waters here are part of a marine sanctuary, prized by divers for visibility that can exceed twenty metres. A light marks the approaches to this cluster, warning vessels off the reefs that have torn open more than one hull. Visiting requires a day permit, and the journey out is itself part of the appeal, crossing deep blue water far from any inhabited shore.

## 6. Cape Price Lighthouse (North Andaman)

At the northern reaches of the archipelago, near North Andaman and the town of Diglipur, Cape Price marks one of the more exposed headlands of the group. The northern islands are wilder and far less visited than the area around Port Blair, with Saddle Peak, the highest point in the territory at 732 metres, rising above the forest inland. The light at Cape Price serves the shipping that works its way up the eastern seaboard toward Myanmar, in waters where the next reliable mark may be many nautical miles away.

## 7. Interview Island Light

Interview Island, off the western coast of Middle Andaman, is a protected wildlife sanctuary famous for its population of feral elephants, descendants of animals once used for logging. Human habitation is essentially forbidden, and a restricted-area permit is required even to approach. The light here is a true wilderness beacon, surrounded by mangrove and dense forest, its keepers among the most isolated in the DGLL network. The island gives a sense of how remote these lights can be, far from any road or settlement.

## 8. Smith Island Light (near Diglipur)

Smith Island, connected to neighbouring Ross Island in the north by a natural sandbar, lies off the coast near Diglipur and forms part of one of the most beautiful coastal landscapes in the islands. The twin-island sandbar is a draw for the few travellers who make it this far north. The light marks the approaches through the surrounding reefs and shallows, in a corner of the Andamans where tourism is only beginning and the sense of frontier remains strong.

## 9. Mayabunder Light

Mayabunder, on the northern part of Middle Andaman, is a small port and the gateway to the upper islands, with ferry connections winding through mangrove creeks. The light here serves vessels threading the narrow channels and protected waters of the interior coast rather than the open Bay of Bengal. It is a working aid for local and inter-island traffic, marking a settlement that functions as a staging post for journeys further north toward Diglipur.

## 10. Havelock and Neil Approach Lights (Swaraj Dweep and Shaheed Dweep)

The two most visited islands after Port Blair, Havelock and Neil, were renamed Swaraj Dweep and Shaheed Dweep in 2018. Havelock's Radhanagar Beach is among the most celebrated in Asia, and the daily ferries crossing from Port Blair rely on a series of minor lights and harbour beacons to make safe landfall at the jetties. These approach lights are humble compared with the great offshore towers, but for the thousands of visitors who cross each week they are the final guides into anchorage among the reefs.

## Visiting the Andaman Lights

Reaching these lights takes planning. Port Blair is the only major airport and the hub for every onward journey, and most islands are served by government and private ferries that can be disrupted by the southwest monsoon between roughly May and September. Several islands, including the Nicobars and tribal reserves, are closed to visitors entirely, and others such as Interview Island require a restricted-area permit arranged in advance. The DGLL maintains the network across distances that mainland India never has to contemplate, and the keepers work in isolation that few modern lighthouse services still demand. For the traveller, the rewards are reefs, jungle, colonial ruins and a sense of standing at the edge of the country.
