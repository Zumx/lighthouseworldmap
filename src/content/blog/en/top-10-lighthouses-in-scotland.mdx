Scotland's lighthouse heritage is inseparable from the Stevenson family, whose three generations of engineers — Robert, his sons Alan, David and Thomas, and their sons in turn — built more than 85 towers across the Scottish coast and islands between 1800 and 1940. The Northern Lighthouse Board, established by Act of Parliament in 1786, commissioned these structures to bring order to a coastline of extraordinary violence: drowned reefs, tidal races that exceed eight knots, Atlantic swells built over two thousand miles of open ocean, and fog that descends without warning from the hills of Skye or the Orkney cliffs. The results are among the finest lighthouse engineering achievements in the world. Bell Rock in the Tay approaches, Skerryvore off Tiree, and Muckle Flugga at the northernmost point of Britain represent different solutions to the problem of placing a light where the sea is most hostile. Each one remains active today, maintained by a team that reaches them by helicopter and occasional tender vessel.

## 1. Bell Rock Lighthouse, Angus

Bell Rock lighthouse, completed in 1811 by Robert Stevenson, stands on a submerged sandstone reef in the North Sea 18 kilometres off Arbroath. The reef is covered to a depth of about 4 metres at high water and was responsible for at least 50 wrecks before the lighthouse was built. Stevenson worked from the precedent established by John Smeaton at Eddystone, constructing a dovetailed granite tower 35 metres tall whose base diameter narrows as it rises, distributing wave force along the curve of the masonry. The light's character is a white flash every five seconds with a range of 18 nautical miles. Bell Rock has been continuously active since lighting and was automated in 1988. It is the oldest surviving sea-washed lighthouse in the world. There is no visitor access to the rock; the lighthouse is visible from the Arbroath shore on clear days.

## 2. Skerryvore Lighthouse, Argyll

Skerryvore, designed by Alan Stevenson and completed in 1844, stands on an isolated reef 25 kilometres southwest of Tiree in the Inner Hebrides. Stevenson considered it the finest work of his career, and the engineer David Bremner wrote that it was 'the most graceful tower in the world.' The granite tower is 48 metres tall and 12 metres in diameter at the base, narrowing to 7 metres at the gallery. The reef on which it stands covers to 3 metres at high water and is exposed to the full force of the Atlantic fetch. The light shows a white flash every ten seconds and is visible for 23 nautical miles. The tower survived a devastating fire in 1954 that gutted the interior; it was rebuilt and automated in 1994. A shore exhibition in the Hebridean Centre at Tiree explains the construction. There is no access to the rock itself.

## 3. Muckle Flugga Lighthouse, Shetland

Muckle Flugga, on the northernmost rock of the British Isles, was built by Thomas and David Stevenson in 1858 under conditions of extreme difficulty; the original temporary structure was erected in just eight weeks to assist the Baltic Fleet during the Crimean War. The permanent tower, completed in 1876, is 20 metres tall on a rock that rises 64 metres above the sea. Its light, white with a red sector, shows a group of two flashes every twenty seconds and is visible for 22 nautical miles. The lighthouse marks the passage between the Atlantic and the North Sea around the Shetland archipelago, one of the most traffic-dense stretches of ocean in the northern hemisphere. It was automated in 1995. The lighthouse is visible from the Hermaness National Nature Reserve cliff path on Unst; this is one of the finest lighthouse viewpoints in Britain.

## 4. Ardnamurchan Lighthouse, Highland

Ardnamurchan Point is the westernmost point of mainland Britain, and its lighthouse — designed by Alan Stevenson in an Egyptian Revival style and completed in 1849 — is one of the most architecturally distinctive in Scotland. The granite tower, 36 metres tall, is flanked by keepers' cottages and service buildings in matching stone; the lantern room is fitted with a first-order lens producing a light visible for 24 nautical miles. The point marks the northern approach to the Sound of Mull and the dangerous Ardmore shoals offshore. Automation came in 1988. The lighthouse visitor centre is open seasonally from April to October and includes the original lens mechanism; the tower can be climbed for views across the Small Isles to Skye and Mull. The single-track road from Salen passes through birch woodland and is one of the finest drives on the west Highland coast.

## 5. Neist Point Lighthouse, Skye

On the most westerly tip of the Isle of Skye, accessible via a dramatic stepped path down basalt cliffs, Neist Point lighthouse was built in 1909 by David Alan Stevenson. The white-painted tower is compact at 19 metres, but its cliff-top focal plane of 43 metres gives a range of 16 nautical miles; the light flashes twice every thirty seconds. The station marks the western approach to the Little Minch and warns vessels off the tidal rip that runs across the point. Automation came in 1990 and the former keepers' cottages were converted to self-catering holiday accommodation, among the most spectacularly sited lighthouse lets in Scotland. The walk from the car park is about one kilometre with a steep descent and offers outstanding views of the Outer Hebrides, Dunvegan Head and, on clear days, the coast of North Uist.

## 6. Dunnet Head Lighthouse, Caithness

Dunnet Head, the northernmost point of mainland Britain, carries a lighthouse designed by Robert Stevenson and completed in 1831. The square stone tower is 19 metres tall on a headland 105 metres above sea level, producing a nominal range of 23 nautical miles; the character is a white flash every four seconds. The point divides the Pentland Firth — one of the most powerful tidal races in Europe, regularly exceeding 12 knots — from the more sheltered waters of Dunnet Bay. Vessels rounding Scotland for the east coast ports have passed this light for nearly two centuries. The lighthouse is automated and not open to visitors, but the headland is a Site of Special Scientific Interest and the approach track from Dunnet village passes through moorland alive with great skuas and puffins in summer. The views across the Pentland Firth to the Orkney island of Hoy are exceptional.

## 7. Cape Wrath Lighthouse, Sutherland

Cape Wrath, the most northwesterly point of mainland Britain, is named from the Norse word for a turning point, not from the weather — though its weather is often wrathful enough. The lighthouse was built by Robert Stevenson and first lit in 1828, the same year as Dunnet Head. The white tower is 20 metres tall on a 122-metre cliff, giving a focal plane visible for 22 nautical miles; the character is four white flashes every thirty seconds. The cape marks the corner around which vessels must turn from the northward run up the Minch to the eastward passage along the Pentland Firth. Robert Louis Stevenson spent time at Cape Wrath as a child, accompanying his father and uncle on inspection tours; the experience is widely credited with shaping his imagination. Access requires a passenger ferry across the Kyle of Durness and a minibus journey along a military road; the trip operates seasonally from May to September.

## 8. Fair Isle South Lighthouse, Shetland

Fair Isle, midway between Orkney and Shetland, carries two lighthouses: Fair Isle North, built 1892 on the low northern point, and Fair Isle South, built in the same year at the dramatic southern cliffs. The south lighthouse was designed by David and Charles Stevenson. Its tower stands on a cliff edge and the lantern rises to a focal plane of 80 metres, achieving a range of 22 nautical miles from a relatively modest structure. Fair Isle lies directly in the path of Atlantic depressions tracking northeast toward Scandinavia; it is also one of Britain's most celebrated seabird and migration watchpoints. The lighthouses were automated in 1998 and the former keeper accommodation converted to bird observatory facilities. Access is by small plane or ferry from Shetland; the island accommodates visitors at the bird observatory.

## 9. Rattray Head Lighthouse, Aberdeenshire

Rattray Head lighthouse, completed in 1895 by David and Charles Stevenson, stands on a tidal rock just offshore from the low sandy headland of Rattray in northeast Aberdeenshire. The granite tower is 34 metres tall; the light shows a white flash every five seconds with a range of 24 nautical miles. The headland marks the northeast corner of Scotland and the transition between the North Sea proper and the Moray Firth; it is associated with a tidal race and offshore sandbanks that have claimed numerous vessels. The lighthouse was designed for wave-washed conditions and sits on a rock that floods at high tide, giving it an offshore character despite its proximity to the mainland dunes. Automation came in 1982. The beach approach from St Combs makes the lighthouse one of the more accessible offshore structures in the country; it can be reached on foot at low tide.

## 10. Lismore Lighthouse, Argyll

The Lismore lighthouse, on the small island of Eilean Musdile at the southern tip of Lismore in Loch Linnhe, was designed by Robert Stevenson and completed in 1833. The white tower, 26 metres tall, guards the narrows at the entrance to the Firth of Lorn and marks the approach to Oban and the inner sea lochs. The light shows a three-second white flash with a range of 17 nautical miles and includes red sectors covering the Lady Rock and other hazards in the approach. The station is automated. The lighthouse island can be seen clearly from the Oban to Mull ferry and from the Lismore ferry from Port Appin. Its compact cluster of white buildings against the dark backdrop of the Morven hills is one of the classic west Highland maritime views.

## Visiting Scotland's Lighthouses

The Northern Lighthouse Board maintains around 200 staffed and remote stations across Scotland and the Isle of Man, and a number are open seasonally. Ardnamurchan offers the most complete visitor experience, with its museum and tower tours. Neist Point and Dunnet Head are among the finest lighthouse walks in Britain, accessible without special arrangement. For the great offshore towers — Bell Rock, Skerryvore, Muckle Flugga — the satisfaction is in catching a glimpse from a ferry deck or a clifftop, knowing the extraordinary effort of their construction.
