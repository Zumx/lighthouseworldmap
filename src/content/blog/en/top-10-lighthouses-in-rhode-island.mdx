Rhode Island, the smallest state in the Union, has a lighthouse heritage out of all proportion to its land area. Narragansett Bay cuts deep into the state, placing much of its population and all of its major cities — Providence, Newport, Bristol — on tidal water. The bay's approaches from the south pass through a complex of islands, shoals, and channelled passages that demanded lighting from the earliest period of colonial settlement. Newport was among the ten most prosperous ports in colonial America, and the federal government invested consistently in Rhode Island's lights from the passage of the Lighthouse Act in 1789. The result is a state with nineteen historic lighthouse structures surviving, several of architectural distinction, and a tightly concentrated geography that allows an experienced visitor to reach most of them in a long weekend. The Rhode Island Lighthouse Friends and the American Lighthouse Foundation maintain several of these towers and operate public access programmes that are among the most welcoming in New England.

## 1. Beavertail Lighthouse, Jamestown

Beavertail, on the southern tip of Conanicut Island at the entrance to Narragansett Bay, is the site of the third-oldest lighthouse in the United States. The original 1749 wooden tower — the first lighthouse in Rhode Island — burned in 1753; the granite tower that replaced it in 1754 served until it was replaced by the present structure in 1856. The current granite tower, 64 feet tall with a fourth-order Fresnel lens, produces a flashing white light visible for 15 nautical miles. The keeper's dwelling and fog signal building are intact within Beavertail State Park, which offers some of the finest rocky coastal scenery in southern New England. A small museum in the assistant keeper's quarters documents the lighthouse's history. The site is freely accessible year-round, making it one of the most visited lighthouse locations in Rhode Island.

## 2. Point Judith Lighthouse, Narragansett

Point Judith, at the western entrance to Narragansett Bay, has marked one of the most treacherous passages on the New England coast since 1810. The current octagonal granite tower was built in 1857 by the U.S. Lighthouse Board; its 51-foot height, combined with the elevated point, puts the focal plane at 65 feet above mean high water. The characteristic light — an occulting white and red — guides vessels around the point and into the passage between Point Judith and Block Island. The point's rocky shoals have claimed numerous vessels: a marker near the station commemorates some of the more significant wrecks. The lighthouse is an active Coast Guard station and is not open for interior climbing, but the exterior and grounds are accessible. The commercial fishing dock at Galilee, a five-minute walk away, provides an authentically working-harbour context.

## 3. Castle Hill Lighthouse, Newport

Castle Hill Lighthouse, a granite cylinder of 1890 perched on the rocky point of Castle Hill at the east side of the East Passage entrance to Narragansett Bay, is among the most beautifully situated lighthouses in New England. The 34-foot tower stands directly on the granite ledge above the water; its red sector light guides vessels in the passage and warns of the ledges off Dumpling Rock. The lighthouse is maintained by the Coast Guard and is not open to visitors, but it is easily reached by the path from the Castle Hill Inn — a luxury resort hotel in the adjacent Victorian mansion — and the rocky headland is a public right-of-way. The view of Newport Harbor from this point, with the bridge over the bay and the spires of the city in the distance, is one of the classic New England maritime panoramas.

## 4. Block Island Southeast Lighthouse, Block Island

The Block Island Southeast Lighthouse, completed in 1875 atop the Mohegan Bluffs — dramatic clay cliffs rising over 150 feet above the sea — is the only lighthouse in Rhode Island listed as a National Historic Landmark. The red-brick tower, 52 feet tall with an exterior staircase, originally carried a first-order Fresnel lens of extraordinary power; a replacement fourth-order lens now produces a green flashing light visible for 20 nautical miles. The entire lighthouse was moved 300 feet inland in 1993 as the Mohegan Bluffs eroded toward the foundation — one of the most dramatic lighthouse relocation projects in American history. The Block Island Lighthouse Society manages tours from the adjacent keeper's dwelling. The ferry from Point Judith or Galilee runs year-round and the lighthouse is a 20-minute bicycle ride from the ferry landing.

## 5. Watch Hill Lighthouse, Westerly

Watch Hill Lighthouse, at the southwestern tip of Rhode Island where the coastline meets Fishers Island Sound, was originally established in 1808 and rebuilt in its current form in 1856. The square granite tower, 51 feet tall, shows an occulting white light with a range of about 16 nautical miles, marking the channel between the Rhode Island mainland and the western end of Fishers Island. The lighthouse sits at the end of the Watch Hill promontory, surrounded by the elegant Victorian summer colony of Watch Hill — one of the most exclusive beach communities in New England. The Watch Hill Lighthouse Keepers Association operates a small museum in the keeper's dwelling and opens the lighthouse for tours on Tuesday and Thursday mornings in summer. The flying horses carousel in the village, the oldest carousel in the United States, makes the trip a memorable pairing.

## 6. Rose Island Lighthouse, Newport Harbor

Rose Island Lighthouse, a Second Empire-style frame structure completed in 1870 on a small island in the middle of Newport Harbor, was decommissioned in 1971 when the Newport Bridge was built — its fixed red light was superseded by the bridge lights. The lighthouse is now managed by the Rose Island Lighthouse Foundation as a museum and overnight accommodation: visitors can book the keeper's quarters and spend the night, operating the automated fog signal and maintaining the light as volunteer keepers for the duration of their stay. This extraordinary programme has attracted international attention and the island, accessible by ferry from Newport, is one of the most distinctive lighthouse experience destinations in the United States. The island retains its original garden, stone-walled enclosure, and boat ramp.

## 7. Prudence Island Lighthouse, Prudence Island

Prudence Island, the largest island in Narragansett Bay, is home to a lighthouse established in 1823 and rebuilt in 1852. The existing granite tower and keeper's dwelling are an intact mid-nineteenth century station, owned and maintained by the Town of Portsmouth. The island is served by a small ferry from Bristol and has a year-round population of a few hundred people. The lighthouse marks the southern end of the island and the shoal extending into the East Passage. The combination of accessible ferry, authentic keeper's dwelling, and quiet island landscape makes Prudence Island an alternative to the more crowded Newport lighthouse circuit.

## 8. Conanicut Island (Dutch Island) Lighthouse

Dutch Island Lighthouse, on a low islet in the middle of the West Passage of Narragansett Bay, was built in 1827 and rebuilt in its current form in 1858. The square granite tower with attached keeper's dwelling is now abandoned and accessible only by private boat, giving it the quality of isolation that the more accessible towers lack. The island was heavily fortified during the Second World War and earthworks and concrete bunkers surround the lighthouse. The Dutch Island Lighthouse Society has been working to stabilise the structure. The tower, 56 feet tall, originally showed a fixed white light with a sixth-order Fresnel lens.

## 9. Pomham Rocks Lighthouse, Providence River

Pomham Rocks Lighthouse, a Victorian frame dwelling-tower combination built in 1871 on a small rock in the Providence River, marks the main shipping channel to the city of Providence — the capital of Rhode Island and historically the second-largest city in New England. The lighthouse stands in an industrial landscape close to oil storage tanks and is accessible by private boat. It was decommissioned and threatened with demolition before the Friends of Pomham Rocks Lighthouse began restoration work in the 2000s. The structure has been substantially repaired and periodic open-house events are held; the Providence River approach is visible from several public shoreline points in East Providence.

## 10. Nayatt Point Lighthouse, Barrington

Nayatt Point Lighthouse, a small white granite tower of 1828 on the eastern shore of the upper bay in Barrington, is one of the most modest and least-visited of Rhode Island's surviving towers. The structure is on private property and not open to the public. Its historical significance lies in its role in lighting the shoal-water approaches to Providence and Bristol during the height of Rhode Island's coastal commerce, and in its early date — the 1828 tower is among the older surviving lighthouse structures in the state, though much altered.

## Visiting Rhode Island's Lighthouses

Beavertail State Park is open year-round and free; the museum has seasonal hours. Block Island ferry services run year-round from Point Judith with increased frequency in summer; bring a bicycle. Rose Island overnight stays are bookable through the Rose Island Lighthouse Foundation website. Watch Hill museum tours run Tuesday and Thursday mornings in July and August. Newport is a natural base for the central bay lights: the Castle Hill Inn path, the Cliff Walk, and the harbour ferry all give different perspectives on the water.
