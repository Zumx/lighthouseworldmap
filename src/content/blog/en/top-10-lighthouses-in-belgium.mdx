Belgium's North Sea coast is barely 67 kilometres long — one of the shortest national coastlines in Europe — yet it contains a disproportionate concentration of navigational heritage. The Belgian coast faces the shallowest and most heavily trafficked sea in the world: the Southern Bight of the North Sea, where the Thames Estuary, the Rhine and Scheldt estuaries, and the English Channel all converge in waters rarely deeper than 30 metres. Sandbanks shift, tidal streams run hard, and the density of commercial shipping traffic makes precise navigation essential at all hours. The lighttowers of Ostend, Nieuwpoort, Blankenberge, and Heist, and the offshore buoy and light system managed by Vlaamse Hydrografie, have guided this traffic through the centuries. Most of the Belgian coastal lighthouses are modest in scale but considerable in historical significance.

## 1. Lange Nelle, Ostend

The Lange Nelle — 'Long Nellie' in Flemish — is the landmark lighthouse of Ostend, rising 65 metres from the harbour district to make it the tallest lighthouse in Belgium. The tower was completed in 1949 to replace an older lighthouse destroyed during the Second World War. Its construction used a reinforced concrete shaft of elegant proportions, and the white-painted tower has become as much an urban landmark as a navigational one. The light character is a white flash every five seconds, with a nominal range of 27 nautical miles over the approaches to Ostend's busy commercial and ferry port. The lighthouse is maintained by the Flemish Hydrographic Service (Vlaamse Hydrografie) and is occasionally open to visitors on heritage days; otherwise it is observed from the adjacent waterfront promenade, which is among the most active in the Belgian coast.

## 2. Vuurtoren Nieuwpoort, Nieuwpoort

The lighthouse at Nieuwpoort stands at the entrance to the Nieuwpoortse IJzergeul, the channel connecting the sea to the harbour and marina at Nieuwpoort. The current tower was completed in 1950 after the wartime destruction of its predecessor. It rises 28 metres in white masonry and shows a flashing white light every three seconds with a range of 15 nautical miles over the offshore banks that extend seaward from this part of the coast. Nieuwpoort has a particular significance in the history of the First World War: the sluices here were opened in October 1914 to flood the coastal polders and halt the German advance, an event that determined the line of the Western Front. The lighthouse stands in a town that carries this history prominently; the nearby military cemetery and the King Albert I memorial are closely associated with the lighthouse setting.

## 3. Vuurtoren Heist, Heist-aan-Zee

The Heist lighthouse, built in 1909 on the seaward edge of the town of Heist-aan-Zee (now part of Knokke-Heist), stands 26 metres and marks the eastern approaches to the Belgian coast and the entrance to the shallow Zeebrugge anchorage area. The tower shows a group-flashing white light visible for 14 nautical miles. Heist is at the western end of the Dutch approaches and the lighthouse is an important visual reference for vessels making passage between the Westerschelde and the Channel. The tower is listed as a protected monument by the Flemish Heritage Agency and is open for guided tours; the adjacent beach and fishing harbour give the setting a working coastal character retained from the early twentieth century.

## 4. Vuurtoren Blankenberge, Blankenberge

Blankenberge's lighthouse, completed in 1982 on the harbour arm extending seaward from this popular resort town, is a modern structure of concrete and glass that lacks the historical character of its neighbours but is operationally significant as a port entry light for the Blankenberge leisure harbour. The tower stands 28 metres and shows a green sector light over the safe approach channel, with white and red sectors marking the dangers to either side — a classic Belgian sector light arrangement. The harbour arm is a popular promenade; the lighthouse can be approached closely on foot and is illuminated attractively after dark.

## 5. Lichtschip West-Hinder (Museum Ship), Antwerp

Although no longer in its original location, the lightship West-Hinder deserves inclusion in any account of Belgian navigational heritage. The vessel served as a floating lighthouse on the West-Hinder Bank in the outer Scheldt approaches from 1950 to 1994, marking a dangerous shallow bank that has claimed vessels since the medieval period. The characteristic red-hulled lightship is now preserved at the Museum aan de Stroom in Antwerp, where it forms part of the permanent maritime collection. Its fog signal, light apparatus, and accommodation for the crew of seven are preserved intact. The West-Hinder Bank itself is now marked by a large automated light buoy.

## 6. Loodswezengebouw, Vlissingen (Belgian approach)

The pilots' building at Vlissingen — just inside the Dutch border on the Westerschelde — carries a light that is closely associated with Belgian maritime traffic because the Westerschelde is the tidal estuary leading to Antwerp, Belgium's principal port and one of the largest in Europe. The approach lights and range lights along the Westerschelde are administered cooperatively between the Netherlands and Belgium under a long-standing treaty arrangement. The navigational light system along the Westerschelde includes dozens of leading lights, sector lights, and buoys; the approaches to Antwerp demand precision navigation through channels dredged to accommodate container vessels drawing over 15 metres.

## 7. Westhoofd Lighthouse, Ouddorp (North Sea approaches)

Although technically in the Netherlands, the Westhoofd lighthouse on the island of Goeree-Overflakkee is a primary navigational mark for vessels approaching the Belgian coast from the north. The tower, completed in 1947, stands 55 metres in red-banded masonry and shows a group-flashing white character visible for 28 nautical miles. Vessels bound for Zeebrugge, Ostend, or the Westerschelde estuary use Westhoofd as a major waypoint for landfall after crossing the North Sea from England, and the light has been a reference for Belgian maritime trade since the postwar reconstruction.

## 8. Port de Zeebrugge Lights, Zeebrugge

The port of Zeebrugge, created by the construction of a massive curved breakwater between 1895 and 1907, is now the most important commercial port on the Belgian coast, handling roll-on/roll-off traffic, LNG tankers, and container vessels. The harbour lights on the outer and inner moles are functional rather than historic, but the Zeebrugge Mole was the site of the famous St George's Day Raid of 23 April 1918, when Royal Navy and Royal Marines attempted to block the harbour with obsolete cruisers. The lighthouse at the end of the outer mole — rebuilt since the war — marks the same point where the blocking ships were run aground. The port is active and inaccessible to visitors, but the breakwater can be viewed from the beach at Zeebrugge.

## 9. Lange Nelle (Historical Record), Ostend

The original Lange Nelle, a masonry tower built in 1838 and rising 47 metres, served Ostend until its destruction in 1944. Historical photographs and the lighthouse's archive are preserved in the Ostend City Museum (MU.ZEE) along with the maritime heritage collection. The 1838 tower used a first-order Fresnel lens of French manufacture and was one of the most powerful lights on the Belgian coast of its era, visible for 20 nautical miles over the Flemish Banks. The postwar replacement preserved the name and the approximate location; the museum collection preserves the character and equipment of the original.

## 10. Roi Albert Ier Monument, Nieuwpoort

The King Albert I monument at Nieuwpoort, completed in 1938, incorporates a lighthouse tower as part of its memorial design. The monument commemorates the role of King Albert and the Belgian Army in holding the Yser line during the First World War, and the combined lighthouse-memorial structure is unique in Belgian heritage. The light it carries is more symbolic than operational — a fixed white beam visible for a few miles over the IJzer river mouth — but the integration of the lighthouse form into a national monument reflects how deeply the light tower was embedded in Belgian coastal identity in the interwar period.

## Visiting Belgium's Lighthouses

Belgium's compact coast means that all of its lighthouse sites can be visited in a single long day from Brussels or Bruges. The main resort towns of Ostend, Blankenberge, Nieuwpoort, and Knokke-Heist are served by the De Lijn coastal tram, a remarkable service running the entire 67 kilometres of Belgian coast and stopping within walking distance of every lighthouse on the list. The coastal tram runs year-round; summer is busiest but autumn and winter provide better photography light and smaller crowds.
