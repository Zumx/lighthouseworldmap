Ukraine's coastline stretches across the northern shore of the Black Sea and the Sea of Azov, two connected bodies of water that have been among the most commercially active in the ancient and modern worlds alike. Greek colonies established trading posts along these shores from the seventh century BC; the Bosphorus and the Black Sea were the primary route for grain that fed classical Athens, and the same grain trade continued under Byzantine, Ottoman, and Russian empires. The Russian Empire established a systematic lighthouse network here in the early nineteenth century, and the Imperial Hydrographic Service constructed several stations that remain among the most technically significant in the region. Ukraine inherited this network after independence in 1991, and the Ukrainian Hydrographic Centre has maintained a chain of lights along coasts that have been subject to extraordinary pressures since 2014. Many of the western Black Sea lights around Odesa and the Dnipro-Buh estuary remain operational. [Open the map](/map) to explore the full extent of Ukraine's navigational infrastructure.

## 1. Tarkhankut Lighthouse, Crimea

Tarkhankut occupies the westernmost point of the Crimean Peninsula, marking the cape where vessels entering or leaving the Black Sea through the Kerch Strait must alter course. The lighthouse, completed in 1816 to the design of the engineer A. Rochoux, is one of the oldest working lighthouses in the Black Sea region. The cylindrical white tower rises 38 metres from the clifftop and carries a flashing white light with a range of 16 nautical miles, visible well into the open sea to the west. The stone construction and classical proportions reflect the influence of French lighthouse engineering that the early Russian Imperial service employed. The cape itself is a dramatic limestone promontory cut by wave-eroded grottoes known as the 'Bay of Skulls', popular with divers who explore the clear waters for ancient shipwrecks. Access since 2014 has been restricted due to the Russian occupation of Crimea.

## 2. Vorontsov Lighthouse, Odesa

The Vorontsov lighthouse at the end of the Vorontsov Mole in Odesa harbour is the most recognisable lighthouse in Ukraine, depicted in photographs, paintings, and postcards of the city for well over a century. The stone tower was first built in 1845 and rebuilt in its present form in 1888; it stands 26 metres tall and carries an occulting white light with a range of 16 nautical miles, marking the approach to Ukraine's largest port. The lighthouse is named for Count Mikhail Vorontsov, Governor-General of Novorossiya, who oversaw many of Odesa's major infrastructure projects in the mid-nineteenth century. The mole is a working port installation, and civilian access to the lighthouse itself is restricted, but it can be photographed from the ferry terminal and the harbour area. Odesa's status as Ukraine's main maritime gateway gives the Vorontsov lighthouse a significance beyond its modest physical dimensions.

## 3. Yevpatoria Lighthouse, Yevpatoria

Yevpatoria (Eupatoria) is a coastal city on the western Crimean coast north of Sevastopol, historically a Tatar town and Roman Catholic pilgrimage destination, and in the modern era a resort. Its lighthouse marks the harbour entrance and the approach from the open Black Sea, guiding the ferry traffic and cargo vessels that use the port. The white tower, dating from the mid-nineteenth century, stands on the breakwater and carries a flashing character visible around 14 nautical miles. Yevpatoria was the landing point for Allied forces in the Crimean War of 1853-56, and the lighthouse stood during that conflict. Access, as with all Crimean lighthouses, is restricted since 2014.

## 4. Stanislav-Adzhyhol Front Lighthouse, Kherson Oblast

The Stanislav-Adzhyhol range lights in the Dnipro-Buh estuary near Kherson are a pair of lighthouses forming a leading line for vessels navigating the shallow and shifting approaches to the port of Kherson and the river entrance. The front light is a remarkable iron skeleton tower, considered one of the most architecturally unusual lighthouses in Ukraine: it resembles a factory chimney encased in a lattice of cast iron ribs, standing approximately 64 metres above the flat steppe landscape. Completed in 1910 to the design of engineers from the Russian Imperial Hydrographic Service, the tower is the taller of the pair and was designed specifically to be visible above the horizon from vessels in the Buh estuary. Its light character provides the front bearing of the range; the rear light provides the reciprocal.

## 5. Stanislav-Adzhyhol Rear Lighthouse, Kherson Oblast

The rear light of the Stanislav-Adzhyhol range stands a few kilometres behind the front tower and is the taller of the pair — at approximately 64 metres it is one of the tallest lighthouses in Ukraine. Together, the front and rear lights form a leading line: when a vessel steers until both lights appear vertically aligned, it is on the safe channel course. The tower design mirrors the front light's iron skeleton construction, a distinctive silhouette that has been photographed extensively by industrial heritage enthusiasts. The estuary landscape around both towers is flat, reed-fringed, and strongly reminiscent of the Rhine or Danube delta; the scale of the towers above this low terrain is particularly striking. Both towers remain listed navigation aids, though their operational status has been affected by the conflict in the Kherson region since 2022.

## 6. Zmiinyi Island Lighthouse (Snake Island), Black Sea

Zmiinyi — Snake Island — is a small rocky outcrop in the Black Sea about 35 kilometres from the Romanian coast, which became one of the most widely discussed locations in the world in February 2022. A lighthouse has stood on this island for well over a century, its light marking a critical waypoint for vessels leaving the Danube delta and the port of Odesa bound for the Bosphorus. The white cylindrical tower carries a flashing white character with a range of around 18 nautical miles. The island and its small garrison became internationally famous following exchanges at the start of Russia's full-scale invasion; the lighthouse survived the conflict period, though the small island's infrastructure suffered significant damage. The island's strategic importance as a navigation mark reflects its historical role as a waypoint for Black Sea shipping.

## 7. Tendra Spit Lighthouse, Kherson Oblast

Tendra, a long and narrow sand spit in the Black Sea northwest of Crimea, was the site of one of the great naval battles of the Russian-Turkish Wars: in 1790 Admiral Ushakov defeated the Ottoman fleet here. A lighthouse has marked this isolated spit since the nineteenth century; the low-lying sand and grass provides essentially no elevation, so the tower must be tall enough on its own to project its light across the shallow surrounding sea. The white cylindrical tower stands in a landscape of sand dunes and sea grass, a remote station accessible only by boat. The light character is flashing white, warning vessels off the spit's eastern end where sandbanks extend some distance from the visible land.

## 8. Bilhorod-Dnistrovskyi Lighthouse, Bilhorod-Dnistrovskyi

The Dnister estuary (Dnistrovsky Liman) is a significant inland water body separated from the Black Sea by a sand bar, and the town of Bilhorod-Dnistrovskyi — known in Ottoman times as Akkerman — stands at its northern shore beneath a magnificent fourteenth-century Genovese-Moldavian fortress. The lighthouse marks the channel through the bar and the approach to the liman port. The fortress, with its 34 towers, is one of the best-preserved medieval fortifications in Eastern Europe; the lighthouse operates in the shadow of this extraordinary heritage structure, continuing a tradition of maritime marking that has existed since Genovese traders commanded this coast.

## 9. Odesa Quarantine Breakwater Light, Odesa

The outer harbour of Odesa is guarded by a quarantine breakwater that was constructed in the nineteenth century to control the entry of vessels into this critical trading port. The light at the end of the breakwater marks the harbour entrance channel and complements the Vorontsov lighthouse on the mole. During Odesa's period as the main grain-exporting port of the Russian Empire, the quarantine facilities processed millions of tonnes of cargo and hundreds of thousands of migrants bound for the Americas; the lighthouse was the last Ukrainian light many emigrants saw as their ships cleared the harbour.

## 10. Ochakiv Lighthouse, Ochakiv

Ochakiv, at the entrance to the Dnipro-Buh estuary, marks one of the most strategically significant maritime positions on the Ukrainian coast. The town was a fortress city commanding the entry to the Dnipro — the river that drains much of central Ukraine — and its lighthouse is positioned to guide vessels through the shallow entry channel. The white tower dates from the nineteenth century and serves both commercial and military navigation. The surrounding area is a wetland of considerable ecological value: the Kinburn Spit, separating the Dnipro-Buh estuary from the Black Sea, is one of the finest migratory bird habitats in Ukraine.

## Visiting Ukraine's Lighthouses

The practical reality of visiting Ukrainian lighthouses has been profoundly altered by the ongoing conflict that intensified in 2022. The Crimean lights — Tarkhankut and Yevpatoria in particular — are inaccessible under Russian occupation. The Kherson Oblast lights, including the Stanislav-Adzhyhol towers, are in an active conflict zone and cannot safely be visited. The lights of the Odesa coast — Vorontsov, Bilhorod-Dnistrovskyi, and several smaller harbour lights — are theoretically accessible, but the maritime environment remains under severe restrictions. Any travel planning should consult current official guidance.
