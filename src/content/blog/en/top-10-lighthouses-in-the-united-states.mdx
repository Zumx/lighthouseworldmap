The United States has more lighthouses than any other nation — over 700 surviving structures of historical significance, the legacy of a federal lighthouse programme that began in 1789, when the new Congress passed one of its first acts establishing lighthouse administration under the Treasury Department. From the granite headlands of Maine to the Gulf Coast mudflats, from the Great Lakes to the Pacific breakers off the Oregon coast, the American lighthouse estate reflects the full geographic and industrial sweep of the nation's maritime history. The Lighthouse Board, established in 1852 to reform a system that had grown complacent and inconsistent, drove a generation of high-quality construction that produced some of the finest towers in the world. ## 1. Cape Hatteras Lighthouse, North Carolina

Cape Hatteras Lighthouse is the tallest brick lighthouse in the United States at 63 metres (198 feet) and one of the great works of American engineering. Completed in 1870 to designs supervised by engineer Dexter Stetson, the tower guards Diamond Shoals, the submerged sandbar extending 14 miles offshore at the point where the Gulf Stream and Labrador Current collide in waters that have sunk thousands of vessels. The black-and-white spiral-stripe daymark is instantly recognisable. Its light — Fl W 7.5s, 24 nautical miles — was achieved with a first-order Fresnel lens. In 1999 the tower was moved 870 metres inland to prevent coastal erosion from undermining its foundations — an engineering feat that preserved the lighthouse for at least another century. It is open to climb from April through October; the 257-step ascent is rewarded with views stretching across the barrier islands.

## 2. Portland Head Lighthouse, Maine

Portland Head Lighthouse, completed in 1791 under the direct oversight of George Washington's administration, is the oldest lighthouse in Maine and one of the oldest in the country. The 24-metre white granite tower stands on a rocky headland in Fort Williams Park at Cape Elizabeth, with Casco Bay spreading north and the open Atlantic to the south. The light — Fl(4) W 20s, range 24 nautical miles — marks the entrance to Portland Harbor, once one of the most important ports in New England. The adjacent keeper's house has been a museum since 1989 and houses an extensive collection of lighthouse artefacts. The lighthouse grounds, part of the Cape Elizabeth town park, are free to enter and open year-round; the site is accessible and manages to feel genuinely historic rather than commercialised.

## 3. Boston Light, Massachusetts

Boston Light, on Little Brewster Island in Boston Harbor, holds the distinction of being the site of the oldest lighthouse in the Americas, first lit in 1716. The current tower was rebuilt after the British demolished the original during the Revolutionary War; the 1783 structure, strengthened and heightened to 31 metres in 1859, is the last staffed lighthouse in the United States — the Coast Guard maintains a symbolic keeper in residence as a heritage gesture. The light — Fl W 10s, range 27 nautical miles — marks the approach to Boston Harbor through the outer islands. Access is by ferry from Fan Pier in Boston's Seaport District, running on a seasonal schedule maintained by the National Park Service. Lighthouse preservation advocates regard Boston Light as the spiritual home of American lighthouse heritage.

## 4. Tillamook Rock Lighthouse, Oregon

'Terrible Tilly,' as the keepers called it, stands one mile offshore in the North Pacific breakers north of Tillamook Head — a black basalt rock on which Scottish engineer Alexander Ballantyne's team somehow raised a lighthouse between 1879 and 1881, one of the most difficult engineering projects in American coastal history. The foreman who first assessed the site drowned before construction began. The 21-metre tower, built on a rock that in storms disappears entirely beneath breaking seas, carried a first-order Fresnel lens visible 18 nautical miles and guided traffic bound for the Columbia River. Decommissioned in 1957 and later converted to a columbarium, the lighthouse is not accessible; it is best viewed from Ecola State Park's headland trails, where the tower appears as a dark shape in the surf a mile offshore.

## 5. Pigeon Point Lighthouse, California

Pigeon Point Lighthouse, completed in 1872 near Pescadero on the San Mateo County coast, is the tallest lighthouse on the West Coast at 35 metres. Its white-painted brick tower, constructed under the direction of lighthouse engineer Major George Elliot, marks the rocky headland and offshore dangers that have claimed numerous vessels since the first European ships worked this coast. The lighthouse is named for the clipper ship Carrier Pigeon, which wrecked on the reef in 1853. The original first-order Fresnel lens — an enormous 16-panel optic — was damaged in the early 2000s and removed for restoration; the lighthouse itself, managed by California State Parks, is undergoing restoration as well, and regular tours and hostel accommodation are offered. The headland is an excellent whale-watching point during the grey whale migration in December and March.

## 6. Split Rock Lighthouse, Minnesota

Split Rock Lighthouse, completed in 1910 on a 35-metre cliff above Lake Superior in northern Minnesota, was built in direct response to a catastrophic November 1905 storm that wrecked or disabled 29 vessels on the lake in a single event. The octagonal brick tower, 25 metres tall, sits within a complex of fog signal building, keeper's house and oil house — the whole ensemble preserved in near-original condition by the Minnesota Historical Society. The light — Fl W 10s, now operated only on ceremonial occasions as a heritage light — used a third-order Fresnel lens visible 22 miles across the lake. The access road to Split Rock was built by the Minnesota Highway Department as part of the North Shore Drive; the lighthouse is now the most visited historic site in Minnesota.

## 7. Bodie Island Lighthouse, North Carolina

Built in 1872 as part of the same post-Civil War coastal reconstruction programme that produced Cape Hatteras, Bodie Island Lighthouse rises 50 metres from the flat barrier island terrain of Cape Hatteras National Seashore. Its horizontal black-and-white bands, by Dexter Stetson, clearly distinguish it from the spiral-striped Cape Hatteras to the south. The light — Fl(2+1) W 30s, range 19 nautical miles — marks the shifting inlets of the northern Outer Banks and Oregon Inlet, where powerful tidal currents move enormous quantities of sand. The original first-order Fresnel lens remains in place. The National Park Service restored the tower and reopened it for climbing in 2013; the adjacent keeper's house, a substantial Victorian structure, houses lighthouse heritage exhibits. The surrounding freshwater ponds and marsh are notable for waterfowl.

## 8. Montauk Point Lighthouse, New York

Montauk Point Lighthouse, completed in 1796 and thus the oldest lighthouse in New York State, was authorised by President George Washington and marks the eastern tip of Long Island — the first landfall for vessels approaching New York from the Atlantic. The 28-metre sandstone tower stands on a bluff 19 metres above the sea; the light — Fl W 5s, range 24 nautical miles — guides the heavy traffic of Long Island Sound's eastern entrance and the ocean approaches to New York Harbor. The Montauk Historical Society manages the lighthouse as a museum, and the tower is open for climbing on a seasonal basis. The surrounding Montauk Point State Park offers excellent views of the Atlantic; harbour seals haul out on the rocks below the bluff in winter.

## 9. Heceta Head Lighthouse, Oregon

Heceta Head Lighthouse, completed in 1894 on a 56-metre forested headland on the central Oregon coast, is frequently cited as the most photogenic lighthouse in the United States. The 21-metre white brick tower and the adjacent Victorian keeper's quarters — now a bed and breakfast — occupy a site of singular beauty, with waves breaking on rocks below and old-growth Sitka spruce climbing the cliffs behind. The light — Fl W 10s, 21 nautical miles — employs an original first-order Fresnel lens, one of the few on the Oregon coast still in its original mount. Oregon State Parks manages the lighthouse and the surrounding Heceta Head Scenic Viewpoint; guided tours are offered from late spring through autumn.

## 10. Cape Florida Lighthouse, Florida

Cape Florida Lighthouse, completed in 1825 on the southern tip of Key Biscayne and thus one of the oldest surviving lighthouses in Florida, marks the northern approach to the Florida Keys and the dangerous shoals on the eastern side of the cape. The 26-metre brick tower was attacked during the Second Seminole War in July 1836 when Seminole warriors set the lighthouse on fire while the assistant keeper was inside — he survived by sheltering on the iron lantern deck as the wooden stairs burned below him. The tower was rebuilt and heightened to 33 metres in 1855. It stands within Bill Baggs Cape Florida State Park and is open for guided climbs on a limited weekly schedule. The surrounding beach and the views south across Biscayne Bay make the site one of the most pleasant in Florida.

American lighthouse tourism is well-organised; the lighthouse preservation movement, led by organisations such as the United States Lighthouse Society and dozens of state affiliates, has protected hundreds of structures from demolition since the 1970s. The National Park Service lighthouse programme places many towers within protected park areas, ensuring long-term preservation and public access. Spring and autumn are generally the best seasons — summer brings crowds to the most popular towers, while winter can close coastal roads and ferry services to offshore stations.
