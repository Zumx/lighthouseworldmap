Wisconsin's lighthouse heritage is rooted in the commerce of the Great Lakes. Lake Michigan to the east and Lake Superior to the north once carried more commercial traffic than almost any comparable body of water in the world: schooners and steam-powered bulk carriers hauled iron ore from the Minnesota Range, lumber from the vast northern forests, grain from the prairies, and manufactured goods on the return run. Green Bay, Door Peninsula, the Apostle Islands, and the Keweenaw approaches to Lake Superior all demanded precision navigation in waters where November gales could drive vessels onto uncharted shoals with no warning. The United States Lighthouse Service placed towers along Wisconsin's shores from the 1830s onward, and the architectural variety — stone towers, brick towers, iron skeletal structures, and the characteristic square integral towers attached to keeper's dwellings — reflects the evolving engineering philosophy of the nineteenth century. The United States Coast Guard administers the active lights; many decommissioned stations are managed by county and state park authorities, volunteer lighthouse associations, and the National Park Service. ## 1. Cana Island Lighthouse, Baileys Harbor

Cana Island lighthouse is the most-visited lighthouse in Wisconsin and one of the most striking on all of the Great Lakes. The white-painted octagonal masonry tower, standing 27 metres tall on a low island in Lake Michigan connected to the mainland of the Door Peninsula by a stone causeway, was completed in 1870. Its third-and-a-half order Fresnel lens — unusual in designation but powerful in range — produced a light visible about 15 nautical miles across the northern approaches to Green Bay. The causeway floods during high water periods, and visitors occasionally wade across in summer to reach the island; water levels have been notably higher in recent years due to Lake Michigan's cyclical fluctuations. The lighthouse is managed by the Door County Maritime Museum; guided tours of the tower and restored keeper's quarters run May through October. The setting — limestone bedrock, white birch trees, and the light blue of Lake Michigan — is genuinely photogenic.

## 2. Wind Point Lighthouse, Racine

The Wind Point lighthouse at the southern end of Lake Michigan is the tallest lighthouse in Wisconsin, its red brick tower rising 34 metres above the lake shore north of Racine. Completed in 1880 and designed by Orlando Poe, the engineer who oversaw the construction of several Great Lakes lights in this period, the tower was equipped with a third-order Fresnel lens with a range of 19 nautical miles. It marks the transition from the open lake to the harbour approaches of Racine and Kenosha, Milwaukee's southern neighbours. The lighthouse and adjacent keeper's dwelling are still used by the Village of Wind Point as municipal offices, but the tower is open for tours on designated days. The grounds, maintained as a public park, allow close inspection of one of the finest brick lighthouse towers on the freshwater coast.

## 3. Rawley Point Lighthouse, Two Rivers

The Rawley Point lighthouse near Two Rivers on the Lake Michigan shore is a cast-iron skeletal tower of striking appearance, constructed in 1894 to replace an 1853 masonry tower. The skeletal frame, painted white, supports the lantern room 34 metres above the lake and was prefabricated in sections for assembly on site — a construction method common to the industrialisation-era lighthouse programme. The third-order Fresnel lens produced a light visible about 19 nautical miles. Rawley Point is within Point Beach State Forest; the campground and beach bring significant numbers of visitors who encounter the lighthouse on the forested shoreline. The tower is an active US Coast Guard aid to navigation and is not publicly accessible for climbing, but the skeletal design makes it visually distinctive and well-photographed from ground level.

## 4. Eagle Bluff Lighthouse, Peninsula State Park

Eagle Bluff lighthouse in Peninsula State Park on the Door Peninsula is one of the best-preserved examples of the Great Lakes integral lighthouse style, where the tower is built directly into the end of the keeper's dwelling rather than standing as a separate structure. Completed in 1868 and standing 13 metres above the bluff, the square yellow brick tower and adjacent dwelling look more like a substantial farmhouse than a maritime installation, yet the light guided vessels through Green Bay's northern narrows for over fifty years. The third-order Fresnel lens is still in place in the lantern room and is visible on tours. The lighthouse is managed by the Door County Maritime Museum; Peninsula State Park is one of Wisconsin's most popular state parks, and the lighthouse receives thousands of visitors each season.

## 5. Sherwood Point Lighthouse, Sturgeon Bay

Sherwood Point at the southern end of Green Bay was the last lighthouse to be built on the Great Lakes with a resident keeper, and it remained manned until 1983 — among the latest demanning dates in the Great Lakes system. The red brick tower, completed in 1883, stands 10 metres tall and is attached to a substantial keeper's dwelling; the ensemble represents the working life of a Great Lakes lighthouse station with particular completeness. The light character is a flashing red and white with red sectors covering the shallows. The station is now owned by the US Coast Guard and is not regularly open to the public, but it is accessible on the annual Lighthouse Walk coordinated by the Door County Maritime Museum. The surrounding limestone shoreline of Green Bay is excellent for birdwatching.

## 6. Pottawatomie Lighthouse, Rock Island State Park

Rock Island, the northernmost point in Wisconsin accessible only by ferry and then only to foot travellers (no motorised vehicles), carries the oldest lighthouse in Wisconsin: the Pottawatomie lighthouse, first built in 1836 and the current stone structure dating from an 1858 reconstruction. The two-story dwelling with integral tower stands 10 metres tall on a limestone bluff at the island's northwestern point, and the restored keeper's quarters contain period furniture and interpretive exhibits. Rock Island State Park preserves the island in an essentially undeveloped state; the ferry runs from Washington Island, which itself requires a ferry from the Door Peninsula. The layered island-hopping required to reach Pottawatomie gives it a remoteness that is unusual for a Wisconsin state park, and the limestone forest, beach, and clear Lake Michigan water make the effort worthwhile.

## 7. Apostle Islands Lighthouses, Lake Superior

The Apostle Islands National Lakeshore in the Chequamegon region of Lake Superior preserves six lighthouse stations on islands accessible by cruise boat and private kayak from Bayfield. The stations — on Devils Island, Raspberry Island, Sand Island, Michigan Island, Long Island (La Pointe), and Outer Island — represent the full range of Great Lakes lighthouse architecture from 1839 to 1891. Raspberry Island lighthouse, with its intact late nineteenth-century Craftsman dwelling and restored gardens, and Devils Island with its impressive skeletal tower on the lake's most exposed northern shore, are particularly notable. The National Park Service runs interpretive programmes at several islands in summer. Devils Island lighthouse experienced some of the most violent Lake Superior weather ever recorded; the waves generated by the 1905 Mataafa Storm destroyed the wooden walkway and nearly flooded the fog signal building.

## 8. Two Harbors Lighthouse, Minnesota — and the Duluth Entry

Though geographically in Minnesota, the Two Harbors and Duluth South Breakwater lights define the western approach to Lake Superior that Wisconsin mariners used to reach the iron ore and grain port complex at the western end of the lake. Wisconsin's own Superior Entry lights at the entry to Duluth-Superior harbour from the Wisconsin side are modest operational structures rather than heritage landmarks, but they mark the passage of the iron ore trade that made the Great Lakes lighthouse network necessary. The Duluth Canal Park museum, accessible from the Wisconsin side, interprets this industrial lighthouse heritage.

## 9. Kenosha Southport Lighthouse, Kenosha

The Southport lighthouse at Kenosha is a red brick conical tower completed in 1866, standing 21 metres on a rise above the harbour. It served the commercial and ferry traffic between Chicago, Milwaukee, and the Wisconsin lakefront ports for decades. The lighthouse is now part of Kenosha's lakefront park system; the tower has been restored and is open for seasonal tours. Kenosha's lakefront museum district, which includes a Civil War museum and a museum of transport, surrounds the lighthouse and makes it a hub of a well-developed heritage precinct.

## 10. Milwaukee Lighthouse, Milwaukee Pierhead

Milwaukee's active harbour light stands at the outer end of the North Pier, a red conical structure of the functional but recognisable type that marks harbour entrances throughout the Great Lakes. Milwaukee has had a lighthouse presence since 1838; the current active light is a working aid to navigation for the ferry traffic to Michigan and the commercial vessels serving this major Great Lakes port. The historic 1906 Milwaukee Breakwater lighthouse, further offshore, is a more substantial structure with a square integral design and stands at the northern arm of the harbour; it is maintained by the Milwaukee Lighthouse Association and can be toured by arrangement.

## Visiting Wisconsin's Lighthouses

The Door Peninsula is the epicentre of Wisconsin lighthouse tourism, with Cana Island, Eagle Bluff, Sherwood Point, and Baileys Harbor within an hour's drive of each other. The Apostle Islands require a day trip from Bayfield, with National Park Service boats running June through August; kayaking the islands with a licensed outfitter gives the most intimate lighthouse access but demands fitness and weather awareness — Lake Superior squalls move in quickly. Most Wisconsin lighthouse tower openings operate on Saturday and Sunday between Memorial Day and Labor Day, with longer hours in July and August. The Door County Maritime Museum in Sturgeon Bay coordinates tours and a dedicated Lighthouse Walk day each year.
