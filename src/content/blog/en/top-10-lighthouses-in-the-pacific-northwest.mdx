The Pacific Northwest coast, stretching from the northern California border through Oregon and Washington to the Strait of Juan de Fuca, is among the most dangerous shorelines in the world for navigation. The combination of powerful Pacific swells, dense coastal fog, the Columbia River bar — one of the most treacherous river entrances on earth — and the deep fjords of Puget Sound created an urgent demand for reliable lights from the moment American and European traders began working these waters in the early nineteenth century. The first Lighthouse Board survey of 1851 identified dozens of critical sites, and construction proceeded rapidly through the following decades. The towers that resulted range from elegant masonry structures on exposed headlands to iron-skeleton frames perched on wave-washed rocks offshore. ## 1. Tillamook Rock Lighthouse, Oregon

Tillamook Rock Lighthouse, one nautical mile offshore in the Pacific breakers north of Tillamook Head, was built between 1879 and 1881 under the supervision of chief engineer Major George Elliot — a construction project so arduous that the foreman sent to assess the site drowned before work could begin. The basalt rock was quarried in place while waves broke over the site; the final tower, 21 metres tall, rose from a base only 12 metres above high water in a region where storm seas regularly washed the lantern 48 metres above the ocean. Its first-order Fresnel lens flashed a white light visible 18 nautical miles, marking the approach to the Columbia River and the Oregon coast. Decommissioned in 1957, the rock was later used as a columbarium. No public access is possible; the tower is best seen from the headland trail at Ecola State Park.

## 2. Heceta Head Lighthouse, Oregon

Heceta Head Lighthouse, completed in 1894 on a dramatic 56-metre headland on the central Oregon coast, is frequently cited as the most photographed lighthouse in the United States. The white-painted brick tower, 21 metres tall, stands against a backdrop of forested cliffs with waves crashing on the rocks below — a composition that rewards any camera. The light — Fl W 10s, range 21 nautical miles — marks a section of coast where offshore reefs and the headland itself create navigational hazards for vessels working inshore of the shipping lanes. The lens is a first-order Fresnel, one of the few on the Oregon coast still in its original mounting. The adjacent keeper's quarters, a substantial Victorian residence, operates as a bed and breakfast managed by Oregon State Parks. The lighthouse is open for tours during summer months and is reached via a short trail from the parking area on US-101.

## 3. Cape Flattery Lighthouse, Washington

Cape Flattery, the northwestern tip of the contiguous United States, carries a lighthouse built in 1857 that marks the entrance to the Strait of Juan de Fuca, the gateway to Puget Sound and the entire inland sea of the Pacific Northwest. The 21-metre white brick tower sits on Tatoosh Island, a small rock separated from the cape by a short but rough crossing. The light — Fl(4) W 15s, range 17 nautical miles — covers the entrance to the Strait, where deep-draft vessels from across the Pacific funnel into a narrow passage. The lighthouse is operated by the Coast Guard and is not open to the public; Tatoosh Island is the traditional territory of the Makah Nation. The cape itself is accessible via a four-mile trail from the Makah tribal community of Neah Bay, passing through old-growth forest to arrive at dramatic clifftop viewpoints.

## 4. Yaquina Head Lighthouse, Oregon

The Yaquina Head Lighthouse, built in 1873 near Newport, Oregon, is the tallest lighthouse on the Oregon coast at 28 metres. Its white-painted brick tower, designed by engineer Paul Pelz under the supervision of the Lighthouse Board, stands on a prominent basalt headland thrusting into the Pacific. The light — Fl(2) W 20s, range 19 nautical miles — marks Yaquina Head and the approach to Yaquina Bay entrance, guiding vessels through the complex of offshore rocks and reefs that characterise this stretch of coast. The lighthouse is part of Yaquina Head Outstanding Natural Area, managed by the Bureau of Land Management, and is open for tours throughout the year. The surrounding headland supports a major seabird colony — common murres, tufted puffins, and Brandt's cormorants nest on the offshore rocks — making a visit rewarding well beyond the lighthouse itself.

## 5. Cape Disappointment Lighthouse, Washington

Cape Disappointment Lighthouse, completed in 1856 and thus the oldest active lighthouse on the Pacific coast north of California, guards the north shore of the Columbia River bar — historically one of the most lethal navigational hazards in the Americas. Named by fur trader John Meares in 1788 when he failed to find the river mouth he sought, the cape was the site of a preliminary lighthouse in 1853 before the current masonry tower, 20 metres tall, was established on the cliffs 53 metres above the ocean. The light — Fl(2) W 15s, range 18 nautical miles — works in conjunction with the nearby North Head Lighthouse to mark both sides of the river approach. The Cape Disappointment State Park encompasses both lighthouses and Lewis and Clark Interpretive Center. The Cape Disappointment tower is not publicly open to climb, but North Head (1898), 27 metres tall, can be toured.

## 6. Destruction Island Lighthouse, Washington

Destruction Island, a small rock island three miles offshore from the northern Washington coast, received its lighthouse in 1891 — the last major Pacific Northwest light to be built before the Spanish-American War interrupted federal construction programmes. The 36-metre white brick tower, designed by the Lighthouse Board's engineering office, was built to serve the deepwater shipping lanes along Washington's Olympic Peninsula coast, where Destruction Island's offshore position and the mainland cliffs created severe navigation hazards in the frequent fogs. The light — Fl W 5s, range 20 nautical miles — is still active. The island is occupied by the Coast Guard and is not open to the public; it can be seen from charter boats operating out of La Push. The Olympic Coast National Marine Sanctuary surrounds the island, making it a rich area for whale and sea-bird watching.

## 7. Grays Harbor Lighthouse, Washington

The Grays Harbor Lighthouse at Westport, Washington, completed in 1898, is the tallest lighthouse in Washington State at 36 metres. Its white-painted iron and brick construction was designed by Carl Leick for the Bureau of Lighthouses. The light — Fl(4) W 20s, range 18 nautical miles — marks the entrance to Grays Harbor, the second-largest deep-water port on the Washington coast. The lighthouse is managed by the Westport-South Beach Historical Society and is open for tours from spring through autumn. The surrounding area offers exceptional whale-watching; grey whales pass Westport in large numbers during their spring migration, and the lighthouse headland provides an elevated vantage point. The working shrimp and crabbing fleet in Westport harbour gives the visit a satisfying context of ongoing maritime activity.

## 8. North Head Lighthouse, Washington

North Head Lighthouse, built in 1898 to complement Cape Disappointment Lighthouse directly to its south, marks the approach to the Columbia River bar from the northward-bound lane of coastal traffic. The 27-metre brick tower stands on a headland 73 metres above the Pacific — one of the windiest inhabited spots on the coast, regularly recording gusts above 100 mph in winter storms. The light — Fl W 4s, range 22 nautical miles — is visible from well offshore on the Washington coast and works in tandem with Cape Disappointment to bracket the river bar approach. The lighthouse is part of Cape Disappointment State Park; the keeper's quarters are available as rental accommodation, one of the most atmospheric lighthouse stays on the Pacific coast. The tower is open for tours seasonally.

## 9. Point No Point Lighthouse, Washington

Point No Point Lighthouse, completed in 1879, is the oldest lighthouse in Puget Sound, built on the low sand spit at the northern entrance to Hood Canal and the route to Seattle. The name comes from George Vancouver's frustrated remark when what appeared to be a prominent cape repeatedly failed to materialise as he surveyed the Sound in 1792. The 14-metre white frame tower and accompanying keeper's cottage, both painted white, sit at the edge of the water in a setting of quiet beauty. The light — Oc W 4s, range 16 nautical miles — guides the heavy ferry and tanker traffic through the Sound. The lighthouse is managed by US Fish and Wildlife Service and is open for tours. The keeper's cottage operates as holiday accommodation through the non-profit that manages the site.

## 10. Admiralty Head Lighthouse, Washington

Admiralty Head Lighthouse on Whidbey Island, built in 1903 to replace an earlier structure, marks Admiralty Inlet, the channel linking the Strait of Juan de Fuca with central Puget Sound. The distinctive two-storey red-roofed white structure with an integral tower is one of the most architecturally pleasing on the Sound — more Spanish Colonial in character than the typical New England form. The lighthouse was decommissioned in 1927 when a buoy system took over the marking, but the structure was preserved within Fort Casey State Park. Today it operates as an interpretive center with exhibits on Puget Sound navigation history. The surrounding fortifications of Fort Casey, built at the same period to defend the approaches to Seattle, complement the lighthouse as a study in late nineteenth-century coastal defence.

The Pacific Northwest lighthouse coast is best explored in late summer and early autumn, when fog is less persistent, trails are dry, and coastal weather is relatively stable. US-101 connects the major Oregon lighthouses in a single coastal drive; Washington's towers require more deliberate planning, with several accessible only by ferry or forest road. The combination of dramatic headlands, old-growth coastal forest, and some of the best-preserved nineteenth-century lighthouse architecture in the country makes this one of the most rewarding lighthouse tours anywhere.
