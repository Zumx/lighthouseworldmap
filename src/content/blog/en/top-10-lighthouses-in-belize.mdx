Belize guards the longest barrier reef in the Western Hemisphere, a near-continuous wall of coral running some 290 kilometres down the Caribbean edge of Central America. Behind it lies a shallow lagoon studded with hundreds of mangrove cayes and sandbores; beyond it the seabed plummets into deep ocean. This is a coast defined entirely by the reef, and its lighthouses exist to mark the few safe passages through it and to warn ships off the coral that has wrecked vessels for centuries. From the famous beacon on Half Moon Caye to the harbour lights of Belize City, the lights of Belize are reef lights first and foremost.

## Half Moon Caye Light

The most celebrated light in the country stands on Half Moon Caye, a small crescent island on the southeastern rim of Lighthouse Reef Atoll, the outermost of Belize's three atolls and the one that contains the famous Great Blue Hole. The original lighthouse here dates from 1820, one of the oldest in the region, later supplemented by a steel skeletal tower. Half Moon Caye is now a protected natural monument, home to a celebrated colony of red-footed boobies, and the light watches over some of the clearest diving water in the Caribbean. Reaching it requires a boat trip of several hours from the mainland, which keeps it gloriously remote.

## English Caye Light

The main shipping channel into Belize City threads through the reef at English Caye, a tiny palm-topped island that carries the light marking the entrance to the deep-water route. Every vessel bound for the Belize City harbour passes close by English Caye, where pilots board the larger ships for the careful passage across the lagoon. The light here is a working aid of real importance, since the reef offers no other safe gap for big vessels for many miles in either direction.

## Baron Bliss Lighthouse, Belize City

In Belize City itself, at the tip of the Fort George peninsula, stands the Baron Bliss Lighthouse, beside the tomb of Henry Edward Ernest Victor Bliss, the eccentric English benefactor who left his fortune to Belize despite never setting foot ashore. Bliss died aboard his yacht in the harbour in 1926, and his bequest still funds public works across the country. The lighthouse and tomb together form one of the most visited landmarks in the capital, marking the harbour entrance and looking out toward the reef.

## Sittee Point Lighthouse

South of Belize City, near the village of Sittee River and the resort town of Hopkins, Sittee Point carries a light marking the approach to this stretch of the central coast. The waters here are shallow and reef-strewn, and the light guides the small craft serving the southern cayes and the Garifuna fishing communities of the mainland. It is a quieter, less-visited beacon, but characteristic of the minor lights that dot the Belizean shore between the major channels.

## Bugle Caye Light

Far to the south, off the coast near Placencia, Bugle Caye carries a light marking the southern reaches of the lagoon and the approaches used by boats heading to the southern cayes and toward the Gulf of Honduras. This is a remote corner of the Belizean coast, where the reef breaks into scattered cayes and the water shallows toward the mouths of mainland rivers. The light is a small but essential reference for the fishing and tour boats that work these waters.

## Mauger Caye Light

At the northern end of Turneffe Atoll, the largest of Belize's three atolls, Mauger Caye carries a light marking the northern tip of this vast ring of reef and mangrove. Turneffe is a wilderness of flats, channels and coral, famous among fly-fishers and divers, and the Mauger Caye light is the beacon that defines its northern edge. The name is pronounced locally as "major," and the slender tower is a landmark for the boats crossing between the atoll and the mainland.

## The Atolls and the Outer Reef

Belize is one of only a handful of places on Earth with true coral atolls outside the Pacific and Indian Oceans. Lighthouse Reef, Turneffe and Glover's Reef rise from deep water far offshore, each ringed by reef and dotted with cayes. The lights that mark them, including Half Moon Caye and Mauger Caye, are the outermost beacons of the country, the first landfall for vessels approaching from the open Caribbean and the last warning before the coral. Diving and fishing tourism now bring far more visitors to these lights than commercial shipping ever did.

## Why Belize Marks the Reef, Not the Shore

The logic of Belizean lighthouses is the opposite of most coasts. Elsewhere lights mark the dangerous land; here they mark the dangerous reef that lies offshore, while the actual coastline is low, swampy and approached only through marked channels. The barrier reef and the three atolls are the true hazard, and the most important lights stand on cayes far out to sea rather than on the mainland. This makes a Belizean lighthouse tour inseparable from a boat trip across the lagoon, and the journey to the light is as memorable as the light itself.

## Visiting the Reef Lights

Belize City is the natural base, with day trips and overnight charters running out to Lighthouse Reef, Turneffe and the cayes. Half Moon Caye is the highlight, usually combined with diving the Blue Hole or the famous walls of the atoll. The dry season from late November to April brings the calmest seas and best visibility, while the hurricane season from June to November can make the offshore passages rough or impossible. Whether you reach the lights by dive boat, sailing charter or fishing skiff, they reward the effort with some of the most pristine reef scenery anywhere.
