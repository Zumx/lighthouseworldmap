Cambodia is not the first country that comes to mind when you think of lighthouses, and that is precisely what makes its coast interesting. The shoreline runs for barely 440 kilometres along the Gulf of Thailand, hemmed between the Cardamom Mountains and a warm, shallow sea studded with islands. There are no granite towers from a great age of engineering here. Instead the coast carries a working set of modern beacons, harbour lights and channel markers, built since the 1990s to guide the container ships, fishing fleets and growing stream of tourist ferries that now use these waters. Reading the Cambodian coast as a lighthouse traveller means accepting that the story is recent, practical and still being written.

## A Short Coast in a Busy Gulf

The Gulf of Thailand is shallow, rarely deeper than eighty metres, and its Cambodian corner is shallower still. Reefs, sandbanks and low islands force shipping into defined channels, especially around the deep-water port at Sihanoukville, the only one of its kind in the country. This is where most of Cambodia's seaborne trade arrives, and the approach is marked by a sequence of leading lights and buoys rather than a single dominant tower. The practical consequence for a visitor is that Cambodian lights are clustered: you find them around ports and island groups, not strung evenly along an empty shore.

## Sihanoukville Port Lights

The harbour at Sihanoukville, known formally as Krong Preah Sihanouk, is the natural starting point. The port authority maintains a set of breakwater and pier lights marking the entrance channel into the container terminal. These are utilitarian steel and concrete structures, lit with modern LED optics and often solar-powered, but they are genuine aids to navigation working hard every night. From the waterfront at Ochheuteal or the older town pier you can watch the green and red channel markers blink as ships line up their approach. It is unglamorous, but it is the busiest piece of lit coast in the country.

## Koh Rong and Koh Rong Sanloem

Offshore from Sihanoukville lie the two islands that have transformed Cambodian tourism, Koh Rong and its quieter neighbour Koh Rong Sanloem. The ferry channels between the mainland and these islands are marked by small beacons and lit buoys, and the headlands carry minor lights to guide the fast boats that now run dozens of crossings a day. On Koh Rong Sanloem, the walk across the island from Saracen Bay to the lighthouse point on the western side is a popular half-day trek, ending at a small navigation light with a wide view back toward the Gulf. The light itself is modest; the reward is the vantage.

## Koh Tang and the Outer Islands

Far to the southwest, near the maritime boundary with Vietnam and Thailand, sit the remote outer islands of Koh Tang and Koh Prins. These are sparsely visited, with a complicated military history, but they mark the seaward edge of Cambodian waters and carry minor unmanned lights. For most travellers they are out of reach, but they matter to the navigational picture: they are the first and last Cambodian beacons a vessel sees on the open-water passage across the Gulf.

## Kep and the Southern Approaches

East of Sihanoukville, toward the Vietnamese border, the small resort town of Kep faces a cluster of islands including Koh Tonsay, the so-called Rabbit Island. The waters here are shallow and dotted with fishing stakes, and the approaches are marked by simple poles and lit beacons rather than towers. Kep is a gentle, faded place, and its lights reflect that: low-key markers serving local fishing boats and the small ferries to the islands.

## Krong Kaeb and the Vietnamese Frontier

The far eastern edge of the Cambodian coast runs up against the contested but now peaceful frontier with Vietnam, near the island of Phú Quốc just across the water. Vietnamese lighthouses dominate this stretch of the Gulf, and the names that recur in regional charts belong to that side of the border. The historic beacons of Hải đăng Hòn Dăm, Hải đăng An Thới and Hải đăng Dương Đông on and around Phú Quốc are the powerful lights of this corner of the Gulf, visible far into Cambodian waters and used by Cambodian fishermen as well. They are a reminder that a lighthouse map ignores political lines: light belongs to whoever can see it.

## Why Cambodia Has Few Towers

There is a historical reason the Cambodian coast lacks grand lighthouses. The country's maritime trade was minor under the French protectorate, which concentrated its port and lighthouse investment on Vietnam's far longer and busier coastline. The decades of war and isolation from the 1970s through the early 1990s halted almost all coastal infrastructure. By the time Cambodia rebuilt its maritime sector, the technology had moved on: small solar-powered LED beacons and electronic aids replaced the masonry towers that an earlier era would have built. The result is a coast that skipped the classic lighthouse age entirely.

## Visiting the Cambodian Coast

For the traveller, the pleasure here is not in ticking off historic towers but in experiencing a tropical working coast. Base yourself in Sihanoukville or the calmer Otres Beach, take the ferry out to Koh Rong Sanloem, and walk to the island light. Hire a longtail to nose among the fishing markers off Kep. The best season runs from November to April, the dry monsoon, when the Gulf is calm and visibility is good; the wet season from May to October brings heavy rain and rougher water. Throughout, the lights you see are modern, modest and entirely real, marking a coast that is busier each year.
