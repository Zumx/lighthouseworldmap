A name on a chart looks settled and official, as though it had always been there. In fact the name of a lighthouse usually predates the lighthouse itself, sometimes by centuries. The light is built on a hazard, and the hazard already had a name — given by the fishermen who avoided it, the sailors who were wrecked on it, the local people who saw it from the shore, or the cartographers who first set it down on a map. When the engineers arrived to raise a tower, they inherited the name along with the rock. Tracing those names is a way of reading the coastal landscape and the long human relationship with it, written in geography, religion, language, and disaster.

## Named for the Place It Marks

The simplest and commonest source of a lighthouse name is the geographical feature it stands on or marks. A cape, a head, a point, a rock, a reef, a sandbank — each is a navigational landmark in its own right, named long before any light, and the light takes the name of the place. Cape Hatteras off North Carolina, the most dangerous stretch of the American Atlantic coast, gave its name to the light that warns of its shoals. The Lizard, the southernmost point of mainland Britain, lends its name to the Lizard lighthouse on the Cornish cliffs.

The pattern repeats everywhere a coast has been charted. Point Reyes in California, Cape Otway in Australia, Cabo de São Vicente at the southwestern tip of Portugal — in each the lighthouse simply adopted the existing name of the headland. The name describes the geography for the mariner, which is precisely what a navigational mark should do. A sailor reading the light list does not need to know who named the cape; he needs to know that the light marks that cape and no other.

## The Hazard Itself

Many of the most famous lighthouse names belong not to capes but to the isolated rocks and reefs that the towers were built to mark. These names are often older and stranger, surviving from the languages of earlier coastal peoples. The Fastnet, the lone rock off the southwest coast of Ireland that gives its name to the famous yacht race, derives from an Old Norse phrase, Hvasstein-ey, meaning something like sharp-tooth isle — a name left by the Norse sailors who knew the Irish coast. The rock was a notorious danger long before the great granite tower rose on it.

The Eddystone rocks off Plymouth, scene of the most famous succession of lighthouses in history, take a name that likely refers to the swirling, eddying tide-races around them. The Bell Rock off the Scottish east coast, according to a persistent tradition, was named for a warning bell said to have been placed on it by an abbot of Arbroath centuries before Robert Stevenson built his tower. The Skerryvore, off the Scottish Hebrides, combines a Gaelic word for a rocky reef with one meaning great — the great reef. Each name carries a warning folded into its etymology.

## Saints and the Sacred

The medieval and early-modern coast was a deeply religious landscape, and a great many coastal features — and therefore lighthouses — carry the names of saints. The naming reflected both genuine devotion and the practical fact that headlands, harbours, and islands were frequently associated with a local saint, a chapel, a hermitage, or a pilgrimage route. The lighthouse, raised to save lives, sat comfortably within that tradition of marking the dangerous coast with the sacred.

The examples are countless. Saint Catherine's lighthouse on the Isle of Wight stands near the site of a medieval oratory dedicated to that saint, where a light was maintained for the souls of the drowned. The great French light at the Île Vierge — the Virgin's Isle — off Brittany takes its name from the island's religious associations. Across the Catholic coasts of France, Spain, Portugal, and Italy, the calendar of saints is written into the names of the points and islands that the lighthouses now mark, a layer of meaning older than the engineering.

## Written in Shipwreck

Some lighthouse names commemorate disaster directly. A reef or shoal that claimed a notable ship might take that ship's name, and the light built afterwards would inherit it. Coastlines with long records of wrecks accumulated a vocabulary of place names that read like a casualty list — the Manacles off the Cornish coast, whose name may derive from a Cornish phrase for church-stones but which became synonymous with the ships lost on them; countless rocks and points around the world bearing the names of the vessels that ended on them.

The relationship runs both ways. A lighthouse is, after all, a monument to the wrecks that preceded it, since a light is generally built only where enough ships have been lost to justify the expense. The name that the light carries is frequently the name of the thing that sank them. To read a chart of any heavily trafficked and dangerous coast is to read, in the names alone, a compressed history of maritime loss — the very losses that the lights were finally built to prevent.

## Language Layered on Language

The names of coastal hazards preserve the languages of everyone who has used the coast, layered one over another. The British and Irish coasts carry Brythonic, Gaelic, Old Norse, and English names side by side, each marking the presence of a people who fished or sailed or settled there. The Norse names cluster where the Vikings raided and settled; the Gaelic names hold in the Hebrides and the west of Ireland; the Cornish and Welsh names survive on their respective coasts.

Elsewhere the same layering tells a different colonial or migratory history. The North American coast carries Indigenous names alongside Spanish, French, Dutch, and English ones, each reflecting who charted and named that stretch. A lighthouse name is therefore a small linguistic fossil, preserving a word from a language that may no longer be spoken in the place, fixed in print on the chart and in the light list because the rock it described was dangerous enough to be worth naming and, eventually, worth lighting.

## How the Name Becomes Official

The transition from local usage to official chart name happened through the hydrographic offices and lighthouse authorities that surveyed the coasts and published the charts and light lists. When a survey vessel charted a coast, its officers recorded the names in local use, and those names, once printed, became fixed. Where no name existed, or where the surveyors could not learn the local one, they sometimes coined a new name — after the survey ship, an officer, or a notable feature — and that coinage, too, became permanent once printed.

The lighthouse authorities — Trinity House in England and Wales, the Northern Lighthouse Board in Scotland, the Commissioners of Irish Lights, the United States Lighthouse Service and its successors — adopted these chart names for their stations, and the names entered the official light lists that mariners consulted. From that point the name was settled. The cape, the rock, the saint, or the lost ship that gave the light its name might be half-forgotten ashore, but it remained fixed on the chart, repeated by every navigator who took a bearing on the light.
