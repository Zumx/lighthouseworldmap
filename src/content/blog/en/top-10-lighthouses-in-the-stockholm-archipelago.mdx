The Stockholm archipelago is one of the largest in the world, a labyrinth of some thirty thousand islands, islets, and skerries spread across the Baltic east of the Swedish capital. From the wooded inner islands close to the city to the bare, wave-washed rocks of the outer fringe, it is a seascape that has demanded navigation skill for centuries, for the route to Stockholm winds among countless hazards before reaching open water. The lights of the archipelago, some perched on lonely outer skerries and others marking the great fairways used by ferries and cruise ships today, trace the sea roads that have linked Stockholm to the Baltic and the wider world. They guard a coast of summer cottages, fishing harbours, and the steamboat traditions that still define Swedish summers.

## Landsort, the Oldest Light

At the southern edge of the archipelago, on the long narrow island of Öja, stands Landsort, Sweden's oldest lighthouse still in service. A light has burned here since the seventeenth century, marking the southern entrance to the fairways leading toward Stockholm. The present round stone tower, painted in distinctive bands, rises above a small community of former pilots and keepers. The island, reachable by boat in summer, is a noted spot for bird migration and offers some of the most exposed and atmospheric coastal scenery in the whole archipelago.

## Söderarm at the Northern Gateway

In the outer northern archipelago, the lighthouse of Söderarm marks one of the main approaches from the open Baltic toward the fairway leading to Stockholm and the historic naval town of Norrtälje. The light stands on a small rocky island among a scatter of outer skerries, and the surrounding station, with its keepers' houses, now offers simple accommodation to visitors. Söderarm guarded the strategic approaches to the capital and the route used by shipping bound for the Gulf of Bothnia.

## Almagrundet, the Offshore Tower

Out in the open Baltic east of the archipelago, the caisson lighthouse of Almagrundet marked a dangerous shoal on the deep-water approach to Stockholm. Built on a sunken concrete base far from any land, it was one of the offshore lights designed to withstand the pressure of winter ice. The original lightship that once marked the shoal was replaced by the fixed tower, a lonely sentinel on the route followed by the great Baltic ferries. It speaks to the engineering needed to light shoals that lie miles from the nearest rock.

## Sandhamn and the Sailing Fairway

The light at Sandhamn marks the entrance to one of the most famous fairways in the archipelago, the route long used by sailing ships approaching Stockholm and today the heart of Swedish yachting. The village of Sandhamn, on the island of Sandön, has been a customs and pilot station for centuries and is now a celebrated sailing centre, host to classic regattas. The light guides vessels through the narrow approach where the open Baltic meets the sheltered waters of the archipelago, a passage thronged with boats every summer.

## Grönskär, the Outer Light

On a bare rocky island in the outer archipelago east of Sandhamn stands Grönskär, one of the oldest lights on this coast, sometimes called the queen of the Baltic. The tall stone tower, dating from the eighteenth century, marked the seaward approach to the Sandhamn fairway and once burned coal and later oil. The island, treeless and wind-scoured, is a striking example of the outer skerry landscape, and the lighthouse is open to visitors who make the boat trip out in summer.

## Huvudskär, the Far Outer Skerries

At the southeastern edge of the archipelago, the small light at Huvudskär marks a cluster of remote outer skerries that were once home to a fishing community. The bare rocks, washed by the open Baltic, supported seasonal fishermen who lived in tiny cottages clustered against the wind. The light guided both the fishermen and passing coastal traffic, and the islands today are a protected nature reserve and a haven for seabirds, among the most remote and evocative places in the whole archipelago.

## Svenska Högarna, the Eastern Outpost

The lighthouse of Svenska Högarna stands on the easternmost inhabited islands of the Stockholm archipelago, the last Swedish land before the open Baltic stretches away toward Finland and the Baltic states. The light and its weather station mark a lonely outpost where keepers and meteorologists long kept watch over the eastern approaches. The islands are a nature reserve rich in seals and seabirds, and the station, exposed to every gale from the open sea, embodies the isolation of life at the archipelago's edge.

## Gillöga and the Inner Skerries

Among the scattered skerries of the outer archipelago, the beacon at Gillöga marks one of the smaller groups of islands that dot the seaward approaches. These outer skerry groups, once seasonally inhabited by fishermen, are now uninhabited nature reserves, their lights and beacons guiding the pleasure craft and occasional commercial traffic that thread the outer waters. They preserve the look of the archipelago as it was before the age of summer cottages, bare and wild.

## Stora Nassa, the Wild Archipelago

Stora Nassa is one of the most beautiful and intricate of the outer skerry groups, a maze of hundreds of tiny islands and rocks marked by small beacons. Though it has no great tower, the cluster of navigation marks here is essential to the boats that explore this wild and protected corner of the archipelago. The islands, scoured smooth by ice and waves, are a celebrated destination for sailors and kayakers seeking the untouched outer reaches, where the only shelter is in the lee of bare granite.

## Revengegrundet and the City Approaches

Closer to the city, in the inner fairways, lights such as Revengegrundet mark the channels that carry the heavy traffic of ferries, archipelago boats, and cruise ships toward Stockholm itself. These inner lights, less dramatic than the lonely outer towers, are no less vital, threading the constant stream of vessels through the narrow inner waters past wooded islands and summer villas. They mark the final approach to one of the most beautiful capital cities in the world, reached entirely by sea through the archipelago.
