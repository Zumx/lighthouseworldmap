On the northern end of Anastasia Island, across the inlet from the old Spanish town of St Augustine, a black-and-white spiral tower climbs 50 metres into the Florida sky. Its barber-pole stripes are among the most recognisable daymarks in the United States, and the light it carries still guides vessels into the harbour of the oldest continuously occupied European settlement in the country. The St Augustine Lighthouse is also, by reputation, one of the most haunted, a place where the line between maritime history and ghost story has long since blurred.

## The Oldest Port in America

St Augustine was founded by the Spanish in 1565, more than half a century before the Pilgrims reached Plymouth, and from the beginning its survival depended on the sea. A watchtower stood on Anastasia Island in the Spanish colonial period, and the site has marked the inlet in one form or another for centuries. The shifting sandbars at the harbour entrance made it a treacherous approach, and a proper coastal light was a long-standing need.

The first American lighthouse here, built on the foundations of the old Spanish watchtower, was lit in the 1820s after Florida passed to the United States. But the sea that the tower was built to serve also undermined it; coastal erosion gradually ate away the shoreline until the old tower was in danger of toppling into the water. By the 1870s it was clear that a new lighthouse, set back on higher and safer ground, was needed to replace it. The original tower finally collapsed into the sea in 1880, not long after its successor began work.

## Building the Spiral Tower

The present St Augustine Lighthouse was designed under the United States Lighthouse Board and completed in 1874. It stands 50 metres tall, built of brick made in Alabama and brought down the coast by ship, with its distinctive black-and-white spiral and a red lantern room at the summit. A first-order Fresnel lens, manufactured in Paris, was installed to throw the beam far out over the Atlantic; that original lens, with its hundreds of hand-ground glass prisms, remains in place and operational today, one of the relatively few first-order lenses still lit in an American tower.

The keeper's house, a handsome brick dwelling, was built nearby to house the head keeper and his assistants and their families. The station was a small community, responsible for the light, the oil store, the cisterns, and later the fog signal. The 219 steps of the spiral staircase inside the tower had to be climbed several times a day to tend the lamp, trim the wick in the days of oil, and keep the great lens spotless.

## The Daughters of the Builder

The most famous of St Augustine's legends grows directly from its construction. During the building of the new tower in the early 1870s, the superintendent of works, Hezekiah Pittee, brought his family to live at the site. His children played around the construction works, and among the amusements was a small cart that ran on rails down to the water, used to carry building supplies from the supply boats.

In July 1873 the cart, with several children aboard, ran out of control down the track and into the bay. Two of Pittee's young daughters, Mary and Eliza, drowned, along with a local girl. The tragedy cast a long shadow over the station and the town. The girls are often dressed in period photographs in blue and white, and to this day reports from the lighthouse describe the laughter of children, the figure of a girl in a blue dress on the tower stairs, and footsteps where no one walks. Whatever one makes of such accounts, the deaths were real, and the grief behind the legend is genuine.

## A Haunted Reputation

St Augustine has built a substantial part of its modern identity on its ghost stories, and the lighthouse is at the centre of them. Beyond the Pittee daughters, the legends include a keeper who fell to his death while painting the tower, and a shadowy figure said to haunt the basement of the keeper's house. The station has been the subject of paranormal investigations and television programmes, and it runs popular evening ghost tours that draw visitors from across the country.

It is worth being clear that these are stories, not history, and the lighthouse itself presents them as folklore rather than fact. But the popularity of the haunted reputation has had a real and positive effect: it brought attention, visitors, and funds to a structure that might otherwise have decayed. The ghost tours and the fascination they generate have helped pay for the careful restoration of the tower and the keeper's house, turning legend into a tool of preservation.

## Restoration and the Museum

By the late twentieth century the St Augustine Lighthouse was in poor condition. The keeper's house had been gutted by fire in 1970, the tower was weathered, and the station's future was uncertain after the light was automated and the Coast Guard withdrew its personnel. A local non-profit organisation, the Junior Service League of St Augustine, took on the restoration, raising funds and undertaking a painstaking campaign to bring the buildings back.

Today the station operates as the St Augustine Lighthouse and Maritime Museum, one of the best-run lighthouse museums in the country. The keeper's house holds exhibits on the history of the light and its keepers, and on the maritime archaeology of the surrounding waters, where the museum's own research programme has located and studied shipwrecks in the inlet and offshore. Visitors can climb all 219 steps to the gallery for a sweeping view over Anastasia Island, the inlet, and the old town across the water.

## A Coast of Florida Lights

St Augustine is one of a chain of distinctive Florida lighthouses, each with its own daymark and history. Down the Atlantic coast stand other tall brick towers, and across the state on the Gulf coast a different tradition of lights guards the shallow, island-fringed shore. Among the Gulf lights are the Boca Grande Lighthouse and the Gasparilla Island Lighthouse, both on the barrier islands of Florida's south-west coast near Charlotte Harbor, where the gentle waters and white sand draw a very different kind of visitor than the surf-pounded Atlantic.

Each of these towers tells part of the story of how a low, sandy, hurricane-prone coast was made navigable. St Augustine, with its great first-order lens and its centuries of history reaching back to the Spanish, stands at the head of that tradition, the light of the nation's oldest port, watching over an inlet that ships have used for more than four hundred years.

## Visiting St Augustine

The lighthouse is open to visitors year-round and sits within easy reach of the historic centre of St Augustine, itself a place of narrow streets, Spanish colonial architecture, and the great stone fortress of the Castillo de San Marcos guarding the harbour. A visit combines the climb up the spiral tower, the exhibits in the keeper's house, and the wooded grounds where the live oaks drip with Spanish moss.

For those drawn to the ghost stories, the evening tours offer the tower after dark, with its legends recounted on the very stairs where the figure in blue is said to walk. For those who prefer the daylight history, the museum's archaeology and its working first-order lens are reward enough. Either way, the black-and-white spiral remains one of the defining images of the Florida coast, as recognisable from the sea today as it was to mariners a century and a half ago.
