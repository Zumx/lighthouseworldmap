Venezuela presents a navigational geography unlike any other on the Caribbean rim. Its coast runs for some 2,800 kilometres along the southern edge of the Caribbean Sea, embracing the vast brackish basin of Lake Maracaibo, the offshore archipelagos of Los Roques and Las Aves, the long peninsulas of Paraguaná and Araya, and the muddy delta where the Orinoco empties into the Atlantic at Barima. This is a coast defined by oil. The tankers that load at Maracaibo, Puerto La Cruz and Punta de Palmas depend on a chain of beacons that mark sandbars, coral heads and the narrow dredged channels through which the deepest-draught vessels must thread. The lights here are working tools of one of the world's great petroleum economies, and they range from rusting iron skeletons to elegant masonry towers of the early twentieth century.

## 1. Faro Punta Brava, Lake Maracaibo Bar

The bar at the mouth of Lake Maracaibo is the single most important navigational obstacle in Venezuela, and the lights that mark it carry an outsized responsibility. The dredged channel through the bar allows tankers to reach the oilfields of the lake basin, and the beacons along it must be unmistakable in the heavy haze that hangs over the Gulf of Venezuela. Faro Punta Brava marks the western approach, a slender lattice tower showing a white flash whose range carries across the shallow, silt-laden water to vessels making the difficult turn from the Caribbean into the channel. The constant traffic of laden tankers makes this one of the busiest pieces of marked water in the southern Caribbean.

## 2. Faro de Punta Macolla, Paraguaná Peninsula

At the far northern tip of the Paraguaná Peninsula, Punta Macolla is the closest point of the Venezuelan mainland to the open Caribbean and to the islands of Aruba and Curaçao. The lighthouse here marks the headland that vessels round when sailing between the Gulf of Venezuela and the central Caribbean. Paraguaná is a place of stark beauty: arid, wind-scoured, fringed with salt flats and the dunes of Médanos de Coro to the south. The peninsula's relentless trade wind has made it a centre for kitesurfing, and the lighthouse stands above a coast where the sea is rarely still.

## 3. Faro Cabo San Román, Falcón State

Cabo San Román is the northernmost point of mainland Venezuela, the tip of the Paraguaná Peninsula thrusting toward the Dutch islands. The lighthouse marks a cape long feared by mariners for the confused seas where the Gulf of Venezuela meets the Caribbean swell. From the cape on a clear day the silhouette of Aruba is visible across roughly 25 kilometres of water, and the light here forms one corner of the triangle of beacons — with the Dutch lights of Curaçao and the mainland stations — that governs all shipping bound for Maracaibo. The surrounding landscape is desert, with cacti and the wind-bent divi-divi trees that lean permanently away from the trade wind.

## 4. Faro Adícora, Paraguaná

On the windward eastern shore of Paraguaná, the small fishing and resort town of Adícora carries a modest lighthouse above a coast of long beaches and steady wind. Adícora is one of the oldest settlements on the peninsula, its pastel colonial houses facing a sea that delivers some of the most reliable wind in the Caribbean. The light serves the local fishing fleet and the small craft that work the coast between Paraguaná and the mainland at Coro, the historic colonial capital of Falcón State and a UNESCO World Heritage site for its earthen architecture.

## 5. Faro Fortín Solano, Puerto Cabello

Above the historic port of Puerto Cabello stands the Fortín Solano, an eighteenth-century Spanish fortress that crowns the hill overlooking one of the finest natural harbours in the Caribbean. A navigational light is associated with this commanding position, marking the approach to a port that was once the principal naval base of Spanish Venezuela. Puerto Cabello remains a major commercial harbour, and the colonial centre below the fort, with its colourful waterfront and the narrow streets of the old town, preserves the atmosphere of the eighteenth century. The fort and its viewpoint are reached by a steep road and reward the climb with a panorama over the whole bay.

## 6. Faro de Patos, Isla de Patos

Isla de Patos lies in the Gulf of Paria, in the narrow waters between Venezuela and Trinidad, close to the great delta of the Orinoco. The lighthouse on this small, low island marks the approaches to the gulf and the channels used by vessels bound for the eastern Venezuelan ports and the oil terminals of the delta region. The waters here are stained brown by the immense outflow of the Orinoco, and the island sits at a strategic point on the boundary between Venezuelan and Trinidadian waters. Patos was the subject of a long border dispute resolved in Venezuela's favour in the early twentieth century.

## 7. Faro de Barima, Orinoco Delta

At Punta Barima, where the Orinoco finally reaches the Atlantic at the southeastern extremity of Venezuela, a light marks the entrance to the principal navigable mouth of the great river. This is the frontier with Guyana, a region of mangrove, mudbank and shifting channel where the river deposits its enormous burden of sediment. The Barima light guides the river traffic and the coastal vessels that work this remote and little-visited corner of the country. The delta behind it is one of the largest wetland systems in South America, home to the Warao people whose stilt villages gave Venezuela its name — little Venice.

## 8. Faro Los Frailes, Eastern Islands

The Islas Los Frailes form a small uninhabited archipelago northeast of Margarita Island, a cluster of rocky islets rising from clear, deep water. The light here marks the islands for vessels sailing the eastern approaches and for the diving boats that visit what is one of the finest snorkelling and diving locations in Venezuela. Los Frailes is reached by boat excursion from Margarita, and the contrast between the barren rock above water and the abundant reef life below makes it a celebrated destination. The beacon stands guard over a place where the sea is extraordinarily transparent.

## 9. Faro Cayo Borracho, Morrocoy

In the Morrocoy National Park on the Falcón coast, a maze of mangrove cays, sandbanks and shallow turquoise lagoons spreads along one of the most beautiful stretches of the Venezuelan mainland. A light at Cayo Borracho marks the seaward edge of this labyrinth, where the deeper Caribbean water gives way to the intricate channels between the cays. Morrocoy is a place for small craft, for the boats that ferry visitors to white-sand cays like Cayo Sombrero, and the navigational marks here serve a coast where local knowledge matters more than any chart.

## 10. Faro Choroní, Aragua Coast

On the central coast at the foot of the Henri Pittier National Park, the colonial village of Choroní and its neighbouring fishing harbour of Puerto Colombia sit beneath forested mountains that plunge almost directly into the sea. The light here serves the small fishing fleet that works this dramatic coast, reached from the interior by a celebrated winding mountain road through cloud forest. Choroní is a place of cacao plantations, Afro-Venezuelan drumming traditions, and a sea that crashes against a coast where the Cordillera de la Costa meets the Caribbean. The setting is among the most spectacular in the country.
