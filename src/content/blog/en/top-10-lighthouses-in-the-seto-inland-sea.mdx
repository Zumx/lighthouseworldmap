The Seto Inland Sea — Seto Naikai — is the long, sheltered body of water that lies cradled between Honshu, Shikoku and Kyushu, scattered with more than three thousand islands and famous for a calm that earned it the name of Japan's Mediterranean. When the country reopened to foreign trade in the 1860s, this maze of channels carried much of the nation's coastal traffic, and it needed lighting urgently. The man who lit it was a young Scottish engineer, Richard Henry Brunton, hired in 1868 and remembered ever since as the Father of Japanese Lighthouses. His squat granite towers, built to survive earthquakes, still stand on the headlands and reefs of the Setouchi region. Here are ten lights worth seeking out across this island-studded sea.

## 1. Nabeshima Lighthouse, Sakaide

Standing on tiny Nabeshima island off Sakaide in Kagawa Prefecture, this is one of Brunton's masterpieces and arguably the most beautiful stone lighthouse in Japan. Completed in 1872, it is built of finely dressed local granite in pale grey, a round tower roughly ten metres tall whose masonry has weathered a century and a half almost unmarked. The light sits beneath the great spans of the Seto Ōhashi, the Great Seto Bridge, so that trains and traffic now pass overhead while the old lamp keeps its watch below. Its white flash remains an active aid to navigation, and the tower is registered as an important cultural property — a rare honour for a working lighthouse.

## 2. Esaki Lighthouse, Awaji Island

On the northern tip of Awaji Island, guarding the Akashi Strait that funnels shipping toward Osaka and Kobe, Esaki is among the oldest stone lighthouses in Japan. Brunton designed it and it first shone in April 1871, a stocky granite tower about eight metres high set on a green headland. Damaged in the great Hanshin earthquake of 1995, it was carefully repaired and continues to flash today. From its bluff you look across the strait toward the soaring Akashi Kaikyō Bridge, once the longest suspension span in the world, a striking juxtaposition of the first generation of Japanese coastal lights and the last word in modern engineering.

## 3. Wadamisaki Lighthouse, Kobe

Wadamisaki is a curiosity: a hexagonal iron lighthouse rather than a stone one, and one of the earliest Western-style metal towers erected in Japan. Built in the early Meiji years to mark the approach to the new treaty port of Kobe, it once stood at the harbour entrance and was later moved and preserved. Painted in its historic dark tones, the riveted iron plates and slender lantern room speak of the moment when Japan imported the engineering of Britain wholesale. It is no longer the working harbour light, but it survives as a monument to the opening of Kobe to the world.

## 4. Tsurushima Lighthouse

Out in the eastern reaches of the inland sea, the small light on Tsurushima marks one of the narrow passages that thread between the islands toward the Kii Channel. Like its companions it is a modest white tower, unmanned for decades, its flashing character timed so that a coasting captain could tell it apart from the neighbouring lights without consulting a chart by lamplight. Reaching it means a local ferry and a walk, and few foreign visitors ever do — which is precisely its charm for those who want the quieter corners of Setouchi.

## 5. Tomogashima Lights

Tomogashima is actually a cluster of small islands at the mouth of the Kii Channel, off the Wakayama coast, where the inland sea meets the Pacific approaches. The lighthouse here, another early Brunton-era stone tower, sits among the overgrown brick batteries and tunnels of a former Meiji military fortress. The combination of a pale granite light and ruined red-brick fortifications swallowed by greenery has made the island a cult destination. Ferries run from Kada, and the round tower still marks the strategic narrows where the sheltered sea opens to the ocean swell.

## 6. Ōmishima and Ōkunoshima Lights

The waters around Ōmishima, the largest island of the Geiyo group, are stitched together today by the Shimanami Kaidō, the celebrated cycling expressway that hops island to island between Onomichi and Imabari on a chain of soaring bridges. Small navigational lights guard the channels here, while nearby Ōkunoshima — once a secret poison-gas works, now overrun with tame rabbits — carries its own modest beacon. Cyclists crossing the Tatara and Kurushima bridges pass directly above these lights, looking down on the swirling tidal streams that make the Kurushima Strait one of the trickiest passages in Japan.

## 7. Megijima Light, near Takamatsu

Just off Takamatsu, the small island of Megijima — long associated with the folk tale of Momotarō and his demon-fighting — carries a tidy white light at its harbour. The crossing from Takamatsu takes only twenty minutes, and the beacon is the first thing returning ferry passengers see at dusk. It marks the busy approaches to one of Shikoku's main ports, where ferries fan out to the art islands of Naoshima and Teshima and to the countless rocks of the central sea.

## 8. Shōdoshima Area Lights

Shōdoshima, the second-largest island in the inland sea and famous for its olive groves and soy sauce, is ringed by small lights guarding its ferry routes and its narrow Angel Road sandbar. The waters here are studded with reefs, and the lights mark the safe lanes between Himeji, Okayama and Takamatsu. None is a grand tower, but together they form the kind of working constellation of minor lights that has always kept the Setouchi ferries running safely through fog and dusk.

## 9. Mutsure-shima Lighthouse

Far to the west, near the Kanmon Strait that separates Honshu from Kyushu, Mutsure-shima carries another of Brunton's granite lights, completed in the early 1870s to guide ships into the strategic narrows toward Shimonoseki and Moji. It is a robust round tower built to take the weather of this exposed western gateway, where the inland sea drains out toward the Sea of Japan and the open Pacific. Its keepers once lived in some of the loneliest quarters on the coast.

## 10. Hesaki Lighthouse, Kitakyushu

Guarding the Kyushu side of the Kanmon Strait at Kitakyushu, Hesaki is among the oldest lights in the region, marking the tide-torn channel where currents run hard enough to reverse with the changing water. It anchors the western end of the inland sea just as Esaki anchors the east, and between the two lie the hundreds of islands and the calm, glittering waters that gave Seto Naikai its reputation as the gentlest sea in Japan.

## Visiting the Lights

The inland sea is best explored slowly, by ferry. Onomichi is the classic gateway: from here the Shimanami Kaidō runs south by bicycle, and ferries thread out to the smaller islands. Takamatsu on the Shikoku side is the hub for the art islands and for Shōdoshima. Trains cross the Seto Ōhashi past Nabeshima, while Awaji and Esaki are reached by road from Kobe. Many of the Brunton towers stand on small islands needing a local boat and a short walk, so allow time and check the seasonal ferry timetables; spring and autumn give the clearest light and the calmest water.
