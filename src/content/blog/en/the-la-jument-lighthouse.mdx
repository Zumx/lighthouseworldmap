Off the island of Ouessant, at the wild western edge of Brittany where the Iroise Sea meets the Atlantic, a grey granite tower stands alone in some of the most violent water in Europe. La Jument is one of the great rock lights of France, but it owes its worldwide fame to a single photograph. In December 1989, the photographer Jean Guichard, hovering in a helicopter during a fierce storm, captured the moment a colossal wave reared up and swallowed the tower whole, just as the keeper opened the door at the base to look out. The image, with the tiny figure framed in the doorway and the green wall of water curling overhead, became the most famous lighthouse photograph ever taken.

## The killing ground of Ouessant

The waters around Ouessant, the Fromveur passage and the wider Iroise Sea, are among the most dangerous on the European coast. Strong tidal currents, submerged reefs, frequent fog and the full unbroken force of Atlantic swell combine to make this a notorious ships' graveyard. For centuries vessels rounding the corner of Brittany on the great trade routes were lost here, and the chain of lights built to guard the approaches, of which La Jument is one, was a direct response to repeated catastrophe.

La Jument in particular was prompted by the loss of ships on the rocks south of Ouessant. Its construction was made possible in part by a bequest from a wealthy traveller who, having survived a frightening passage here, left money specifically to build a light. The tower was raised on the Ar Gazec rock between 1904 and 1911, a brutally difficult build given the exposure of the site, where workable days were few and the sea could sweep the rock at any time.

## Building on the edge of the Atlantic

Constructing La Jument was a feat of endurance. The rock could only be worked in calm spells, which on this coast are rare and short, so the building campaign stretched over years. The granite tower rises to a substantial height above its base, with the keeper's quarters and service rooms stacked within the cylindrical shaft in the classic manner of the offshore rock light. Because it was built quickly under pressure of a deadline tied to the bequest, the foundations were not as deep as ideal, and the tower would later prove to flex and shudder alarmingly under the heaviest seas.

In the great storms, the whole structure vibrated, crockery shattered, and the keepers described the tower swaying so violently that they feared it might fail. Reinforcing tie-rods and stays were later added to brace the building against the pounding. Life inside during a storm meant enduring the deafening boom of waves striking the granite, the structure trembling around you, with no possibility of leaving until the sea relented.

## The photograph that made it famous

The events of December 1989 turned La Jument into a global icon. During a severe storm, keeper Theodore Malgorn was alone in the tower waiting to be relieved when Jean Guichard flew out by helicopter to photograph the lighthouse battered by the seas. Hearing the aircraft, Malgorn opened the door at the base of the tower, believing it might be his relief, and stepped to the threshold to look out, unaware that an enormous wave was rearing behind the tower.

Guichard's shutter caught the instant the wave engulfed almost the entire tower, with Malgorn a small figure in the doorway. The keeper, realising his danger at the last moment, leapt back inside and slammed the door; the wave broke over the structure but he survived. The photograph won international awards and was reproduced around the world, becoming the defining image of the lonely, dangerous life of the offshore lighthouse keeper. It captures in a single frame everything that made stations like La Jument legendary.

## Automation and legacy

Like its sister rock lights, La Jument was eventually automated, and the keepers who had endured its shuddering storms were withdrawn. The light now operates unmanned, its beam still sweeping the deadly waters off Ouessant. Yet the human story persists, kept alive above all by Guichard's photograph, which continues to introduce new generations to the extraordinary world of the rock light keepers.

La Jument stands today as a monument to a particular kind of courage. The men who served here lived for weeks at a time in a trembling granite cylinder surrounded by some of the angriest sea on Earth, tending a light on which countless lives depended. The tower endures, battered but unbroken, a symbol of the long French tradition of building lights where building seemed impossible.

## The legendary lights of the Iroise

La Jument belongs to a remarkable family of rock and island lights guarding the Iroise Sea and the western approaches to Brittany. Nearby stand the Phare de Nividic, the most westerly of the Ouessant lights and itself a marvel of difficult construction, and across the waters the Phare de Pen-Men on the island of Groix. Along the indented coast lie smaller lights such as the Phare de Port Manec'h and the Phare du Millier, while the Phare de Kergadec and pier lights like the Mole Nord, Mole Ouest Lechiagat and Mole Sud guard the fishing harbours.

Within this company La Jument is the most famous of all, its silhouette known to people who have never heard the names of its neighbours, simply because of one storm-lashed photograph. To see it standing in the Fromveur, taking the full weight of an Atlantic gale, is to understand both the danger of these waters and the audacity of those who chose to light them.
