Ecuador's coast runs for some 2,200 kilometres along the eastern Pacific, straddling the equator from which the country takes its name. It is a varied shore: the wide, muddy estuary of the Guayas river leading to Guayaquil, the great port and largest city; the bold headlands and beaches of Manabí and Santa Elena, where the Humboldt Current meets warmer tropical water; and, far out in the ocean, the volcanic Galapagos archipelago, a thousand kilometres west, where the equator crosses islands of black lava and unique wildlife. Ecuador's lights range from urban landmarks and harbour beacons to the markers of the most famous islands in the world, and many of them double as viewpoints and tourist attractions in a country where the coast is a centre of fishing, shipping and beach tourism.

## 1. Faro del Cerro Santa Ana, Guayaquil

The most visited lighthouse in Ecuador stands not on a remote cape but atop the Cerro Santa Ana, the hill that rises above the old quarter of Guayaquil, the country's largest city and main port. The faro crowns the hill at the top of a stairway of 444 steps that climbs through the brightly painted Las Peñas neighbourhood, the restored colonial heart of the city. From the lighthouse the view sweeps over the Guayas river, the Malecón promenade and the sprawling metropolis below. The blue-and-white tower beside a small chapel has become an emblem of Guayaquil, photographed far more often than it guides any vessel up the river.

## 2. La Chocolatera, Salinas

La Chocolatera, at the tip of the Santa Elena peninsula near the resort town of Salinas, marks the westernmost point of mainland Ecuador. The light stands on a rocky headland where the Pacific swell breaks in churning brown foam — the chocolate that gives the place its name — and where, on a clear day, you can sense the curve of the continent's edge. The point lies within a naval reserve and is a favoured spot for watching whales, sea lions and the great Pacific sunsets. The setting, at the very end of the land with the open ocean stretching to the horizon, is among the most dramatic on the Ecuadorian coast.

## 3. Faro de San Lorenzo, Manta

The lighthouse at Punta San Lorenzo, on the Manabí coast south of the great tuna port of Manta, marks a bold headland on the central Ecuadorian shore. The light stands on a cliff above beaches and surf, within a coastal area of dry tropical forest and dramatic ocean views. San Lorenzo is a popular excursion from Manta, with its fishing village, its waves that draw surfers, and its clifftop walks looking out over the Pacific. The headland, jutting into the ocean where the cold and warm currents meet, sees abundant marine life and is a fine place to watch the humpback whales that migrate along this coast.

## 4. El Faro de Bahía de Caráquez

Bahía de Caráquez, on the estuary of the Chone river in Manabí, is an elegant little resort city set on a sandy spit between river and sea. Its lighthouse marks the entrance to the estuary, guiding the fishing boats and pleasure craft that use the sheltered water behind the spit. The town, with its high-rise apartments along the waterfront, calls itself an eco-city and looks out over an estuary rich in birdlife, mangroves and the dolphins that frequent the channel. The light stands at the meeting of river and ocean, a quiet beacon on one of the prettiest stretches of the Manabí coast.

## 5. Salinas Yacht Club Light

At Salinas, the principal beach resort of the Santa Elena peninsula, a light marks the approach to the harbour and the yacht club at the western end of the long curving bay. Salinas is Ecuador's most popular seaside town, its high-rise hotels lining a sweep of sheltered beach where the warm water draws holidaymakers from Guayaquil and beyond. The light guides the sport-fishing boats and pleasure craft that work these waters, famed for marlin and sailfish, and the harbour beneath it bustles with the comings and goings of a resort that has been the playground of the Ecuadorian coast for a century.

## 6. Punta Ballena, Machalilla

Punta Ballena — Whale Point — lies within the Machalilla National Park on the Manabí coast, a protected area of dry forest, beaches and offshore islands. The light marks this headland on a coast renowned for the humpback whales that gather here between June and September to breed, drawing whale-watching boats from the village of Puerto López. Nearby lies Isla de la Plata, sometimes called the poor man's Galapagos for its seabird colonies of blue-footed boobies and frigatebirds. The setting, within one of Ecuador's most important coastal national parks, combines navigation with some of the richest marine wildlife on the mainland shore.

## 7. Faro de San Cristóbal, Galapagos

On San Cristóbal, the easternmost of the Galapagos islands and seat of the archipelago's provincial capital, a light marks the approach to Puerto Baquerizo Moreno, the administrative centre of the islands. The light guides the vessels that supply the archipelago and the tour boats that explore it, into a harbour where sea lions sprawl on the benches and beaches of the town itself. San Cristóbal, where Charles Darwin first landed in 1835, is a gateway to the islands, and its light stands at the threshold of the most celebrated wildlife sanctuary on earth, a thousand kilometres out in the Pacific.

## 8. Puerto Ayora Light, Santa Cruz

Puerto Ayora, on Santa Cruz island, is the largest town in the Galapagos and the hub of the islands' tourism, home to the Charles Darwin Research Station. The harbour light guides the cruise vessels, dive boats and inter-island ferries through the rocky approach to the busy bay, where the water teems with marine iguanas, rays and reef fish. Santa Cruz, with its giant tortoise reserves in the highlands and its lava tubes, is the heart of a visit to the archipelago, and the light marks the gateway to the islands' main port, a working aid amid one of the world's great natural wonders.

## 9. Punta Pitt, San Cristóbal

At the northeastern tip of San Cristóbal, Punta Pitt is a dramatic headland of eroded volcanic tuff, marked by a navigation light on one of the Galapagos's wilder corners. It is one of the few places in the islands where all three species of booby — blue-footed, red-footed and Nazca — nest together, and the eroded cliffs and green olivine beach make it a striking landfall. The light guides the tour vessels that visit this remote point, far from the settled towns, where the volcanic origins of the archipelago are written plainly in the layered rock above the surf.

## 10. Faro de Esmeraldas

On the northern coast, near the city of Esmeraldas at the mouth of the Esmeraldas river, a lighthouse marks the approach to one of Ecuador's principal northern ports, the terminus of the trans-Andean oil pipeline. The light guides the tankers and cargo ships through the river mouth into a port set on a coast of beaches, mangroves and the lush, rain-fed forests of the green province. Esmeraldas, with its Afro-Ecuadorian culture and its marimba music, marks the wetter, more tropical northern end of the Ecuadorian coast, and its light stands where the river meets the warm Pacific.
