Norway's coastline is among the longest in the world. Measured along every fjord, island, and inlet, it extends for more than 100,000 kilometres — a labyrinthine geography of deep water passages threading between mountains and skerries that has shaped Norwegian civilisation from the Viking age to the present. The same topography that made Norway a maritime power also made its coast exceptionally dangerous to navigate, and from the seventeenth century onward a systematic programme of lighthouse construction worked to light the passages, headlands, and offshore rocks that claimed ships on the route between Europe and the Arctic. The Kystverket (Norwegian Coastal Administration) now manages over 200 major lighthouses and thousands of smaller beacons. Many of the older stations are protected as heritage sites, and a number have been converted to accommodation or cultural venues. The ten lighthouses that follow represent the range and character of Norway's extraordinary maritime heritage. [Open the map](/map) to locate them along the full length of the Norwegian coast.

## 1. Lindesnes Lighthouse, Vest-Agder

Lindesnes is the southernmost point of Norway, a low but dramatic headland of pink Precambrian granite where the Skagerrak meets the North Sea. The lighthouse here holds the distinction of being the oldest in Norway — a coal-fire beacon was first established on the point in 1655, making it one of the oldest continuously lit stations in Northern Europe. The current white-painted cylindrical tower, the fourth or fifth structure on the site, dates from 1915 and stands roughly 17 metres tall; the light is 50 metres above sea level with a range of about 17 nautical miles. The light character is a white flash every ten seconds. A substantial lighthouse museum operates in the converted keeper's buildings, open from May to October, with one of the best collections of lighthouse artefacts in Scandinavia. The headland itself, lashed by waves and wind, conveys the extremity of this famous cape.

## 2. Kjeungskjær Lighthouse, Trøndelag

Kjeungskjær is one of Norway's most photographed lighthouses: a white wooden-framed lighthouse building perched on a bare skerry of polished rock just above the waterline in the Bjugn Archipelago south of Trondheim. The lighthouse, built in 1880, is a rare surviving example of the inhabited lighthouse on an exposed skerry — the keeper's dwelling and lighthouse equipment are all contained in a single multi-story wooden building that sits almost at sea level, wave-worn rock on all sides. The light character is a white flash every five seconds, visible for about 13 nautical miles through the coastal archipelago. The lighthouse is designated a heritage site by Riksantikvaren (the Directorate for Cultural Heritage). It is accessible by boat from Valsneset, and in summer the lighthouse sometimes receives visitors; the surrounding skerry landscape is extraordinary in its austerity.

## 3. Tungenes Lighthouse, Rogaland

Tungenes Lighthouse stands at the northern end of Jæren, the long flat agricultural coast south of Stavanger — the most featureless stretch of the Norwegian coast and one of the most hazardous, where the sea breaks directly onto sand beaches and rocks with no shelter. The lighthouse, built in 1828 and rebuilt in its current form in 1937, stands on a granite headland at the tip of the Tungenes peninsula, a welcome high point on this otherwise low coast. The white cylindrical tower, roughly 22 metres tall, produces a white flash every five seconds visible for about 17 nautical miles across the northern Jæren approaches. The lighthouse is managed as a heritage site and museum; the surrounding nature reserve is excellent for coastal walking and birdwatching, particularly during spring and autumn migration. Ferry services to the nearby island of Rennesøy pass close to the headland.

## 4. Slåtterøy Lighthouse, Vestland

Slåtterøy is a windswept skerry west of Haugesund at the outer edge of the Sunnhordland Archipelago, at the point where the open Atlantic is first encountered by vessels approaching the Norwegian coast from the west. The lighthouse, first established in 1849 and rebuilt in its current substantial stone tower form, stands about 18 metres tall on the bare rock; the light is some 37 metres above sea level with a range of approximately 18 nautical miles. The character is a white flash every five seconds. The station was of critical importance for the Bergen-route shipping that carried fish, timber, and passengers along the Norwegian coast; the nearby Ryvarden station (see below) was established around the same time to complete the coverage. Slåtterøy is accessible by boat from Sunnhordland; the lighthouse buildings are managed as accommodation by Slåtterøy fyrstasjon, one of several Norwegian lighthouses converted to cultural heritage stays.

## 5. Ryvarden Lighthouse, Vestland

Ryvarden occupies another outer-island position north of Slåtterøy, on a rocky islet in the Sunnhordland Archipelago that faces directly onto the open Norwegian Sea. The lighthouse, first lit in 1859 and rebuilt in its current form in 1913, is a red-painted brick cylindrical tower standing about 20 metres tall; the light is approximately 24 metres above sea level. Its white flash every five seconds is visible for about 17 nautical miles. Ryvarden Kulturfyr has developed the lighthouse buildings into a centre for contemporary art exhibitions, concerts, and accommodation, making it one of the most creatively active of Norway's repurposed lighthouse stations. The island is accessible by boat from Husnes or Sunnhordland; the combination of the raw seascape, the heritage lighthouse, and the art programme has made Ryvarden a destination in its own right during the summer season.

## 6. Strømtangen Lighthouse, Viken

Strømtangen fyr stands on the eastern shore of the Oslofjord, near Moss, marking the approach to Oslo through one of the longest inland fjord passages in Norway. The lighthouse, a white-painted octagonal wooden tower with an attached dwelling, was first established in the mid-nineteenth century on a low rocky point. The Oslofjord lighthouse tradition is older than most in Norway — the fjord was the approach to the medieval capital of Christiania (later Oslo), and navigation lights were essential for the heavy timber and cargo traffic on the fjord. Strømtangen is now automated; the surrounding area is accessible from Moss and the lighthouse can be viewed from the road along the fjord shore. The inner Oslofjord landscape — wooded shores, white cottages, and the characteristic smell of sea salt mixed with pine — provides a gentle contrast to the exposed Atlantic stations farther north.

## 7. Fladholmen Lighthouse, Vestfold

Fladholmen fyr stands on a small island in the Vestfjord, part of the Vestfold archipelago on the western side of the Oslofjord. The lighthouse is a characteristic nineteenth-century Norwegian coastal station — a white-painted masonry tower with keeper's dwelling attached, on a rocky islet accessible by boat from Nøtterøy or Tjøme. The Vestfold coast was historically one of the most important maritime regions in Norway: Sandefjord was the base of Norway's whaling fleet, and the fjord commerce between Tønsberg, Larvik, and Oslo sustained the region's economy for centuries. Fladholmen is automated; the island and lighthouse can be reached by private boat, and the surrounding archipelago is a popular cruising destination for Norwegian and Swedish yachtsmen.

## 8. Homlungen Lighthouse, Viken

Homlungen fyr stands at the entrance to Hvalerfjorden in the southernmost part of the Oslofjord, on one of the Hvaler islands near the Swedish border. The lighthouse, first lit in 1867, is a typical Norwegian coastal tower in white-painted masonry, standing about 16 metres tall on a rocky point. Its light marks the main approach to the inner fjord from the Skagerrak. The Hvaler Archipelago, some 800 islands and skerries, is a popular summer destination for Norwegian boaters, and Homlungen is one of several lighthouse stations in the archipelago. The lighthouse is now automated; some of the surrounding buildings have been converted to holiday accommodation. The Skjærhalden ferry service provides access to the Hvaler islands, and the lighthouse is within the Ytre Hvaler National Park.

## 9. Kavringen Lighthouse, Oslo

Kavringen fyr stands on a rocky islet in the inner Oslofjord, within easy sight of the city of Oslo. The lighthouse is one of the innermost in the fjord system and marks a turning point in the shipping lane approaching Oslo Harbour. The current white cylindrical tower dates from the late nineteenth century and is a modest but functional structure typical of the inner fjord stations. The surrounding Oslo Archipelago — Nakholmen, Bleikøya, and dozens of smaller islets — is the city's playground for kayakers, swimmers, and small-boat sailors. Kavringen is visible from the ferry to the Aker Brygge waterfront and from the boats that serve the outer islands. The lighthouse is automated and active; the islet itself is accessible by kayak in calm weather.

## 10. Struten Lighthouse, Nordland

Struten fyr, north of the Arctic Circle in Nordland, represents Norway's extensive lighthouse heritage in the far north. Positioned on the Helgeland coast — a labyrinthine stretch of outer islands, reefs, and coastal mountains that includes the famous Torghatten mountain with its natural tunnel arch — Struten marks a passage in the inner led (inshore navigation channel) that was used by the Hurtigruten coastal express steamer from its inauguration in 1893. The lighthouse, a white-painted masonry tower with attached dwelling, is typical of the Helgeland stations: compact, heavily built to withstand arctic winters, and positioned on rock at the water's edge. The Helgeland coast is accessible from Bodø or Mo i Rana; the inner led through this region is one of the most scenically spectacular in Norway.

## Planning Your Visit

Norwegian lighthouses are at their most dramatic in stormy autumn and winter conditions, but the most practical visiting season is late May through September, when daylight is extended (and north of the Arctic Circle, midnight sun conditions apply in June and July), ferry and boat connections are frequent, and lighthouse museums and cultural venues are open. Many coastal lighthouse stations that have been converted to accommodation require advance booking, particularly in the peak summer weeks of July and early August. The Kystverket website and the Norsk Fyrliste (Norwegian Light List) provide technical data on all stations. For heritage accommodation at historic lighthouse stations, the selection is extensive and often spectacular.
