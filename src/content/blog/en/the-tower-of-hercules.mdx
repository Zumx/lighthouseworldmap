On a rocky peninsula at the northwestern tip of Spain, where the Atlantic batters the Galician coast, stands a tower that has been showing a light to sailors for almost two thousand years. The Tower of Hercules at A Coruna is the oldest functioning lighthouse in the world and the only lighthouse surviving from Roman antiquity still in active service. Its weathered ashlar walls enclose a Roman core that predates almost every other structure in continuous use anywhere on Earth, and the light at its summit still sweeps the approaches to one of Galicia's busiest ports.

## A Roman Beacon on the Edge of the World

The Romans called this corner of Iberia Finisterrae, the end of the earth, and to a Mediterranean people the storm-lashed Atlantic seemed a frontier of the known world. The tower was built, most likely in the late first or early second century AD, to guide ships along this dangerous coast and into the natural harbour that the Romans knew as Brigantium. An inscription carved into a rock near the base records the name of the architect, Gaius Sevius Lupus, from the province of Lusitania, who dedicated the work to Mars. Few ancient monuments preserve their builder's name so directly.

The original tower was a square structure of several storeys, with an external ramp winding around it to allow fuel to be carried up to the fire that burned at the top. For centuries that flame, fed by wood or oil, was the lighthouse's only light source, visible to ships feeling their way along a coast notorious for fog and sudden squalls.

## Legends of Hercules and Breogan

The tower's name comes from a myth that links it to the hero Hercules. According to the legend, Hercules fought the giant Geryon here for three days and three nights, slew him, and buried his head beneath the spot where the tower would rise. A skull and crossbones appear on A Coruna's coat of arms in memory of the tale. Galician tradition adds another layer: the Celtic king Breogan is said to have built a great tower from whose top his sons glimpsed the distant shores of Ireland, an origin story that ties Galicia to the Celtic world and is still cherished in regional identity.

These stories matter not as history but because they show how deeply the tower is woven into the culture of the region. For the people of A Coruna it has never been merely a navigational aid; it is the emblem of the city itself.

## Decline and Neglect

After the fall of Rome the tower endured, but its purpose faded. Through the medieval centuries it stood largely dark, used at times as a fortress or a quarry of dressed stone for nearby building. The Roman ramp decayed, and the upper works crumbled. By the seventeenth century the structure was a battered shell, still impressive in its bulk but no longer a reliable light.

That neglect, paradoxically, helped preserve the Roman fabric, since no one rebuilt it from scratch. When attention finally returned in the age of Enlightenment, engineers found a substantial Roman core waiting to be brought back to life.

## The Eighteenth-Century Restoration

The decisive intervention came between 1788 and 1791, when the engineer Eustaquio Giannini undertook a thorough restoration under the Bourbon crown. He encased the Roman walls in a new outer skin of neoclassical masonry, added an internal staircase to replace the lost ramp, and raised the tower to a new height, crowning it with a proper lantern. The careful work preserved the ancient core while giving the lighthouse the form it largely keeps today, rising to about fifty-five metres.

This restoration turned a ruin back into a working light and set a standard for respectful treatment of an ancient monument. Visitors who climb the internal stair can still see, through openings, the Roman masonry concealed within the eighteenth-century shell.

## A World Heritage Light

In 2009 UNESCO inscribed the Tower of Hercules on the World Heritage List, recognising its unique status as the only Roman lighthouse still operating and a monument of nearly two millennia of maritime history. The surrounding headland has been laid out as a sculpture park, with works that echo the tower's themes of sea and legend, and the site draws visitors from around the world.

The light remains an active aid to navigation, maintained alongside Spain's modern network of coastal lights. Up the Galician coast and around the peninsula stand many later towers that share its work: the Faro da Estaca de Bares marks the northernmost point of mainland Spain, while Cabo Prior, the Faro de Cabo Home and the Faro do Tenlo guide ships through the rias of the Galician shore. Each is a descendant, in spirit, of the Roman beacon at A Coruna.

## Galicia's Wider Coast of Lights

To understand the Tower of Hercules is to enter the wider world of Spanish lighthouses, which ring a coastline of extraordinary variety. On the rugged Cantabrian shore stand the Faro de Tazones and the Faru de Lluces in Asturias, and the Faro de Llanes above its harbour. Eastward along the Mediterranean the character changes entirely, with the Faro de Cabo de Gata rising above the volcanic cliffs of Almeria, the Faro de Cabo de Palos near the Mar Menor, and the Faro de Portmán marking an old mining coast.

These lights span Roman antiquity, the great age of the Fresnel lens, and the modern era of automation, yet they share a common purpose with the tower at A Coruna. Standing on its gallery, looking out over the grey Atlantic, it is easy to feel the continuity: the same sea, the same need, the same human answer raised stone upon stone across the centuries.

## Visiting Today

A Coruna makes the tower easy to reach, set on a green headland a short distance from the old town. The climb up the internal staircase passes the exposed Roman walls before emerging on the gallery, where the wind off the ocean is rarely still. Below, the sculpture park and a reconstructed Roman-style inscription stone reward a slower walk, and the city's seafood restaurants make a fitting end to the day.

For anyone drawn to maritime heritage, the Tower of Hercules is a pilgrimage: a place where the ancient and the working sea-light are one and the same, and where the line of keepers and engineers reaches unbroken from a Roman architect to the present.
