Brazil's coastline extends more than 7,000 kilometres from the border with French Guiana in the north to the Uruguayan frontier in the south, encompassing mangrove estuaries, coral reefs, barrier islands, exposed Atlantic headlands, and the delta systems of major rivers. Navigating this coast has never been straightforward: shallow banks extend far offshore in the north-east, the trades and the Equatorial Counter Current complicate approaches near the equator, and the southern coast faces persistent fog and storm swell from the South Atlantic. The Diretoria de Hidrografia e Navegação of the Brazilian Navy has maintained navigational lights here since the nineteenth century, building a collection of towers that ranges from sturdy colonial-era masonry on the Bahian headlands to iron-framework structures assembled from parts fabricated in Europe and shipped to Brazil in the early twentieth century.

## 1. Farol da Barra, Salvador, Bahia

The Farol da Barra at Salvador in Bahia is Brazil's most iconic lighthouse: a squat white octagonal tower perched on the tip of the headland guarding the entrance to the Baía de Todos os Santos, the All Saints Bay. The first lighthouse on this site was built in 1698, making it one of the oldest in the Western Hemisphere. The current tower dates from 1839, stands 22 metres, and shows a white and red sector light visible for 28 nautical miles — the red sector covering the shoal areas of the bay mouth and the white sector the safe passage. The lighthouse base is occupied by the Museu Náutico da Bahia, which holds navigational instruments, charts, and maritime artefacts from the colonial and imperial periods. The site is open daily; the lighthouse is active and maintained by the Navy. The sunset view from Farol da Barra across the bay entrance is one of the most celebrated in Salvador.

## 2. Farol de Santa Marta, Santa Catarina

The Farol de Santa Marta on the headland of the same name in the Santa Catarina coast was completed in 1891 by the French firm Barbier & Fenestre, which supplied the iron tower sections prefabricated in France. The tower stands 27 metres on a cliff 67 metres above sea level, giving a focal plane height of 94 metres and a light range of 25 nautical miles. The character is a white flash every fifteen seconds. Santa Marta is one of the most photographed lighthouses in Brazil: the tower's striped black-and-white daymark stands against the Atlantic on a headland accessible only by boat or on foot across beach dunes. A small community of fishermen and lighthouse workers occupies the headland; accommodation is available in simple pousadas in the village, and the setting has attracted a small but consistent community of lighthouse visitors for decades. The headland is a whale-watching point during the humpback migration from June to November.

## 3. Farol das Conchas, Paraná

The Farol das Conchas at Pontal do Sul, Paraná, marks the northern entrance to the Paranaguá Bay estuary, one of Brazil's most important port approaches and the outlet for vast quantities of soya and grain from the interior. The lighthouse was completed in 1883 as a classic masonry tower of 18 metres, painted white with a red lantern room. The light character is a group of two white flashes every ten seconds, with a range of 16 nautical miles. The Paranaguá approaches involve complex navigation through shallow channels that shift with seasonal sediment movements, and the light at Conchas is the primary seaward mark for vessels entering. The lighthouse is within the APA de Guaraqueçaba protected area; the tower is visible from the beach at Pontal do Sul.

## 4. Farol de São Tomé, Rio de Janeiro State

The Farol de São Tomé guards the approach to the oil-producing region of Campos, where the Cabo de São Tomé — the most easterly cape of Rio de Janeiro State — extends into the Atlantic. The current tower was completed in 1904 to replace an earlier light, and stands 30 metres in white masonry on a low sandy cape. Its flashing white character is visible for 24 nautical miles over the approaches to the Campos Basin, now heavily trafficked by supply vessels for the offshore oil platforms of the pre-salt layer. The lighthouse is maintained by the Brazilian Navy; the village of São Tomé nearby has grown in recent decades with the oil industry but retains a fishing community.

## 5. Farol da Ponta dos Garças, Espírito Santo

The Farol da Ponta dos Garças on the northern coast of Espírito Santo state marks the approach to Vitória, the capital of the state and a major export terminal for iron ore from the Quadrilátero Ferrífero of Minas Gerais. The tower, completed in 1889 in the characteristic ironwork style imported from Europe during the Brazilian Empire period, stands 12 metres on a headland. Its sector light — white over the safe channel, red over the shoals — covers the complex approaches to Vitória Bay, where the tidal flat system extends widely and the dredged channel requires precise alignment. The lighthouse is active; the Vitória Bay waterfront is accessible and provides views of the structure.

## 6. Farol de Itacolomi, Bahia

The Farol de Itacolomi on the island of the same name off the coast of Belmonte in southern Bahia marks one of the most historically significant coastal waypoints in Brazil. Portuguese navigators used the distinctive hill profile of Itacolomi — the name means 'young stone' in Tupi — as a landmark from the earliest days of the colonial coast route between Bahia and Rio de Janeiro. The lighthouse, erected in 1893, stands 12 metres on the island's highest ground. Its group-flashing character is visible for 16 nautical miles. The surrounding waters are part of the Corumbau and Prado coast, which retains some of the least disturbed Atlantic Forest in Brazil.

## 7. Farol da Ilha Rasa, Paraná

The Farol da Ilha Rasa ('Flat Island Lighthouse') in Paraná Bay was built in 1872 on a low sand island near the southern entrance to Paranaguá Bay and marks one of the two principal navigational channels into this important port. The iron-framed tower was fabricated in England and assembled on site — a common approach for remote Brazilian lighthouse sites in this period. The tower stands 18 metres and shows a single white flash every five seconds, range 15 nautical miles. The island is within a protected natural area; access is by boat from the Paranaguá waterfront.

## 8. Farol de Albardão, Rio Grande do Sul

The Farol de Albardão, completed in 1859 on the barrier beach south of the Lagoa dos Patos outlet in Rio Grande do Sul, is the southernmost lighthouse in Brazil and one of its most isolated. The tower stands 31 metres on a flat sandy peninsula where the Patos lagoon meets the Atlantic — a location with no road access. The light character is a single white flash every ten seconds with a range of 21 nautical miles, marking the dangerous bar at the entrance to the Lagoa dos Patos, one of the largest coastal lagoons in the world and the waterway serving Porto Alegre and the southern interior. The lighthouse is staffed; access is by boat or helicopter.

## 9. Farol de Macaé, Rio de Janeiro State

The Farol de Macaé marks the port of Macaé on the northern Rio de Janeiro coast, which has grown from a small fishing town to a major offshore oil services base since the development of the Campos Basin in the 1970s. The lighthouse tower, built in 1893, stands 20 metres on a rocky promontory and shows a flashing white character visible for 18 nautical miles. The port handles constant traffic from supply vessels, anchor handlers, and crew boats servicing the offshore platforms, and the lighthouse remains an active navigational reference for this traffic. The Macaé foreshore has been developed with a maritime park where the lighthouse can be approached.

## 10. Farol dos Abrolhos, Bahia

The Farol dos Abrolhos on the Abrolhos Archipelago, 65 kilometres off the Bahia coast, marks one of the most ecologically important marine areas in the South Atlantic and one of the most historically significant navigational hazards on the Brazilian coast. Charles Darwin visited the Abrolhos reef complex aboard HMS Beagle in 1832. The lighthouse, completed in 1859 on Ilha Santa Bárbara, stands 25 metres on a low coral island and shows a white flashing character visible for 15 nautical miles, warning of the extensive reef system. The Abrolhos National Marine Park encompasses the islands and reefs; access is by organised tour boat from Caravelas and is strictly regulated. Humpback whales gather in the warm waters around Abrolhos from June to November in one of the largest concentrations on the South American coast.

## Visiting Brazil's Lighthouses

Brazil's lighthouse heritage spans an immense geographic range, and visiting comprehensively requires considerable planning. The most accessible and historically significant sites — Farol da Barra in Salvador and Farol de Santa Marta — are well set up for visitors and can be reached independently. The more remote towers, particularly in the far south and the offshore islands, require boat transport and advance planning. The Brazilian Navy controls most active stations and access policies vary; some lighthouses have adjacent maritime museums or heritage facilities that are independently managed and more freely accessible.
