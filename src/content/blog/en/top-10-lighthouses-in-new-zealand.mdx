New Zealand's position in the South Pacific, flanked by the Tasman Sea and the Pacific Ocean, gives it a coastline of exceptional length and navigational complexity. The country stretches nearly 1,600 kilometres from Cape Reinga in the far north to the Bluff in the south, with a deeply indented coastline of more than 15,000 kilometres when all the sounds, harbours, and island shores are measured. The waters around both main islands are demanding: the West Coast of the South Island faces unimpeded Tasman swells, the Cook Strait between the islands is notorious for its violent wind-wave conditions, and the East Cape region of the North Island fronts the Pacific with few natural harbours. New Zealand's lighthouse history runs from the early colonial period — the first lighthouse was established at Pencarrow Head on Wellington Harbour in 1859 — through a concentrated period of Victorian construction. Today Maritime New Zealand (formerly the Maritime Safety Authority) maintains the network of automated lights. [Open the map](/map) to find them across the full length of both islands.

## 1. Cape Reinga Lighthouse, Northland

Cape Reinga, at the northern tip of the Aupouri Peninsula, is the most evocative lighthouse in New Zealand. For Maori, Te Reinga Wairua — the leaping place of spirits — is where the spirits of the dead descend to return to the ancestral homeland of Hawaiki. The lighthouse, a white cylindrical tower 10.6 metres tall perched on a cliff 165 metres above sea level, was built in 1941 on the site of an earlier structure. Its flashing white light is visible for about 26 nautical miles across the convergence of the Tasman Sea and the Pacific Ocean. The meeting of the two bodies of water is visible from the lighthouse grounds as a line of disturbed water; the boiling, standing waves where the currents collide are visible on calm days from the lookout below the tower. The cape is accessible by road from Kaitaia; the lighthouse grounds are open daily and the site is one of the most visited in Northland.

## 2. Castlepoint Lighthouse, Wairarapa

Castlepoint Lighthouse stands on a rocky reef headland on the Wairarapa Coast of the North Island's eastern shore, a dramatic position where a limestone bluff rises above a small lagoon and beach. The white cylindrical tower, built in 1913, stands 26 metres tall on the reef; the light is 52 metres above sea level and visible for 19 nautical miles across the Pacific approaches to the Wairarapa coast. The light character is a white flash every fifteen seconds. The lighthouse is accessible by a walkway across the reef causeway from Castlepoint village, and the surrounding area — the lagoon, the reef, the surf beach — is a popular coastal recreation area managed by the Department of Conservation. The lighthouse is automated and active; the exterior is open to visitors year-round. The wild coast stretching north and south from Castlepoint is one of the least developed on the North Island.

## 3. Nugget Point Lighthouse, Catlins

Nugget Point, on the southern Catlins Coast of the South Island, takes its name from the stack of rounded rock "nuggets" that extend into the sea from the point. The lighthouse, a white cylindrical tower built in 1870, stands on the very end of the point above the stacks; the light is 76 metres above sea level and visible for about 26 nautical miles across the approaches to the Catlins coast and Toetoes Bay. The light character is a white flash every fifteen seconds. The headland, accessible by road from Owaka, is a Department of Conservation reserve rich in wildlife: New Zealand sea lions, elephant seals, Hooker's sea lions, and various seabirds including yellow-eyed penguins (hoiho) are seen regularly on the rocks below the lighthouse. The walk to the tower tip takes about 15 minutes from the car park and is one of the most rewarding short lighthouse walks in New Zealand.

## 4. Akaroa Head Lighthouse, Banks Peninsula

Akaroa Head Lighthouse stands on the southern entrance to Akaroa Harbour, the drowned volcanic crater that forms the most distinctive feature of Banks Peninsula south of Christchurch. The original lighthouse was built in 1880; the present white cylindrical tower dates from 1980 and stands about 10 metres tall on the headland, with the light elevated to around 77 metres above sea level. The character is a white flash every ten seconds with a range of about 14 nautical miles. Banks Peninsula is the only significant volcanic landform on the South Island's east coast and was the site of New Zealand's sole French settlement, at Akaroa village. The headland is accessible via a steep road from Akaroa township; the lighthouse is active and automated, and the surrounding land is farmed. The view of the harbour entrance and the Pacific beyond is excellent.

## 5. East Cape Lighthouse, Gisborne Region

East Cape is the easternmost point of the New Zealand mainland and the first land to see the sun rise each day (except on outlying Chatham Islands). The lighthouse, a white cylindrical tower built in 1922, stands on a steep rocky headland about 154 metres above the Pacific. The approach involves a climb of over 800 steps from the car park below — one of the most demanding lighthouse walks in the country. The light character is a white flash every ten seconds, visible for about 19 nautical miles across the Pacific. The cape is off the East Cape Road, one of New Zealand's most scenic coastal drives, winding between Opotiki and Gisborne through a succession of Maori communities, beaches, and headlands. The isolated location, the physical challenge of the climb, and the significance of the site as the first place on the mainland to greet each new day give East Cape a particular atmosphere.

## 6. Tauroa Point Lighthouse, Northland

Tauroa Point lighthouse marks the entrance to the Hokianga Harbour on Northland's west coast, one of the most significant harbours in New Zealand's early Maori and European history. The Hokianga was the arrival point of the great Polynesian navigators and the area where missionaries first established themselves in the north. The lighthouse, a modest white structure, stands on the northern headland of the harbour entrance, guiding vessels across the notorious Hokianga Bar — a shifting sandbar at the river mouth that has wrecked many vessels. The surrounding coastline is one of New Zealand's most remote and least-visited, facing the full force of the Tasman Sea across vast rolling surf beaches. The lighthouse is automated; the area is within the Waipua Forest and Hokianga region accessible from Dargaville or Rawene.

## 7. Godley Head Lighthouse (Disused), Canterbury

The Godley Head Lighthouse, built in 1905 on the northern headland of the entrance to Lyttelton Harbour — Christchurch's port — is now disused and listed as a heritage structure. The white conical tower, standing about 7 metres tall on the 135-metre headland, was the guide into one of New Zealand's most important commercial ports. Lyttelton Harbour is the main port for the South Island's Canterbury Plains, and the lighthouse marked the northern approach past rocks and shoals. The headland is accessible via the Godley Head Walkway from Taylor's Mistake Beach, one of Christchurch's finest coastal walking routes. The disused lighthouse, the remaining keeper's cottage, and the extensive First and Second World War coastal defence fortifications on the headland create an unusual combination of maritime and military heritage.

## 8. Point Jerningham Lighthouse, Wellington

Point Jerningham Lighthouse stands on the eastern shore of Wellington Harbour, guiding vessels through the Cook Strait approaches and into the harbour. The Cook Strait is one of the world's most notoriously turbulent bodies of water, where regular southerly gales funnel through the narrow passage between the North and South Islands, and the Wellington Harbour entrance requires careful navigation in bad weather. The small lighthouse at Point Jerningham, on the Hataitai suburb shore, is a modest automated structure but holds an important position in the navigation of one of the most challenging maritime approaches in New Zealand. The surrounding hillside suburb offers excellent views of the harbour and the ferries that make the three-hour crossing to Picton several times daily.

## 9. Bean Rock Lighthouse, Waitemata Harbour

Bean Rock Lighthouse stands on a small rock at the entrance to the Tamaki Channel in Waitemata Harbour, Auckland. The small white lighthouse, built in 1871 as a "spark-plug" style cast-iron structure, was originally staffed by a keeper who lived in the cylindrical dwelling at the base of the light. It is now automated and stands as an iconic feature of the Auckland harbour vista — visible from the ferry to Waiheke Island and from Kohimarama Beach. The lighthouse was built to mark Bean Rock, a navigational hazard at the junction of several channels in the upper harbour. Though a modest structure by comparison with the great coastal towers, Bean Rock is historically significant as one of Auckland's original lighthouse installations and a familiar element of the city's maritime identity.

## 10. Katiki Point Lighthouse, Otago

Katiki Point Lighthouse stands on a headland at the southern end of Shag Point, on the South Island's Otago coast between Oamaru and Palmerston. The white wooden lighthouse, built in 1878 on a cliff above the sea, is automated and active; its light marks the point and the rocks extending into the Pacific below. The surrounding Department of Conservation reserve is significant for wildlife: rare yellow-eyed penguins nest in the scrub below the lighthouse, fur seals haul out on the rocks, and the area is considered one of the most accessible penguin-watching locations on the South Island. The lighthouse is reached via the Shag Point Recreation Reserve road from the coastal highway; the combination of the working lighthouse, the wildlife, and the views south toward the Otago Peninsula makes this an excellent coastal detour.

## Planning Your Visit

New Zealand's lighthouse network spans an enormous geographic range, and no single journey covers all the highlights. The Cape Reinga and Castlepoint lighthouses are day-trip accessible from their nearest towns; East Cape and Nugget Point require a specific itinerary. The best visiting conditions are from November to April, during the Southern Hemisphere summer and early autumn. The Department of Conservation manages most of the coastal reserves around lighthouses; opening hours for any lighthouse with staffed facilities should be confirmed in advance.
