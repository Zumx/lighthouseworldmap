A lighthouse made redundant by satellite navigation is a beautiful, enormous liability. The light that once justified every penny of its upkeep is switched off or reduced to a cheap solar lantern, and what remains is a salt-blasted tower, often on a site reachable only by boat, demanding money it no longer earns. Saving such a building is rarely a matter of sentiment alone. It is an exercise in hard arithmetic — capital costs, running costs, ownership models and revenue streams — and the towers that survive are usually the ones where those numbers can be made, however precariously, to balance.

## Why authorities want rid of them

The starting point is the position of the lighthouse authorities. Bodies such as Trinity House in England and Wales, the Northern Lighthouse Board in Scotland and the United States Coast Guard automated their towers through the late twentieth century to cut the heavy cost of resident keepers. Automation removed the people but not the buildings, leaving these agencies as reluctant landlords of historic structures with no operational need for the living quarters, the outbuildings or, increasingly, the towers themselves. Maintaining a remote station purely as heritage is a cost no navigation budget is designed to carry, and so the pressure to transfer, lease or sell has been relentless.

## How ownership changes hands

The mechanisms vary by country but share a logic. In the United States the National Historic Lighthouse Preservation Act of 2000 created a formal pipeline: surplus lighthouses are offered first, at no cost, to public bodies and qualified non-profits willing to preserve them and provide public access. Towers that find no such steward are then sold to the highest bidder at public auction, and over the years these sales have ranged from token sums to several hundred thousand dollars for accessible, characterful properties. Elsewhere, long leases, peppercorn rents to trusts, and outright sales to private owners all feature. The common thread is that the authority sheds the liability, and a new owner takes on both the asset and its appetite for cash.

## The capital cost of rescue

Restoration bills are formidable, and the location is the multiplier. The fabric problems are predictable — failed renders, corroded ironwork, rotten window frames, water ingress, lantern glazing to replace — but doing the work on an isolated rock or headland turns ordinary trades into a logistical campaign. Materials and scaffolding must be landed by boat or flown in by helicopter, weather windows are short, and skilled craftsmen must be housed on site. A modest lantern-room overhaul and re-glazing can run into tens of thousands; a full structural restoration of a major offshore tower can climb well into seven figures. These are the sums that decide, more than any heritage listing, whether a tower is realistically savable.

## Where the money comes from

No single source usually covers a rescue; the funding is stitched together. Heritage grant bodies — in Britain the National Lottery Heritage Fund and historic-buildings agencies, with comparable schemes across Europe and North America — supply much of the capital, typically on condition of public benefit and access. Local authorities, harbour trusts and tourism bodies chip in where a saved lighthouse promises to anchor a visitor economy. Charitable appeals and membership trusts raise the rest, often pound by hard-won pound. Private buyers fund their own restorations outright, betting that a converted keeper's cottage or holiday let will eventually repay the outlay. The package matters as much as the total, because grant conditions shape what the building must afterwards become.

## Making the saved tower pay

A rescued lighthouse cannot simply be mothballed; it must generate enough income to cover its own perpetual maintenance, or it will slide back into ruin. The proven models are few but effective. Holiday accommodation is the workhorse: keepers' cottages, and occasionally the towers themselves, let to visitors who pay a premium for the romance of sleeping beside the sea. Museums, visitor centres and paid tower climbs draw day-trippers and fund a small staff. Cafés, weddings and events squeeze revenue from the site and its setting. The most successful projects blend several of these, spreading risk across the seasons, because a single bad summer can otherwise undo years of careful budgeting.

## When the sums refuse to add up

Not every tower can be saved, and the economics are honest about it. Stations on tide-washed rocks, with no room for accommodation and access only in calm weather, offer almost no route to revenue, and their restoration costs are the highest of all. Some are simply too remote, too small or too dangerous to open to the public, and for these the realistic options narrow to minimal weatherproofing, managed decline, or eventual loss to the sea. Charitable trusts must make cold-eyed choices about which buildings their limited funds can sustain, and the painful truth is that affection alone keeps no tower standing once the rain gets into the structure.

## The hidden cost of running it on

The capital bill to restore a tower is only the opening payment; the harder economics lie in the years that follow. A saved lighthouse is a building of demanding materials in a punishing climate, and its maintenance schedule never relents. Salt air corrodes metalwork and eats render; spray finds every imperfect joint; the lantern glazing weathers and must be renewed; lime and limewash on a traditional tower need regular reapplication. Insurance for a property in such an exposed position is rarely cheap, and access for any repair carries the same boat-or-helicopter premium that inflated the original restoration. A trust that wins a grant for the headline rescue but has no plan for this grinding annual outlay has merely bought time, not security. The smartest projects build an endowment or a dependable income stream from the outset, treating the perpetual upkeep, not the restoration, as the real test of whether a tower can survive.

## A balance worth striking

For all the harsh arithmetic, lighthouses are saved, and in growing numbers, because the equation has more than one side. Against the costs sit the pull of the buildings themselves, the visitors they draw, the regeneration they can seed in a struggling coastal town, and a public affection that opens wallets where a spreadsheet alone would not. The towers that endure are those where heritage value, a workable income and committed stewardship meet. Each rescue is a small, deliberate bargain struck between the past and the future — proof that a thing can stop being useful and still, on the right terms, be very much worth keeping.
