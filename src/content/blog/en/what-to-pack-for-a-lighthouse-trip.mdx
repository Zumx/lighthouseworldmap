A lighthouse stands where it does for one reason: that is where the danger is. Headlands, reefs, exposed capes and the ends of breakwaters are the natural homes of beacons, and they are also the windiest, wettest, most weather-beaten places on any coast. Packing for a lighthouse trip is therefore not packing for a gentle seaside outing; it is packing for the exposed edge of the land, where conditions can swing from calm sunshine to driving spray in the time it takes to walk out along a pier. Get the kit right and these are some of the most exhilarating places to stand; get it wrong and you spend the visit cold, soaked and cutting it short.

## Footwear Comes First

Nothing shapes the comfort of a lighthouse visit more than what is on your feet. The approaches are rarely smooth: weathered granite cobbles, slick boardwalks, salt-greased steel pier decking, muddy clifftop paths and tide-washed rock all feature. A pair of supportive walking shoes or light hiking boots with a genuine grip pattern is the baseline. For rocky shore approaches and slippery harbour walls, a stiffer sole and ankle support pay off, and waterproof footwear is well worth it where paths cross splash zones or boggy moorland.

Inside the towers, the surfaces change again. Many open lighthouses have steep, worn spiral stairs of stone or iron, sometimes with shallow treads and a single handrail, occasionally finishing with a near-vertical ladder to the lantern. Smooth-soled fashion shoes are genuinely hazardous here. If a station requires you to remove shoes or supplies overshoes to protect historic floors, that is worth knowing in advance — but the general rule holds: dress your feet for rock and wind, not for the promenade.

## Layers and the Windproof Shell

The classic mistake is dressing for the temperature shown on a forecast rather than the wind chill on an exposed headland. A mild 15°C inland can feel bitter on a cape with a steady onshore gale, and the difference is almost entirely wind. The most valuable single garment for lighthouse visiting is a genuinely windproof, water-resistant shell — a hooded jacket that seals at the cuffs and hem. Under it, layers you can add and shed matter more than one heavy coat, because you will warm up on a climb and chill instantly when you step out onto an open gallery.

A warm hat and a buff or neck gaiter are small items that transform a windy visit, and gloves are no indulgence on a spring or autumn clifftop. In summer the opposite trap waits: open coasts reflect strong sun and offer no shade, so sunscreen, sunglasses and a sunhat earn their place even on a cool day. The sea breeze hides how much sun you are getting until the burn appears that evening.

## Protecting Cameras and Phones

Lighthouses are irresistible to photographers, and salt spray is merciless to electronics. The fine mist thrown up at an exposed station drifts much further than it looks and settles invisibly on lenses and screens, where dried salt is far harder to clean than fresh water. A simple dry bag or even a sealable freezer bag for a phone or compact camera is cheap insurance. A microfibre cloth kept in a sealed pocket lets you wipe a lens between shots before salt etches it.

For anyone carrying a proper camera, a lens hood doubles as spray protection, and a spare battery matters because cold drains them fast on an autumn headland. Consider, too, that the best light at a lighthouse is often the worst weather for gear: the dramatic storm-sky images that photographers prize are taken in exactly the spray-laden conditions most likely to ruin an unprotected camera.

## Water, Food and the Long Approach

It is easy to forget that many of the finest lighthouses sit at the end of a substantial walk with no facilities. A remote cape light may be a few kilometres from the nearest car park along an exposed path, and there will be no café, no tap and no shelter at the tower. Carrying water and a snack turns a tiring trudge into a pleasant outing, and on warm exposed walks dehydration creeps up faster than it does in shade.

Check opening times and tides before relying on anything at the site itself. Volunteer-run stations keep limited hours; island and causeway lights may only be reachable at low tide and can strand the careless when the water returns. A small daypack carrying water, a snack, the windproof shell, a hat and gloves, sun protection and your camera kit covers nearly every lighthouse visit, from a five-minute harbour stroll to a half-day clifftop hike.

## A Few Things Worth Adding

Beyond the essentials, a handful of items repeatedly earn their place. Binoculars transform a visit, letting you read the daymark patterns, watch the seabirds that colonise these cliffs, and pick out the offshore lights and reefs the tower was built to mark. A printed map or downloaded offline map matters where the mobile signal fails, which is often, exactly at the remote sites you most want to find. A power bank guards against a flat phone after a morning of photographs and navigation.

Finally, pack a little flexibility of plan. Coastal weather changes fast, ferries to island lights are cancelled in swell, and a tower that was open yesterday may be shut for maintenance today. The travellers who enjoy lighthouses most are those who treat the forecast as advice rather than promise, carry the kit to be comfortable in whatever blows in, and are happy to admire a tower from the windy base when the climb is closed.
