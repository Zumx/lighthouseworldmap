Romania's Black Sea coast stretches approximately 245 kilometres from the Danube delta in the north to the Bulgarian border near Vama Veche in the south. Short as it is, the coast presents a navigational complexity that belies its length: the shallow, shoal-infested waters off the Danube delta — which discharges more fresh water into the Black Sea than any other European river — the ancient port of Constanța, one of the oldest continuously occupied cities on the Black Sea, and a succession of holiday resorts whose commercial traffic requires careful management. Lighthouse history here begins with the Ottoman and then Romanian state navigational administrations of the nineteenth century. The lighthouse at Sulina, at the principal Danube mouth, was established by the European Commission of the Danube — an extraordinary international body created by the Congress of Paris in 1856 to manage the river's navigation — and remains a monument to the diplomatic and engineering effort required to keep the Danube delta open to shipping. Today the Romanian Naval Authority (Autoritatea Navală Română) maintains the coast's active lights.

## 1. Sulina Lighthouse (Old), Sulina

The old lighthouse at Sulina, at the mouth of the Sulina arm of the Danube — the only navigable channel maintained for sea-going vessels — was built in 1802 and repeatedly modified during the nineteenth century under the European Commission of the Danube. The Commission, constituted by the major European powers after the Crimean War, undertook enormous dredging, channelling, and marking operations to turn the delta's shifting distributaries into a reliable navigation corridor. The original white tower was supplemented and then replaced by the active lighthouse, but the old tower survives as a heritage structure in the town of Sulina — a place of extraordinary historical layering with cemeteries containing the graves of sailors from a dozen nations who died on this remote frontier. The tower marks what was once the primary gateway between the Black Sea and the entire river system of central Europe.

## 2. Constanța Lighthouse (Carol I / Genoese Lighthouse), Constanța

The lighthouse in the centre of Constanța's harbour is one of the most historically layered structures on the Black Sea. The present white cylindrical tower, 8.5 metres tall, built in 1860 and modified since, stands in the general area of the ancient Greek city of Tomis — the place of Ovid's exile — and near the remains of what may have been a Roman-era lighthouse or navigation tower. It is sometimes called the Genoese Lighthouse in reference to the medieval Genoese trading colony that controlled Black Sea commerce in the fourteenth and fifteenth centuries. The lighthouse marks the outer mole of Constanța's commercial harbour and is surrounded by a remarkable concentration of heritage: the Roman mosaic museum, the Ovid monument, the Orthodox Cathedral, and the Great Mosque are all within walking distance. The lighthouse is active and not open to visitors, but it is visible from the harbour promenade.

## 3. Tuzla Lighthouse, Tuzla

The cape at Tuzla, about 20 kilometres south of Constanța, carries a white lighthouse marking a modest promontory that is nonetheless significant for coastal navigation: the shoaling of the seafloor at this point creates hazards for vessels cutting the corner between the main Constanța approaches and the southern coastal route. The tower is a functional automated structure of the twentieth century, solar-powered and without keeper facilities. The surrounding landscape — low limestone cliffs, thin coastal scrub, and the wide expanse of the Black Sea — is characteristic of the Romanian Riviera south of Constanța, a stretch of coast that was heavily developed for resort tourism during the communist period.

## 4. Sfântu Gheorghe Lighthouse, Sfântu Gheorghe

The Sfântu Gheorghe lighthouse marks the southern arm of the Danube delta — the Sfântu Gheorghe channel — which is the least-modified of the three main delta distributaries and flows through a landscape of reed marshes, willow islands, and sand dunes that is among the wildest in Europe. The lighthouse, a white tower of modest height, guides vessels using the Sfântu Gheorghe channel, which is shallower than the maintained Sulina arm and used primarily by local fishing boats and tourist vessels. The delta village of Sfântu Gheorghe, at the river mouth, is accessible by passenger ferry from Tulcea and is one of the few delta communities with significant tourist infrastructure. The Danube Delta Biosphere Reserve — a UNESCO World Heritage Site — surrounds the lighthouse on all sides.

## 5. Mangalia Lighthouse, Mangalia

Mangalia, the southernmost resort town on the Romanian coast before the Bulgarian border, has a lighthouse marking the small port at the mouth of the Mangalia canal. The harbour is used by fishing vessels, a small naval facility, and pleasure craft. The lighthouse is a white tower of mid-twentieth century construction, functional in character. Mangalia itself is built over the ancient Greek city of Callatis, and excavations throughout the town have revealed Greek and Roman remains including a necropolis partially visible beside the modern seafront road. The juxtaposition of communist-era resort hotels, Pontic Greek archaeology, and a working lighthouse makes the town an unusual and underappreciated destination.

## 6. Midia Cape Lighthouse, Năvodari

Cap Midia, north of Constanța near the resort district of Năvodari, carries a lighthouse marking the cape and the offshore oil and gas platforms that have proliferated in Romanian waters since the discovery of Black Sea hydrocarbon deposits. The cape is also the location of the Port Midia, a bulk terminal primarily used for grain exports. The lighthouse is an automated modern installation and the cape is surrounded by petro-industrial infrastructure that contrasts sharply with the holiday resort character of the coast a few kilometres to the south. The shallow waters off Midia are a critical habitat for the critically endangered Black Sea bottlenose dolphin.

## 7. Capul Doloșman (Dolosman Cape) Light

The cape of Doloșman in the Dobrogea plateau, where the limestone escarpment of the Macin Mountains reaches the sea at the point where the Razim lagoon system meets the Danube delta zone, carries a minor navigational light marking the headland for vessels transiting the northern coastal sector. The cape is within the Danube Delta Biosphere Reserve and the surrounding area is of high ecological value. The ancient Geto-Dacian fortification of Argamum is located near the cape, adding archaeological interest to what is otherwise a working beacon on a remote headland.

## 8. Histria Area Beacons

The ancient Greek city of Histria, founded around 657 BC near the southern shore of Lake Sinoe north of Constanța, was the oldest Greek colony on the Black Sea coast and remained occupied into the Byzantine period. The lake has separated from the sea since antiquity but the area carries navigation marks associated with the shallow coastal zone and the approach to the Danube delta's southern sector. The Histria archaeological site is one of Romania's most significant classical sites, and the lighthouse heritage of this coast extends conceptually, at least, back to whatever navigation marks the ancient city established at the entrance to the lagoon harbour it once commanded.

## 9. Sulina New Lighthouse, Sulina

The active lighthouse at Sulina — the functional replacement for the old nineteenth-century tower — is a modern automated structure at the end of the Sulina channel's outer training wall. It marks the precise point where the Sulina arm of the Danube discharges into the Black Sea, a position of fundamental importance for the navigation of the entire Danubian trade corridor. The training walls extending several kilometres into the sea from the original river mouth were one of the great engineering achievements of the European Commission of the Danube, and the lighthouse at their end marks the seaward limit of the Commission's work. The walk out along the training wall to the lighthouse, across the flat delta landscape with the river on one side and the sea approaching on the other, is one of the most atmospheric lighthouse approaches in Europe.

## 10. Constanța Port Approach Lights

The outer approach to Constanța harbour — the busiest port on the Romanian Black Sea coast — is marked by a series of sector lights, approach buoys, and breakwater-end beacons that together form a comprehensive traffic separation system for the container ships, tankers, and bulk carriers that call here year-round. The outer mole light, a red-and-white striped steel tower of twentieth-century construction, is the most visible of these marks from the city waterfront. Constanța handles the bulk of Romanian trade and the lighthouse infrastructure here is primarily operational rather than historical, but the harbour walk gives views of working port activity alongside some of the finest Roman and Byzantine heritage on the Black Sea coast.

## Visiting Romania's Lighthouses

Constanța is the natural base for the coast: the historical lighthouse in the harbour area is visible from the promenade, and the Roman mosaic and Ovid square justify a half-day in the old city. Sulina requires the Tulcea ferry — a journey of several hours through the delta — and is most enjoyably combined with a stay of at least one night to experience the delta landscape at dawn and dusk. The delta ferry service operates year-round but with reduced frequency in winter. Sfântu Gheorghe is the quietest and most rewarding of the delta mouths, reachable by the same ferry system. Mangalia and the southern resort towns are best visited outside the July–August peak.
