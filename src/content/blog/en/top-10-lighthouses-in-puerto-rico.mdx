Puerto Rico carries one of the densest concentrations of nineteenth-century Spanish colonial lighthouses anywhere in the Caribbean. Between 1846 and 1898 the Spanish colonial administration built a coordinated system of faros around the island and its offshore cays, designed as a single illuminated chain that would guide vessels along the approaches to San Juan and through the dangerous reef-strewn passages of the Mona and Vieques channels. When the island passed to the United States after the Spanish-American War, the new administration inherited a nearly complete network, and many of those original towers — built of brick and rubble masonry, faced with stucco, and topped with imported French and Spanish optics — still stand on their headlands. Several have been restored as museums, parks, and visitor centres.

## Faro de Punta Higüero, Rincón

The lighthouse at Punta Higüero, on the western tip of the island near Rincón, marks the southern entrance to the Mona Passage — the strait separating Puerto Rico from the Dominican Republic, one of the busiest and most hazardous shipping channels in the region. The original tower dates from 1892, was damaged by the 1918 earthquake and tsunami, and was rebuilt. The surrounding park is now one of the best whale-watching sites on the island, with humpback whales passing close offshore between January and March. The white octagonal tower above the bluff is among the most photographed on the island.

## Faro de Punta Los Morrillos, Cabo Rojo

Standing on the dramatic limestone cliffs at the south-western corner of the island, the Cabo Rojo light — also recorded as Faro Los Morrillos — was completed in 1882. The white tower rises above pale cliffs that drop sheer into turquoise water, with the salt flats of the Cabo Rojo Salinas stretching inland behind it. The light marks the meeting of the Mona Passage and the Caribbean Sea and the approach to the southern coast. It is reached by a walk across exposed headland, and the surrounding cliffs are an unfenced and genuinely dangerous edge. The restored tower and its setting make it one of the most visited faros on the island.

## Faro de Las Cabezas de San Juan, Fajardo

At the north-eastern tip of Puerto Rico, the lighthouse at Las Cabezas de San Juan crowns a peninsula now protected as a nature reserve. Built in 1880, the neoclassical tower and keeper's quarters sit within a 316-acre reserve managed by the Conservation Trust, encompassing mangrove, dry forest, beach, and one of the island's bioluminescent lagoons. The faro itself houses an interpretation centre, and guided tours combine the maritime history of the light with the ecology of the surrounding habitats. The view from the gallery takes in the islands of Culebra and Vieques to the east.

## Faro Castillo del Morro, San Juan

The lighthouse atop the Castillo San Felipe del Morro is the most historically resonant of all the island's lights, standing within the sixteenth-century Spanish fortress that guards the entrance to San Juan Bay. A light has marked this point since 1846; the present tower, rebuilt several times after damage including bombardment during the 1898 war, sits at the highest point of the fortress walls. The fort and its light are now part of the San Juan National Historic Site administered by the United States National Park Service, and the headland draws more visitors than any other on the island.

## Faro de Culebrita

On the tiny uninhabited island of Culebrita, east of Culebra, stands one of the oldest operational lighthouse structures in the Caribbean, completed in 1886. The hexagonal tower and its colonial keeper's quarters sit on a hill above beaches considered among the finest in the archipelago. The light is reached only by boat and a hot uphill walk; the structure has suffered from neglect and storm damage and has been the subject of repeated restoration efforts. The reward for the climb is a panorama across the reef-fringed cays of the Spanish Virgin Islands.

## Faro de Isla de Mona

The ruins of the lighthouse on Isla de Mona — the Ruinas Faro de Isla de Mona — stand on the remote, waterless island in the middle of the Mona Passage, roughly halfway between Puerto Rico and Hispaniola. The cast-iron tower, fabricated and shipped in sections, was lit in 1900 and is unusual for being among the few American-era rather than Spanish-era structures in the group. Mona is now a nature reserve famous for its iguanas, seabird colonies, and caves, accessible only by special permit and a long open-water crossing. The lighthouse stands disused, a rusting skeleton against the island's limestone plateau.

## Faro de Isla Caja de Muertos

On the small island of Caja de Muertos, off the south coast near Ponce, the lighthouse of 1887 marks the southern shipping approaches. The island — its name translates as Coffin Island, from its silhouette — is a nature reserve reached by ferry from the Ponce area, with snorkelling reefs and hiking trails leading up to the restored faro on the high ground. The neoclassical tower is one of the better-preserved Spanish-era lights and commands a long view along the Caribbean coast.

## Faro de Punta Mulas, Vieques

The light at Punta Mulas guards the northern approach to Isabel Segunda, the main town of Vieques. Built in 1896, the square tower and attached keeper's dwelling sit just above the harbour and have served as a maritime museum. Vieques also carries the Faro de Puerto Ferro on its southern shore, a more isolated tower near the island's famous bioluminescent bay. The two Vieques lights, together with the faros on Culebra and Culebrita, illuminate the eastern passages where the deep Atlantic swell meets the shallows of the Spanish Virgin Islands.

## Faro de Punta Tuna, Maunabo

On the south-eastern corner of the main island, the Faro de Punta Tuna has marked the turn between the Caribbean and Atlantic coasts since 1893. The tower and quarters sit on a low headland between two beaches near Maunabo, and the structure has been restored and reopened to the public. The light remains active and is one of the few on the island still showing in its original location, character, and tower.

## Faro de Punta Borinquen, Aguadilla

At the north-western corner, the Punta Borinquen light marks the northern entrance to the Mona Passage opposite Punta Higüero across the strait's mouth. The current tower replaced an earlier Spanish faro damaged in the 1918 earthquake; the ruins of the original still stand nearby on the bluff above Aguadilla. Together the Borinquen and Higüero lights frame the Puerto Rican side of the passage, completing the western flank of the island's colonial illuminated ring.
